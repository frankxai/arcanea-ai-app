# Episode Two: Groundwork

The basement had a crack running along its eastern wall, mid-height, three millimeters wide at the top and tapering to nothing near the floor. Someone had filled it with mortar at some point, probably six years ago judging by the cure pattern. The mortar was already failing — stress fractures in the patch, the original crack speaking through. Not structural. The east wall wasn't load-bearing; that was the north wall, the one with the anchor bolt sunk into the harbor-district bedrock two floors below.

Lira knew all of this within thirty seconds of sitting down.

She hadn't gone looking. The Foundation Gate didn't require her to search — it presented, the way a good memory presented things you hadn't asked to remember. She'd learned, in twenty-two months of trying to live with it, that the useful skill wasn't suppression. It was triage. Which information mattered now, which could be filed, which could be ignored entirely. The crack in the east wall: filed. The anchor bolt: filed. The weight distribution of five people across sixty square feet of stone floor: present, background, the constant low frequency she'd stopped being able to separate from thought.

The man who'd been waiting for her — who had known her name — poured water from a ceramic jug into a tin cup and set it within her reach and then sat back down on his crate and looked at her the way she'd looked at Davan's hands in the market: steady, offering rather than demanding.

"You said there was a lot to talk about," she said.

"My name is Hadem. I've been running this safehouse for two years." He said it like a job title. Not pride, not apology. Just fact. "Before this one, a different one in the Millward district. Before that I was running."

"Like me."

"Like everyone who ends up here. Yes." He glanced at Davan, who had found a crate and positioned it with his back to the north wall — instinct, she recognized, the same instinct that had put her back to walls for twenty-two months. "Your boy is going to be all right. The first major manifestation is always the worst."

"He's not my boy," Lira said. "I met him three hours ago."

"I know. That's not what I meant." Hadem picked up his own cup, turned it between his hands. "I meant he has someone with him who knows what it is. That makes a difference."

Across the basement, against the west wall, the other two were watching with the particular attention of people who'd learned to evaluate newcomers quickly and thoroughly. The girl with the amber-gold eyes — Fire Gate, she'd thought at the end of Episode One, and thought it again now, the warmth from her direction not imagined but real, a body temperature running several degrees above normal, the stone floor warm where she'd been sitting. Twelve years old, maybe eleven. She had the eyes of someone older, the specific age that wasn't years but events.

The other was a boy, a little older than Davan, fourteen or fifteen. He was sitting with his head slightly tilted, his gaze directed at the wall between the two lanterns with the focused inattention of someone listening to something other than the room.

"Sight Gate," Lira said.

The boy turned to look at her. His eyes were ordinary — she didn't know what she'd expected, that they would glow or shift color — but his focus was extraordinary. When he looked at her she had the uncomfortable sensation of being assessed more thoroughly than the light allowed for.

"Rennic," he said. "Yes."

"What can you see?"

He considered the question with more seriousness than she'd expected. "Through the walls, mostly. It started as — I kept seeing shapes in my peripheral vision that weren't there. Shadow-shapes." He glanced at Hadem, a look that communicated something she didn't have the shared history to decode. "Now it's through walls. Structural walls, not partitions. The clearer the load path, the clearer my vision through it."

She thought about that. A Sight Gate ability that followed structural lines. "You can see the Registry officials?"

"Two above ground level on the south street. One moving west." A pause. "He's stopped."

The word moved through the basement. Hadem set down his cup.

"How far out?" Hadem said.

"Eighty feet. He's standing at the corner of Saltline and the cooper's row." Rennic's head tilted again, tracking. "He has a device. Some kind of reader."

Lira had never seen a Gate-Touched signature reader, but she'd heard about them. The Registry had been deploying them in the harbor districts for the last eighteen months. They measured frequency — not the way the Gate resonated inside a person, but the ambient bleed, the trace that a Gate left in the air around someone who'd been active recently. She'd known, in the abstract, that her manifestation at the market would have left a signature.

She'd been so focused on getting Davan clear that she'd left her own trace in a fish market on the wrong side of a harbor wall.

"How sensitive are those readers?" she asked.

"I don't know," Hadem said. "I've never been inside one." He was moving, though — methodical, not panicked, the controlled speed of someone who had rehearsed this. He went to the far end of the basement and lifted a section of flagstone that had been mortared to look fixed. Below it: a hatch, a second ladder, an opening into a passage that went west.

"That's where we go," he said. "When we go."

The girl against the west wall had stood up. She was very small. She had her hands tucked into her sleeves, which Lira had noticed as a habit when she first sat down and understood now as deliberate — she was damping something, holding something in. The fire hadn't shown itself, not in this basement, not with five people in a small space and two lanterns already burning. She was holding it in.

"Mira," the girl said. Not introducing herself, exactly. Just correcting the silence where her name hadn't been.

"How long have you been here?" Lira said.

"This safehouse? Three weeks." Mira looked at the passage Hadem had opened with the expression of someone who'd made this journey before. "The Underground? Six months."

Six months. The girl was eleven or twelve. Her Gate had opened six months ago, which meant at ten or eleven. Lira had thought, finding the Underground at twenty with two years of hiding behind her, that she'd come to it late. She revised the estimate.

"There's a next safehouse," Lira said to Hadem.

"Yes."

"You know where it is and nothing else."

"I know the mark pattern. I know the city sector. I don't know the address, the personnel, the number of residents." He was ready to go, everything on his person, a pack that looked permanently pre-assembled. "That's the design. Any one safehouse can be found, can be compromised. The network survives because no node contains the whole network."

"Who designed it?"

"No one knows. It's older than anyone I've spoken to. Older than the Gate-Touched being classified as a Registry priority." Hadem was at the ladder, looking up. "Before the Registry, before the classification system, there were still people who manifested and ran. The Underground appears to have been built for them, or by them, or both. It has the quality of something that grew rather than something that was planned."

She thought about the chalk mark she'd walked past for seven months. Not planned. Just placed by someone who needed it to be there, and maintained by someone who came after. An organism rather than an architecture.

"Rennic," Hadem said.

"He's moving again. West, not south." A beat. "He's not coming here."

"He may have triangulated the signature and found the market instead," Hadem said. "That gives us time but not much."

He looked at Lira. Not commanding — asking, in the way he'd been asking since she arrived, the way that recognized she was new but was not new to running.

"Four flights of stairs above us," she said. "Six people, office weight distribution, moving across the ground floor. They've been there — " she felt for it, the composite of evidence, the accumulated load history of the floor above telling her what had compressed and worn and settled — "more than a year. They're not Registry. Registry field agents have centralized weight; these people have distributed posture. Workers."

Hadem looked at her.

"I can feel the building," she said. "The whole building. Everyone in it."

She hadn't said that before. Not to anyone. She'd known for several months that the ability had grown — that it was no longer the dock and the harbor wall, no longer the immediate structure under her feet, but the building she was in and the buildings adjacent and the streets connecting them, a continuous structural conversation that she participated in the way a fish participated in water. She hadn't said it because saying it made it real in a way that made it heavier.

"Is there a way out the Registry hasn't mapped?" Hadem said.

This was what she'd been calculating since she came through the hatch.

The passage Hadem had opened went west. The Registry, if it had any competence at all, had surveyed the streets west of the safehouse during any previous interest in the building. A passage going west led into the mapped grid. A passage going west was what a Registry extraction team would watch.

What the Registry wouldn't watch was a load-bearing wall with no door.

"The north wall," she said. "Anchor bolted into the bedrock. Follow the bolt pattern down and you're under the harbor district's foundation layer. The builders ran utility lines down there before the district was fully developed — gas mains, water mains, some of them disconnected when the street grid changed. A disconnected line is an empty pipe. An empty pipe is a passage."

"How big?"

"The main runs are two feet across. You can move through them crawling." She felt for the specific route, reading the structural logic. "There's a junction point thirty feet north, under the Saltline intersection. The junction opens onto a decommissioned water cistern from the first harbor expansion. It's not on any current Registry survey — it predates their mapping system by sixty years."

Hadem stared at her. Not doubt — something more complicated. The expression of someone adjusting a prior estimate.

"You're reading this," he said. "Right now. You're reading the building and the ground underneath it."

"The Gate has grown," she said. "I don't know why. I don't know what else it's going to do." She stood up. "Do you want the route or not?"

---

Getting into the north wall required removing a section of the stone floor — old flagstones, not the modern concrete of the upper city, the kind of layered construction that accumulated centuries of modification and left gaps between them. Hadem had a crowbar. She guided him to the specific stone: third from the north wall, second from the west, the one that rang differently when tapped because it was sitting over a void rather than fill.

It came up in pieces. Below it, a gap, and below the gap, the anchor bolt and its descending line into the original harbor bedrock.

Rennic went first, because he could see. Then Mira, then Davan, then Hadem, then Lira. She pulled the stone back as close to position as she could from below, which was not very close and would not fool anyone for long. It didn't need to. It needed to buy them the time between the Registry finding the empty safehouse and the Registry finding the empty passage.

The utility pipe was exactly where she'd said it was.

Two feet across. Crawling. Hadem had a pocket torch; the light swung ahead of Rennic's legs and lit the curve of old iron and the mineral deposits that had built up over decades of disuse. The air was cold and smelled of rust and mineral water and, faintly, the particular organic smell of old earth — not rot, something older, the smell of stone that had been closed for a long time.

Lira could feel the city above her.

This was the thing that had changed, the thing she was still learning to name: it wasn't just the building anymore. In the utility pipe, with the bedrock on all sides, she was in direct contact with the foundational layer, and the foundational layer was connected to everything that had been built on it. She could feel the Registry officials above — the distinctive posture-weight she'd learned to recognize at the market. She could feel the water table and the decommissioned cistern ahead and the harbor at her back and, distantly, the weight of the whole harbor district pressing down on ancient stone that had been holding it for four centuries without complaint.

It was too much. She had learned to triage.

Foundation Gate. She filed the harbor district. She filed the water table. She kept the Registry officials and the cistern and the forty feet of pipe between her and the next node.

They made the cistern in twenty minutes.

It was large enough to stand in. Twenty feet across, dome-roofed, the stone old enough that the construction style predated anything she'd been taught in school history. Not just pre-Registry. Pre-Academy. Someone had built a water cistern here when the harbor district was a fishing village on a stone shore, and the city had built up around it and eventually over it and forgotten it.

The others stood in it and breathed.

Davan was shaking. Not from cold — the cistern was warmer than the pipe. He was doing the particular shaking of someone whose body was only now processing what had happened to it hours earlier. He sat down on the stone shelf that ran along the cistern wall and put his head in his hands, and the water on the floor — there was always water in old cisterns, an inch, two inches of groundwater seep — rippled away from his feet in a slow concentric ring.

He hadn't noticed.

Lira sat down beside him. Not to comfort — she wasn't good at comfort, didn't have the easy warmth for it. She sat close enough that he could feel the presence of someone who wasn't afraid of him.

"You're doing it again," she said quietly.

He looked at his feet. At the water. "I know."

"It's not bad. It's just — you're doing it."

"How do you stop?"

"You don't, exactly. You learn to notice it. Notice changes how it runs." She thought about how to say what she knew. Twenty-two months of figuring this out alone, in a fish market, against a dock, in warehouses and alleys and rented rooms. She'd never had to put it into words before. "The Gate is always open. Mine is. I don't think it closes all the way. But there's a difference between it running in the background and it running you."

"How do you get there?"

"I don't know yet," she said honestly. "I'm still figuring it out too."

Mira, across the cistern, had unwrapped her hands from her sleeves. In the dark she was faintly luminous — not glowing, but holding something warm in a way the torchlight didn't quite account for. She was watching Lira with the calculating attention of someone who'd decided to take a person seriously.

"There are Academies that teach this," Mira said.

"The Academies take Registry referrals," Lira said.

"Some of them."

"All the ones I know about." She looked at Hadem. "Is there another kind?"

Hadem had taken off his pack and was sorting through it with the efficiency of someone who knew exactly what was in it and where. "There are stories," he said. "Underground stories. People who went to Academies through back channels. Not through the Registry. Not as wards." He set two wrapped packages on the stone shelf — food, probably. "I don't have names. I don't have addresses. I have what this safehouse knew, which is: it's possible. The mark pattern points somewhere."

"The harbor," Lira said.

He looked up.

"You were going to take us to the next safehouse." She'd been thinking about this since the water jumped in the fish market, since she looked at Davan's hands and understood what the alternative was. "The next mark pattern leads to another basement in another building in another district, and we stay there until another sweep and another passage and another cistern. And then the one after that." She looked around the dome of the old cistern. "We've been hiding. All of us. This whole network has been hiding."

"It keeps us alive," Hadem said.

"It keeps us small." She heard how it sounded. She didn't soften it. "I don't want the next safehouse. I want to get to an Academy. Not through the Registry. On our own terms."

The silence in the cistern had a particular quality. She felt the others considering it — not in the abstract, but the specific physical consideration of people who had given up imagining better options and were being asked to imagine them again. That was a vulnerable thing to do. She knew. She'd stopped doing it herself for most of twenty-two months.

"The harbor district connects to the outer docks," she said. "The outer docks have shipping routes. Shipping routes go places the Registry doesn't fully control." She looked at Rennic. "What can you see from here?"

He tilted his head. The long pause of someone looking through earth.

"There's a pier," he said slowly. "The far end of the outer dock. It's older than the others. There's a woman standing on it." He paused. "She's been standing there for a while. She's not a dock worker. She's waiting."

"For ships?"

"For us," he said. "I think."

---

The outer dock in late afternoon had the deserted quality of a working place after the work was done — empty crates, coiled rope, the smell of pitch and salt fish faded to a background note. The pier Rennic had described was at the end, weathered, the planking older than the main dock structure by several decades. It jutted into the harbor at a slight angle, as if the dock had been built before the current harbor plan and the plan had been built around it rather than over it.

The woman was there.

She was maybe forty, brown-skinned, her hair braided and wound close to her head in a style Lira didn't recognize. She wore an insignia on her jacket collar — a small silver pin, a star inside a circle — that Lira also didn't recognize and had never been taught to recognize.

She looked at the five of them and nodded, once, the nod of someone who had expected them.

"You took the cistern route," she said. "Good instinct."

"You know about the cistern," Hadem said.

"I know about most of the Underground's exit routes in this district. I've been watching this sector for three months." She looked at Lira. "You made a different calculation than the mark pattern would have taken you."

"The mark pattern leads to another safehouse," Lira said.

"Yes."

"And you're—"

"Not the safehouse." The woman touched the silver pin at her collar. "This is the Starlight Corps insignia. I'm Captain Senna Dhar, Oceanic Sector, specialist rotation." She looked at each of them in turn — Davan, Mira, Rennic, Hadem, and finally back to Lira. "The Corps has a different offer than the Registry. Different from the Underground too, if you'll hear it."

The harbor moved below the pier. Lira could feel it: the whole outer dock, the tidal force pushing against the piling foundations, the patient weight of salt water that had been here longer than the city and would be here after. She stood on the pier and looked at the woman with the silver pin, and she thought about seven months of walking past a chalk mark, and she thought about Davan's hands, and she thought about twenty-two months of being careful.

She had been so careful.

She was so tired of careful.

"We're listening," she said.
