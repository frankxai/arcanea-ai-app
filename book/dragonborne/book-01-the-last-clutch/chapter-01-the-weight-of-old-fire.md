# Chapter One: The Weight of Old Fire

The grain came down wrong.

Theron had been moving sacks since before light, which was the kind of work that let the mind go quiet, and he'd been grateful for that — eight sacks along his shoulder, angle them right, let gravity do what gravity does. But the ninth sack had a weak seam and the seam chose now to fail, and forty pounds of millet came down across the back of his neck and into the gap between his collar and his skin, and every grain found a way to work itself deeper with each step he took.

He set down the remaining sacks. He stood in the doorway of Aldmer's granary with millet in his hair and the morning sun cutting low and flat across the Ember Wastes, and he thought about nothing. This was a skill he had spent two years developing, the deliberate maintenance of blankness, and he was getting better at it.

He reached back and scraped grain out of his collar with two fingers. A few pieces fell to the hard-packed earth. A pigeon watched from the roof beam.

"Go ahead," Theron told it.

The pigeon descended with the businesslike air of something that had better things to do than acknowledge him. Theron left it to the millet and went back for the next load.

---

The Ember Wastes were not, technically, a wasteland. The name was old, from before the first Forge students figured out that volcanic soil grew things differently than ordinary earth — not lush, nothing here was ever lush, but productive in a stubborn way, crops that tasted of minerals and lasted twice as long in a cellar. Aldmer grew three types of grain, two root vegetables, and one variety of pepper that turned your tongue to ash and made you feel, briefly, that you understood something about the world. The workers who'd developed a taste for it put them in everything.

The town of Ashkeld sat at the eastern edge of the Wastes, where the volcanic black rock gave way to ordinary dust-coloured nothing. Population: six hundred, if you counted generously. A weekly market. A tavern that served two things. A shrine to Draconia, Goddess of the Fire Gate, which doubled as the surgery because the shrine-keeper was the closest thing to a healer within forty miles and also because people felt better about cauterized wounds if someone was watching over the proceedings divinely.

Theron had arrived fourteen months ago from the direction of the Forge, which was to the west, deeper into the Wastes, and which he had not looked at since. He'd told Aldmer he was passing through. Aldmer had handed him a shovel and told him to stay as long as he liked, or as long as he was useful, which he'd correctly guessed were the same thing. Aldmer was not a complicated man. Theron found this restful.

He moved grain until midmorning. Then he patched a stretch of roof on the eastern storage house where the weather had been getting in. Then he ate — flatbread, last night's soup reheated, the dangerous pepper cut thin — and was stacking the empty dishes when the birds went quiet.

Not the way birds went quiet when a hawk appeared. This was different. A settling. A collective decision made without discussion, the way a market goes still when someone important walks in.

Theron looked up from the dishes.

The shadow was enormous. He registered the shape of it before his eyes adjusted to the sky — too large to be a cloud, too defined to be smoke, moving at a speed inconsistent with anything he had a name for. A second shadow appeared beside the first, then four more in a ragged formation to the south. He stood slowly, the dish still in his hands, and watched them pass.

Migration. It had to be. He'd heard Vaelstrom's kind moved south in the hotter months, Storm-singers riding the thermal columns above the Wastes. He'd never seen it. Most people hadn't.

Then one of the shadows banked, caught the light differently, and he saw scales.

Storm-singers were silver. These were not silver. These were emissaries from five of the great nations, their riding dragons — not bonded, not even close to bonded, trained transport beasts — carrying banners he could make out even at this distance because the banners were designed to be visible from the ground. The Crystalline Crest of the Luminary. The crowned forge-hammer of the Draconian Compact. The three waves of Thal'Maris. Two others he didn't recognize immediately, and then he did: the doubled helix of the Gate-Touched Registry, which was not a nation but had the manners of one, and the black sun of the Void Ascendants, which meant someone important had died or something important had broken.

They were all heading west.

Toward the Forge.

"Aldmer," Theron said, not loudly, but Aldmer was already in the doorway behind him.

"I've been watching," Aldmer said. He sounded like a man updating an account he'd kept for some time. "Third formation this week."

"What's happened?"

Aldmer was quiet for a moment. "Word came through the market two days past. From a glassblower out of Pyralis who had it from a rider who had it from someone at the Forge itself." He paused. "The egg cracked."

Theron said nothing.

"*The* egg. Embera's. They say she laid a clutch. Three of them, maybe more. And one of the clutch — just one, so far — started moving."

Two years of careful blankness. Theron had built it stone by stone, mortared it with manual labor and bad wine and the deliberate absence of thought about anything that happened before. He felt the first stone shift now.

"That's not possible," he said. "Dragons don't lay clutches. Not for—"

"Centuries, yes. And yet." Aldmer looked at him sideways in the way Aldmer did when he was saying something without saying it. "You worked at the Forge."

"I was a student. Briefly."

"Still."

Theron set the dish down on the nearest flat surface. He was aware of his own hands in a way he usually wasn't. "It doesn't change anything."

Aldmer shrugged the shrug of a man who had planted seeds long enough to know that what didn't change anything today changed everything eventually. He went inside. Theron stood in the yard and watched the last of the formation shadows move west until they were gone.

The birds, cautiously, began making noise again.

---

It landed at three in the afternoon, in the empty field between Aldmer's property and the edge of the Wastes proper, and the landing displaced enough air to knock a haystack flat and sent every animal within a quarter-mile into brief, undignified panic.

The workers ran to look. Theron, who was patching fence on the north side, heard the impact and the animal chaos and stood up slowly and looked over the fence line.

Obsidius was larger than the last time Theron had seen him, which should not have been possible — a two-thousand-year-old Dragon did not grow substantially in two years — but the Ember Wastes were a different kind of space than the Forge's caldera, and scale worked differently here. There was nothing to measure against except flat land and sky, and against those two things, Obsidius seemed to fill the available air.

He was dark, Fire-blooded but dark-scaled, the color of obsidian cooling — not quite black, not quite brown, a color that existed in the space between the two the way a long thought exists in the space between questions. His eyes were amber, steady, and currently fixed with the patient precision of geological formations on Theron Valke, who had no fence left to pretend to fix.

"I wasn't going to come," Theron said.

"No," Obsidius agreed. His voice was the kind of low that you felt in your sternum rather than heard with your ears. "You were going to stand there holding those fence boards until I said something interesting."

"Are you going to say something interesting?"

"I thought I'd start with a question." Obsidius settled his wings — carefully, deliberately, aware of the fence lines and the haystack and the six workers who were now gathered at a respectful distance trying to look like they weren't watching. "I'd like to know what you think the difference is between running away and walking away."

Theron set the fence board down. "I'm not having this conversation."

"You're already having this conversation."

"I came here to—"

"Work. Yes. You came here to perform the physical mechanics of a life without having to participate in the interior of one." The amber eyes did not blink. "It's not a criticism. I've done the same. The philosophical retreat is not without merit. But there's a distinction between choosing quietness and using quietness to avoid a reckoning you've been owed for twenty-six months."

"Twenty-five."

"Twenty-six. I've been counting." A small pause, the length of a deliberate thought. "Theron. The eggs are calling a name. I wanted to tell you myself, rather than let you hear it from someone who would make it an announcement instead of a fact."

The workers had stopped pretending not to watch.

"It sounds like yours," Obsidius said.

Theron picked up the fence board again. He didn't know why. He just needed something in his hands. "That's not how it works. Dragon eggs don't—"

"Typically, no. These eggs are not typical. Nothing about this clutch has been typical. Embera is not a typical Dragon — she is the first born in two centuries, which means she was shaped by two centuries of a world without young fire in it, and she has apparently decided to bypass several steps that the Dragon process usually takes." A slight adjustment of posture, the massive neck shifting an inch to bring one eye more directly level with Theron's face. "Seraphine is beside herself. Pyralith has said three things in a month, which is unprecedented. The Forge is in chaos, as you may have observed from the aerial traffic."

"I noticed."

"And you're fixing a fence."

"The fence needed fixing."

"The fence was fine." Another pause. "I spoke with Director Cassara. She asked me to tell you that the Crucible trial — what happened — has been reviewed. The determination that you were responsible for Arvel's death has been withdrawn."

Something caught in Theron's chest and stayed there.

"They withdrew it eleven months ago," Obsidius continued, "and it took me three months after that to find you, and then I waited another two months because you were in a very specific kind of grief that does not benefit from interruption. But I've calculated that we've arrived at the end of that grace period."

"Cassara sent you."

"Cassara asked me. I came because I wanted to. These are different things."

Theron put the fence board down again, for good this time, and looked across the flat expanse of the Wastes toward where he knew the Forge was, though he couldn't see it from here. Two years' distance. A direction he'd built a discipline around not facing.

"If I go back," he said, "and the eggs—" He stopped.

"Yes?"

"If the eggs are calling — which they're not, that's not a real thing — but if they were, and I go back, and then the hatchling bonds with someone else. Which it would. Because the Crucible already proved I'm not—"

"The Crucible proved you were twenty-three and terrified and in the grip of a bond attempt that should have been supervised more carefully. It proved that the Forge's protocols for partial-bond dissolution were inadequate. It proved that Arvel had been hiding an injury for three months that compromised his fire-resonance during the trial." Obsidius shifted again, and this time there was weight behind it, the implication of something vast making itself clear. "It proved nothing about you."

Theron stood in the dirt of Aldmer's north pasture with millet still in his collar and a fence board at his feet and two years of constructed emptiness and felt all of it resisting this, pushing back against the weight of a very old Dragon being patient at him in a field.

"The eggs are probably responding to the proximity of anyone with Fire Gate affinity," he said. "There are forty Forge students within the Forge's walls right now. Any one of—"

"Theron."

"—of them would be a better candidate than someone who—"

"Theron."

He stopped.

"You've been arguing with me for twelve minutes," Obsidius said. "That's not usually what happens when someone has no intention of going."

---

He told Aldmer that evening. Aldmer listened the way Aldmer listened to everything — with the complete attention of someone who had long since given up expecting the world to be small. At the end, he said: "Your back wage is forty-two marks. I'll have it in the morning."

"I don't need—"

"You'll need passage on something going west, and horses out of Ashkeld cost more than they should because Maren at the stable knows she has the only horses in forty miles." Aldmer poured from the clay jug. "Take the money. It's owed."

Theron took the money.

He slept badly and woke before dawn and stood in the yard looking west while the sky went from black to the particular grey-purple that preceded light in the Wastes — a color that always made him think of cooling metal, of the Forge's outer walls at first shift when you could still see stars on one side and the volcano-glow on the other. He hadn't let himself think of it in the specific sensory way — the smell of sulfur and hot stone, the sound of hammers in the training halls, the particular way sound moved inside Mount Pyralis, swallowed by rock and returned compressed. He hadn't let himself think of Arvel's face either, but that was a different kind of discipline.

He thought of both, now, and let himself.

---

Four days of riding brought the Forge into view.

It looked the same. He'd expected it to look changed, the way places look changed when you've changed inside them, but Mount Pyralis had been here for longer than cities, longer than nations, longer than the concept of "the same" had applied to anything in Arcanea. It looked exactly like what it was: a thing that would still be here when everything he'd been worrying about had become sediment.

The road approaching from the east passed through the Crucible Estates — the training villages where first-year students lived before they were admitted to the mountain proper — and then through the outer gate, carved from the volcano's own basalt into an arch wide enough for three riders abreast. The arch was new. Or not new, but newer than the rest; he'd watched its carving in his second year, the Forge's master stonecutter spending three months on the relief panels that framed both sides: dragons and students in the act of bonding, rendered in a style that made the moment look inevitable, which struck Theron now, as it had then, as one of the more optimistic lies anyone had thought to tell in stone.

There were more soldiers than he remembered. Not Forge guards — Compact soldiers, the crowned-hammer insignia, two full cohorts from the look of it, camped along the road's northern verge. Luminary riders too, in the pale riding leathers, and three tents flying the Registry helix that Theron gave a wide berth to without quite deciding to.

He gave his name at the outer gate. The gate-student — young, nervous, clearly not sleeping enough — checked a list and found him on it, which meant Obsidius had sent word ahead, which meant Theron had not actually decided anything because the decision had been made for him, and he was annoyed at this and knew it was also fair.

Inside the outer wall, the Forge was organized chaos. Delegations from six nations, he counted. Scholars with equipment he didn't recognize. Three separate groups of what looked like Registry assessors moving in tight, purposeful clusters. A pair of Luminors in the open courtyard, in formal rank-dress, in conversation that had the careful blankness of an argument being conducted in complete silence between people who knew better than to argue here.

And above all of it, the mountain.

The Forge had always felt like a living thing. Pyralis breathed through its vents, smelled of sulfur and hot mineral water, radiated a warmth that varied by location and hour and the volcano's own cycles in ways the Forge masters tracked as carefully as any curriculum. Standing inside the outer wall again, Theron felt it settle over him like the first familiar weight of something he'd carried long enough that its absence was the stranger sensation.

Cassara found him at the courtyard's edge before he'd made it twenty steps.

She looked older. Not much — she'd been forty-one when he left, and Mages aged slowly once they'd passed their fifth Gate, so she was probably functionally forty-three now. But something had settled into the corners of her eyes that hadn't been there before.

"Theron."

"Director."

She looked at him the way she'd always looked at him, which was with the complete assessment of someone who had trained too many people to bother with the performance of assessment. "You look like you've been doing manual labor in bad weather."

"I have been."

"Good. Grief that doesn't work itself through the hands goes somewhere worse." She turned and started walking, the clear assumption that he would follow. He did. "I'm going to tell you what I know, and then I'm going to take you to the clutch chamber, and then what happens will happen at its own pace. I can't predict it. No one can. Seraphine is currently not allowing anyone near the eggs who hasn't been cleared by her personally, which has made the delegation visits very interesting."

"The delegations are here to claim the hatchlings."

"The delegations are here to attempt to establish a political claim to the hatchlings before bonding occurs, yes. It won't work — you can't claim a Dragon bond through treaty, which they know, but the claim itself has strategic value even if it doesn't succeed." She moved through the courtyard quickly, and people moved out of her way with the ease of long practice. "What you need to understand before you go in there — and I know Obsidius told you some of this, but he's a Dragon and he tells it in the way Dragons tell things, which is philosophically accurate and practically incomplete — is that the eggs have been generating a resonance pattern since they were laid. Draconic heat-memory. The kind that functions like a call. And over the past three weeks, since the first egg showed movement, that pattern has developed a frequency signature that our resonance readers can't match to any current student or staff member."

Theron said nothing.

"We ran the pattern against the Forge's complete historical record. Eighteen hundred years of registered fire affinities." She paused at the entrance to the mountain proper — the great carved door, open, leading into the basalt-walled corridor that descended toward the caldera. "It matched one record. Partial. Approximate. The match isn't perfect because the person in question had their fire-resonance disrupted by an incomplete bond dissolution twenty-five months ago." She looked at him directly. "Disruption changes the resonance signature. Like a bell that was struck and then cracked — it still rings, but differently. The egg is calling a cracked bell."

Theron looked at the door.

"That's not a reason to walk away," Cassara said. "It might be the reason they're calling."

---

The clutch chamber was deep. Not the famous Dragon Roosts on the caldera rim, the public space where visiting dignitaries could observe from behind safety glass — this was lower, older, part of the original mountain structure that predated the Forge entirely. Seraphine had chosen it, which meant she'd known it was here, which meant it was one of those Dragon things that mortals found disconcerting: knowledge held across thousands of years, casually applied.

The heat built as they descended. Not the ambient volcano warmth of the upper halls — this was directional, coming from ahead, coming from below, the specific quality of heat that had intent behind it. Theron had not felt this in twenty-five months and his body remembered it before his mind did, a kind of cellular recognition, every mechanism in him that had been opened by three years of Fire Gate training and one incomplete and catastrophic bond attempt responding to the temperature the way metal responds to proximity of a magnet.

He'd closed those mechanisms down after the Crucible. He'd thought he'd closed them completely.

He had not.

"Seraphine," Cassara said at the chamber door, not calling — addressing, the distinction mattered. "He's here."

A silence that had mass to it. Then: "I know." Low, resonant, and coming from everywhere at once the way heat came from everywhere. "Come in, then. Slowly."

The chamber was roughly the size of the Forge's great hall, except this space had not been built — it had been found, a natural volcanic cavern that had been used as a Dragon nest long before the Forge's founders arrived. The walls were black-glassed basalt, smooth from millennia of heat. The floor sloped toward a central depression, and in the depression: three objects, each about the size of a large millstone, each radiating a light that was not quite firelight, not quite anything he had a name for.

And Seraphine.

She was large — he'd known she would be large, he'd seen her twice before from a distance, but the chamber compressed the scale in a way that made her immediate, present, inescapable. Her scales were dark amber-gold where the inner light hit them and deep red-black in the shadows, and her eyes were on him with the focused warmth of something that had decided to trust him and hadn't told him yet.

The eggs.

Three of them. One cracked at the equatorial seam — not broken, not damaged, cracked in the specific way that meant process, that meant something working from inside toward out. From the crack, a light. Not the diffuse warmth of the other two. A concentrated point, white-gold, steady as a heartbeat.

The crack was wider than it should be for the stage it appeared to be at.

"It's moving faster than it should," Theron said.

"Yes," Seraphine said.

"Why?"

"Because it's found what it was looking for." There was something in her voice that was not quite emotion and was not quite the absence of emotion. "When a Dragon hatchling calls, it calls into the world, and the world either answers or it doesn't. This one called. The world answered slowly." The amber eyes shifted to the egg. "It's been waiting two years. It decided to stop waiting."

Theron crossed the chamber floor. He was not sure when he decided to — he was moving, and the moving felt like something separate from decision, like the kind of thing that had been decided before he arrived and he was only now catching up to it. The heat intensified with each step. His fire-resonance, the one he'd broken and closed and walled off with two years of grain and fence boards and bad wine, was opening. He could feel it. Not pain — recognition. The bell Cassara had described, cracked and ringing.

He crouched at the depression's edge, two feet from the nearest egg.

The crack in the third egg — the one with the light — widened.

Not dramatically. Not the explosive emergence of stories. Just a quiet widening, the way dawn works, the way things become inevitable by becoming more themselves. The light spilled out slowly, filling the depression, and it was not firelight. Theron knew firelight. He'd trained in it for three years, he'd slept beside it, he'd been remade by its failure and tried to unmake himself from what remained. This was older.

This was what fire remembered before it learned to burn.

He put his hand out. Not toward the egg — parallel to it, palm up, a question rather than a demand. The light reached his palm and the cracked bell in his chest struck a note, clear and fractured and unmistakably itself.

Behind him, Seraphine made a sound he didn't have a word for.

The crack widened further.

Something pressed against the inside of the shell.

He waited.
