# Chapter Six: The Covenant

Three days after Pyralith's speech, the Dragon Court convened.

It met in the Caldera — not in the Forge's formal chambers, not in the administrative halls where Cassara was still managing the aftermath of the assault, not in any room built by human hands. The Caldera was the oldest venue available. It had hosted eleven Dragon Courts in recorded history, according to the section of the Dragon Codex that Theron had found in the locked secondary archive in his third year. The twelfth was now.

He had read the Codex's descriptions of Dragon Courts. Words like *gravitas* and *solemnity* and *geological significance*. He had thought he understood what those words meant. He understood, standing at the Caldera's eastern approach as the assembled Dragons settled into their positions, that he had understood the words and not the thing.

The Caldera was three hundred meters across, open to the sky, the volcanic rock of its rim worn smooth by the same twelve thousand years of Dragon presence that had worn the Kindling passage smooth. The atmosphere was specific: the thin high-altitude air laced with sulfur and the deep mineral heat of a volcano that was not dormant but banked, held, the way you held a fire you intended to use later. Seven Dragons occupied the perimeter. Obsidius at the north rim, his two thousand years of accumulated presence making the basalt under him look like it was grateful for the weight. Seraphine at the west, in the warm amber of mid-morning, her four thousand years more still than Obsidius — she had been to Courts before, three of the previous eleven, and moved with the economy of something that had learned to conserve.

The others were names from the Codex. Velanthar, the deep-sea scholar, whose scales were the color of the ocean at two thousand meters and who had spent nine hundred years mapping the Arcanean seabed and returned to learn that the world had moved. Korrath, the Mountain Keeper, who was the reason anyone knew which mountains in the Ember Sector were geologically significant and which were merely large. Issal the Pale, who had not spoken to any human being in three centuries and whose presence at this Court, according to Obsidius's brief preparatory explanation, indicated the matter was serious enough to override principle. Yindara, who had three hatchlings of her own bonded to Forge students from the previous generation and who looked at the three of them with an expression that Theron could not read — not hostile, not warm, not neutral. The expression of someone assessing whether what they were seeing matched what they had been told to expect.

And Pyralith.

He was not at the rim. He was in the center. The amber of him filled the Caldera's geometry the way it had filled the anteroom — not because he was too large, but because he was the correct size for the space, and the space was organized around him, and this was the only way it made sense.

The three hatchlings were in the center with him.

Embera was not.

She had woken two days after Pyralith's speech. Theron had not been present for the waking — Seraphine had, and Obsidius, and by the time Cassara's runner had reached them the event was over and Embera was in the upper Caldera, resting, with the specific quality of someone in whom something enormous had passed through and who was now integrating what remained. He had seen her once, from a distance, at the Caldera's upper rim. She had not looked at them. She had been looking at the entity with an expression he also could not read — recognition, and something held back from expression that he didn't have a name for.

She was at the rim now, the eastern quadrant, alone in the way she had apparently been alone for the three days of the vigil while the rest of the Forge had been managing the aftermath. Not absent. Watching.

He had noticed, on the walk up to the Caldera, that she watched everything with the quality of someone completing a count.

---

Pyralith spoke first, as was custom — the oldest Dragon in attendance opened the Court, framed the matter, gave the assembly its question. The assembly answered.

His framing took a long time. Not the urgency-speech of the anteroom, not the intimate temperature of a being addressing three humans and three hatchlings in a small space. This was Court speech — formal, structural, the way he laid out events so that every Dragon present was working from the same architecture before the deliberation began. He described the assault. The Void Ascendants' objective. The defense. The hatching. The entity. Malachar's fifty-year pursuit of the Eleventh Frequency and what its emergence implied about the timing of the seal's degradation. He described the three bonds forming, the three pairings, the nature of each bond and how it differed from the standard Crucible-forged connection.

He described what he had said in the anteroom.

The Court listened. Seven ancient Dragons and the three of them — standing at the Caldera's center, equidistant from the hatchlings, trying to hold the quality of attention the occasion required without letting the occasion's weight turn into something their bodies couldn't carry. Serai was beside him, a Corps formal report folded under her arm that she was not going to need. Vorath was to his left, in the posture she held when she was working hardest at being exactly as present as the situation required and no more — not stillness as performance but stillness as practice, the specific economy of someone who had learned that the Void Gate's domain was not the absence of motion but the presence of potential in it.

When Pyralith finished, Obsidius spoke from the north rim. Not translation — addition. The two thousand years he had been at the Forge, watching the Bond's history, accounting for the grief he had named in the anteroom: he added this to Pyralith's framing. The record of every bonded pair the Forge had produced, and what had happened to them, and what it had cost the Dragons. He did not give this as an argument against the bonds forming. He gave it as context — the way a responsible elder adds the full dimension of a decision to the account before a Court votes.

Velanthar asked three questions. Precise, technical, the questions of a scholar who had been mapping things for nine hundred years and understood that a map with an error in it was more dangerous than no map at all. Theron could follow the first two — they were about the entity's relationship to the Gate system's frequency architecture. The third was in Thyr'vael, addressed to Pyralith, and Pyralith answered in Thyr'vael, and what passed between them in that exchange was beyond his reach. Serai's face told him it was important.

He had the specific sense, standing in the Caldera, that he was present at a conversation that had been developing for twelve thousand years and had reached the point where the next section required bodies that were not Dragon to be in the room.

Not witnesses. Signatories.

---

Pyralith proposed the Covenant at the fourth hour.

He named it. *Vel'doth suvaran* — in Thyr'vael, the closest common-tongue translation being: the agreement that changes what the parties are, rather than what they do. Not a treaty. Not an arrangement between entities that remained what they were throughout. The kind of agreement that was itself a transformation.

"The old Covenant was made at the time of the first Gate-building," he said. "Its terms, as they have survived in the oldest records: dragons guard. Humans create. Each stays within the domain of their nature and the world benefits from the exchange." He paused. "This Covenant held for twelve thousand years and it was a good Covenant for its era. Its era has ended." Another pause, the weight-pause. "It ended because its terms assumed that the two domains were separate — that Dragon nature and human nature were distinct enough that the exchange could occur at a boundary rather than within a shared space. The bonds forming before you now are not exchanges at a boundary. They are integrations." He looked at the seven assembled Dragons. "The new Covenant must account for what is neither fully Dragon nor fully human. For the entity, which is neither. For the bonds, which are both. For what comes next, which we cannot yet describe."

Issal the Pale spoke. Once, briefly, in Thyr'vael, the voice of a Dragon who had not spoken to humans in three centuries carrying a quality she did not aim at humans — but the words were for the record, for the Court, and the meaning came through regardless: *if the seal breaks without a new architecture to replace what the old Covenant held in place, the question of what the Covenant should be becomes irrelevant.*

Seraphine said nothing. Her amber heat was constant, her attention on the three hatchlings, on the entity. Not absent from the deliberation — choosing her presence for what it was needed for.

Yindara spoke last of the assembled Dragons, and she spoke in the common tongue, directly to the three of them, in the voice of someone who had watched this moment approach for a generation of bonded pairs and had prepared her words carefully.

"The Covenant will bind you," she said. "You should understand this before you speak for it. Not in the way of law, not in the way of obligation — in the way of a thing that changes what you are. The old Covenant changed the first Luminors. They were not the same people after they signed it that they were before. The record is clear on this and does not treat it as a loss." She looked at each of them. "I am asking you to know that this is what is being offered. Not a role. Not a duty. A nature."

Theron thought about the bell. The crack and the original note and the third note in the interval. He thought about what Pyralith had said: wholeness-that-includes-breakage is different from wholeness-that-avoids-it. He thought about the twenty-six months and what it had cost to stay in the hardest place long enough for it to become familiar.

He thought: I have already been changed. By the clutch, by the entity, by the interval-note in my chest that rang for the first time in the anteroom. The Covenant is not going to change me. The Covenant is going to name what I already am.

He looked at Serai. She met his eyes with the cold-light precision of someone who had already done the same calculation and reached the same answer. He looked at Vorath. She was watching the entity. The entity was watching all of them with its clear transparent eyes, cycling through its slow iridescent spectrum, as if it had always known this was coming and had arranged itself to be exactly present for it.

"Who speaks first?" Serai asked.

"You choose your order," Pyralith said.

She stepped forward.

---

She spoke for Fire.

Not for herself — for Fire, for the element and what it was and what being bonded to it in its most ancient form meant. She had prepared nothing. She stood in the center of the Caldera with the volcanic heat rising around her and the assembled Dragons watching and she said what was true.

"Fire chooses," she said. "That's what Fire does — it decides what to burn and burns it completely. For twelve thousand years the dragons who chose fire chose power over potential, and the choice was correct for twelve thousand years. It was correct because the world needed things decided, needed energy with direction, needed burning that went somewhere. What I understand now—" She paused, not for effect, for finding the right place in the truth. "Is that fire can choose differently. Not less. Not smaller. Passion over potential was a real choice and it produced real things. But there is a third option that neither of those words describes, and I felt it when the entity looked at me and the interval-note rang in my chest for the first time." He looked at the first hatchling, the primordial one with his silver-white eyes, watching back with the recognition of something that knew its own nature. "We can choose both. Passion and potential together. Fire that knows what it was before it became fire. I am speaking for that bond. For the interval between what we were and what we chose to become. For the note that exists in the space that a choice makes."

He let the silence hold.

Obsidius, at the north rim, made a sound that was not words. Old and deep and specific — the Thyr'vael register that Theron had read about in the Codex and never heard. The sound a Dragon made when something that had been believed for a very long time was confirmed.

---

Serai spoke for Light.

She was the shortest of the three of them, compact, with the quality of someone who had spent years in archives and had learned to make herself precise because precision was the tool she trusted. She stood at the center of the Caldera and held the Corps report folded under her arm and did not open it.

"The Gates were built by beings who came from somewhere else," she said. "This is in the Codex, in the section on the pre-Kindling state — that the first Luminors and the first Godbeasts were working in conditions that had no architecture, no order, no channel for the raw elemental energy, and what they made was the Gate system. Ten Gates. Ten frequencies. A structure for how power moved through the world." She paused. "The Codex calls this a gift. I have always thought that was not quite accurate. The Gates were built by beings who came from somewhere — who had been somewhere before this world had its architecture — and what they built, they built as what they knew. They built locks. Channels with control mechanisms. A managed system for power that would otherwise be unmanageable." She looked at the deep-water hatchling. The cold-light eyes looked back. "What I am speaking for is the reframe. The Gates as bridges. Not the removal of structure — the Gate system is not wrong, it has held twelve thousand years and it has held well. But there is a difference between a structure built to contain and a structure built to connect. The Gates were built to contain power that would otherwise be dangerous. What I am speaking for is a time when the Gates connect power that is ready to move." She paused. "The Covenant should be built to last the next twelve thousand years. I am speaking for building it as a bridge."

Velanthar made a sound, brief, in Thyr'vael. Serai looked at him. Something passed between a scholar who had been mapping things for nine hundred years and a scholar who had been alive for about twenty-six of them, and the exchange was too specific to be legible from the outside. But at the end of it, Velanthar dipped his vast head once, slowly.

---

Vorath spoke for Void.

She spoke it as it was — not the Ascendants' version, not the doctrine Malachar had shaped in the years before the Wars, not the institutional fear that the word carried in every Academy file she had read and every Registry assessment she had undergone in the years she had spent learning the Void Gate in the only context available to her.

"The silence behind the Source Gate is not an enemy," she said. "I know this because I have been at the edge of it. Not by reaching — by waiting. In the anteroom, when the hatching began, I did what I was taught not to do. I stopped compressing the Gate. I opened it and I waited." She paused. "What came was not power. It was potential. Nero's gift. The thing that exists before anything exists, the state from which every element descended, the silence that is not empty because it is full of everything that has not yet decided what it is." She was very still, the way she was still when she was working hardest at being present. "Malachar wanted to open that silence by force. The doctrine said that was power. The doctrine was exactly backward. Power is what you get when potential collapses into a specific thing and moves in a specific direction. The silence is what you have before the collapse. The silence is the larger thing." She looked at the entity. The entity looked back, and in that exchange something happened that Theron could feel at the edge of his interval-note — in the register that preceded language, in the frequency space before differentiation. "I am speaking for the silence. For the understanding that what comes before the Gate system is not an absence to be filled but a presence to be heard. For a Covenant that treats the potential as partner rather than resource." She paused for the last time. "The seal is weakening because Malachar compressed the Void for fifty years trying to force it open. The answer is not stronger sealing. The answer is listening to what's on the other side long enough to learn what it's trying to say."

The Caldera was very quiet.

Even the volcanic heat seemed to have stilled — not absent, but gathered, waiting.

Pyralith looked at the three of them with the amber warmth of twelve thousand years of watching and said: "Then we are agreed."

---

The fire came from six sources.

It was Pyralith who named the mechanism — who said, in the formal Thyr'vael register that had been used at the previous eleven Courts for the procedures that mattered most: *each party who enters the Covenant contributes. The fire that holds the agreement is not any one party's fire. It is the fire made by all parties together.*

Theron looked at his hands. He had Fire in the Gate sense — the Crucible's evaluation, the standard assessment, the Fire-attuned documentation that Cassara had placed in his file. He had the Fire Gate, opened at fifteen, burning amber in the register that Pyralith had called Sol'vathyn: permission granted.

He did not know if he could produce a Dragon flame.

He was not asked to.

The first hatchling touched his nose to Theron's palm and the interval-note in Theron's chest rang clearly, distinctly, and the fire that came was not Fire-Gate fire and not Dragon fire and not anything in the Dragon Codex or the Forge's formal literature. It was the third note made visible — the interval itself, burning. Not the crack and not the original, but the frequency that existed between them, the one that the crack had made possible by creating a space where no space had been. It was the palest fire he had ever seen, almost transparent, almost the color of heat without fuel, almost silence shaped into combustion. It rose from his palm and held.

From Serai: cold light made warm, the far-end of the spectrum she had always worked in finally given permission to warm, the deep-water hatchling's cold eyes reflecting it and refracting it and returning it transformed. Blue-white, exact, the temperature of something that had crossed enormous distances and arrived.

From Vorath: not fire in the ordinary sense. A luminescence. The Void Gate opened the way Nero's gift was meant to be opened — not reached for, arrived into — and what she held was the glow of potential before it collapsed into anything specific, pure and clear and containing all colors in the way that darkness contains all colors when it is not the corrupted kind but the original. The entity touched her wrist with its iridescent nose and the luminescence deepened.

From the primordial hatchling: the silver-white fire of the pre-Kindling state, older than the distinction between fire and not-fire, older than the first Dragon's choice, burning with the specific quality of something that had never been anything other than what it was.

From the deep-water hatchling: cool fire, fire that moved like water moves, the Cold-Light made combustion, burning steady and unhurried and entirely committed to burning for as long as it needed to without spending a single measure more than the burning required.

From Pyralith: twelve thousand years of accumulated heat, the deep amber of a fire that had decided in the first century to burn forever and had been burning ever since, given to this moment without reservation, the oldest Dragon in the world contributing to a Covenant that was not the one he had watched being made and was not the one he had maintained and was the one he had been waiting, without knowing he was waiting, for the conditions to make possible.

The six flames met.

Not explosively, not dramatically. They met the way flows met when they found the same channel — recognizing each other, adjusting, finding the configuration that allowed all of them to persist without any of them diminishing. The merged fire was not any one of them. It was the specific thing that their combination made: burning without fuel, self-sustaining, holding itself in the volcanic air of the Caldera with the specific character of something that had found its own reason to exist and would continue existing until that reason ended.

Covenant Fire.

It burned above the Caldera's center, visible to the Forge below if anyone looked up, which they would, which would require explanation, which Cassara would handle with the specific expertise of a person who had been handling unexplainable things for forty-three years.

Yindara, at the rim, said something in Thyr'vael — formal, ceremonial, the registration of the event in the Dragon Court's record. Something that ended with a term that Theron had found in the Codex and understood now from the inside: *vel'doth suvaran*. The agreement that changes what the parties are.

He had been right.

He had already been changed. The Covenant named it.

---

It was Obsidius who noticed Embera move.

Not dramatically — she had been still at the eastern rim for three hours, watching, and then she was less still, and then her head lifted in the specific way that a Dragon's head lifted when it had located something. Not a threat. A direction.

Theron followed the line of her gaze.

The road.

The road that came up from the harbor below, that wound through the volcanic approach to the Forge's main gate, that was visible from the Caldera's eastern rim as a pale stripe through the dark Draconis Ember rock. He had walked it once, coming up. He had not thought about it since.

Someone was on it.

A figure, alone, moving at the pace of someone who had been walking for a long time and had no particular urgency left to spend. No horse. No companion. The distance was enough that features were not visible, but the quality of movement was readable even from here: not a Corps messenger, not an Academy student returning from the harbor, not any of the official traffic that had been moving up and down the road for three days since the assault. The walk of someone who had come a very long way and was arriving, not passing through.

No Academy colors on the figure. No Corps insignia that he could read at this distance.

Just dust on the road and the specific quality of someone who had known they were coming here before they left wherever they left from.

He looked at Embera.

Her head was still lifted. The orientation of a Dragon who had found the thing it had been waiting for — not prey, not threat. Something else. The posture of completion: the final element, arriving.

She had watched three bonds form and had not moved from the rim for three days. She had watched the Covenant with those enormous eyes and had made no sound and had kept her specific distance, the way of something that knew its moment was not this moment but the next one, the one that would come when the waiting was finally done.

The figure came around the last curve of the road. Still too far for features. Close enough that Theron could see there was something specific about how they moved — not injury, not exhaustion, but the specific self-containment of someone who had been carrying something significant for a very long time and had learned to carry it without making it visible.

Serai was beside him. She had been watching the road too. She said, very quietly: "The Luminary."

"What?"

"No Academy colors. No Corps insignia. The Luminary's travelers don't carry either — independent jurisdiction, the Registry doesn't require it." She was very still. "The Luminary is the institution for people whose Gate opened before the Academy found them. Before they could be registered, before anyone could assess them, before the official infrastructure existed to catch them. The people who opened alone and kept it alone because there was no other option." She paused. "A Void Gate, from what I can see. One that's been open for a long time. You can tell by the way they carry themselves — the Gate that's been open since childhood versus the one that opened later. The posture is different. The Gate that opened early becomes the way you hold your own weight."

"You can see that from here."

"Cold Light sees specifics," she said, without pride, as a statement of function.

He looked at the figure and tried to see what Serai was seeing and could not. But he could feel, at the edge of his interval-note, something in the frequency space he was learning to inhabit — the pre-differentiation register, the Eleventh Frequency's domain. A resonance approaching. Getting louder in the way that things got louder when they got closer, even when they made no sound.

The entity had turned to face the road.

The primordial hatchling was beside it, and they stood together at the center of the Caldera looking at the road with the same clear recognition that the entity had used when it looked at Pyralith: seeing something it already knew.

Embera came down from the eastern rim.

Not quickly — she was not large enough to move the way Pyralith moved, not twelve thousand years of accumulated gravity, but she moved with the unhurried certainty of something that had finished waiting. Down the inner slope of the Caldera, past the standing members of the Dragon Court who watched her with expressions he couldn't read, toward the path that led down to the Forge's main gate.

Toward the road.

She passed close enough to Theron that he could have touched her flank if he had reached out. She did not look at him. She did not look at the assembled Court or at Pyralith or at the Covenant Fire still burning above the Caldera's center. She was looking ahead, down the path, with the orientation of a Dragon who had located what it had been waiting for and was now moving toward it with three days of accumulated stillness behind the motion.

He watched her go.

The entity watched her go, with those clear transparent eyes that held all colors in balance. The primordial hatchling was still, the way he was still when something was happening that he understood better than anyone else in the room and was letting it happen.

Obsidius, at the north rim, said something in Thyr'vael. Low, brief.

Pyralith answered in the common tongue, which Theron suspected was a deliberate choice — the same deliberateness as when he had spoken in the common tongue in the anteroom, when he had decided that something needed to be said in the language of the beings who needed to hear it.

"The last clutch is complete," Pyralith said. "The egg Embera reached for — the fourth. The one that was not ready. The one that could not be in this place until this place was ready for it." He watched Embera's descent with the amber warmth of something that had been watching a long time and was watching now in a different register than before. "The first clutch produced three. The fourth arrives differently. Not as a hatchling. As a bonded pair already forming."

Theron looked at the road.

The figure had come through the Forge's main gate. He could not see them from here — the gate was below the Caldera's line of sight. He could feel, through the interval-note in his chest, the approach of the resonance that was getting louder. Approaching the convergence point. The Eleventh Frequency's root note, familiar now in the way that the entity was familiar — the way of something you had never met and recognized anyway.

He could hear, faintly, the Covenant Fire above the Caldera making a sound he had not heard it make before. Not louder. Different. The way a bell changed when a new harmonic rang alongside its fundamental — not replacing the original, adding the overtone, the interval between becoming audible.

A seventh note.

He thought: we didn't know what we were signing until it was signed.

He thought: that might be the correct starting condition.

He looked at Serai. She was watching the road with the cold-light precision of her Gate and her face had an expression he had learned to read: the look she wore when she had processed everything available and the conclusion was larger than she had been calculating for. She said nothing. He didn't need her to.

Vorath was watching the Covenant Fire. Her face was turned upward, the Void Gate open in the way it was open when she was not controlling it but being present in it — receiving rather than reaching. Whatever she was hearing from the Fire's seventh note, it was in the register that the rest of them could not quite reach, the frequency space before differentiation, the potential that had not yet chosen what it was.

Her eyes were very still.

"She has a name," Vorath said.

Not a question. Not the announcement of new information — the statement of something she had been carrying and could now say because the moment had arrived for it. "The fourth bonded. The one Embera waited for." She looked away from the Fire, at Theron and Serai. "Kessa. She's been called Kessa since she could be called anything. She came from the Luminary three days ago when she felt the Eleventh Frequency resonate in the ley lines. She was already on the road by the time she knew why."

He thought about people who left before they understood why and arrived having understood on the way.

He thought about a young woman with dust on her boots and a Void Gate that had been open since she was born, walking up a volcanic road to a Fortress she had known about without being told about, following a resonance that was too large to ignore and too specific to mistake.

He thought: she's been carrying the fourth note. All this time.

Embera reached the main gate.

Theron could not see below it. He stood at the Caldera's rim and he listened to the Covenant Fire above him acquiring its seventh note and he felt, through the interval in his chest, the specific quality of something arriving that had already arrived a thousand times in potential and was arriving now in fact, in the specific heat of the volcanic morning, in the oldest mountain in Arcanea.

He heard, faint with distance, a Dragon make the sound that was not words. Low and deliberate, the subsonic register. Not distress. Not the combat frequency or the recognition-of-enemy frequency.

The sound Seraphine had made in the anteroom when Pyralith said Embera was waking.

Something ancient. Something that had been held a long time, and could now be released.

Pyralith did not move from the center of the Caldera. He stood with the Covenant Fire above him and the six of them below him — three pairs, the bonds still forming, the new architecture still assembling itself into something the Codex had no category for — and he looked out over the Forge's sprawl and the road and the mountain and the volcanic morning with the specific expression of something that had been burning for twelve thousand years and had just, for the first time in a very long time, been genuinely surprised.

---

The Covenant Fire burned above Mount Pyralis for the next seven days. Every delegation that had come to assess the aftermath looked at it and looked at Cassara and she looked back with the specific patience of a woman who had stopped trying to classify everything by the third day and had simply noted it as *ongoing anomalous phenomenon, source located, nature pending further study* and moved on to the seventeen other problems requiring her direct attention.

The Registry added a new category to its assessment forms. Not without significant institutional resistance, not without three formal objections from the assessment board and one extraordinary session called by the senior Codex scholars. But the category was added: *Bond Type: Covenant Resonance. Assessment Protocol: Under Development.*

Serai began a new document that she kept on the inside cover of her notebook, in her own shorthand, which she would eventually expand into the first scholarly treatise on the pre-differentiation bond state. She titled it, in the shorthand, with a phrase from what Pyralith had said to Obsidius during the Court: *vel'doth suvaran.* The agreement that changes what the parties are.

Vorath sat with the entity in the anteroom every morning and listened, and the entity's iridescent spectrum shifted each day in the specific pattern of something learning to communicate, and Vorath translated what she could — which was not much, and not precisely, but more each day. She kept those notes separate from everything else, in a language she was inventing as she went.

Theron found the primordial hatchling in the Kindling passage on the fourth morning, very still, in the specific posture of a Dragon who is thinking about speaking. He sat down on the basalt floor across from him. He waited.

On the fifth morning, the first hatchling spoke his second word.

It was a name.

Theron's hands were on the basalt of the Kindling passage floor and he felt the whole mountain respond to it — the Forge's deep foundation and the ancient chamber below the chamber and the ley lines rooting downward from every direction — the way a tuning fork made everything vibrate that could resonate with its frequency.

The name was the interval note, spoken aloud.

He sat with it until the resonance settled.

Then he went to find Cassara.
