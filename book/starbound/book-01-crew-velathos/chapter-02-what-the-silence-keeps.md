# Chapter Two: What the Silence Keeps

Reva did not sleep.

This was not unusual. She hadn't slept well in new environments since she was nineteen and a port garrison in the Oceanic Sector had been quietly overrun while the relief watch dozed, and she'd pulled four of her crew out of rising water in a basement they'd been left in while the rest of the garrison got away. She'd been the watch that night. She'd been asleep. She hadn't been asleep in a new place since.

She sat at the meeting hall table in the second hour after midnight with her coat on and her field log open and her pen cap off, not writing. Listening. The fire had been steady for two hours — not the four-second interruptions, not the whitening. Just fire. She found this less reassuring than it should have been. The rhythm had been the tell. Its absence now felt like a held breath.

Delth was asleep against the far wall with Soot heavy across his legs, the hound's sides moving in slow, even cycles. Professor was in the corner doing something quiet with its instruments. Kirin was supposed to be sleeping on the bench along the south wall. He wasn't.

"I know you're awake," she said, without looking up.

A pause. Then the rustle of a coat. "How."

"Your breathing changed forty minutes ago."

Kirin sat up on the bench and looked at the fire. He had a particular relationship with fire that Reva had spent two years learning to read. When he was thinking through a problem, he watched the fire with the focus of a craftsman examining work in progress. When he was in a bad state, he watched it the way you watch something you're not sure you trust. Right now he was watching it the way you watch a door you expect to open.

"It's too quiet," he said.

"Yes."

"Not the village quiet. Different quiet. The breathing stopped."

She looked up then. The lanterns in the square outside the window were steady and gold, nothing more. "When?"

"Maybe an hour ago. Maybe more. I've been watching the fire and it's just—" He gestured. "Fire."

"Which should be ordinary."

"Which is the least ordinary thing it's done all day." He stood and walked to the hearth and crouched before it. The fire regarded him with its usual frank warmth. He held his hand to the side of it, not over it. "When I was a student, before I understood anything about how fire talks, I thought silence meant safety. If nothing unusual is happening, nothing unusual is happening." He paused. "Now I know fire doesn't go quiet because the situation has resolved. It goes quiet because something in the situation has changed and it's waiting to tell me what."

Reva closed her log. "What do you think changed?"

He was quiet for a moment. "I think we got noticed."

---

She woke Yael first.

Yael was the lightest sleeper on the crew, a condition she'd inherited from years of navigating by starlight and which meant her eyes opened with the immediate focus of someone who had never quite fully arrived in sleep. She was upright and oriented inside two seconds.

"The rhythm stopped," Reva said.

Yael was already looking at the window. At the dark sky above the roofline where the overcast had thinned slightly in the past hour, showing one clean band of stars above the marsh ridge. She looked for a long moment.

"It's still here," she said. "But it's—" She stopped. Turned to look at Reva with an expression Reva had seen before, once, in the deep caves of the Void Frontier, when Yael had read the starlight off a chamber wall and understood something about the formation that she'd had trouble putting into words. "It was breathing to itself. Now it's breathing at us."

"The lantern that stayed white," Kirin said, from across the room.

"Yes." Yael was pulling on her second boot. "That was the tell. We stayed. We lit fires and cooked food and slept in its space. It's—reassessed us." She looked at the fire. "It read you through the flames, Kirin, all evening. Not consuming. Reading. You had a flame out for six hours in a space where everything else goes silent. You're—" She considered. "You're a frequency it can examine. And it's been examining."

"And now?"

Yael stood. "And now it's decided to take something."

---

It was Delth who noticed first.

He woke without cause at the third hour, which happened to him sometimes — the bond with Soot was not one-directional, and when the hound processed something in her sleep that her waking mind hadn't flagged, the processing sometimes reached through to him like a hand on the shoulder. He'd learned to trust it.

He lay still for a moment and listened. Soot's warmth against his legs. The fire. Reva and Yael talking quietly. The normal inventory of sensation.

Then he reached, as he did automatically a dozen times a day, for the thread of the bond. Not for information — just the way you check a handhold, the reflexive certainty that a thing is there.

The bond was there. Soot was beside him, alive, warm, breathing. He could feel her. He put his hand on her back and felt the slow heat under her fur and the rhythm of her ribs.

He could not hear her breathing.

He sat up.

She was breathing. He could feel it — her chest moving against his leg, regular and deep. He pressed his ear toward her. Nothing. No sound. He watched her exhale and the breath condensed slightly in the cool air of the hall and he watched it and heard nothing.

"Captain," he said.

Reva crossed to him in four steps. She crouched and listened.

"She's breathing," Delth said. "I can feel it. I just can't—"

Reva pressed two fingers lightly to Soot's throat. A moment. "Her pulse is normal." She looked up at Kirin, who had come to his feet.

"It took the sound," Kirin said.

"The sound of her breathing."

"Specifically." He looked at the fire, then at the window, then at the fire again. "Not the sound of the room. The fire is crackling. The wood is settling. It's only—" He crossed to the bench and picked up his water cup and knocked it against the wood of the table and the knock rang normally. Then he picked up Delth's metal tool case, which Delth kept close at hand, and set it down on the stone hearth.

Silence.

"Objects belonging to us," Yael said.

"Objects belonging to her," Kirin corrected. He looked at Soot. "She came into Tetharn already bonded. Already herself. She has—a distinct frequency. A signature it wants." He crouched beside the hound, who had woken now and was looking at Delth with the specific wide-eyed patience of an animal who has noticed something is wrong and is waiting for the person she trusts to help her understand what to do about it.

Delth scratched her ears slowly. His face was very still.

"She doesn't know," he said. "She can hear herself. She's not in distress. She just—" He turned his palm up, the gesture he used when Soot gave him something the language didn't quite cover. "She says it's like looking at a reflection that isn't synchronized. Like watching her own breath from outside it."

"It took a copy," Professor said.

They all looked at it.

Professor had been standing at the window. It turned now with its notebook in its hands, its dark glass eyes reflecting the fire.

"Not the sound itself," it said. "A copy of the frequency. The pattern that makes the sound hers and not another hound's. The specific resonance signature of her breathing, which is unique — every creature's respiration has a signature, shaped by the particular configuration of lung and bone and the elemental composition of the being." It paused. "It collects frequencies. It absorbed a sample of the stone striking water not to silence the stone, but to take the stone's frequency for its archive. The shadow-suppression at the wall — it was not consuming light's interaction with the surface. It was cataloguing it." Professor's voice was doing something careful and particular. "It's been here for a very long time. And it has been collecting, for all of that time."

"Collecting what?" Reva said.

"Everything. Sounds, light-angles, resonance patterns." Professor looked at the toy boat on the table. "People."

---

The silence stretched.

Soot licked Delth's hand. Delth kept his face still.

"Not consuming," Reva said. "You said that last night. Yael said it too. They might not be dead."

"They are not dead." Professor said this with the certainty it reserved for things it had worked out fully. "To consume a frequency is to destroy its pattern forever. What this entity does is — closer to what a Veloura Glass does. Stores emotional memory. Holds the impression exactly." It looked at its notes. "The two hundred and thirty people of Tetharn were not killed. They were archived."

The word arrived in the room with a weight.

"Preserved," Yael said.

"Yes. Intact. Frozen at the moment of collection. Everything about them as they were at that moment — every frequency, every resonance signature, every sound they were in the process of making — held." Professor set down its notebook. "Like specimens. The way the old scholars of the First Age preserved samples of Luminor Fragments — not to use them, but to hold them. To study them. To have them."

"Then they're alive," Kirin said.

"That is a more complicated question than it appears."

"Professor."

"They are intact. They are not progressing. They are exactly as they were at the moment of collection, and they will be exactly that, as long as the collection holds." The brass joints of its hand adjusted slightly around the pen. "This is not the same as living."

Another silence.

"Then we go find the collection," Reva said.

---

Nobody argued this time.

They moved out of the meeting hall into the square, Kirin ahead with a flame that they'd agreed he should keep burning regardless — a reference point, a known frequency in the space. The lanterns were back to ordinary gold. The village was dark in the way villages are dark before dawn, every shape crisp and still.

"Where?" Delth said.

"Below," Reva said.

Kirin looked at her.

"Where do you archive something? Where does something vast and old and rooted keep what it collects?" She was already moving toward the well. "We dropped a stone. The well swallowed it."

"The well's a surface feature," Kirin said, following. "It's a hole someone dug. Whatever is below this village is—"

"Old," Yael said. "Older than the well. But the well goes down into it. The first families who settled here, three generations back — they dug their well into the edge of something that was already here. Something with the patience of geological formations." She stopped at the well's edge and looked down into the dark. "Something the birds knew about. Something that didn't notice the village for a very long time, and then did."

Professor was beside her, measuring. "I'm reading an anomalous cavity approximately eight meters down. Extending — I cannot determine the extent without going down. But the resonance signature is consistent throughout this entire village, and it's coming from below." It looked at the well's rope, the wooden frame, the iron crank. "Standard construction. The bucket rope extends nine meters."

Reva looked around the square. The waiting faces of the open houses. The chalk fish-weight tallies on the meeting hall's front board, visible through the window.

"Kirin, you're with me. Yael, Delth — stay up here. If the rope goes wrong, get the others."

"Captain," Delth started.

"If we don't come back up, we don't come back up. If you come down after us we lose the crew whole. Stay up here." She looked at Soot. "The breathing is still gone?"

Delth checked. "Yes."

"Then it's still active. It's still — here and processing. Which means what it has is still stored." She gripped the rope. "If we can find what it has, we can find them."

Kirin looked at his flame. It was burning white at the edges — not the full whitening, but a fringe of it, the way a voice sounds when it's speaking more carefully than usual.

"It knows we're going," he said.

"Then we don't keep it waiting."

---

They went down.

The well walls were smooth limestone, wet with condensation, narrowing slightly before opening again. Kirin went first, the rope in both hands, flame summoned around his fist — not for heat but for light, the old fire-mage's trick of using combustion as a lantern. Eight meters down. Ten. The rope ran out just as his feet found purchase.

Not the bottom of the well.

A ledge. Stone underfoot, solid and level. He looked down and his flame cast light on nothing — a drop, steep, with a darkness below that didn't behave like ordinary darkness. He pressed himself to the well wall and reached up for Reva's feet as she descended.

She landed beside him without a word.

They were in a throat. A narrow passage of carved stone — he corrected himself: not carved, worn. Worn the way riverstone wears, smooth and shaped by long passage of something. Not water. The passage was dry. Something else had moved through here, for a very long time, and the stone remembered.

"Forward," Reva said.

The passage opened.

---

He had been in caverns. The Fractured Caves of the Ember Sector, which were enormous — ceilings you couldn't see from the floor, chambers wide enough to echo for seven seconds. He had been in the underground vaults of the Arathis Compact, which were immense and cold and smelled of old metal. He had a reference for large.

This was not large in the way caverns were large. It was large in the way a thought is large — size that pressed against the limits of what the mind could hold and then kept going.

But he didn't think about the size first. He thought about the light.

Every surface was lit.

Not with fire — fire needed fuel and air and this place had air but he could see no source. The light was internal to the space itself, a faint luminescence that rose from the floor and the walls and from the things that filled the space, the things that were everywhere, arrayed in rows and clusters and formations that had their own logic, their own organizing principle that he couldn't yet name.

Crystals.

He had to correct himself again immediately. Not crystals in the ordinary sense. Not stalagmites, not mineral formations. These were — contained. Each one was distinct, a separate piece, the size of various things: some the size of a fist, some the size of a cart, some taller than he was. Each was translucent, each was lit from within, and inside each one—

"Kirin," Reva said.

She was looking at one of the larger pieces. He crossed to her.

Inside it: a morning. He could see it. A kitchen, rendered in perfect detail, a woman at a stove with her back to him. The light through the window she faced was early-morning light, flat and golden. On the counter to her right, a pot with its lid ajar, steam suspended — caught, frozen, unmoving. The woman's posture — the specific angle of her shoulders that meant she was leaning in slightly, about to stir something. All of it still. All of it present.

"That's a person," he said.

"That's a person," Reva confirmed.

"She's in there."

"She's in there."

He moved to the next. A man mending a net, his hands at the moment of pulling a knot tight. Behind him, through an open door, a slice of harbor in morning light, one of the fourteen boats at its slip. The net's texture was visible in the crystal. The man's expression — not looking up, concentrating. Caught in an ordinary moment.

And the next: two children at a table, one pointing at something outside the window, the other turning to look with a mouth partly open and an expression of beginning-delight, a fraction of a second before the expression would have completed itself.

Frozen.

"They're all here," Reva said. Her voice was doing something very careful and controlled. She was walking between the formations, reading each one, and her face was the professional face she used when she could not afford to have any other face. "Two hundred and thirty. They're all here."

"They're intact," he said.

"They're—" She stopped at a formation to her right and looked at it for a long moment. Inside it: a very old man, seated, a cup in both hands, looking out a window. The light on his face was the specific quality of late-morning light. His expression was private and unremarkable, the face of a person alone without an audience. The kind of face nobody but a collector would ever see. "They are exactly as they were."

The space went on. Past the villagers, in the deeper rows, other formations. Some very old — the crystal that held them had a different quality, a settled density, like glass that has been transparent for centuries and developed a slight antiquity to its color. He looked closely at one.

A forest scene. Trees he didn't recognize, the foliage wrong for anything in the current era — thick-lobed, enormous, the kind of growth associated with pre-Luminor climate. Two figures, very small in the frame of the scene, standing in the trees doing something he couldn't make out. Their clothing was from no period he could place.

"First Age," he said.

Reva came to his side.

"The trees. Look at the canopy." He pointed. "Those are old-growth. Pre-settlement. Before the Luminor Fallout changed the soil composition." He looked at the two figures. "Whatever this thing has been doing — it was doing it when these people were alive."

Reva was quiet for a moment. Then: "Count the First Age formations."

He looked. Down the far rows, the density of them. The settled, ancient light in their depths. His flame felt very small.

"Many," he said.

"Many," she agreed.

---

They found the Archivist at the center of the space.

It was not what he had expected, which he could admit now that he was standing in front of it, because he hadn't known he'd had an expectation until it was not met. He'd imagined something with presence — not a creature, necessarily, but something he could read as intentional. Something that looked like what it was.

The Archivist was a shape. That was the best word he had. A shape that existed in the space between the crystal formations, near the floor, pooling and shifting the way deep water moves — not fluid but its own kind of solid, a solid that had no obligation to hold the same configuration from one moment to the next. It was darker than the space around it but not by much. It was present the way certain kinds of knowledge are present: impossible to look at directly, clear in peripheral vision, impossible to deny.

It was looking at him. He could feel that. It was looking at his flame.

"You're an Archivist," Reva said.

The shape shifted. Not toward them — a lateral movement, a sort of attention.

"I know what you are," she said. "The original Athenaeum cataloguers. Before the Falls. Before the institutional collapses in the Fourth Age." She was reading it the way she read everything — without expression, letting the information organize itself. "Something went wrong."

The shift again. Kirin felt his flame lean toward the shape — not toward warmth, toward something like recognition.

"It can hear you," he said.

"Yes." She didn't look at him. "But it can't answer the way we answer. It collects. That's the whole mechanism. It takes a frequency and holds it." She was still looking at the shape. "It doesn't produce frequency. It lost that."

He understood then. The things it had taken — the stone's sound in the well, Soot's breathing, the shadows that wouldn't fall — it wasn't silencing them. It was reading them. Taking a sample. The way you'd take a core sample from a glacier, a section of something ongoing, a moment extracted from its sequence.

"It doesn't know they're people," he said.

"It knows. It knew when it collected them." Reva's voice was very quiet. "But it's been here since before the First Age, Kirin. It has been collecting since — since the forest we saw in those deep formations was alive. Two hundred and thirty people to it are—" She paused. "Rare. Significant. That's not comfort, but it's not malice either."

"What did it lose?"

She looked at the shape. "The ability to put things back."

---

He thought of the toy boat on the table upstairs.

One person, small, the last one through. Soot's trail ending at a child's toy on a crate. He looked at the formations around them and found, after a moment, the ones that were smallest — not children in every case, but at the scale of children's lives: a boy, maybe eight, midway through the act of throwing a stone toward what the angle suggested was a cat on a fence post. His face alight with pure mischief, a fraction of a second before the throw. Preserved in exactly the quality of his particular eight-year-old joy.

The shape near the floor moved again, slowly, and Kirin felt his flame lean toward it again.

"It's not indifferent," he said.

"No," Reva said. "That's the problem."

He looked at her.

"If it were indifferent, the answer would be simple. You break the collection, you walk away." She was looking at the deep formations now, the old-growth forest and the pre-Falls figures in their ancient light. "But look at what's in here. All of it. Not just the village." She walked slowly down the far row. He followed. "These formations — whatever is in them, it's the only record anywhere of what these moments looked like. This forest is gone. These people are gone from every other record. Whatever happened here—" She stopped at a formation that held a seascape, an ocean that didn't look like any ocean in the current maps, rendered in perfect clarity. "This is a library. A real one. The kind the Athenaeum was supposed to build and never quite did, because the Athenaeum was built by people and people get things wrong and then the building burns down." She turned back to him. "If we free the villagers, we break this. All of it. This is how it works — we've been down here twenty minutes and I can tell already that the whole collection is one system. It doesn't have parts. It's one continuous archive."

Kirin looked at the boy with the stone and the mischief face.

"The boy is in there," he said.

"Yes."

"The First Age seascape doesn't require a living body to exist."

"No."

"Then they're not equivalent."

"No." She said this as if the word cost something. "They're not."

He looked at the shape, still pooled near the center of the space, still tilting its whatever-it-had toward his flame. Reading him. Still reading him, even now, adding to what it had.

"Can it understand us?" he said.

"It's been in this village for six hours with us," Reva said. "It's been reading your fire since you walked through the door. It read Yael's starlight. It sampled Soot's breath. Whatever understanding it has access to—" She paused. "We are probably the most thoroughly read people in this room."

He held his flame out toward the shape. Not toward it — the way you hold something out in offering rather than demand. The way you open a hand.

"We're going to free them," he said. "The people. The ones who were alive when you took them. That part—" He stopped. Started again. "That part of the collection wasn't yours to take. The rest of it — the seascapes, the forest, the things that are already past — that's yours. That's what you were made for." His flame sat steady in his palm, warm gold, the fire that ran when he meant comfort rather than combat. "But the living ones. We need them back."

The shape was still. The stillest it had been since they entered.

Then Kirin's flame went white.

Not the four-second whitening. Not the lantern-pulse. A slow, spreading white, moving from the center of the flame outward, a change not of temperature but of character — the flame becoming something it recognized from the First Age formations, a light with the quality of something very old deciding to be understood.

He held very still.

The white light spread from his palm to the floor between them and the nearest formation — the one holding the woman at the stove with her hand reaching toward the pot. The formation began to vibrate. Not to crack — to resonate. The sound of it was a frequency below hearing, something he felt in his chest rather than his ears.

The woman in the formation turned.

She turned, inside the crystal, and looked at her own hands, and made the movement of someone completing a motion that had been interrupted mid-gesture. She stirred the pot. The steam moved. She turned further and looked at the kitchen around her with the expression of someone recalibrating — not alarmed, not confused, but recalibrating in the way you recalibrate when something is the same as it was but your sense of its weight has shifted.

The formation was still there. The woman was moving inside it.

"They don't know," Kirin said. He was barely breathing. "They were preserved so completely that they don't know any time passed."

The white light moved from formation to formation, slowly, without urgency. One by one the small frozen dramas began to complete themselves — the man's net knot pulling tight, the children finishing their moment of shared looking, the old man at the window shifting his cup slightly. All of them still inside their formations, still within the crystal, but no longer frozen. Moving.

"It's releasing them," Reva said.

"Not yet." He watched the white light work its way through the rows. "It's—waking them. There's a difference."

The shape near the center of the space was very still. He watched it and he thought: it knew what to do. It's always known what to do with living things. It had just forgotten that knowing was not the same as choosing.

"The rest stays," he said, not to it exactly, more to the space. "The First Age formations. The seascape. The forest. Everything that's irreplaceable because the moment it holds is the only record left anywhere." He looked at the deep rows. "That stays."

The shape moved once — the lateral attention, the way it turned itself toward something.

"We'll send people," he said. "Scholars. People who know how to study a collection like this." He looked at Reva. She was watching him with the expression she used when she was going to tell him in an hour that what he'd done was exactly right and she should have thought of it. "The Athenaeum's descendants. The ones who're still doing this kind of work. We'll send them here, and this place won't be a secret, and what you've kept here won't be kept only by you."

The white light had reached the far rows. The deep formations sat quiet and unchanged, their ancient light unaltered. Everything that had been alive and taken from its living — all of it moving again, waking, contained but no longer frozen.

---

They surfaced at the grey edge of dawn.

The rope was as they'd left it. Kirin went first, came over the rim, stood in the square with his hands on his knees for a moment and then straightened. Reva came up behind him.

Yael and Delth were at the well's edge. Soot was pressed against Delth's leg with the precise quality of attention she gave to things she'd been waiting on.

"Soot," Delth said.

The hound cocked her head.

"Can you hear her?"

He listened. His face changed. He crouched and pressed his palm to her back and his face changed again. He put his forehead down against the top of her skull, briefly, the way he sometimes did when the bond gave him something he needed to just hold for a moment without looking at anyone.

"Yes," he said.

In the square, the lanterns were all ordinary gold. The overcast had broken fully while they were below and the sky to the east was beginning to pale, the stars at the horizon going first. The village was silent in the way a village is silent before its people have woken, before the chimneys have started, before the ordinary machinery of a morning has got going.

Below them, it was different.

"They're awake," Kirin said. "They're still in the formations. But they're awake and they're moving and they're—" He stopped. "They're going to start asking questions."

"Yes," Reva said.

"Two hundred and thirty people, in a library below their village, who were in the middle of breakfast yesterday morning and are about to discover considerably more time has passed. They are going to have many questions."

"Yes."

"And when they come up—"

"We'll be here," Reva said. She was already turning toward the well, looking at the rope, considering. "We'll need to find another way in. The well is too narrow." She looked at Professor. "There's an entrance to the passage. We came up from the ledge but there must be access from the north side, beyond the tanner's wall. Something collapsed, or was sealed."

"I'll look," Professor said, and moved.

Kirin stood in the square. The fire in the meeting hall was visible through the window — still burning, still gold, not white. The place where the entity breathed its four-second rhythm was quiet now in a different way. Not a held breath. A settled one.

He thought: we came to find what happened to two hundred and thirty people.

He thought: we found a library.

He thought: those are not the same problem and they are also, entirely, the same problem, and he had not known when he walked through this square yesterday morning which kind of problem it was.

Delth was on his feet, Soot beside him, and he caught Kirin's eye across the square with the look he used when he was running a precise internal accounting of what had just happened and was waiting to find out if he'd missed anything important.

Kirin shook his head. They hadn't missed anything. Or if they had, it was the kind of thing that would only become clear later, in a different chapter, with more information than they currently had.

That was, in his experience, how it usually went.

---

By the time the sun was fully up, Professor had found the access — a sealed door in the foundation of the northernmost house, behind a false wall that had been built with enough time and intention that the original builders hadn't known it was there. Beyond it, stairs, descending. The kind of stairs that had been used for a very long time and then not used, and were ready.

They set Professor at the door and went below again, and the process of bringing two hundred and thirty people up out of the archive and into the morning took until midday, and it was not clean or easy or lacking in the questions Kirin had predicted.

He answered the ones he could. Reva answered the ones that required the captain's authority. The ones that had no answers yet, those he listened to carefully and wrote down in the small notebook he kept in his coat pocket, because in his experience those were the ones that mattered most for what came next.

The Archivist stayed in the deep part of the space, among the First Age formations. When the last of the villagers was up, and the stairs were empty, and the archive was quiet again, Kirin went back down alone for a moment and stood at the entrance to the central space and looked at the shape near the floor, still and patient in its own particular way.

He didn't say anything.

He put his hand out, palm up, the same gesture he'd made before. Not an offering. A question. The same question he'd asked the first time, with no words — the kind of question that doesn't become clearer by being articulated.

The shape looked at his flame for a long moment.

His flame went white.

Just for a second. Then back to gold.

He thought: it's not better. It's not fixed. The mechanism that made it do what it did to this village is still intact, and we can't dismantle it from here, and the scholars who come will have to work with it as it is. But it knows the difference, now, between a living frequency and a dead one. Between what's still in the world and what has already left it.

Between what's yours to keep and what isn't.

He went back up into the morning.

---

Reva was sitting on the edge of the well, writing in her field log.

Around her, the village was starting to sound like a village again. Someone had started a fire — someone else, not the crew, one of the villagers — and the smoke from it rose straight up in the still morning air. Two people were in a doorway in what looked like a very careful conversation. A child ran across the square, which was the first running Kirin had seen in Tetharn and which registered in his chest as something he hadn't realized he was waiting for.

"We need to send word to the Corps," Reva said.

"Today?"

"Today. And to the Archive Institute — the one in Arathis, the independent one, not the Corps' cataloguing department. They'll want to send someone who knows what they're looking at." She paused in her writing. "And we need to stay. At least until a Corps liaison arrives. These people have come up from a night that lasted — they don't know how long yet — and they're going to need someone to tell them what we know."

"Which isn't much."

"Which is more than they have." She looked at the chalk numbers on the meeting hall board. The fish-weight tallies. The unfinished business of an ordinary morning. "The collection below. We can't move it. We don't know if it can be moved. The Archivist is still there and still doing what it does and the villagers are going to have to decide what to do about living above it." She closed her log. "That's not our decision to make."

"No."

She stood. She was quiet for a moment, looking at the village. The smoke. The child.

"The toy boat," she said.

He'd been thinking about it too.

"It's still on the table inside," he said.

"Go get it." She looked at the square, scanning. "The child who ran just now — watch where they go."

He went in and got the boat off the table and came back out, and the child had stopped near the entrance to one of the houses on the south side, standing in the doorway with both hands on the frame, looking at the square with the expression of someone who has just stepped out into a morning that feels slightly too bright.

Kirin crossed the square and crouched to the child's level. She was maybe seven. She had the coloring of someone whose family had been here three generations, and she was looking at him with the specific alert caution of a child assessing whether someone is going to be useful or not.

He held out the toy boat.

She looked at it. Then she looked at his face. Then she took it with both hands and held it the way the paint-worn hull told him she'd held it many times before — thumb on the bow, fingers curled under the keel.

She didn't say anything. Kirin didn't either.

Then she turned and went inside, carrying the boat. He heard a voice inside the house — higher, sharper, calling a name. The child's voice answering. Another door, and footsteps.

He stood up and went back to where Reva was standing.

"Report will take most of this morning to write," she said.

"I'll help with the transmissions."

"I know you will." She looked at him. "You did something right, down there. The way you handled the release."

"You had already figured it out."

"Figuring out and knowing what to do with it are different skills." She turned back to her field log. "The fire going white — you kept your hand steady. That mattered."

He looked at his palm. The flame he called up was warm gold, the way it ran when he meant comfort rather than combat.

The smoke from the first rekindled fire rose straight into a clear morning sky.

"Tide patience," he said.

She didn't look up from her log. "Tide patience," she agreed.
