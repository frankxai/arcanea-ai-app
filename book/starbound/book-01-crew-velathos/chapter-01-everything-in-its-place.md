# Chapter One: Everything in Its Place

The harbor came into view around the headland at the third hour past dawn, and for a moment Kirin thought they'd been given the wrong coordinates.

"Captain," he said.

"I see it."

"The boats—"

"I see them."

He counted. Fourteen fishing vessels in their slips, plus two larger cargo rigs tied to the outer dock. Everything fastened correctly. Everything present. The morning sun came in flat across the water and lit the village above the harbor in clean yellow light, and the village looked fine. It looked exactly as it should look. That was what made the back of Kirin's neck prickle.

Reva Saltmark stood at the bow with her arms folded and her coat open despite the cold, reading the water the way she read everything — methodically, without expression, as if the information she needed was already there and simply hadn't organized itself into useful form yet. She'd been standing that way for eleven minutes. Kirin had counted.

"No smoke," Delth said from behind him, quieter.

Kirin looked. The village had forty or fifty chimneys. None of them had anything rising from them. Not extinguished, not thin trickles of morning fires just getting started — nothing. Not a thread.

"Maybe they bank their fires differently here," Kirin said.

Nobody agreed with him, which was fair.

---

Professor stood at the stern making notes in the small leather-bound book it carried everywhere, the one with the clasp made of Laeylinn Jade that had, on three separate occasions, sprouted a single green shoot overnight and then thought better of it. Kirin had watched it happen each time. He hadn't asked.

"The temperature differential between sea and land suggests the village has been without significant heat sources for somewhere between eighteen and thirty-six hours," Professor said, without looking up. "This is consistent with the timeline in the mission brief. The last contact from the village was forty-two hours ago."

"What kind of contact was it?" Kirin asked.

"The ordinary kind. A boat captain reporting his cargo manifest. Nothing alarmed. Nothing unusual. He listed his catch weights, his ETA at the Arathis docks, and then signed off." Professor turned a page. "He has not been seen since. His boat is the third slip on the left."

Kirin looked at the third slip on the left. The boat rocked gently in the morning tide. Its ropes were properly coiled.

He didn't like that detail. He couldn't have said exactly why.

Soot came up beside him and pressed her nose to his hip, which was Soot's way of making sure he hadn't fallen off the world. He put his hand on the ember hound's back and felt the steady warmth under her fur — not the warmth of a fire but the specific warmth of a body that ran slightly hotter than bodies should, as if something at her center was banked but never went entirely out. She smelled of iron and old smoke and the particular clean-dirt scent of a creature that had been clean for two days and was beginning to develop opinions about this.

Delth came to stand beside her, and when his hand found her back his scarred fingers landed in the gaps between Kirin's without either of them doing it deliberately. They stood that way for a moment.

"She smells the shore," Delth said.

"Anything unusual?"

"She's not sure. Salt. Fish. Wood. The ordinary things." He paused. "She says there are no birds."

Kirin looked at the sky above the village. He'd been looking at the harbor so intently he'd missed it. No gulls riding the thermals above the fish processing buildings. No crows on the rooflines. No starlings in the eaves.

"Birds leave before we know to leave," Delth said.

---

Yael came up from below when they were fifty meters from the dock, her collar turned up against the wind and her hands in her pockets, and she stood at the rail and looked at the village for a long time without speaking. In the full morning light, the faint silver luminescence of her skin was invisible. It only showed after dark, which she was grateful for, and which everyone on the crew was carefully non-observant about.

"Anything?" Reva said.

"The starlight from last night still lingers." Yael turned her head slightly, as if listening to something at the edge of sound. "It's hard to read. Diffused. Like someone stirred the water before I could look into it."

"Recent interference, or old?"

"Recent. Within the last day." She paused. "The stars don't record what happened. They record the shape of what happened. Whatever this was, it happened fast. The impression is clean. No buildup, no warning." She looked at the captain. "One moment the village was itself. The next moment it wasn't."

Reva nodded slowly. It was the nod she used when information confirmed what she'd already suspected and she had decided not to find this satisfying.

"Tie us off," she said. "We go in on foot."

---

The dock was well-maintained. Kirin noticed that too. No rotting planks, no loose cleats. Someone had painted the bollards within the last year. He was beginning to find the village's good upkeep unsettling in the way that a locked door is less frightening than an unlocked one standing open.

Soot stepped off the boat ahead of all of them and stood on the dock with her nose working and her tail low but not tucked. A cautious position. An I-don't-know-yet position.

"Good girl," Delth said, landing beside her.

The five of them walked the dock in silence. Reva first, then Delth and Soot, then Professor with its notebook still open, then Kirin and Yael at the rear. The dock boards made the sounds dock boards make. The water made the sounds water makes. Everything was completely normal except for the fourteen fishing boats that should have been at sea three hours ago and the fifty chimneys that should have had smoke and the several hundred people who should have been here and were not.

The village was called Tetharn. The Corps brief described it as a salt-fishing community, two hundred and thirty inhabitants, three generations of the same twelve families, known for a particular method of cold-curing fish that the Arathis markets paid a premium for. Seasonal trade, steady population, no notable arcane signatures in the regional survey. The kind of place that got along well enough without being remarkable.

The brief also noted, in the way briefs noted things that the writer thought were too minor to lead with: *No distress signal was received. Corps monitoring picked up the silence.*

Kirin had been doing this long enough to know that the absence of a distress signal was usually worse than the signal itself.

---

The main street ran from the harbor uphill to a small square with a well. The houses on either side had their doors standing open. Not broken open — standing open. As if someone had been in the middle of ordinary morning business and simply stepped out for a moment and hadn't come back.

Through one doorway, Kirin could see a table with three bowls on it. Breakfast still in the bowls. He walked to the doorway and looked without crossing the threshold, and the porridge in the bowls had crusted over but not molded, which put the last meal somewhere in the eighteen-to-thirty-six-hour window Professor had already calculated.

"Not illness," Reva said, from the middle of the street. "Illness leaves evidence. You'd see people down. You'd find bodies in doorways. Illness doesn't clear a village."

"Neither does flooding," Kirin said. "The harbor's calm. There's no waterline on the walls."

"Fire leaves ash. Raid leaves blood and wreckage." Reva had her hands clasped behind her back. "Nothing has been taken. Nothing has been broken." She paused. "These people walked out of their homes in the middle of breakfast."

"Or were walked," Yael said, quietly.

Nobody replied.

---

Soot led them through the village at her own pace, nose down, tail still low. Delth walked with her, matching her movements with the ease of something practiced so long it had stopped being practice and become a second nature. When Soot paused, Delth paused. When she turned, he turned. Through the bond they shared, he was reading everything she read — but Soot's reading of a place was not vision. It was a three-dimensional landscape of scent, a map of what had been here and how long ago and whether it was still here somewhere underneath.

He came back to the group in the village square with his hands in his coat pockets and a look on his face that Kirin recognized as the look he got when something Soot was telling him didn't match anything he had a word for.

"She can track them," Delth said. "Two hundred and thirty separate scent trails, all moving in the same direction. Not a panic run. Walking pace, or near to it. They went north, up through the village and out the back road."

"The back road goes to the marsh," Reva said. She had the regional map. She didn't have to unfold it — she'd been reading regional maps since before Kirin was born and she filed them somewhere she could access without looking. "The marsh runs three kilometers to the inland ridge. Nowhere to go after that except through the ridgeline passes, and the nearest pass is a full day's walk."

"She loses the trail at the northern edge of the village," Delth said. "At the tanner's back wall."

"Loses it how?" Reva asked.

He paused. "The trail ends."

"They turned around?"

"No." He was choosing his words carefully, which meant Soot had given him something that language didn't quite cover. "The trail ends the way a thread ends. They were walking north. Then they weren't anywhere Soot can find."

Yael had gone very still.

---

They found the wall together. Delth and Soot leading, the rest following the hound's deliberate pace through the narrow passage between the tanning sheds, out into a yard where someone had left a half-finished barrel and a set of tools laid neatly on a workbench.

The tools were laid neatly. Kirin kept having to notice details like that and he was beginning to understand that the neatness itself was the wrongness. Not chaos. Not haste. Everything in its place.

The wall was unremarkable. The back wall of the northernmost house, stone and mortar, two meters high. The village ended here. Beyond it, the marsh grasses started, brown and long in the late-season cold, moving in the wind.

Soot stopped three feet from the wall.

She didn't growl. She didn't back away. She sat down and looked at the wall with the specific quality of attention that animals give to things they cannot file into any category they've been given.

"Here," Delth said.

They all looked at the wall.

"It's a wall," Kirin said.

"It's where the trail goes," Delth said.

---

Kirin raised his hand and summoned a small flame. Not for light — the morning was bright enough — but because his fire had told him things before that his eyes couldn't. He let it sit on his palm, a flame the size of a lemon, warm gold the way his fire always ran when he meant comfort rather than combat. He held it toward the wall.

The flame didn't cast a shadow.

He moved closer. The flame was real — he could feel it against his palm, feel the familiar heat-that-was-also-his-own-pulse — but the wall behind it should have had a shadow at this angle and didn't. He moved the flame left and right. Nothing. He turned and held the flame out toward the workbench, and the workbench had a clean, ordinary shadow.

He turned back to the wall.

"Captain."

"I see it."

"Does that happen normally with walls?"

"Not in my experience."

He pushed the flame closer, until his arm was extended fully, the fire two handspans from the stone. He watched his own shadow pool at his feet, sharp and clear. The wall had nothing. As if it was declining to participate in the ordinary business of light and dark.

Professor had its notebook out. "Fascinating. The suppression is localized — I can confirm the effect extends approximately one meter from the wall surface in any direction. Beyond that radius, light behavior is normal." It scribbled something. "This is consistent with a Kyuro Void Crystal resonance effect. Kyuro Void Crystal absorbs frequency, including light's interaction frequency with physical surfaces. But at this scale, you would need an extraordinary quantity of the material, or a single piece of exceptional—"

"Professor," Reva said.

"Yes?"

"Is it a naturally occurring deposit?"

"No. Kyuro Void Crystal does not form naturally at the surface. It requires significant pressure and very specific geological conditions that—" Professor looked up from its notebook. "No. Someone put this here. Or something."

---

They had been in the village for two hours when Kirin noticed the silence.

Not the absence of people — he'd been aware of that since the harbor. This was a different silence. He stopped in the middle of the square, next to the well, and listened.

No wind. But the marsh grasses were moving.

No birds. He'd known that.

No water in the well. He walked to it and looked down and the water level was fine, three meters down, clear and still. But the well made no sound. He dropped a small stone and watched it hit the surface and saw the ripples but heard nothing. Nothing at all.

He pressed his ear toward the opening. Silence, deep as a held breath.

"Professor," he said.

Professor crossed to him. Looked down the well. Made a note. Then stood upright with an expression that, on a face made of cleverly-articulated brass and dark glass eyes, still managed to communicate something between discomfort and professional excitement.

"It's not a dampening effect," Professor said. "A dampening effect reduces sound. This is different." It paused. "Something is consuming the sound. Actively. Not preventing it from being made — the physical process of the stone striking water occurred, the vibrations were generated. But they were absorbed before they could travel."

"Absorbed by what?" Yael asked.

"By whatever is still here."

The square was quiet. The open doors of the houses looked at them from three sides like waiting faces.

"It can't absorb sound without leaving a trace," Reva said. It wasn't a question. It was the working-through-a-problem tone she used when she was three steps ahead and confirming each step.

"No," Professor agreed. "Whatever consumes frequency — sound, light behavior, scent beyond the wall — it does so by processing it. The way a Kyuro Void Crystal processes frequency. But Kyuro Void Crystal is inert. It doesn't seek." Professor looked at the open houses. "This seeks."

Yael said, very quietly: "It's been in here the whole time we've been walking around."

Nobody moved.

"The starlight reading," Reva said. "The diffusion. You said it was stirred."

"Yes."

"What stirs starlight?"

Yael's jaw was set the way it set when she was making herself look at something she didn't want to look at. She'd been doing this long enough that she did it automatically and mostly without expression, but Kirin knew the jaw. He'd seen it in the Fractured Caves of the Ember Sector and in the underwater dark off the Sapphire Coast and once in a very ordinary market street in Arathis where neither of them had ever been able to explain what they'd sensed there.

"Something large," she said. "Something that's been here long enough that its presence has become the background. We walked into it the same way you walk into a room that's been lived in for years. You stop noticing what lived there. You notice it as air." She looked at the captain. "It didn't hide. We just didn't know what we were looking at."

Reva stood in the center of the square for a long time. Not unmoving — she shifted her weight, turned her head, catalogued. The water Gate made her patient in a way that Kirin, who had the fire Gate pulling at him every time he stood still, had never entirely learned. She would wait three hours for the right wave. He had watched her. He had timed it.

She needed four minutes.

"The village didn't go silent," she said. "It was silenced. And whatever silenced it is still here because it doesn't leave." She turned to the group. "It can't follow us out, or it would have consumed the whole coast by now. It's bounded. Local. Possibly anchored to the wall, possibly to something below the village — Delth, I want Soot to check the cellar of every house on the north side before dark. Professor, I want every frequency anomaly mapped and recorded. Yael, I want a second starlight reading at dusk when you can actually see what you're reading." She looked at Kirin last. "Keep the fire warm. Not as a weapon. As a reference point. If it starts going dark—"

"I'll notice," he said.

"Good." She looked at the open houses around the square. "We stay the night."

"Stay the—" Kirin stopped. Tried again. "The thing that made two hundred and thirty people walk out of their breakfast and vanish at a wall. We're staying."

"We came to find out what happened to them." She was already walking toward the largest of the houses on the square — the village meeting hall, by the size of it. "The answer is here. It doesn't come with us when we leave."

"That we know of," he said.

"That we know of," she agreed, pushing the door open. It swung wide. Inside, the meeting hall was neat as everything else. Chairs in rows. A cold hearth. A board at the front where someone had been tallying fish weights in chalk. She stopped in the doorway and looked at the chalk numbers. "Come help me get this fire started."

Kirin looked at the square behind him. The open houses. The well that had swallowed a stone in silence.

He thought: the thing that doesn't leave is in here with us.

He thought: she knows that.

He thought: she's staying anyway.

He went inside.

---

By midafternoon they had mapped three things.

The first was the wall. Professor had identified the effect extending not just from the physical surface but from a point four meters below the ground — something buried. Possibly a fragment. Possibly more than one. The shadow-suppression radius ran in a rough oval twenty meters across, and at the oval's center the flame went not dark but *white*, a colorless non-light that cast its own kind of nothing.

The second was the well. And then the fish-drying shed. And then the floor of the tanning building and the stone hearth in the meeting hall. Everywhere Kirin held his fire and watched the shadows carefully, there were places where the effect crept. Not constant. More like the presence of something vast and slow breathing in and out, its attention moving through the village the way tide moves through a harbor. When the attention passed over you, the fire went white and the sound dropped and Soot went very still and pressed into Delth's leg.

The third thing Delth and Soot found in the cellar of a house on the north side: a child's wooden toy. A carved boat, the paint worn off the hull from handling. Someone had set it carefully on a crate. The scent trail ended there — one person, small, the last one through before whatever had happened finished happening.

Delth came upstairs and set the toy on the meeting hall table without comment. Nobody looked at it directly after that, but nobody moved it either.

---

The sun went down behind the marsh ridge at the sixth hour and Professor lit three lanterns and placed them at intervals in the square outside while Kirin kept a broader flame in the hearth and Reva sat at the table writing in her field log with the same unhurried precision she brought to everything. Yael had gone to the roof of the meeting hall an hour before sunset and hadn't come down.

Kirin sat by the hearth and watched the fire. He did this when he needed to think — let the fire be itself and read what it told him. Fire talked in movement, in the angle of lean and the quality of color. A nervous fire ran thin and blue-edged. A fire in a sick space curled back from itself, as if trying not to touch the air. His fire in the meeting hall hearth was doing something he hadn't seen before: it was burning correctly in every physical sense, consuming the wood, producing heat, but every few minutes it would simply stop casting light. Not go out. Not change. Just... decline to illuminate. Like a voice choosing not to speak.

The toy boat sat on the table. He didn't look at it.

"Kirin." Delth's voice, across the hall. The forge mage was sitting on the floor with his back against the far wall, Soot along his side, working on something with his hands — Kirin could hear the small precision sounds of tools on metal, a sound that Delth needed the way Kirin needed the fire. Something to do. Something physical to anchor in. "You okay?"

"I'm thinking about the shadows."

"Think quieter, you're keeping Soot awake."

"Soot is definitely asleep."

Soot thumped her tail once against the floor without opening her eyes. Point to Delth.

"The fire went white again," Delth said, without looking up from his work.

"I know."

"For about four seconds."

"I know."

"Each time it's four seconds?"

Kirin counted back. The rhythm of it. "Yes. About four seconds, and then—" He stopped. Started again. "About every twenty minutes."

Delth set down his tools. Looked at the ceiling, which was what he did when he was calculating. "Breathing," he said. "Roughly."

"Something breathes every twenty minutes?"

"Something as big as a tide breathes every twenty minutes." He picked up his tools again. "That's actually less frightening. It's got a rhythm."

"How is that less frightening?"

"Rhythms you can work with."

The fire went white. The lanterns in the square outside went white simultaneously, which was new. Kirin came to his feet. Four seconds. Then back to gold.

Yael came down from the roof.

---

She crossed the meeting hall to the table and sat across from Reva and for a moment she pressed both palms flat against the wood and breathed, which was something she did when she'd seen something she needed to be inside a body to process.

"It's large," she said.

"How large," Reva said. Still writing. She'd stop writing when she needed to.

"The harbor is the edge of it. The marsh is the edge. The ridge is the edge." Yael looked at her hands on the table. "We're inside it. We've been inside it since we came in around the headland."

The pen stopped. Reva looked up.

"The harbor," Kirin said.

"Yes."

"The boats were inside it."

"Yes."

"We anchored inside it."

"Yes."

Reva was quiet. The way she was quiet when the wave she'd been waiting three hours for had arrived. "It didn't take us. It took the village."

"It wasn't hungry for us," Yael said. "Or we're not the right—" She paused. "It's old. Very old. The star-impression of it goes back further than I can read clearly. It didn't come here for the village. I think the village grew up inside it over generations. The village grew up inside a thing that was already here and didn't notice them for a very long time."

"And then it noticed," Reva said.

"And then it noticed." Yael looked at the toy boat on the table. Looked away. "The starlight says it doesn't consume. It... keeps. The impression is of something that collects. Holds. Preserves." Her voice was careful and flat, which was how Yael's voice went when she was handling something fragile. "They might not be dead."

Nobody spoke.

The fire went white. Four seconds. Came back.

"Still have a rhythm," Delth said, from the wall.

"Still have a rhythm," Kirin agreed, and hated how much comfort he took from it.

---

Reva wrote until the second hour past dark and then closed her log and looked at the fire.

"It doesn't leave," she said, to no one in particular. To the room. "It has been here longer than the village. It may have been here longer than the region. It keeps what it takes." She stood and walked to the window and looked at the lanterns in the square and the dark houses around it and the sky above the roofline where no stars were visible because the overcast had come in off the marsh an hour ago. "And it lets us stay because we're not what it came for."

"Or it doesn't know we're here yet," Kirin said.

She turned from the window. "No. It knows." She said this with the confidence of someone who had been reading environments her whole professional life. "We landed inside it. We walked through it for four hours. The fire has been going white every twenty minutes and it's been watching us through the fire." She looked at Kirin. "It's been watching *you*. You're running a temperature gradient it can read. You're a frequency it can taste."

"That's wonderful news," Kirin said.

"It hasn't done anything."

"Also wonderful."

"Which means it's deciding." She walked back to the table and sat. "We have until it decides to find out what it is. What it wants. Where it put two hundred and thirty people." She laid her hands flat on the table the same way Yael had. "And whether any of this can go the other way."

The room was quiet except for the fire and Soot's breathing and the small sounds of Delth's hands on metal. Outside, the lanterns in the square threw their white-gold light across the cobblestones and the open doorways of the empty houses.

The fire went white.

One.

Two.

Three.

Four.

Gold again.

In the square, one of the lanterns stayed white.

Kirin stood up. Reva's hand on his arm, brief and light, the way you stop someone not from danger but from moving too fast into it.

"Tide patience," she said.

He sat back down. The lantern outside pulsed once, white and cold, and then it went back to gold, and Kirin sat with his hands on his knees and his fire burning correctly in the hearth, and he watched the light through the window because the thing that didn't leave was reading him through the fire and he wanted it to know he was still here.

That he intended to stay.

That he intended to have an answer by morning.

The fire agreed with him. It always did.

*End of Chapter One*
