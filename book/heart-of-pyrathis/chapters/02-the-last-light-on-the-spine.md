# THE HEART OF PYRATHIS
## Chapter Two: The Last Light on the Spine

---

The ember-veins were still glowing.

Korvash sat on the edge of the sleeping-platform and stared at his forearms in the dark. Three hours since the call had faded from his bones. Three hours of lying still with his eyes closed, performing sleep for the benefit of no one, while the city shifted and muttered around him.

He'd felt the veins go dormant a thousand times. The brief warm flicker of an almost-shift, the hope climbing, then the subsidence â€” like a fire that takes once and doesn't catch. By morning there was always nothing. Just skin.

These were still lit. Faint, orange-gold, threading from his wrists to his elbows in branching lines like cracks in cooled lava before the magma drains away. Still there. Still warm.

He didn't know what to do with that.

From the other platform, Renn's breathing slowed and deepened and then stopped being breathing and became snoring. Korvash listened to his pack-brother sleep the sleep of something large and uncomplicated and felt something between love and envy he'd long ago stopped being ashamed of. Renn had shifted at age seven. First in their cohort. He'd come out of it grinning, matted in dark fur, enormous even then, and looked at Korvash with an expression that managed to be apologetic and delighted at the same time. They didn't speak of it. They didn't need to.

Korvash pulled on his work gear in the dark.

---

By the time Renn woke, Korvash had already been outside twice. The thermal vents along the lower district were running cool â€” he could tell from the color. The usual shimmering curtain of distortion had gone to simple steam, white and ordinary, rising from channels that should have been too hot to approach without a heat-coat.

He'd watched a child press their bare foot to the stone walkway above a vent channel. Testing. The child's face: puzzled, disappointed, afraid to name what the disappointment meant.

The parents pulled them away without explanation.

Renn emerged onto the platform pulling his vest closed, already scanning. He saw the ember-veins on Korvash's forearms. He looked at them for two full seconds. Then he went inside and started making breakfast.

This was, Korvash understood, an act of love so specific it had no name.

He sat at the low table while Renn moved around the cook-space with the natural authority of someone who had always taken up exactly the right amount of room, cracking ash-eggs and setting the iron plate over the vent-coil. The coil glowed weakly. The eggs cooked slower than usual. Neither of them mentioned it.

"Council's calling a session," Renn said. "Heard it from the Maret-pack on my way to the cistern. The east-slope vents dropped another degree overnight. Drev's getting advisory from the Thermal Guild."

Korvash looked at his forearms.

"I need to report what I heard," he said.

Renn slid the eggs onto a stone plate. Set it in front of Korvash. Sat down across from him with his own.

"I know," Renn said.

"They won't believe me."

"I know."

"Drev especially."

"I know." Renn ate. "Go anyway."

---

The Pack Council hall sat at the highest point of Kaldros's caldera rim, built from black basalt and reinforced over eight generations until it looked less like a building and more like something the volcano had decided to grow. The heat-stones in the walls were dark. Korvash noticed that before he noticed anything else â€” those stones were supposed to stay lit for a decade between charges, and three of them on the east face had gone to cold black. No one had replaced them yet. Maybe no one had noticed. Maybe everyone had noticed and no one wanted to say it first.

Inside, the chamber smelled of old smoke and stress. Four pack-elders sat at the curved basalt table. Drev occupied the center position, which meant Drev was running the session, which meant this was going to go a particular way.

Pack-Elder Drev was a Cinderfang who had shifted every day since the age of eight and had never once let anyone forget it. His ember-veins ran from knuckle to shoulder, thick and bright, and he'd built his entire identity on them in the way some people build identities on the wrong things and call it character. He wasn't cruel. He was worse: confident.

Three other pack matters were already in discussion when Korvash was admitted. Thermal Guild reports. Subsidence in the east wall. The slow-light in the vent channels. Korvash stood at the floor-mark and waited. Around the hall's edges, other pack members waited for their own business. A woman near the wall stress-shifted twice while she stood there â€” a ripple of dark fur across her shoulders and then gone, then back, then gone. She looked mortified. No one looked at her.

When Drev finally acknowledged him, it was with the careful neutrality of someone who has already decided.

"Korvash. You've requested to address the Council."

"Last night," Korvash said, "a dragon called. I heard it. The call came from the Dragon's Spine â€” east face, I think Ignara's peak, based on the resonance direction. The call wasn't general. It was specific." He paused. "It was for me."

The chamber did not erupt. That would have been better. Instead, it went the kind of quiet that has a texture.

"A dragon call," Drev said.

"Yes."

"Dragon calls travel through the ember-veins." Drev's gaze moved â€” briefly, precisely â€” to Korvash's forearms. To the place where, if the veins were strong enough, they'd show even at rest. "The reception of a dragon call requires active resonance pathways. To receive one individually â€”" He stopped. Let the implication settle.

"I know what it requires," Korvash said.

"We're dealing with a thermal crisis," Drev said, not unkindly. "Two more peaks went dark overnight. The eastern vents are cooling at a rate that gives us perhaps six weeks before the lower ward loses heat stability. This council needs actionable information." A pause. "What you're describing isn't that."

"The peaks going dark is connected," Korvash said. "The call said â€”"

"Dragon calls don't speak in words."

"I know. But I know what I felt."

"You felt a disturbance. You interpreted it." Drev's voice remained even. "The dragons don't speak to cold-born, Korvash. They never have. That's not cruelty â€” that's physiology. Their communication runs through fire-pathways. Without active ember-veins, there is no channel." He looked at the rest of the council. "We need the Thermal Guild assessment by midday and we need a structural report on the east wall before the third bell. Does anyone have anything actionable to contribute?"

Korvash stood at the floor-mark another moment.

Then he walked out.

---

The figure was sitting on the council hall steps.

Three feet tall. Grey-green skin the texture of old volcanic glass, wrinkled in the manner of something that has been slowly compressed by time into a denser, more patient form. Ears like satellite dishes swiveling independently, one tracking a merchant's cart in the lower district and one, apparently, tracking Korvash as he came through the door. Enormous amber eyes that caught the dim morning light and held it the way gemstones hold light â€” gathering it, concentrating it, giving it back changed.

Across the small figure's back, strapped in a harness clearly custom-made for a body this size, was a shard-blade. The blade was sheathed, but the hilt was visible over one shoulder â€” taller than the figure's head, the crystal shard catching even the muted morning and throwing it in fractured directions.

The figure looked at Korvash.

"Heard the call, did you?" they said. Their voice was dry as pumice, warm underneath. "Good. Expected you, I did. Longer than anticipated, you took."

Korvash stopped on the steps.

"Who are you?"

"Sitting on cold steps for three days, I have been." The amber eyes moved past him toward the council hall door. "Dismiss you, did they?"

"How did you know I was in there?"

"Read minds, I can." Stated as fact. "Also, three days in Kaldros, I have been. Watch the council hall, anyone waiting to be dismissed does." They rose from the step. They were astonishing in motion â€” not slow, not careful, but precise in the way that very old things are precise. Every movement landing exactly where it intended. "Thresh. My name is Thresh. Ashborn, I am. Two thousand years, give or take. Keep track poorly, I do, of centuries."

Korvash knew what Ashborn meant. Every Cinderfang child did â€” they were the elder order, the readers. Mediators between pack consciousness and the deeper signals of the planet. Most packs hadn't seen one in living memory. Most packs assumed they were gone.

"You're real," Korvash said.

"Philosophical, this is?" Thresh tilted their head. "Or surprised, are you?"

"I â€” the Ashborn haven't been in Kaldros in â€”"

"Forty years." Thresh descended the remaining step and stood at street level. They came approximately to Korvash's sternum. They did not appear to find this relevant. "Forty years ago, reason to come, there was not. Now there is." They looked east. "Heard the call, you did. The ember-veins â€” still lit this morning, they are."

It was not a question.

Korvash looked at his forearms. In the daylight, faint, but still there. "Yes."

"Surprise this, does it?"

"Yes."

"Should not." Thresh began walking along the rim-path, apparently assuming Korvash would follow. He did. "The call was not disturbance. Not dream. Not â€” " they made a gesture that conveyed, precisely, Drev's entire argument â€” "interpretation. Ignara called you. The last of the great fires with enough heat left to reach through stone and distance and into a body that has never fully opened." They paused. Glanced back. "Cold-born, they called you?"

"My whole life."

"Wrong, they were. Dormant-born. Different thing entirely." The amber eyes were steady. "The fire in you, too deep it sits to surface easily. But go out, it has not."

They walked in silence for a moment. Below the rim-path, the city moved in its morning rhythms â€” but the rhythms were off. Too many people standing still. Too many people looking east.

"The planet is dying," Thresh said.

They said it the way you say the time, or the weather. No drama. No preamble.

"The volcanic system of Pyrathis is not geology," Thresh continued. "Taught this, were you, in pack-school?"

"As metaphor," Korvash said. "That the planet is alive. That the fire is â€”"

"Not metaphor." The amber eyes caught his. "Literal. The volcanic system â€” the deep-fire, the thermal flow, the dragon-peaks â€” this is the nervous system of a living world. The dragons are not animals that happened to evolve on a volcanic planet. The dragons are what the planet grew in order to think." A pause. "Consciousness given heat. Awareness made fire. The planet knows itself through the dragons."

The city below them. The dimming vents. The cool stone under the child's bare foot.

"And they're dying," Korvash said.

"Gone, most are. Cold peaks â€” dead neural pathways. Dark thoughts that will never fire again." No sentiment. Just fact. "One remains with fire enough to speak. Ignara. Old. Very old, even by my accounting. The last active awareness of the planetary mind." Thresh stopped walking. Looked east, where two peaks that had been smoking yesterday stood grey and still this morning against the pale sky. "Called you, she did. An ember in the dark, she found. Faint. But there."

"Why me?"

Thresh looked at him for a long moment. The amber eyes did something complicated.

"Answer that, Ignara must," they said. "My role â€” get you there."

"That's an Ashborn."

Renn had arrived without announcement, which meant he'd been following Korvash since before the council hall. He was standing ten feet away, his pack-bag already on his back â€” the good one, the one he brought on long hauls â€” and he was looking at Thresh with an expression of genuine fascination.

Thresh turned. Regarded Renn. The satellite ears rotated toward him.

"Observant, this one is," Thresh said.

"I heard he was going somewhere," Renn said to Korvash, not accusatory, simply stating a fact about the arrangement of events. "Figured I'd come."

Korvash looked at the pack-bag. "You were eavesdropping from the hall door."

"I was concerned for my pack-brother and maintaining a respectful distance." Renn looked at Thresh. "You're really Ashborn? We learned about your order in cohort-school."

"Discontinued, it is not," Thresh said. "Reduced. One." They tilted their head. "Shift perfectly, you do. Smell of it on you â€” recent shift, this morning. Proud of it, your pack is."

Renn shrugged, which was the most honest thing he could have done. He was proud of it. He knew it cost something to be proud of it in front of Korvash. He came anyway.

"I shift perfectly," Renn said. "He hears dragons. I know which one I'd rather have on a bad day." He looked at Korvash. "Where are we going?"

Korvash looked east.

The Dragon's Spine ran along the horizon like a broken crown â€” twenty peaks in the geological record, most of them dormant before Korvash's parents were born, three of them still smoking as recently as a year ago. Now two. The dark peaks had a particular quality in morning light. Not just cold. Absent. The way a voice goes absent from a room after the person leaves, the way you keep almost hearing them and then don't.

Ignara's peak was the tallest. The easternmost. Still, from here, a faint thread of smoke or heat-shimmer rose from its summit. Still there.

"The Spine," Korvash said. "Ignara's peak."

Renn nodded. "How long a walk?"

"Four days, in good conditions," Thresh said. "Good conditions, these are not." They looked at the cooling vents, the slowed lava-channels, the grey stone that should have been warm underfoot. "Fast, we must move. Faster each day, the cold comes."

"I'll get a second pack-bag," Renn said, and went.

Just like that.

Thresh watched him go. "Good one, that is."

"He's always been," Korvash said.

They stood at the rim-path's edge. Below them, Kaldros spread in its bowl of ancient caldera-stone, the city that had been built on heat for eight generations now beginning to feel the future in its bones. The lava-channels ran dimmer. The thermal vents put up their ordinary steam. Three people in the market district had stress-shifted in the past hour â€” Korvash had seen it from the rim, the involuntary ripple of fur and back again â€” and the merchants around them had pretended not to notice.

Children asking why the floor is cold, and their parents with no answers.

"Drev won't send anyone after us," Korvash said. "He doesn't believe me."

"Believe you, he will," Thresh said. "When cold, his own feet get. But too late, that will be, for Drev's belief to matter." They looked up at Korvash. "The pack council â€” dismissed you, they did. Hurt, that did."

It wasn't a question. Korvash didn't treat it like one.

"It always hurts the same way," he said. "You'd think it would stop."

"Stop, it won't. But matter, it will less." The amber eyes were steady. "Other things will matter more."

---

Renn came back with the second bag inside eight minutes. He'd packed for four days, which meant he'd done it before, which meant he'd had it ready, which meant he'd packed it last night after Korvash came home from the ridge with lit ember-veins and said nothing.

Korvash looked at him.

"Don't," Renn said, and shouldered both bags until Korvash took his own.

They stood at the eastern gate of Kaldros. Through it, the road ran down the caldera's outer slope and into the ash-plains that stretched to the foothills of the Spine. The road was good for the first day â€” trade-hardened, marked with heat-cairns that were supposed to glow at night. Most of the cairns had gone dark. You'd have to navigate by the peaks themselves, the few that still showed any light.

And one that still called.

"Pleasant, the journey will not be," Thresh said. They had positioned themselves between Korvash and Renn in the manner of someone accustomed to occupying the middle of things. "But necessary. And possible." They looked up at Korvash. "Possible, because heard the call, you did. And because dismiss you, the Council did, and walked out anyway, you did. And because waiting, no one will do."

"No more inverted syntax for the important parts?" Korvash said.

"Funny, you think you are," Thresh said. "Remember that, you should. Ahead, humor will be harder to find."

Behind them, Kaldros glowed in the morning â€” but dimmer than yesterday. Dimmer than last week. The caldera city that had burned bright for eight generations was still burning, but the fire in it was settling, lowering, going toward something that looked from a distance like sleep and was not sleep at all.

Korvash felt the ember-veins in his forearms. Still lit. First night they'd ever held. He didn't know what it meant yet. He knew what it felt like.

It felt like being called from very far away by something enormous and afraid.

He walked through the gate.

Renn walked beside him.

Thresh walked slightly ahead, already oriented, the shard-blade rising over their small shoulder like an impossible standard.

East. Toward the dark mountains. Toward the one peak still alive.

The gate closed behind them, and the city's last light dimmed another shade.
