# Chapter One: The Third Descent

The door was warm.

Not the Forge-warm that surrounded it — Mount Pyralis ran its heat through every stone in the Academy's deep levels, the ambient temperature of two thousand feet of volcanic rock pressing against the structure from below. That warmth Vor had catalogued before descending to the Forge's sixth sublevel, where the training halls gave way to maintenance corridors and then to the carved passage that led here. That warmth was background. Expected. It had a quality she had begun, in the two days since she'd arrived at the Academy, to almost enjoy.

The door's warmth was different.

She held her palm one inch from the iron surface and felt it: not the passive heat of a heated wall but the active heat of something that was still happening. Still unresolved. The corruption behind the door was not a static thing. It breathed.

She noted this in her shorthand: *Door surface: active thermal signature distinct from ambient. Corruption not quiescent.*

The Hollow Root had been quiescent in its outer layers. The Weeping Halls had been quiescent for the first two levels. This dungeon was not quiescent from the outside.

That was new.

She looked at the seal — Draconia's, carved into the iron in the pre-Fall temple style, the same precision of faith she had seen in the Foundation steps and the Flow Temple's hydraulic channels. The carving held, the way it always held. The carving was the only part that held. But the seal was cracked, a single fissure running from the lower-left corner to the upper-right, and from the fissure a thread of dark smoke emerged at a rate so slow and constant it had the quality of a permanent feature, something that had been weeping for long enough to be architectural.

She drew it. The crack angle. The smoke color. The line of heat-damage in the stone floor where the smoke had been drifting for three thousand years, the volcanic dust discolored to a specific brownish-grey that matched nothing else in the corridor.

Three thousand years of leaking. She made a note: *Internal pressure maintained. Not losing force.* And then below that: *Or gaining it slowly.*

She didn't know which. She would find out.

---

Cadis arrived first, which she'd expected. In the two dungeons before this one he had developed the habit of showing up early and doing nothing with the earliness — not scouting, not preparing, just being there before he was needed, which she had come to understand was his version of readiness. He was twenty-seven now. The Foundation-touched steadiness that had made him valuable at the Hollow Root had deepened into something more considered in the years since. He looked at the door without expression.

"Worse than the Weeping Halls approach?" he asked.

"Different," she said. "The Weeping Halls was gradual. This is already—" She considered the word. "Present."

He nodded as if that confirmed something he'd suspected, and moved to his position to her left. He was running warmer than usual, she noticed — the Forge's deep levels agreed with his Fire secondary in a way that had nothing to do with comfort and everything to do with the particular alertness she associated with someone operating in an environment where they felt their element.

She filed this under: *useful, and also watch him.*

Lessen came down the passage from the upper training hall, her cooling array already active — the small intricate weave of water-working she had developed for this specific expedition, three thin channels of drawn moisture flowing in slow circulation around her wrists and the back of her neck. The Ember Labyrinth had required adjustments to their usual preparation. Lessen had spent a week at the Forge's water-supply systems, calibrating what she could carry in terms of moisture reserve. She had been precise and unhappy about the calibrations.

"Still eight levels?" Lessen asked, looking at the door.

"Eight confirmed by the Dungeon Atlas. Actual count may differ."

"May differ down, or may differ up?"

"The Atlas doesn't specify which way it's wrong. Only that it's from a single expedition, forty years ago, and single-expedition records have a documented error rate of—"

"I know," Lessen said. "I've read the Atlas." She settled into position behind Vor, adjusting the cooling array. The faint sounds it made — the movement of small water, barely audible — were already familiar enough to be comforting, which Vor noted with the specific awareness that comfort and complacency shared a border.

The fourth member of the team was not here yet.

Vor looked down the passage toward the stairs. She had asked Ash for this expedition three weeks ago, sending the letter through the Academy's internal courier system to the Dragonborne quarter, which was what they called the converted stable block that housed the bonded riders and their dragons on the mountain's eastern face. She had kept the letter short. *The third dungeon is the Fire Gate temple. You trained here. The dragon may provide information on the original trial that I can't access by other means. I'd value your presence.*

She had not added: *I've been thinking about you since we spoke at the Weeping Halls publication event, and I think you understand something about fire that I need to understand before I walk into it.*

She'd left that part in the interior margin of her map case, where she kept the notes that were not for the Compendium.

---

Ash arrived with the dragon.

This was, technically, against the Academy's sixth-sublevel regulations, which specified that bonded animals above thirty kilograms required a handling permit for the deep training corridors. Vor was not the right person to say this, and Cadis was too diplomatic, and Lessen had opened her mouth and then closed it when she looked at the dragon properly.

It was not large. For a dragon — for a Void-bonded hatchling now perhaps two years grown — it was, Vor understood from the research she'd done, in the small range of its kind. She had read descriptions. She had looked at illustrations. Neither had prepared her for the specific quality of the animal's presence, which was not size but something else: a contained intensity, a thing-that-is-looking-at-you with complete and unhurried attention. The dragon was the color of dark volcanic glass in the body and coal-smoke at the edges, where the Void aspect of its bond colored the fire-energy. Its eyes were amber-gold, the same amber-gold as Draconia's carvings in the seal above the door.

Vor looked at those eyes and revised several of her assumptions about what the next eight levels would contain.

Ash herself looked — not unchanged, because three years changed everyone, but continuous. The same stillness Vor had catalogued at their first meeting: not absence but direction, pointed inward. She was twenty years old now, taller by an inch or two, her left hand bearing two scars at the palm that hadn't been there in the Chronicle's descriptions — Bond-scars, the healing-marks from the first contact with the hatchling's fire, which ran at a frequency that changed what it touched. Her eyes were the dark brown they'd always been. She looked at the door with the expression of someone encountering a thing they'd been carrying in their mind for years and finding it smaller than expected.

"It's wrong," she said.

"Yes," Vor said.

"I can feel it from here. The temperature is wrong." Ash hadn't moved toward the door. She was standing at the corridor's edge, the dragon at her left, its wing curled against her shoulder in the way the Academy's Dragonborne texts called a posture of attention. "The Forge runs at a specific signature. Fire at a specific frequency — Draconia's frequency, it's always been in the walls here, you get used to it. This is—" She paused, and her right hand moved slightly, the involuntary gesture Vor had noticed twice already, a reaching-toward-information that wasn't quite physical. "This is the same frequency but inverted. Like hearing a note and hearing its resonant opposite at the same time."

"The corruption takes the gate's virtue and turns it into its corresponding vice," Vor said.

"I know what the corruption does. I've been walking past that door for three years." Ash looked at her. "I just wanted to say it out loud before we went in."

The dragon made a sound. Not quite a vocalization — lower than a growl, more like the movement of a thing that had been still and decided to be less still. Ash's hand came up and settled on the edge of the wing without looking.

"He knows," Ash said.

"What does he know?"

"That we're going in. He's been — not reluctant, he's not reluctant. Something else." She frowned. "Anticipatory."

Vor looked at the dragon. The dragon looked at Vor with its amber-gold eyes and she had the specific sensation of being read by something that was not performing the act of reading, that was simply receiving her the way a well-calibrated instrument receives data. She wrote nothing about this in her shorthand. Some things did not reduce to notation.

"The entry mechanism," she said. "The Atlas documents it as: you extinguish a fire that means something to you. Not a candle. Something you built, or that guided you, or that someone else would miss."

A silence.

"You read the Atlas," Lessen said.

"Of course I read the Atlas."

"I mean all of us. The entry requirement applies to all of us."

"Yes."

Cadis looked at his hands. He had a fire already — Vor had seen him light it in the passage, a small contained flame above his palm that he'd been burning since they descended, his Fire secondary providing the fuel almost without effort. It was a practice habit, an absent thing, the kind of flame he kept when his hands needed something to do. He'd kept that particular flame for two years now, on and off, a small constancy. He looked at it for a moment and then closed his fist around it.

The flame went out.

Lessen had no fire to extinguish in the literal sense. She stood for a moment, then reached into her vest pocket and produced a small sealed glass vial — Vor recognized it: the condensed water-memory, the personal navigational aid Lessen had developed after the Mirror Depths, a tool she'd said once was "the most useful thing I've built." She looked at it. Then she held it above the floor until the seal cracked from the heat of the corridor and the memory-water spilled out.

The vial was empty. Lessen put it back in her pocket anyway.

Ash reached into the pack at her back and produced a lantern. Not a working lantern — the glass was cracked, the fuel long since gone, the wick charred beyond use. It was visibly old and had been maintained in that state deliberately: cleaned but not repaired, kept but not replaced. She held it for a moment with both hands. Then she set it on the floor of the corridor and looked at it and said nothing.

The dragon was watching the door.

Vor took out her firestarter. She had carried one for fourteen years, the same design she'd bought at the start of her career from a tool shop near the Athenaeum: steel striker, flint, a thumb-sized reservoir of oil. She'd replaced the flint twice and the reservoir once. The striker was original. She had started fires in five dungeons with this tool, fires that were part of the mapping methodology — the sound of fire told her things about a space that she couldn't learn otherwise, the way flames moved in response to air currents and stone surfaces. She'd never kept a fire in the way Cadis kept his, but the tool was hers in the way that any used thing was yours: through function, through time, through the specific accumulation of moments where it had served.

She struck it. A small flame appeared. She held it, watched it for a moment, and then extinguished it between her fingers.

The door opened.

---

The first thing was the heat.

Not the transition she had anticipated — the Forge's deep heat as a baseline, the dungeon's heat as an escalation — but an immediate, qualitative shift that she felt in the lungs before she felt it on the skin. The air inside the door was dry in a way that the Forge's ambient moisture was not, a scouring dryness that arrived with the heat and changed the texture of breathing. She noted this and kept moving, because the door was already narrowing the perception-window and she needed five steps in before she could read the first chamber properly.

She got five steps in and stopped.

The first chamber was the Cooling Room — she had known it intellectually from the Atlas: *Level 1 does its first work by removing fire, not adding it.* What she felt instead was heat, and the wrongness of the heat, which was the dungeon's signature: give you the wrong version of your element at the threshold. The Weeping Halls had run warm where water should have been cold. The Hollow Root had pressed heavily where stone should have been stable. The Ember Labyrinth's heat was real and extensive but it had a quality she filed under *needs further categorization* and proceeded to gather data on.

Lessen's cooling array responded immediately, its circulation increasing. Vor marked: *cooling array engaged at entry. Level 1 heat sufficient to trigger active response.*

Cadis looked alert in the way he'd looked alert since the passage. The Fire secondary that had merely agreed with the Forge's ambient heat was now receiving something more specific, something Vor watched move through his posture — not comfort, but a kind of activated attention that was a step closer to readiness than she usually saw in him until Level 3 of an expedition.

"Cadis," she said quietly.

"I know," he said.

"What do you know?"

He thought about it, which she appreciated. "It's pulling. The heat is—" Another pause. "Invitational. It wants me to stop managing it and just let it." He looked at her. "I won't."

"Note it when you notice it happening," she said. "The pull is data."

She turned to Ash and the dragon.

Ash had stopped three steps inside the door. She was not looking at the room. She was looking at the walls, and her right hand had made the reaching-motion again — toward information, toward the stone's structural memory, the particular attunement that her arson conviction had made visible to anyone paying attention. She was reading the walls the way she'd read the walls of the shop at thirteen, before the fire, before the sentence the walls had already started.

The dragon was pressed against her left side. Not cowering — it was not the posture of a frightened animal, it was the posture of an animal that had found a point of reference and was using it to gather its assessment of a new space. Its head was up. Its eyes were moving across the room with the same quality of unhurried reading that Vor had catalogued in the corridor.

"The dragon is quiet," Vor said.

"He's listening," Ash said. "He can hear—" She stopped, and the frown appeared again. "He can hear the structure. What it was. He said—" She stopped again.

"He speaks to you."

"Not in words. In—" She shook her head. "Impressions. Shapes. The way you'd explain something to someone who didn't have the vocabulary yet." She looked at Vor. "He says the original trial is still here. Not gone. Underneath."

Vor took out her notation cloth.

---

She mapped the Cooling Room while Cadis ran basic reconnaissance and Lessen's water-work measured the humidity at five points across the chamber. The room was thirty feet by forty, stone that matched the pre-Fall carving style at the door — the same precision, the same implicit respect for the person who would occupy the space. The ceiling arched, not vaulted. The floor was level. The original builders had not been trying to disorient.

The current conditions were a distortion of that. The heat that filled the room had no natural source — no forge in the walls, no volcanic vent in the floor. It came from below, which she already knew, but it distributed itself with a deliberateness that suggested mechanism. Not even temperature. Gradients. The heat was higher near the walls than in the center by four degrees, which was wrong in a way she noted with particular attention: a room built to welcome you to its center would run warmer in its center. This room ran warmer at its edges, which was a room that was pushing you inward.

She noted: *Level 1 thermal gradient: inverted. Original function — draw challenger to center, to warmth. Corruption: center is cooler, edges are warmer. Effect: challengers tend toward walls. Walls are the dungeon's territory.*

She added: *Also functional as a kind of containment. You move toward the walls and the walls have you.*

"Ember-Eaters," Cadis said, from the far end of the chamber. He said it without alarm, which was the right tone — the Atlas described them as small, designed to extinguish light sources rather than kill. "Three of them, in the northeast corner. They're not moving."

"They're waiting," Vor said.

"For what?"

"For one of us to light something." She looked at her map. "Don't."

The dragon had moved away from Ash's side — not far, eight feet — and was circling the room's center in a slow pattern that was methodical enough to be intentional. Ash watched him with the specific quality of attention she gave the dragon when she was receiving information through the bond, her stillness deepening from its usual directed state into something more receptive.

"He's mapping it," Ash said.

Vor looked at her.

"The way you do. Not the physical—" Ash frowned, working out the language. "Not the walls and ceiling. The other thing. What the room was before." She paused. "He says this room was warmer than anywhere else in the temple. It was the first threshold, the first place you felt Draconia's fire. You were supposed to enter cold and leave warm. Welcomed." She looked at the walls. "He hasn't seen that in a long time."

Vor wrote this in the interior margin.

---

The second level was the Flue Network, and the Flue Network was where the physical danger declared itself.

The shafts connected at odd angles, some upright-wide, some requiring a crawl. The heat was eruptive rather than constant — spiking in the northwest quadrant, lower in the southeast, cycling at roughly seven minutes, consistent enough to be predictable and short enough to punish any pause above four. Lessen read the pattern with instruments adapted from water-pressure calibration tools. Cadis navigated the timing. The dragon could not take the smaller shafts; Ash ran a bond-method that distributed her awareness across the two routes, tracking what the dragon encountered on the parallel path and adjusting accordingly.

At the level junction, when the dragon rejoined them: "Level 2 original function," Ash said. "A breathing exercise. Not physical. Learning how to exist in spaces that are too hot, too tight, too difficult. Not resistance. Not surrender." She paused. "Breathing through."

Vor wrote it down.

---

Level 3. The Rage Furnace.

She had read the Atlas entry. She had noted the key mechanism — *emotional amplification, suppressed frustrations and old grudges rise and compound, teams fracture under directed conflict* — and she had briefed the team at the Forge before descent. She had believed she understood it.

She'd been on Level 3 for twenty minutes before she understood she had not understood it.

The heat was extraordinary. Not the eruptive heat of the Flue Network but a sustained, settled, comprehensive heat that sat at the limit of what the body managed without active intervention. Lessen's cooling array was running at its highest calibration. Cadis's secondary Fire affinity was the only thing preventing him from the same thermal stress the rest of them were managing. And the heat was not the mechanism. The heat was the medium.

The mechanism was what the heat carried.

She became aware of it first as a quality of her own thinking — a sharpness that was not her usual sharpness, the particular edge she brought to technical problems. This sharpness had no object. It was looking for an object. And when a person is carrying sharpness that needs somewhere to land—

Cadis had been moving too slowly since the junction. Not dangerously slowly. Not incompetently slowly. Three steps slower than she would have moved, which she would not normally have noticed, which she was noticing now with a particular irritation that had nothing to do with Cadis and everything to do with the heat in the walls.

She put the pen down.

She looked at the irritation. She held it at arm's length in her thinking and looked at it. She had been in two dungeons before this one. She knew what dungeon mechanisms felt like from the inside, which was how they were supposed to feel from the inside: indistinguishable from your actual experience. You had to examine the feeling's origin. You had to ask: *does this begin in me, or does it begin in here?*

The irritation at Cadis's pace began in the room.

She knew his pace. He was moving at exactly his professional rate for Level 3 conditions, accounting for the heat and the terrain and the specific posture he adopted in emotionally amplified environments, which was slightly more deliberate than usual, each step planted before the next committed. She had catalogued his pace across two dungeons. He was not slow.

She filed: *Rage amplification active by Level 3 onset. Personal irritation at team member without legitimate cause. Origin: dungeon. Not personal.*

And then she heard it: twenty feet ahead, Cadis and Lessen, who had been navigating in comfortable parallel silence since the Weeping Halls, were not silent.

The sound was brief. A clipped word from Cadis — not angry, not loud, but distinct, the sound of a comment that was not supposed to be a comment — and then Lessen's response, which was a silence that was a different kind of sound than her usual working silence.

Vor moved up.

Cadis was looking at the ceiling. His expression was the contained professional expression he wore when something had happened that he was in the process of deciding to not let happen. Lessen was three feet away with her cooling array and her notation cloth and the stillness she deployed when she was managing herself.

The dragon was between them.

Not physically between them in an interventionary sense — the shaft was wide enough here, the dragon had positioned itself at the equidistant point between Cadis and Lessen with the quality of precision that Vor had begun to associate with intentional movement. Its head was down slightly. Not in distress. Considering.

"That was the labyrinth," Vor said.

Cadis looked at her. "I know."

"State it."

He was quiet for a moment. She waited. She had learned, across two dungeons, that Cadis's pauses were not reluctance but his processing time, the space he needed to bring something from the level of knowing to the level of speech.

"I said something to Lessen about her pace in the southeast shaft." He looked at Lessen. "It was — the comment was about pace. Not about her judgment." Another pause. "The distinction seemed important in the moment. I'm not sure it was."

Lessen's stillness had shifted slightly. Not toward warmth — toward accuracy. The quality she had when she was calibrating.

"I withdrew from the junction early on the last exchange," Lessen said. "Not because of you. Because the heat was making every noise in the shaft feel like something aimed at me." She looked at the walls. "That's also the labyrinth."

"Yes," Vor said. "Both of those are the labyrinth. Name it and keep moving."

She looked at them both. She looked at the dragon.

The dragon looked back.

"That's not all it does," Ash said, from behind her. She had been quiet since Level 3 entry, which Vor had tracked without comment. "The anger doesn't just turn outward. It finds the things you haven't said yet. The real complaints. The ones you've been not saying because you're professional about it and you like each other and the timing was never right."

"And those complaints," Vor said.

"Are real," Ash said. "That's the problem. Some of them are real, and the labyrinth knows it, and it uses them." She looked at the walls. "The original trial wasn't about suppressing the anger. It was about knowing which anger was yours and which was the dungeon's."

Vor wrote in the margin: *Level 3: Rage Furnace mechanism targets legitimate grievances as well as suppressed frustrations. Effectiveness increased because the material is genuine. Not all of what the labyrinth surfaces is fabrication. Some of it is real, and real anger in a hot room with nowhere to go is more dangerous than invented anger.*

She added: *Method: name it, attribute it, keep moving. Hold space for legitimate grievance without acting from it here.*

---

The descent to Level 4 was steep — not stairwork, a graded slope, the stone worn smooth from three thousand years of passage. Vor went carefully. The acoustic mapping of a sloped surface required additional verification. She worked in sections.

The heat peaked at the slope's midpoint and then, strangely, lessened. Not much — the transition was degrees, not relief — but enough to be noticeable, which was what made it notable. The dungeon's heat structure had been consistent-to-increasing across Levels 1 through 3. A decrease at Level 4 entry was not what the Atlas had described.

She filed: *Thermal anomaly at Level 4 threshold. Decrease in ambient heat contrary to Atlas record. Possible level-specific mechanism.*

At the base of the slope, the passage opened.

The Smoke Walk was not what the Atlas had prepared her for either, but for the opposite reason — the Atlas had been accurate about the visibility and the chemical quality of the smoke, but not about its depth. The smoke did not start at head height and work down. It started at foot level and worked up. The floor was invisible.

She stopped.

Lessen came up beside her. Cadis stood just behind. Ash and the dragon were at the back.

They all stood at the edge of the smoke and looked at it.

"Floor is still there," Cadis said. He reached a foot forward and tested the smoke-covered surface. Stone. Level. "Visibility issue, not a structural issue."

"The elementals move through it," Vor said. "The Atlas calls them Smoke-Wraiths. They're colder than the ambient smoke. The only detection is thermal differential."

Lessen held up one hand, running a small calibration check on her cooling array's sensitivity settings. "I can detect thermal differential. Not precisely, at this ambient temperature, but—" She recalibrated. "I can feel approximately three degrees difference at eight feet."

"That may be enough." Vor looked at Ash. "The dragon?"

Ash had the receiving-stillness again. "He sees heat. All heat." She paused. "He says there are four of them in the near passage. Two on our left, one right, one ahead."

"He can see through the smoke."

"He sees in heat-signature." She paused again. "He also says—" Her expression changed slightly, the frown of translation that appeared when the bond communication was complex. "He says there's something on this level he hasn't encountered before. Not a threat. The word he's — the impression is closer to recognition."

"Recognition of what?"

"He doesn't know yet."

Vor looked at the smoke. Level 4, the Smoke Walk — original function: navigation through blindness, learning to trust what you couldn't see. Corruption: everything that approaches through the smoke is cold, and the only way to distinguish it from the ambient is to feel the difference.

She wrote nothing in the margin. She put the notation cloth away.

"Single file," she said. "Dragon forward. Lessen reads thermal differential. I map by sound. Cadis rear." She looked at each of them. "If you feel the labyrinth in you, say so. We're four levels in."

She stepped into the smoke.

---

Level 5 was what the archived first-person account had warned her about: *not analogous to heat. Analogous to being inside a decision.*

The burn started at the upper arms and worked inward, not peripheral like the Level 3 walls but centered, with the pressure of something that required resolution. Lessen's cooling array ran at maximum. Cadis, the Fire secondary translating where the others were enduring, moved through it with the look of someone reading a difficult text. The dragon kept pace with Ash at the passage's edge and ran a low continuous vocalization — steadier than its usual sounds, something with duration.

"What is that?" Vor asked.

"He's counting us," Ash said. "Making sure we're still all here."

Cadis looked at the dragon. The dragon looked back. Something passed between them — Vor filed it without classifying it, the way she filed all the things she would need more time to understand.

She focused on the map.

---

The passage from Level 5 opened onto a landing that was wider than the passages, a broad flat space cut from the mountain's interior with the same pre-Fall precision as everything original in the Ember Labyrinth. The heat at the landing was higher than Level 5 in ambient terms but the burn-quality was gone, which Vor catalogued with particular attention: *Level 6 threshold. Thermal intensity increases but mechanism-quality decreases. Transition between physical and psychological floors confirmed.*

She turned and took inventory.

Cadis: standing, his expression carrying the particular quality of someone who had just been through something and was in the process of deciding what to do with it. His Fire secondary was giving him information she couldn't fully access. She filed: *Cadis, Level 5 — affected by mechanism in a different register than expected. Monitor.*

Lessen: cooling array still active but not at maximum. Her hands were at her sides and the stillness she wore was the working stillness, the competent-under-load expression. She looked at Vor and did not say *I'm fine* because she never said *I'm fine*, she said with her expression whether she was fine or not, and her expression said: *functional, and thinking about something.*

Ash: the receiving-stillness was fully present. She was standing with the dragon at her left and her right hand making the reaching-toward-information gesture, and the gesture was more pronounced than it had been at any point in the descent. She was reading something. Her eyes were half-closed.

"Ash," Vor said.

Ash opened her eyes. "He can feel the sixth level from here," she said. "The Crucible Mirror." She looked at Vor. "He says it's wrong. Not wrong the way the other levels are wrong — they're inverted. He says this one is—" She stopped. "I don't have the word he's using. Counterfeit. A thing that calls itself what it isn't."

"The Atlas documents it as a replica of the Academy's Crucible. The bonding trial chamber. With a corruption-construct of a dragon that simulates the bond process and then shows every failure mode."

Ash was quiet.

"If you want to stop here—"

"I don't want to stop here." The stillness did not waver. "I want to go down."

The dragon lowered its head and looked at Level 6's entrance.

It made a sound.

Not the steady vocalization from Level 5. Not the recognition-sound from Level 4. Something else entirely — a sound that rose and fell in a way that had the quality of language without being language, a thing that had meaning whether or not Vor could decode it. She looked at Ash.

Ash was looking at the entrance, and her expression had changed. Not the frown of translation. Something that went further in, something that took a moment to arrive at the surface.

"He hasn't seen uncorrupted fire in a long time," she said. "Not this dungeon's fire. The original fire." Her voice was careful, the careful quality of precision around something large. "He can feel it beneath the corruption. What the temple was. What it was supposed to do." She paused. "He says it's still here."

Vor looked at the entrance.

She looked at her map.

She looked at the map case, at the three volumes inside it, at the specific weight of three dungeons' worth of documentation on her shoulder.

"Level 6," she said.

They descended.

---

*End of Chapter One*
