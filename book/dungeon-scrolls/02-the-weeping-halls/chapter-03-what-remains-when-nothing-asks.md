# Chapter Three: What Remains When Nothing Asks

Levels 5 and 6 she will not write about at length.

She will document them. She is documenting them, has been documenting them since they descended from the Sympathy Chamber, in the running shorthand she keeps for expedition-in-progress — the acoustic mapping, the hydraulic annotations, the notation marks for trial mechanisms and corruption signatures and structural observations. The Compendium will receive complete records. The methodology addendum for the Weeping Halls will be thorough.

But the personal record she keeps — the notes on the interior margin of the map case, the ones that had accumulated alongside the technical documentation — those she would write afterward. Not now. What happened on Levels 5 and 6 would require the distance of afterward before she could write about it accurately.

What she noted, in the margin, immediately after ascending from Level 6, in the broad dry landing that connected Level 6's exit passage to the Level 7 approach:

*The dungeon escalates. Levels 1-4: mechanisms using what you've lost. Levels 5-6: mechanisms using what you fear losing. Qualitatively different. The first kind asks about the past. The second kind asks about the future. Working hypothesis: Level 7 asks about neither.*

She had stared at that last line for a moment after writing it.

Then she had written:

*The Sympathy Chamber had Cadis's mother's voice. Level 6 had the sound of my acoustic mapping going wrong — not a voice, not a person, a sound that the dungeon understood was functionally identical to those for me. It knew. It always knows the specific thing.*

Then she put the pen away and looked at the passage to Level 7.

---

Cadis and Lessen were behind her, giving her the ten minutes she had asked for. She had asked for ten minutes on grounds of needing to rebuild the acoustic map of the landing chamber, which was true, but they both knew the second purpose of the ten minutes, which was that she required brief and periodic solitude between levels and they had both come to know this without her having said it explicitly. This was the benefit of working with people who were good at observation.

She did the acoustic mapping. The landing chamber was wide and relatively dry — the hydraulics here were minimal, the level boundary a point where the dungeon's water system underwent one of its periodic regressions, drawing back to the lower regions before committing to them again. She mapped the ceiling height, the wall intervals, the passage geometry. Then she stood in the center of the chamber and let the map settle in her mind, the geometry becoming familiar.

Then she listened past the geometry, into the passage.

Level 7. The Sympathy Terminus.

She had the prior addendum entry on it — her own notes from the survey work she'd done on the Weeping Halls documentation before this expedition, pulled from expedition records in the Athenaeum's Dungeon Register. The register entry was sparse: a single expedition, from forty-one years ago, had reached Level 7 and reported it dry. That was the full account. Dry. The word stood alone in the expedition notes without context or detail, which either meant the expedition had been too rattled to write more, or that the dryness was the detail — the thing so unexpected in a water dungeon that it consumed the observation.

She had thought about this a great deal.

A dry room in the Weeping Halls. The only dry room in the dungeon. After four levels of water, of warmth, of flooded corridors and acoustic corridors and the specific overwhelming humidity of a dungeon whose entire mechanism was built from emotional resonance carried in water: silence. Dryness.

The absence of the dungeon's medium.

She had built a preliminary hypothesis and written it in the expedition-prep notes: *A Flow trial without water is a Flow trial in its original register — emotional truth stripped of mechanism. What remains when the vehicle for emotion is removed? What do you feel when nothing is making you feel?*

She had thought she understood it.

Standing at the passage entrance now, with six levels in her body and the specific depletion that came from six levels of the Weeping Halls specifically — the emotional labor of those levels was different from the physical labor, and the physical labor was considerable — she thought she might have understood the question without understanding the answer.

She went back for Cadis and Lessen.

---

They descended in the order they had established by Level 3: Vor ahead, Cadis to her left, Lessen behind.

Not behind by much. Never behind by much. In the earlier levels, Lessen's positioning had been a thing Vor tracked with peripheral attention — the way you tracked the positioning of any expedition member in a dungeon environment, the basic situational awareness that was part of the methodology. By Level 5, she had stopped tracking it as a calculated thing and started knowing it the way she knew the acoustic map, automatically and continuously. Lessen's presence was a fixed point in her awareness of the space. She did not have to think about it. It was simply there.

The passage to Level 7 was steep — not stairs, but a slope, the stone worked into a series of broad descending angles that would have been manageable in dry conditions and were manageable now, carefully, in the thin film of moisture that the dungeon's hydraulics left even here at the boundary. She went slowly. The acoustic mapping of a sloped passage was imprecise in ways she disliked, the slope angle introducing variables into the sound's bounce pattern that required additional verification steps. She worked the passage in sections, each section confirmed before the next.

The passage smell changed halfway down.

Not wrong — the word wrong had a specific meaning for passage smell in dungeon contexts, the meaning of something actively corrupted, actively harmful. This was not wrong in that sense. This was: different. The warm-water smell of the Weeping Halls, the mineral-damp that had been the ambient characteristic of every level, the specific heaviness that she had come to think of as the smell of accumulated grief — that smell reduced, and then it stopped.

At the bottom of the slope, the passage opened, and the air that came through it was dry.

She stood in the passage entrance and breathed it.

Cold. Not the cold of the upper mountain approaches, not the cold of exterior environments — the cold of a room that has been sealed from warmth for a long time, that has settled into an interior temperature with nothing to disturb it. The still-cold of a space that has not been breathed in.

Forty-one years, possibly. Since the last expedition.

She breathed it.

Behind her, she heard Cadis inhale. Heard the quality of his inhale change when the dry air reached him.

She stepped into the Sympathy Terminus.

---

The chamber was smaller than the Sympathy Chamber above.

That was the first thing: the scale was human, not cathedral. Twenty feet in each direction, the ceiling at twelve, the walls plain stone without the intricate bowl-curve carvings of Level 4. No crystalline formations. No carved channels for water. No acoustic engineering that she could detect in the first mapping pass — the chamber was, in acoustic terms, a room. A regular room. A room built without any special consideration for how sound behaved in it, the room you would get if you asked someone to build a room without telling them why.

It felt like stepping outside after a long time underground.

Which was wrong — she was underground, deeper than she had been in any of the preceding levels. But the quality of the air, the dryness, the plainness of the walls, the complete absence of the dungeon's layered mechanisms: it had the quality of an exterior space. Of a place that the dungeon had not reached.

She mapped it. The geometry was simple and she had it in thirty seconds.

Then she stood in the center of the chamber and listened.

Not for voices. The chamber was silent. Not the silence of a space where sounds had been removed, but the silence of a space where sounds had never been added — original silence, unworked, the silence of stone that had not been asked to carry anything.

She listened until she was certain she had heard everything the chamber had to offer.

The chamber had nothing.

No acoustic resonance beyond the basic reflective properties of the walls. No temperature variation that suggested flow channels or hydraulic mechanisms. No formation at the center, no pool, no crystalline structure. A flat stone floor, plain walls, an arched ceiling, and the dry still air that had been in here, she calculated, for the better part of half a century.

She opened her mouth to speak and then did not speak, because the quality of the silence was such that it seemed wrong to break it without necessity.

She looked at Cadis.

He had stopped at the chamber entrance. He was standing very still with his hands at his sides and his face had the expression she had learned to read as: something has stopped working. Not distress — he was not distressed. The functional, focused expression he wore in difficult conditions had simply —

Paused.

She crossed to him.

"Cadis."

He looked at her. His eyes were present. "I can't remember," he said. "Her voice." He said it as information, flat, the way he said all information. "The Sympathy Chamber had her voice. Level 5 had —" He paused. "I can hear the echo of what the chamber had. I know the chamber was using her voice. But I can't—" He looked at the ceiling, which was twelve feet of plain stone. "The specific quality of the first syllable. The way she said my name when I was a child. I could hear it perfectly, in the Sympathy Chamber. I can't hear it now. It's—"

He stopped.

"Gone," he said.

She understood.

Not lost — gone was a different word from lost. Lost implied something had been misplaced. Gone meant the Sympathy Terminus had taken it. The absence of mechanism had produced its own mechanism: in the silence, in the dryness, in the chamber that had nothing in it, what was passive had been taken first.

Cadis's memory of his mother's voice had been reinforced and amplified and weaponized by the Sympathy Chamber. The weaponization had been removed by their intervention. What remained had been, she now understood, still dependent on the chamber's resonance — sustained, at some level, by the dungeon's accumulated frequency even as they disrupted it. And here, in the Terminus, cut off from that frequency entirely, it had gone with the water.

She looked at Lessen.

Lessen was standing in the center of the chamber, where Vor had stood a moment ago, looking at her hands.

---

Vor watched her.

Lessen's hands were open in front of her — not a gesture, a state. Palms up, fingers slightly spread, the way a person held their hands when they were checking for rain and there was no rain. She was looking at them with the focused attention she brought to navigation instruments, to waterproof notation cloth, to dungeon walls that had hydraulic information in their seepage patterns.

She was looking at her hands the way she looked at something she expected to find information in.

She was not finding it.

Vor saw this happen in real time: the three-year-old grief for Renae that Lessen had been carrying with the precision and deliberateness of a person who had decided that carrying it was necessary and important — that grief, which had been present in Lessen in every preceding level as a kind of internal light, the thing she had offered freely in the Sympathy Chamber and that had made her offering so potent — was gone.

Not that she had stopped grieving. Vor did not think that was what was happening. Lessen's face was not the face of someone who had been healed. It was the face of someone who had been emptied. The feeling was still there — she was still the person who had lost Renae, still the person for whom that loss had been an organizing experience of three years. But the feeling was passive now, and the Terminus was taking what was passive.

What remained was the woman standing in the center of a dry room with her palms open, looking at her hands, in the silence.

Lessen looked up. Her eyes found Vor's immediately — not seeking reassurance, seeking information. Assessing.

"It's taking the grief," she said.

"Yes."

"The passive version." Lessen's hands closed slowly. "The version I'm carrying without choosing." She looked at the ceiling, then the walls, the same methodical room-reading she did in every new space. "The grief that's just — ambient. Background. Constant." She looked at Vor again. "The part I don't decide to feel."

Vor nodded.

Lessen was quiet for a moment. Her hands had closed all the way now, not fists — the ordinary closed shape of hands at rest.

"I can't feel her in the room," she said. "The way I've been feeling her in every room for three years. The sense of her. The weight of it." She paused. "It's not gone the way — it's not healing. She's still dead. I'm still the person she's gone from. But the constant weight of it, the ambient — the room is taking it. Like the room took Cadis's memory of her voice."

"The corruption is weaponized passivity," Vor said. "The prior levels weaponized feelings that were already present and passive — they amplified what was there and aimed it. The Terminus is the inversion: it takes what's passive and leaves only what you actively hold."

Lessen looked at her hands again. Open this time.

"I know," she said. "I know that's what it is." Her voice was careful, the careful quality of someone working through something in real time, the way she calculated hydraulic pressure with a stylus on notation cloth. "The question is what I actively hold."

---

Vor documented the room.

She worked methodically, the way she worked in all the dry and the stillness. The Terminus was, structurally, one of the simplest spaces she had mapped in two years of Weeping Halls preparation and seven levels of its interior — bare stone, basic geometry, nothing to document beyond dimensions and material and the absence of mechanisms. She documented the absence. She had notation symbols for this: the symbol for no resonant formation, the symbol for no hydraulic connection, the symbol for a space that the dungeon's mechanisms had not entered.

She mapped the Sympathy Terminus as what it was: a room that the dungeon had not been able to reach.

The corruption of the Weeping Halls, she noted in the margin, had been built outward from the Sympathy Chamber — the crystal at Level 4 as the anchor, the amplification moving up through the dungeon, each level's mechanisms connected to the accumulated resonance below. The Terminus was below. The corruption had not come down here. It had grown up.

The original trial room was here. She had not needed to find it in the usual sense, had not needed to listen past the corruption for the surviving room the way she had in the Hollow Root and in the Sympathy Chamber. The original trial room was the entire Terminus, unchanged, because the corruption had grown in the other direction.

She wrote this carefully. The implication was significant: the original trial of Level 7 had survived not through resistance to the corruption but through its position. The dungeon's corruption was a system of amplification, and amplification required a medium, and the Terminus had sealed itself from the medium. Not through design — or not the designer's design. The dryness had been the trial mechanism, and the dryness had also been the seal that kept the corruption out.

The trial had saved itself.

She sat back on her heels and looked at the notation, and the implication opened outward.

The trial of Level 7, the original trial — sit in silence and feel what remains when all stimulation is removed — had been designed to work through absence. The Weeping Halls was, everywhere else, overwhelming. Water and resonance and memory and voices and the specific flooding of the senses that a dungeon built on emotional truth achieved by making every challenge feel like the thing you were most vulnerable to. And then: the Terminus. Dry. Silent. Nothing asking anything.

What the original trial wanted to know was not what you felt when the dungeon pushed you toward feeling. It wanted to know what was there in you, organically, voluntarily, without amplification or prompting. What you brought with you that wasn't being carried for you by the dungeon's systems.

What was yours.

---

Cadis was sitting on the floor near the entrance. Not on his heels — fully seated, cross-legged, the position of someone settling in for a long stay. He had his knees and his hands in his lap and his face was the expression of a person conducting an inventory.

She crossed to him.

"What do you have?" she asked.

He looked up. "What do you mean."

"In the room. What do you feel right now."

He was quiet for a moment. The methodical kind of quiet, the kind that meant he was answering properly and not quickly.

"Tired," he said. "Foundation-tired, specifically — depleted, not injured, the kind that food and a long sleep will address." He paused. "Concerned about Lessen." He looked toward the center of the room, where Lessen was still standing. "That concern is present and it's mine. I chose it. It's not the dungeon's." Another pause. "The work. What we're doing here." He looked at the ceiling. "Not abstract satisfaction. The specific regard I have for completing things that are worth completing. That's still here."

She nodded.

"My mother," he said, "is — I know she's important to me. I know I love her. I can't feel it the way I normally feel it, in the specific warm way, but I know the structure of it. The dungeon took the feeling. Not the fact." He looked at his hands. "The fact is still here. So is the grief about the voice being gone. That grief is active, I'm deciding to feel it. The Terminus isn't taking it."

She filed this: *active grief survives. Chosen grief survives. Passive grief — the kind carried in ambient, the background weight — does not.*

"Stay here," she said. "I need to go back to Lessen."

---

She found Lessen in the same position: center of the room, hands now loosely closed.

She looked at her face.

Lessen was not in distress. She was in the specific state Vor had come to recognize as her working state, the state she occupied when she was doing something hard with full attention — navigating a difficult tidal passage, translating a hydraulic problem into notation, making a decision in a dungeon corridor with incomplete information. The state of competence under load.

"Tell me what you have," Vor said.

Lessen looked at her. Not startled — she had heard Vor's approach, the acoustic habits of their expedition partnership were months old now. "The work," she said. "The survey. The hydraulic mapping." She glanced at the notation cloth rolled in her vest. "The interest in this room specifically. That's all still here."

"What else."

Lessen looked at her hands again. Then she looked up.

"Vor," she said. She said it in a specific way — not the working address, the expedition address. Something beneath that. "There's nothing else."

Vor waited.

"Without the ambient grief—" Lessen was precise, the way she was always precise, the way precision was her courtesy, her gift, the thing she offered instead of the warmth she had not yet rebuilt the infrastructure for. "I thought it was everything. I've been carrying it for three years and I thought it was — the weight of her. The record of her. I thought the grief was her, in me. And without it, I thought there would be nothing."

"There isn't nothing," Vor said.

"No," Lessen agreed. "But it's—" She stopped. The precise voice went quiet and then came back quieter, without the precision, which was as close as Lessen came to rawness. "It's very small. What's here. Without the grief. There's the work and the interest and the fact that she matters to me. But the fact is thin. The dungeon took the weight of it and left the structure and the structure is — I know she existed. I know she mattered. But the warmth of it, the way she was present in every room for three years—"

She stopped.

Then she said: "I have to choose."

Vor waited.

"Right now," Lessen said. "In this room. I have to actively choose to hold her. If I don't choose, if I let it be passive, the room will take it. And if the room takes it I'll have the structure without the — I'll know she mattered. I won't feel it." She looked at Vor with the direct, considering look she used when she was testing the reliability of information. "That's the trial, isn't it."

"Yes," Vor said.

"Not sit in silence and see what you feel. Sit in silence and decide what you're going to hold."

"Yes."

Lessen nodded slowly. Not agreement with a new proposition — recognition of something she had already understood at a level below articulation, the recognition rising to meet the articulation now. "The dungeon couldn't build a trial about active choice," she said. "The corruption had to wait for passive things, things that were being carried without being held. It couldn't take what was actively gripped."

"No."

"Because active choice isn't an emotional state. It's a decision." Lessen looked at the room — the ceiling, the walls, the plain stone that had sealed out everything the dungeon had built in the levels above. "The room was designed to strip everything else so the decision was visible. Not to take everything. To take everything except what you chose."

She stood for a long moment in the center of the room.

And then Lessen made her grief active.

Vor watched her do it. There was nothing visible in it — no gesture, no sound, no physical signal that something had changed. But the quality of Lessen's stillness shifted. The hands that had been loosely closed closed with intention. The face that had been precise and empty became, very subtly, present. Not the presence of emotion happening to someone. The presence of someone deciding to be in an emotion.

Lessen chose to grieve.

In a room that was trying to take everything that wasn't chosen, she chose grief, and the grief was hers, and the room had nothing to take.

---

Vor crossed to the eastern wall and set her map case down.

She worked for an hour.

It was the longest she had spent on a single chamber in the Weeping Halls expedition. Not because the chamber was technically complex — she had established in the first thirty seconds of acoustic mapping that it wasn't. She worked for an hour because the chamber deserved it. Because the chamber was the original trial room and it was intact, entirely intact, and she was the first expedition member to reach it and document it in full in forty-one years, and because the thing this chamber had been designed to ask was a question worth recording carefully.

She drew the chamber precisely: the dimensions, the ceiling arch, the plain walls, the floor that had no pool set into it, no formation, no carved channel. She drew the chamber as what it was: empty. A room that had chosen emptiness as its mechanism.

She drew, in the notation she had developed across the Hollow Root documentation and refined across six levels of the Weeping Halls, the absence. The notation for dry. The notation for sealed-from-corruption. The notation for surviving-room — the mark she put on chambers where the original trial mechanism had not been reached.

Then she wrote in the margin, in the small writing she used for the notes that would not go into the formal Compendium report:

*The Weeping Halls' trial structure, reconstructed:*

*Level 1-3: The dungeon establishes what you carry. Every mechanism in the upper levels is a diagnostic. The Mirror Pools show you who you are. The acoustic corridors amplify what moves you. The Current Maze makes you move with the dungeon's pressure rather than against it. These are not trials — they are measurement.*

*Level 4: The Sympathy Chamber uses the measurement. The corruption weaponizes what the dungeon learned about you. The original trial asks: what do you feel, honestly? The corruption asks the same question with the weight of everyone who has ever answered it in despair.*

*Levels 5-6 ask about what you fear losing. I need more distance to write about these accurately.*

*Level 7 — the Terminus — asks the question the whole dungeon has been building toward: when there is nothing left to make you feel, what do you choose to feel?*

She stopped writing and looked at what she had written.

*The trial is not about emotional honesty. That is the preparation. The trial is about emotional will. The Weeping Halls wants to know if you are a person who carries feeling passively — who is carried by it — or a person who chooses.*

She added, after a moment:

*Lessen answered this correctly.*

She closed the pen.

She sat on the floor of the Sympathy Terminus and looked at the room, which was the simplest room in the dungeon — twelve feet by twenty feet by twelve feet of plain stone, dry air, absolute silence — and she asked herself what she would have answered.

She asked herself what she held, actively, in the room's terms.

The work. Yes. She had always known this — the work was not ambient, it was not background, it was not something she carried without choosing. It was the thing she had been continuously re-deciding to do for fourteen years, re-choosing through each difficulty, through the second expedition to the Weeping Halls when she and Roak had spent four hours finding the exit route, through the letters from expedition guilds who wanted a method she couldn't give them yet, through six levels of a dungeon that had spent seven levels learning everything it needed to hurt her and had used most of it. The work was chosen. The room had not touched it.

Cadis. Yes. Their fourteen years was not passive — it had been maintained by both of them, the partnership a thing they renewed by showing up, by trusting the prior decisions when the current information was uncertain. Not passive. It was there.

And fear. She had written about her fear in the Sympathy Chamber — the fear of Level 5, Level 6, Level 7. She was in Level 7 now, and the fear was still here, the specific fear of not being capable of what the remaining floors required. The Terminus hadn't taken it. Because she was looking at it directly, holding it with awareness, which was not the same as being afraid without knowing you were afraid.

She sat with this.

Then she sat with the harder question: what had the room taken from her?

She didn't know. That was the nature of the taking — what left passively left without announcement. She might not know until afterward, the way you didn't know what a room smelled like until you'd been outside it for long enough to return and notice.

She wrote: *I will not know what the Terminus took until I have left and returned to the conditions where it was present. This is part of the trial's design: the taking is invisible from inside the absence.*

---

Three temples.

She thought about this as she packed the map case.

The Hollow Root: Foundation Gate temple, corrupted through isolation from question, restored by being shown what it had been. The intervention: a map. A reconstruction. The dungeon meeting its own original form and recognizing it.

The Weeping Halls, Level 4: Flow Gate temple, corrupted through weaponized accumulation. The intervention: direct offering. Lessen giving what the dungeon sought to extract, freely and deliberately, disrupting the mechanism that only knew how to take.

The Weeping Halls, Level 7: the original trial of the same temple, preserved intact through its own design. The intervention: none. The chamber had waited, sealed, for forty-one years, and it was still here, still asking the question, waiting for someone who could answer it.

Seven more.

She shouldered the map case, which was heavier than it had been when she descended into the Weeping Halls. Not significantly — the map sheets were lightweight, the good paper she used weighed little, the notation was compact. But it was heavier than it had been in the Hollow Root. Two dungeons now. Two sets of trial documentation. The archive growing.

She was about to stand when Lessen said something.

Not to Vor — or not only to Vor. Lessen had a manner of saying things that were directed at the room, at the information, at the problem, in the way that scientific observation was not addressed to any person but was nonetheless being communicated. The statement aimed at its own truth, and whoever was listening was welcome to it.

"You're not restoring dungeons," Lessen said.

Vor looked up.

Lessen was looking at the chamber walls, the plain stone, with the reading-attention she brought to hydraulic data. "The Hollow Root is a Foundation Gate temple. The Weeping Halls is a Flow Gate temple." She turned her head. "Every dungeon in the Arcanean system is a Gate temple. They're all corrupted. They're all the same corruption — something closed the question at the center." She paused. "The Hollow Root had its trial room sealed. The Weeping Halls had its Sympathy Chamber weaponized. Different mechanisms, same source — Malachar's corruption took the Gate temples and inverted them. Made the places that asked questions into places that broke people."

Vor waited.

"You're restoring them," Lessen said. "But what you're restoring isn't the dungeons." She looked at the ceiling, the twelve feet of plain stone, the arch. "A Gate temple in full function is a Gate. The Hollow Root, if it fully restores, will be the Foundation Gate working again. The Weeping Halls, if it fully restores, will be the Flow Gate working again. You're not mapping dungeons." She looked at Vor. "You're repairing the locks."

Vor heard this.

She had known it. She had not not known it — the connection between the Gate temples and the Gate system had been in the early Compendium research, had been in the archive materials she had pulled for the Hollow Root reconstruction, had been in the back of her understanding of what the dungeons were structurally. She had known it the way you knew something you had not yet had occasion to say aloud.

Lessen had said it aloud.

Vor looked at the map case. At the notations inside, the leather-treated inside cover, the notes accumulating alongside the expedition records.

*They can be found.*

*And found, they can be given back to themselves.*

*The method is different each time. The principle is the same: meet the trial where it is. Not the corruption. The trial.*

She opened the cover and added below those lines, in the smallest writing she had:

*The Gates are not closed. They are locked. The keys are the trials.*

Then she closed the case.

---

Seven more dungeons.

She said this to herself as a number, the way she had been saying it since Level 4 — as arithmetic, as an accounting of where she was in the work. Not despair. Not distance. Just: the number. The precise and honest accounting.

Seven more dungeons. Seven more Gate temples. Seven more corrupted trial systems, each one a different Gate and a different mechanism and a different kind of question the corruption had inverted. The Sight Gate, the Heart Gate, the Voice Gate. The Crown Gate. The Starweave Gate and the Unity Gate and — at the end of it, if they reached the end of it — the Source.

Each one would be different. Each one would require the thing this work had always required: going in, mapping, and finding the original trial mechanism under whatever had grown over it. Meeting the trial where it was. Not the corruption. The trial.

She stood.

Cadis was on his feet, his Foundation-depleted expression replaced with the alert professional expression he wore when he was ready to work and had assessed that working was what the moment required. He had his supplies sorted and his documentation in order. He looked at her with the steady regard of fourteen years of partnership and said nothing, because nothing was what was called for.

Lessen had her notation cloth in hand.

"Level 8," Vor said.

They descended.

---

Three temples, partly restored. Seven remaining. The Compendium would receive the full technical record before the end of the month. The addendum for the Weeping Halls was going to require two more expeditions and a year of reconstruction work before the restoration reached the depth it had reached in the Hollow Root. The letters would start again when the addendum was published. She would answer them the same way she had answered the last ones: *I have documented what occurred. I cannot yet tell you what produced it or whether it is repeatable. I am going to find out.*

The map case was heavy on her shoulder.

Seven more locks.

She carried the keys.

---

*End of Chapter Three*
