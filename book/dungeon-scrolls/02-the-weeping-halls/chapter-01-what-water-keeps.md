# Chapter One: What Water Keeps

The letters started before she had finished the Hollow Root addendum.

Not from the Compendium — the Compendium knew better, or at least it knew her well enough to wait. These came from places she had not expected: from the small expedition guilds in Cinderwall's lower wards, from the Academy Exploration divisions at Pyralis and the Luminary, from three separate researchers who had never met each other and whose letters arrived within a week of each other and all said, in different registers and with different kinds of formality, the same thing.

*Is it true that you restored one of the trial rooms?*

She read the first letter twice. Then she put it in a drawer and did not answer it for two weeks.

Not because she didn't have an answer. She had several answers, precise ones, drawn from fourteen pages of the addendum she was currently on her third pass of revising. She had the answer. What she didn't have was certainty about what the answer meant in anyone else's hands.

The cartographic record was clear: she had mapped the Hollow Root's Core chamber accurately, she had drawn a reconstruction of the original Foundation Gate temple from Compendium archive materials, she had laid that reconstruction in the Core chamber beside the center stone, and the dungeon had responded. The corruption had contracted. The original trial mechanisms on the upper floors had resumed partial function. The Kaelith Stone's resonance had strengthened.

She had done this. The letters were not wrong.

The problem was that the letters treated this like a method. Like a set of steps that could be transferred. And it was not a method — or not yet, not provably, because it had worked once in one dungeon and she did not yet know if it would work again or what it required or whether there was a version of it that didn't require fourteen years of specific dungeon cartography work and Cadis's particular Foundation-sense and the three months of reconstruction research that had preceded the intervention.

She wrote this, eventually, to each of the letter-writers. She wrote it carefully, not to discourage but to be precise: *I have documented what occurred. I cannot yet tell you what produced it or whether it is repeatable. I am going to find out.*

The finding out, of course, required another dungeon.

---

She had mapped the Weeping Halls twice before. Never deep — no one she knew had been deep into the Weeping Halls and returned clean. The first time she had gone nine years ago, early in her systematic dungeon work, when she had still been building the methodological foundations she would spend the next decade refining. She had documented the first two levels, come out with the beginnings of the Mirror Pools entry in the Compendium, and a persistent discomfort with reflective surfaces that had lasted six months and left no permanent mark except the note she had added to her procedural files: *Flow dungeons require different ink strategy. Standard drops absorb into wet stone. Revise notation tools before return.*

The second time was four years ago. She had gone deeper — Level 3, just into the Current Maze, enough to establish the basic hydraulic architecture and map the passage configurations before a Current-Shaper redirected the exit passage and she and her then-partner Roak had spent four hours finding the original route back. They had made it out. The addendum from that expedition was still incomplete, listed in the Compendium's active-research index with a notation she had added herself: *Further survey required. Not sole-entry viable.*

She had known, for four years, that going back to the Weeping Halls required two things she had not yet assembled: a Flow Gate specialist and a new notation method for wet stone.

She had the notation method now, developed across the eighteen months since the Hollow Root, tested in three non-dungeon cave systems and one flooded quarry west of Cinderwall. She had discovered, somewhat by accident, that she could map acoustically. The resonance of chambers told her their dimensions more reliably than her eyes in limited visibility, and unlike ink on stone, sound did not dissolve in water. The method was slower than her standard approach and required building a kind of attention she had not used before — the attention of a musician learning notation rather than a painter learning proportion — but it worked. She had filled a notebook with the shorthand she had developed for acoustic mapping: the symbols for chamber height based on echo decay, for passage width from lateral bounce, for corridor length from time-of-flight. The ink she used on her skin still worked above the waterline. Below the waterline, she memorized.

For six months she had memorized. She had memorized the notation the way she'd memorized dungeon layouts in her first years: by practicing in the dark, by reconstructing from memory with her eyes closed, by demanding precision from herself until precision became habit.

The Flow Gate specialist she found in a way she had not expected.

---

Her name was Lessen Vaur, and she came not through the Compendium's referral network but through a message from the Abyssal Athenaeum's senior archivist — a woman Vor had worked with once on sourcing the pre-Fall Flow Temple documentation, who wrote three sentences and said nothing more than: *There is someone you should meet if you are planning what I think you are planning. She has tried the Weeping Halls before. She knows more about Flow dungeon hydraulics than anyone currently alive. She will not come to you; you will need to go to her.*

Lessen Vaur was thirty-four and lived in a coastal town called Greybreak, four hours by road from the Weeping Halls' canyon approaches. She ran a small maritime survey operation out of Greybreak's eastern harbor, mapping tidal passages for fishing vessels and occasionally for the Athenaeum when they needed coastal geological data. She was not difficult to find. She was not difficult to reach. She was simply quiet in a way that made the space around her feel attentive.

Vor met her in the harbor offices, in a room that smelled like damp rope and old paper, and laid her plan on the table the way she laid every plan: directly, without prelude, with the specific questions she needed answers to included alongside the information she was providing.

Lessen listened. She looked at the Compendium documentation Vor had brought — the partial addendum from the second expedition, the annotated atlas entries, the notes on the entry ritual and the first-level hydraulics. She touched the corner of the acoustic mapping notebook with two fingers, not opening it, just making contact with the thing that was new.

"You use sound," she said.

"Ink doesn't hold below the waterline. I've been developing an acoustic method for six months."

Lessen looked at the notebook. "I mapped the first four levels of the Weeping Halls using physical traceline — chalk on a reel, extended as I moved. It worked until Level 4 and then the traceline pulled. The chamber on Level 4 has something that interferes with physical records." She paused. "Sound might hold better."

It was not a question. It was an observation. Vor noted this: the manner of a person who was used to working in conditions where questions were a luxury you took when you had time and otherwise you just noted what was true and moved from there.

"You've been to Level 4," Vor said.

"Yes."

"The Athenaeum's records list the last successful expedition as reaching Level 5 before retreat."

"That was four years before my run." Lessen's voice was precise. "I went in alone. I reached Level 4, documented what I could, and came out. The Sympathy Chamber on Level 4 —" She stopped. She looked at the harbor window, the grey water beyond it, the seabirds working the tide line. "I lost three hours in there. I didn't lose myself. But I lost three hours."

Vor waited.

"My partner," Lessen said. "Renae. She died in the Mirror Depths dungeon three years before my run. The chamber had her voice." She said this without expression, not because she had no feeling about it but because she had decided, in the manner of someone who has made this decision many times in many contexts, to present the information without the feeling attached to it. "It had her exact timing. The way she paused before she said my name. The chamber didn't know that — it just had everything it needed from me when I entered." She turned from the window. "I know what's in there. I know what it will do. That's why you should take me."

Vor looked at her.

"You're worried I'll freeze," Lessen said. "Like I did at the chamber."

"The Sympathy Chamber is Level 4. There are six more floors past it."

"I know the structure. I know the mechanism. I've been thinking about Level 4 for three years." Her voice was the same. Precise, flat, decided. "When it happened the first time, I was surprised. I won't be surprised again."

This was not the reassurance that it might have been from a different kind of person. From Lessen it had the quality of a technical assessment. She had analyzed the failure. She had spent three years analyzing it. She was telling Vor what the analysis had produced: not confidence, exactly, but preparation.

Vor took the acoustic mapping notebook and opened it to the first page of her notational shorthand. "Learn this," she said. "I want you to be able to read a map I made acoustically before we enter. Two weeks."

Lessen looked at the shorthand. She looked at it for a full minute, the way a navigator looked at an unfamiliar instrument, learning its logic before its application.

"One week," she said.

---

Cadis came because she asked him to, and because she knew he would say yes, and because she wanted, at least once more, the specific clarity of his Foundation-reading in her peripheral awareness as she worked. He had trained two of his own students in the method she'd taught him, which was not the same as the method she'd taught herself, and the students were good — competent, careful, promising — but they were not Cadis, who had spent six expeditions learning not just her method but the spaces between the method, the hesitations and recalibrations and silent re-reads that were the most important part of how she actually worked.

She told him about Lessen. She told him about the three hours in the Sympathy Chamber, the lost partner, the three years of preparation.

He was quiet for a moment. He was quiet in the way he always was when he was processing something he felt the emotional weight of without yet knowing what the structural implication was.

"She grieves toward things," he said, eventually. "Not away from them."

Vor looked at him.

"Some people," he said, "when they carry something, carry it behind them. They face away. They move so the thing they carry is always at their back. It's still there, still weight — they just orient so they don't see it." He paused. "The way she described coming back. Knowing what's there. The three years of thinking about Level 4. She's not turning away from it. She's facing into it."

Vor thought about this.

"That may be what the Weeping Halls requires," Cadis said. Not a prediction — an observation. The way he made all his observations: precise, patient, without forcing more than he could demonstrate.

---

The approach to the Weeping Halls took them south along the coast road to the point where the cliffs broke into canyon country — the limestone eaten away by millennia of sea spray into a network of narrow passages, some wide enough for a cart, some barely shoulder-width, all of them leading eventually toward the sound of falling water. They reached the canyon mouth at mid-morning, when the light came in at an angle that caught the bioluminescent algae on the walls and made it pulse in soft greens, like something breathing.

Vor had seen it before. She noted, as she always noted, the way the beauty did its work regardless of knowing it was doing it. She noted the warmth in the air, slightly elevated over what the canyon walls and direct sun could account for. She noted the birds — seabirds nested in the upper limestone, exactly the kinds of birds that nested in undisturbed cliffs, and the fact of undisturbed birds meant the canyon felt safe to things that had no reason to flee. The Weeping Halls made itself feel like a place that nothing would flee from. This was documented.

They stopped at the Compendium's marked survey point — a small cairn she'd built on the second expedition, three boulders balanced against a canyon wall above the high-water line — and Vor made the first acoustic record of the approach. She stood still for two minutes with her eyes closed, building the canyon geometry from sound: the specific double-echo that told her the walls were roughly forty feet apart, the height differential from the seabird calls above, the distance to the waterfall from the way its sound shifted with the canyon's bend.

She opened her eyes and wrote the notation on the back of her left hand in three lines.

"Entry," she said.

The entry ritual for the Weeping Halls required speaking a memory you had never shared with anyone. Not a confession — not necessarily something shameful, though it could be — simply something kept. Something that had been yours alone.

Cadis went first. His eyes were level, his voice quiet, and what he said was too quiet for Vor to hear more than the shape of it — not the words, just the fact of words being spoken to the canyon air. The water in the passage shifted slightly. She felt it rather than saw it: a change in the quality of the air pressure, the sound of the waterfall deepening by a half-register.

Vor went next. She had prepared for this the way she prepared for everything: she knew what she would say before she came. The memory she had chosen was from the first month of her systematic dungeon work, before the method was a method, when she had been in the second-level passages of a minor Foundation dungeon in the eastern ranges and had found, in a chamber that no record showed, a carving of Lyssandria in her oldest iconographic form — the goddess as the ground itself, the earth as a face, patient and ancient — and had sat in front of it for twenty minutes not mapping, not noting, just looking. She had never written this in any addendum. She had never told anyone. It did not seem like a piece of cartographic data. It seemed like something that belonged only to the chamber and to her.

She said it. The water shifted, the same change Cadis's speaking had produced, and the canyon passage opened fifteen feet ahead — a space that had not been an opening before, the limestone parting the way stone did not part, with the exact silence of something that had been waiting.

Lessen went last. She stood at the edge of the water, her boots already wet from the passage flow, and she looked at the opened entrance for a moment before she looked at the canyon air.

What she said was her own. Vor did not hear it. She watched Lessen's face while she said it, the way she watched every team member's face at a significant threshold: not to intrude, but to know. Lessen's face was still. Not controlled — it was not the face of someone managing something. It was more like the surface of deep water: not empty, but the depth was below the surface, not at it.

The canyon passage widened after she spoke. Noticeably more than it had for Cadis and Vor. The opening was wider and the light through it was different — warmer, deeper, the bioluminescent pulse stronger. As if the Dungeon was acknowledging something that had been worth keeping.

---

Level 1 was as Vor's previous two expeditions had established it: flooded corridors, ankle to knee-deep, well-lit by algae, the current gentle. The walls were white limestone layered with formations that would have been beautiful in any other context — curtain-drapes of mineral deposit, columns of travertine, the slow patient architecture of water given centuries to work. The Dungeon had been given centuries to work on these too. The beauty was real and so was what the beauty was doing.

The water was warm.

She had written this in the previous addendum. She had noted the temperature differential and hypothesized about mechanisms — heated springs below the canyon floor, geothermal proximity — and then she had noted, carefully and separately, the psychological effect: *warmth has a physiological association with safety. The Dungeon's first level uses this association deliberately. The water should not be warm. It is warm. The warmth is accurate to human comfort and is therefore a method.*

Standing ankle-deep in it now, she understood again what the noting hadn't fully captured. The water was the temperature of a hot bath taken somewhere familiar. The temperature of a compress on an injury that wasn't serious. The temperature of a room when someone who loves you has left it recently warm.

She notated her response rather than the feeling, which was her standard method: *note the stimulus, note the response, do not mistake the response for the stimulus.* The response was: ease. Willingness to slow down. A reduction in the specific alertness she maintained in dungeons, the fine-grained surveillance that was her practiced protection. She noted this and kept the alertness. The noting was the protection.

Cadis walked beside her and said nothing. He was reading Flow resonance the way he read all resonances: with patient attention, hands free of the walls, the sensation building in him from whatever his particular sensitivity was to. He had described Flow-reading to her once as like hearing a very long and emotional piece of music — not the specific notes, but the key it was in, the mood it was building, the thing it was moving toward. He had said that Flow-energy felt like being in water yourself, even standing on dry ground.

She wondered what standing in actual water felt like to him. She did not ask. They were in Level 1 and she was building the acoustic map.

She stopped at intervals and listened. The first-level acoustics were complex — the water itself created ongoing sound that she had to work to hear through, a constant middle-frequency wash that her method needed to subtract. She had practiced this in the flooded quarry: listening past the water noise to the reflected signals, the ones that came back from walls and had information in them. She listened. She noted. She moved on.

Lessen walked behind Cadis. She moved the way she moved everywhere — quietly, efficiently, with the specific economy of someone who worked in flooded coastal passages and had spent years calibrating exactly how much movement wet stone required and no more. She carried her own survey tools: a depth gauge, a flow meter, a roll of waterproof notation cloth that she used a wax stylus on. Her approach was different from Vor's — she worked from the water's physical properties rather than from sound — but the two methods would produce complementary data. Vor had understood this when Lessen had described her approach in Greybreak. Different instruments reading the same structure.

The second-level descent came an hour into the approach — a shift in the floor, a darkening of the water, the first Memory-Drowners visible at the edges of the passage. They were water-things, nearly transparent, moving with the current in a way that suggested they were part of the current until the current did something the dungeon wanted it to do. Vor had documented them before. She notated their position and the current behavior and moved on, keeping the team clear of the pool margins where the Drowners worked best.

This was where the water changed.

Not the temperature — the temperature stayed warm. But the quality of the warmth shifted, the way the same temperature could feel different depending on what produced it. At Level 1 the warmth had felt like the residue of something comfortable. At the Level 2 transition it felt like something with a body heat. Something close.

She notated this too. *Temperature maintained, emotional valence shift at descent point. Not physical change — perceived change. Note.*

---

Level 2 was pool-country: shallow, wide chambers connected by narrower passages, the pools ranging from calf-deep to chest-deep, the water clear enough in some that the bottom was visible and in others carrying a faint clouding that the light moved through beautifully. Memory-Drowners worked the pool edges. She had documented their behavioral patterns from the second expedition: they moved with current, stayed in the shallower margins, used contact rather than projection to initiate the memory-sequence. Keep out of the shallows with standing water. Move through pools by wading through the deeper centers where the current ran. She had the protocol.

The first pool she crossed, chest-deep, the current in its center pushing gently south, was the one she had documented on the second expedition's approach. She recognized the travertine column formation at the pool's far wall, the two-tier ledge structure above the waterline to the east. She stopped at the column and listened. She built the chamber's geometry from sound: roughly thirty by twenty, the ceiling at around fifteen, the acoustic decay telling her the walls were smooth limestone rather than rough.

She had turned to begin notation when Cadis stopped.

He stopped the way he stopped when something significant was happening in the resonance: not urgently, not with alarm, but with the specific stillness of someone who is receiving information and does not want to interrupt the receipt of it.

She waited.

"The water," he said, finally.

"What about it."

"It's not carrying emotional energy the way a Flow dungeon should." He paused. He was still reading, not yet done. "Flow energy moves. It flows — the way the Gate name describes. The natural expression of it is current, is movement, is feeling passing through rather than accumulating." He looked at the pool surface. "This dungeon's water doesn't move emotion through. It stores it. It's taken something about Flow energy and inverted it — the feeling doesn't pass, it accumulates. Every person who has ever been in this water left something in it. Every memory given at the entrance, every grief in the Sympathy Chamber, every moment of despair in the deeper levels." He paused again. "The water remembers all of it. It carries all of it. Right now, standing in it—"

"You're standing in everyone who has ever been here," Vor said.

"Yes."

She looked at the pool. The water, warm and clear and lit by the bioluminescence, had that quality she had noted on the second expedition — the quality of something too warm for its temperature. The temperature of accumulated grief, stored and held and carried, gentle and devastating.

She understood, in that moment, why the dungeon's entry ritual asked for a kept memory. It wasn't consuming it. It was adding it to the collection. It was making you part of the archive.

She wrote this on her hand, above the acoustic notation: *Water as memory-storage. Not moving — accumulated. Challenger's entry memory absorbed into reservoir. Dungeon contains layered grief of all prior challengers.*

Then she wrote, smaller: *Do not treat as metaphor. This is the mechanism.*

---

They came to the waterfall on the west wall of the third pool in the second hour. Vor had known it was coming — she had documented it from the second expedition, the anomalous flow that moved upward rather than down, the entry point for the deeper canyon system's inner passages. She had written in the preliminary atlas entry: *Passage into Level 2's interior system via a fall that flows against gravity. Mechanism unknown. Suspected dungeon hydraulic inversion.* She had not gone through on the second expedition. This time she had.

The waterfall was small — maybe four feet wide, the water thin and sheeting down the wall, except that down was wrong. The water came from the floor and fell toward the ceiling, or more precisely, the water moved in the direction that gravity should have prevented and didn't. It didn't cascade or rush — it moved with the specific grace of water that had found a path and taken it, unconcerned with the incongruity.

Vor stood in front of it and looked at it.

She could feel the mist on her face. Warm. The water was still warm.

She put one hand into the fall. The water moved over her hand going up, with a pressure and a weight that was entirely consistent with falling water, just in the wrong direction.

"It reads you as you pass," Lessen said. She had come up beside Vor, studying the fall with the professional attention of someone who worked with water movement for a living. "Every unusual hydraulic system I've mapped in the coastal passages — the ones with anomalous flow behavior — they respond to pressure differential, to turbulence introduced by passage. This would respond to whoever enters." She looked at the point where the water met the ceiling. "It's going to know how you feel when you go through."

"It reads your emotional state," Vor said.

"And keeps it. Same as the pools." Lessen's voice was neutral. Information. "You'll want to know what it does with what it keeps."

Vor did not know. It was in the category of things she did not yet have enough data to determine. She wrote the observation and moved forward.

She stepped into the waterfall.

The water went over her going up — over her face, her shoulders, her arms held slightly out from her body — and she felt it. Not physically. She felt it the way she felt the Dungeon's attention in every dungeon she had entered: the sense of something receiving. She was being registered. Her emotional state at this moment was being noted by whatever intelligence the Weeping Halls used for attention.

What she felt at this moment: methodical. Alert. A faint current of apprehension that she had been maintaining at exactly this level for two hours, the reliable low hum of appropriate caution. Underneath that: the specific clarity that she felt when she was doing work she understood and had prepared for.

And underneath that, further, the thing she rarely went near while she was in the field: the gravity of what she was actually doing. Not this dungeon specifically. The project. Nine corrupted temples still to map. Whatever she had begun with the Hollow Root and its map and its center stone, this slow and uncertain work of returning something to what it had been.

The fall passed over her and let her through.

---

Level 2's interior passages were narrow and low — she had to move carefully, aware of ceiling clearance, the acoustic mapping easier here because the chambers were smaller and the sound bounced faster and cleaner. She built the geometry in chunks: this passage six feet wide, ceiling at eight, forty feet long before the T-junction. Junction: left passage closes in ten feet, not a dead end but a collapsed section, partial flow route still active. Right passage widens, ceiling rises, the acoustics opening up into something larger.

She stopped at the junction and listened to the larger space.

The echo decay was wrong.

She listened again. The decay pattern for a thirty-foot chamber should have had a specific signature — she had calibrated this in the quarry, in the cave systems. What she was hearing was longer. Softer at the edges. The acoustic profile of a space that wasn't empty.

"Something is in the right passage," she said.

Cadis: "Flow elementals. Three, I think. They're not moving."

"Static?" Flow elementals in her prior experience were current-driven, fluid, in motion. An unmoving Flow elemental was—

"They're listening," he said.

She stopped.

The passage was quiet except for water. The water was quiet except for its warmth and its memory. And the three Flow elementals ahead were listening, the way the dungeon had listened at the waterfall, the way the pools had listened when she stood in them.

She understood: the dungeon was not tracking them through threat. It was tracking them through attention. It was building, as they moved deeper, an increasingly detailed record of what they were — their emotional states, their habitual rhythms, the particular shape of each person's inner weather. It was filing this away for later use.

She noted this without feeling afraid of it, because fear was data she did not want to give and because she was already afraid in the low-register way she was always afraid in dungeons, and that fear was already filed.

She moved through the right passage. The elementals parted. They did not touch. They watched with the watching-quality of water that has seen many things and is adding this one to the record.

---

The memory came an hour later, on the long flooded passage that led to the Level 3 descent point. It came for Cadis first.

He stopped mid-stride.

She knew he'd stopped from the change in the sound of his movement — she was tracking each team member acoustically by this point, the specific footfall rhythm of each in the water, the way she had learned to track her whole team by sound rather than constant visual check. Cadis's footfall stopped.

She turned.

He was standing in knee-deep water, looking at the wall.

The wall was showing something. Not a crack, not a formation — the limestone was lit from within, bioluminescence usually a steady soft green, but this section was moving. Shifting. The light in it was producing shapes. She looked at the shapes and understood in one moment what they were: a memory, projected in light through water-stone, detailed and specific and correct. Cadis as a boy, very young, in a room she didn't know. Cadis and a woman, stone-cutting work in her hands, showing him something in a slab of granite. The woman's hands moving. The boy's face watching.

She had never heard Cadis speak about his family. In six expeditions she had never asked.

"That's accurate," Cadis said. His voice was the same. The same steady precision. "She taught me Foundation-resonance reading. Before the Academy." He paused. "She's been dead for nine years."

The memory played on the wall. The woman's hands in the stone. The boy watching with his whole face.

Vor made the call before she had finished making it. She didn't calculate it. There was nothing to calculate. She stopped, and by stopping she told the team they were stopped, and she watched the wall with Cadis.

She did not say anything. She let him look.

The memory was quiet. The dungeon was not weaponizing it — not yet, not here, not in the way the Level 4 Sympathy Chamber was built to weaponize. Here it was just playing it back. Here it was demonstrating: *we know you. We have what you carry. Look.*

The demonstration was sufficient. He did not need to be manipulated by it to be moved by it. He was moved by it because it was true.

After two minutes he said: "Noted," and moved forward.

Vor moved with him. She wrote on her arm, in the acoustic notation: *L2 memory projection — accurate, non-coercive. Wall-projection mechanism distinct from pool-contact mechanism. Dungeon is reading and returning, not manufacturing. Weaponization is downstream.*

Then she stopped writing and listened. Because from somewhere in the passage behind her, not far, Lessen had also stopped.

Vor did not turn around immediately. She waited.

She heard, from behind her, the sound of water.

Just water. Moving. The current doing what currents did.

Then she heard the timing. The pause-before-a-name, exactly as Lessen had described it from the first run. The specific rhythm of a particular person's way of speaking. The dungeon had been carrying this for years, waiting for the right resonance to play it back into.

Lessen's breathing changed.

Vor turned around.

Lessen was standing still in the passage. Not frozen — she hadn't lost her balance, her hands were at her sides, her feet were placed correctly. She was still. The way you were still when you were listening so hard that motion became an interference.

The passage was playing sound. Not projection on a wall — pure sound, coming from the water itself, carried in the acoustic space the way music was carried, the way the dungeon's warmth was carried. A voice Vor had never heard. A voice that had Lessen's name in it with an exact and specific weight.

The voice said her name twice.

Then it was quiet.

Lessen stood in the quiet for a long time. Vor did not count the time. She held the team in place and she did not count.

Then Lessen started walking.

She didn't say anything. She started walking, the same measured pace she had been maintaining, and she passed Vor without looking at her, and she continued up the passage toward the Level 3 descent point.

Vor walked beside her.

After another minute Lessen said, without turning her head, without changing her pace: "It's not her. But it has her timing."

Vor looked straight ahead.

"Yes," she said. "I know."

They descended.
