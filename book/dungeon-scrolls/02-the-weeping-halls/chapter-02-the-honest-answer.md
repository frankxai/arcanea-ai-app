# Chapter Two: The Honest Answer

Level 3 was everything the previous addendum entry had predicted and several things it hadn't.

The Current Maze was well-documented: flooded corridors, variable depth, breath-holding passages, currents that reversed without forewarning. She had mapped the entry sequence on the second expedition — the initial chamber layout, the first four passage choices, the location of the two main Current-Shapers she and Roak had spent four hours avoiding before finding the exit route. She had that map memorized. She had confirmed the entry chamber's geometry acoustically within the first minutes of descent and found it consistent.

What the prior addendum had not captured was the way Level 3 talked.

Not with voices. The dungeon had used voices in Level 2 — the wall-projection, the acoustic memory in the passage. Level 3 was different. The water here moved faster, the currents more complex, and in the constant overlapping sound of water-through-passage there were patterns. She heard them first as accident: a particular chop of water against a wall corner that had a rhythm she recognized. Then she heard it again, different pattern, different passage, and she understood it was not accident. The dungeon was using the water itself as a medium. Not to speak, not exactly — nothing coherent, no words — but to play back. The sound of water over stone that you had heard in a specific remembered place. The pitch of rain on a particular kind of roof. The quality of a fountain in a courtyard you had known when you were young enough that the fountain felt large.

Not enough to stop you. Just enough to make you feel where you were was somehow continuous with somewhere else, somewhere prior, some earlier life that felt safer than this one.

She notated this as a separate mechanism — distinct from the wall-projections, distinct from the acoustic memory in the passages, the dungeon's third emotional instrument — and kept moving. The acoustic mapping was harder here because the ambient sound was more complex, more layered, and the meaningful signals were harder to isolate. She was slower. She worked in intervals, stopping every fifty feet to listen carefully, building the geometry in chunks.

The Current-Shaper she encountered at the Level 3 T-junction was the one she had documented before: a being of directed water that occupied passage intersections and could redirect flow in a ten-foot radius. It wasn't aggressive. It just changed the current, suddenly, from passable to very much not, and challengers who weren't paying attention to their footing found themselves pushed against walls or swept into dead-end passages before they understood what had changed. She had a counter for this: she let the current change, followed it to where it was directing rather than fighting it, and read the new configuration from where she ended up. The Maze, she had found on the second expedition, was designed with a logic to it — the Current-Shapers' redirections made sense if you accepted that you were not controlling your own path and allowed the dungeon to tell you what it wanted you to know about the passage structure.

This felt like giving the dungeon too much. She had noted this on the second expedition. Standing in Level 3 now, with four more floors below her and the Sympathy Chamber waiting at Level 4, she understood it differently: you could not navigate the Weeping Halls by resistance. It was a dungeon of water. Resistance to water was exhaustion. You moved with it and you paid attention to the direction it moved you and you noted what it showed you and you stayed oriented.

This was going to be the lesson the whole dungeon wanted to teach. She was going to have to keep being taught it.

---

They came out of the Current Maze two hours later, through a low passage that required full submersion for the last twenty feet, and surfaced into a chamber that was, for a moment, exactly what Vor had needed and hadn't known she needed: big. A full vaulted ceiling, twenty-five feet overhead, dry limestone above the waterline where the walls curved upward into the arch. The water here was only knee-deep, still warm, the current minimal. The acoustic space was generous. She stood in it and listened and built the map of it in fifteen seconds and felt, in the space between notes, that she had been in narrow passages for long enough that simple largeness was a gift.

She noted this, briefly: *chamber response — relief. Dungeon uses this. Rest here briefly and carefully.*

Then she looked at the eastern passage, which was the descent to Level 4.

The Level 4 passage was narrow again. It descended via a staircut into the stone — not water-filled, just wet, the limestone walls dripping with humidity — and at its bottom the passage bent right and she couldn't see beyond the bend. But she could hear beyond it. She could hear the specific acoustic signature of a very large, very high chamber with unusual surface properties. Not the flat-echo of stone walls. Something with more texture, more absorption.

And she could hear, faintly, from the direction of the bend, the first voices.

Not distinct. Not yet. At this distance they were the quality of voices heard through two walls — human cadence and rhythm, the shape of words without their content. But the cadence was personal. The rhythm was someone specific.

She looked at Cadis.

He was already looking at the passage.

"It's louder than I expected," he said. "The resonance. Level 4 is amplifying." He paused. "Flow energy that's been corrupted into accumulation has a specific quality — it builds. Each challenger adds to the pool. What's in Level 4 now is everything anyone has ever felt in the Sympathy Chamber, amplified by the crystalline formation at the chamber's center. It's not—" He stopped. He was working for accuracy. "The chamber was built to ask a question with tenderness. What it does now is the same question but weighted by everyone who has ever broken there."

Vor thought about this.

A room asking a question with the weight of everyone who had ever answered it wrong. Or everyone who had been unable to leave.

"Lessen," she said.

Lessen had been standing at the chamber's edge, away from the descent passage, looking at the limestone wall with a professional attention that Vor recognized: she was reading the water flow patterns in the stone. She had been doing this periodically since Level 1, building her own complementary map of the dungeon's hydraulic system, the data accumulating in the rolls of waterproof notation cloth.

She looked up.

"Level 4 is ahead," Vor said. "I need to know your state."

It was not a gentle question. She had not framed it gently because Lessen did not benefit from gentle framing of operational questions. She had understood this by the third hour of working beside her.

Lessen rolled the notation cloth and tucked it away. She looked at the passage entrance. She looked at it for five seconds — not hesitation, assessment.

"I know what will happen," she said. "I've known for three years what would happen. The only variable was what I'd do." She looked at Vor. "I know what I'm going to do."

"What are you going to do?"

Lessen's voice was the same: precise, flat, decided. Carrying things the way it always carried things — not at the surface.

"I'm going to listen," she said. "And I'm going to let it run."

Vor looked at her.

"Not surrender to it," Lessen said. "Listen to it. There's a difference." She paused. Something moved in her expression, briefly, not at the surface but visible through it. "I know the difference."

Vor believed her.

"We move together," she said. "Nobody stops alone."

---

The Sympathy Chamber opened at the bottom of the descent stair like a held breath releasing.

It was vast. She had not been here before and so had no measure against a prior visit, but the vastness had a quality beyond dimension: the chamber felt as large as it sounded, which was to say it felt like the inside of a cathedral, the ceiling lost in the acoustic shadow above her head. The walls were limestone carved into what had been, before the corruption, a flowing terraced design — bowl-curves cascading from ceiling to floor, surfaces meant to move water in gentle descent. The water was still here, still moving in the carved channels, but slowly, and the channels had another purpose now: they were the resonant surface. The dungeon's acoustic engine. The bowl-curves were tuned. She understood, hearing the chamber's acoustics with her method's ear, that every curve had been designed by something that understood sound, that had taken the original temple's flowing-water architecture and inverted it into a chamber that was functionally an instrument.

The focal point was visible from the entrance: a crystalline formation at the chamber's center, rising from the floor, roughly eight feet tall, translucent pale blue, the Veloura Glass that the DUNGEON_ATLAS materials noted as Leyla's material — the glass that stored emotional memory. It was enormous. Centuries of accumulated grief, stored in the molecular structure of the crystal, and the crystal was using the bowl-curve walls to amplify it outward.

She listened to the chamber the way she listened to all chambers, for the geometry. Then she listened past the geometry. Then she heard what was in the geometry.

Voices.

Not one voice — a chorus of very specific voices, each distinct, each calibrated to someone. The chamber was not playing a single memory for every challenger. It was playing, simultaneously, the individual resonant frequencies of everyone currently in it, and the vast accumulation of everyone who had ever been in it, and weaving those into a texture of loss that had the specific quality of being simultaneously impersonal and utterly personal. Abstract grief and your specific grief, indistinguishable, the same frequency.

She noted this with precision on her arm. She was grateful for the notation. It gave her hands something to do.

Cadis walked to her left and said nothing. He had both hands out from his body. He was working, reading the Flow corruption with everything he had, and his face had the quality of someone adjacent to something very large that was not threatening them but was not indifferent to them either.

Lessen walked ahead of them.

Not ahead by much. Three steps, four. But she walked into the Sympathy Chamber before the others, and she stopped in the middle of the chamber floor, and she stood there and let the chamber do what the chamber did.

Vor watched her.

The crystal at the center sang.

Not a literal note — not a sound her ears could track. But Cadis went very still and she felt it, the resonance traveling through the water on the floor and up through the soles of her boots and into the specific registers of her body that emotion lived in, and what it played was not a sound. It was a shape. A shape of feeling that she recognized as belonging to her, exactly her, the specific configuration of her own experience, and from that shape it was building something — finding the voices, finding the memories — she felt the tug of it, the way the Sympathy Chamber built its pull. The way the second expedition's accounts described: the compelling sense that what you were looking for was here, in this direction, if you only moved slightly closer.

She stood very still.

Cadis said: "The crystal is the anchor point. Everything the chamber stores and amplifies routes through it." He had to speak slightly louder here — the chamber's acoustics were excellent and the ambient noise of directed grief was considerable. "If there is an original trial mechanism anywhere in this level, it will be past the crystal, not in front of it."

She was already thinking this.

She was also watching Cadis's face, and what she saw there was: the voice of his mother. Not obvious. He was not weeping, not frozen. But there was something in the set of his mouth that she recognized from fourteen years of working beside him in difficult conditions — the expression of someone who was doing the right thing and it was costing him.

She looked at him.

"I know," he said, before she could speak. "I know what I'm hearing. I'm here."

She trusted him.

---

Then Cadis sat down.

Not dramatically. He didn't collapse, didn't falter. He walked to the nearest step in the terraced wall and he sat, the way you sat when you needed to put something down briefly, the weight of it requiring a moment of rest. He sat and he looked at the chamber floor.

She crossed to him immediately.

"Cadis."

He looked up. His eyes were fully present. Not lost, not pulled under. But she recognized what was happening: the chamber had his mother's voice and the weight of it was not the kind of weight that twelve years of practice with Foundation resonance fully insulated against, because it was not Foundation energy. It was Flow. It was love and loss and the specific human inability to be entirely armored against someone who mattered to you.

"I can hear," he said, "her exact way of saying my name." He was precise about this. Not distressed — precise. "The way the first syllable was slightly longer. The particular pitch she used when I was a child and she was calling me from another room. I cannot—" He stopped. He started again. "I cannot stop hearing it."

She understood.

She said: "I know what I'm doing. I don't need you moving for twenty minutes. Sit. Stay oriented."

He looked at her. Something settled in his face — not relief exactly, something more like acknowledgment. She was not asking him to be fine. She was asking him to stay.

"Yes," he said.

She left him there, sitting on the step with his mother's voice in the chamber's air, and she moved toward the crystal.

---

The chamber floor was flooded ankle-deep, same as Level 1, but here the warmth was different. She had described it in her Level 1 notes as the temperature of something recently left warm. Here it was the temperature of something that had never gone cold. The kind of warmth that was slightly oppressive, slightly too much, that had you wanting to take off a layer you were not wearing.

She walked to the crystal and looked at it.

Veloura Glass: liquid at rest, solid under pressure, stores emotional memory. She was looking at eight feet of stored emotional memory. She could see into it, the crystal translucent, and what she saw inside was not visual exactly — nothing she could describe to someone else — but the impression of layers, densities, the dark and light strata of what the glass had been receiving for centuries. Each challenger's surrender in this chamber, the moment of giving in to the voice, the hours of sitting and listening, all of it encoded in the molecular structure of a material that had been built by the universe specifically to remember feeling.

She stood at the crystal's base and listened.

The acoustic focal point was here. She could feel it physically: the bowl-curves in the walls all converged their reflections at this point, so the sound arriving here from every direction was simultaneous and precise, the voices layered and exact and each one calibrated for someone specific. The chamber was played by the crystal and heard in the crystal and it had been doing this for a very long time.

She thought about the Hollow Root and what she had done in its Core chamber. A map. She had given it a map of what it had been.

She looked at the Veloura Glass.

If the crystal was the instrument, then the chamber's corruption fed through the crystal. The amplification of accumulated grief — the centuries of kept sorrow — was a corruption of what the original Flow trial had asked. The original trial had asked challengers to feel what they truly felt. Not what they should feel. Not what the situation called for. What was actually there.

The corruption had taken that question and built a mechanism that made the feeling so overwhelming that leaving became impossible. It had taken the trial's depth and made it a prison.

The crystal was not inherently the corruption. The crystal was Veloura Glass, Flow Gate material, designed to hold emotional memory. That was its nature. The corruption was what it was holding — not memory as archive, not memory as honest witness, but memory as bait. Memory weaponized against the person to whom it belonged.

She could not remove the crystal. She did not have that kind of capability. She could not cleanse it the way she could not have cleansed the Hollow Root by hand.

But the Hollow Root had not required cleansing. It had required seeing.

She pressed her hand flat against the crystal's surface.

Cold. Not body-temperature, not room-temperature — genuinely cold, below the ambient warmth of the chamber, the cold of something that was dense with what it had absorbed. The pressure of thousands of griefs. She held her hand against it and felt the cold and felt the weight of it, and she did not pull away.

She listened.

Not to the voices the crystal was playing outward. She listened to what was inside it. Past the weaponized projection, past the centuries of accumulated surrender — she listened for the thing the chamber had been before. What it had been designed to ask.

The question was there. It was always there. Under everything the corruption had built over it — the same question, patient and specific, asking in the exact original register of what Leyla's Flow trial had been designed to ask.

*What do you feel?*

Not what you should feel. Not what you want to feel. Not what the moment requires or what the people around you expect or what it would be easier to feel. What is actually there. What is true.

She stood at the crystal and asked herself.

She asked herself with the same precision she used for every observation. The same method: note the stimulus, note the response, do not mistake the response for the stimulus, and above all do not mistake the constructed response — the performed one, the one built to be acceptable — for the actual one.

What did she feel?

She felt: the weight of this room. The oppressive warmth. The tiredness of Level 3's navigation and the low-register apprehension of knowing there were four more levels below this one. The specific grief of standing in a chamber that held thousands of people's deepest losses, not abstractly but in the glass under her hand, and being the kind of person who found this information fascinating rather than purely devastating. Fascination tinged with the guilt of finding devastating things interesting.

She felt: fear. Not of this moment — of the fourth floor. The fifth. The seventh. Not of dying in the dungeon. She had been making peace with the possibility of dying in dungeons since the second year of her systematic work, and peace was the wrong word but it was the closest word. Not of dying. Of arriving at the level where she would have to make the reconstruction, the way she had made it for the Hollow Root, and not being certain she could do it. Not being certain it would work.

She felt: tired.

She felt: the thing underneath tired, which was something closer to hunger. The specific hunger of a person who had been doing necessary and important and difficult work for fourteen years and had begun, in the past year — in the months since the Hollow Root, since the letters, since the reputation she had never asked for — to understand the full scope of what the work was. Nine more temples. Each one its own kind of damage, each one its own kind of precision required, each one requiring the equivalent of what had taken her fourteen years to develop with the Hollow Root.

The hunger was not ambition. It was not pride. It was the desire to complete something that was worth completing, and the knowledge that she might not live long enough to do it, and the decision she had been making and remaking every time she descended into another dungeon: that she was going to try anyway.

She held all of this at once. She did not organize it into a more presentable shape. She held it as it was.

The crystal went still under her hand.

Not cold-and-dense anymore. Still cold. But the quality of it changed — the way a room changed when something that had been holding its breath released it. The density remained; the weight of what was stored could not simply leave. But the amplification — the mechanism by which the stored grief was being weaponized outward, played back at the people in the chamber with the specific intention of keeping them there — that mechanism stuttered.

She kept her hand on the crystal and did not move.

---

Lessen had been standing in the chamber for the length of time it had taken Vor to cross to the crystal — long enough. Vor had tracked her by peripheral attention and by sound, the specific acoustic presence of a person standing still in moving water, and Lessen had been still and present and present and still, and the chamber had been playing what the chamber played.

Now Lessen did something.

She closed her eyes.

And she added to it.

Vor saw this but did not understand it immediately — she understood it two seconds later, when the quality of the chamber's sound changed again. Not the stuttering change from the crystal under her hand. Something different. A change in what the room was receiving.

Lessen was not fighting the chamber. She was not trying to leave, not trying to keep the voices at a distance. She was standing in the chamber's frequency and she was offering her own — the grief she actually carried, the loss that was hers, fully present and fully given, not extracted, not taken, not surrendered in despair but offered deliberately. As a contribution. As a choice.

The chamber had been designed to take.

Lessen was giving willingly.

She gave what was hers to give: three years of grief, specific and exact, the quality of losing someone in the Mirror Depths dungeon and spending three years learning what to do with that loss, not learning to be over it — that was not what three years had produced — but learning what it meant that you continued to carry it. What the carrying was for. The grief itself as something valuable, something that had Renae's shape in it, something that was her only remaining record of what it had felt like to be known by a specific person who was no longer there to know her.

She gave it openly. The way the Flow Gate's original trial had asked challengers to give: what do you feel, actually, without flinching. The dungeon had been given this many times. Every challenger in the Sympathy Chamber had eventually felt this, had felt the real thing under the constructed version, and the chamber had taken it from them as they surrendered.

Lessen was not surrendering. She was giving.

The chamber did not know what to do with this.

Vor could hear it: the acoustics shifted. The bowl-curves in the walls that had been routing the amplified grief outward began, fractionally, to route differently. Not much. Not a restoration — nothing as simple or complete as what had happened in the Hollow Root. But the weaponization stuttered again, harder this time, the mechanism designed for taking confronted with something it was offered and had no protocol for receiving.

Lessen opened her eyes.

Her face was wet. She did not touch her face. She looked at the crystal, at Vor's hand against it, and she walked across the chamber to stand beside Vor.

"It doesn't know what to do with someone who gives," she said. Not triumphantly. Observationally. The same precision, the same flat delivery, and behind it — not hidden but present in the way depths were present — the full weight of what she had just done, and what it had cost, and what it was worth.

"No," Vor said.

She kept her hand on the crystal.

Together, the two of them stood at the focal point of the chamber's corruption, one offering the honest account of what she felt and one offering her grief freely, and the mechanism that had been holding thousands of challengers in this room for centuries stuttered and stuttered and did not stop but could not quite begin again with the same force.

---

At the back of the Sympathy Chamber, past the crystal, past the terraced walls and the bowl-curves and the channels of water that had been carrying grief for so long the limestone itself had the character of something that had been wept on for centuries, there was a room.

She had not been here before. No extant expedition record mentioned it. She had not known it was here except in the way she now knew things about dungeons — the way they felt, the layers beneath the corruption, the thing the original temple had been designed around. She had known there was something past the crystal because the original trial had to exist somewhere, the way the original trial in the Hollow Root had existed in the Core chamber.

The room was dry.

Completely dry. Not damp, not humid — dry in the way of stone that had never let water in, that had been sealed from the dungeon's hydraulics by something the dungeon couldn't penetrate. It was small: twelve feet square, the ceiling at eight, the walls plain limestone without carving or formation. In the center of the floor, a pool.

Not flooded — a pool set into the stone, perfectly circular, roughly three feet across. Still water. Absolutely still. No current, no warmth, no movement. The water was clear and the bottom was visible and the bottom was pale grey stone with nothing on it.

She crouched beside the pool and looked at it.

Words on the surface. Not carved — written in the water itself, the way words sometimes appeared in the surface tension of very still water. She had seen this in the Hollow Root: the dungeon's original trial questions, preserved in the architecture. The Weeping Halls' question was in the water.

She read it.

*What do you feel?*

Four words. The simplest question the dungeon had ever asked. The question underneath all the voices and the warmth and the memory projections and the crystal's amplification and the terraced walls and the seven levels of careful mechanism. The question the Flow Gate had been built around. What Leyla had wanted to ask every challenger who descended, before Malachar had taken the question and turned it into a trap.

*What do you actually feel?*

She sat on the floor of the dry room beside the still pool and she asked herself a second time.

What she felt, right now, in this moment:

Tired. She had said this to the crystal. It was still true.

Fascinated. The dry room was a surviving room — the same category she had identified at the Sight Gate years ago, the chambers where the corruption could not take hold, where the original temple character surfaced like a word remembered. The Weeping Halls had a surviving room. She wanted to map it with every instrument she had.

Afraid of the fourth floor. This was no longer the fourth floor — this was the fourth floor and they were in it and they were alive and the chamber's mechanism had been disrupted and they had found the original trial room. What she was now afraid of was the fifth floor, and the sixth, and the seventh, and what waited below.

Sad. This was the word she had been holding at a slight distance. The specific sadness of standing in a room that had been built to ask the most honest question possible and had spent centuries being used to prevent honest answers. The sadness of the Weeping Halls specifically — not the Hollow Root's sadness, which had been the sadness of something that had carried a question for three hundred years without being heard. This was the sadness of something that had been listening to the answer for centuries and taking it as fuel. All those challengers sitting in the Sympathy Chamber. All those people hearing the voice of someone they had lost. The sadness was for them, the thousand years of them, and it was also, she admitted, for herself. For the version of herself who, in twenty minutes or an hour or a day, would descend to Level 5, and 6, and 7. For the version of herself who was tired.

The trial accepted honest answers.

She felt the room change around her — not physically, nothing as dramatic as the Hollow Root's response, no settling of stone or shift of resonance. Something quieter. The still pool's surface remained still. But the quality of the stillness changed: it became less the stillness of something waiting and more the stillness of something that had just received what it was waiting for, and had nothing left to wait for.

---

She rose from the floor of the dry room and went to her map board.

She had been notation-mapping acoustically for six hours. Now she worked formally, the map case open, the good paper out, the ink that held in low humidity. She drew the dry room precisely. She drew the still pool. She drew the surviving room's relationship to the Sympathy Chamber, the connection between the original trial point and the amplification mechanism of the crystal, the way the dungeon's corruption had grown up around the trial room without being able to enter it.

She drew the bowl-curves in the Sympathy Chamber walls. She drew the crystalline formation and her hand against it. She drew, in the notation she had developed for the Hollow Root's trial documentation, the question in the water: *What do you feel?*

She noted in the margin: *The Sympathy Chamber's corruption feeds through the crystal but the original trial mechanism is downstream — the crystal is not the trial, it is the corruption of the trial. The trial asks the question the corruption weaponizes. They are the same question in different registers: what do you feel when you cannot leave, versus what do you feel when you choose to stay and answer.*

She noted in smaller writing, below that: *Lessen's method — offering grief freely rather than surrendering to extraction — disrupted the chamber's mechanism. This is the second disruption documented in a Dungeon trial chamber. Each disruption used different approach. Foundation: mirror of original temple, demonstration of what the dungeon had been. Flow: direct engagement, offering what the dungeon sought to take. Both worked through the trial's original mechanism rather than around it.*

She put the pen down and looked at the drawings.

Then she opened the front cover of the map case — the leather inside she had treated, the same compound she'd used for the Hollow Root case, clay from the Foundation Gate's construction region — and she added the Weeping Halls notation to the inside cover, alongside the note from the Hollow Root expedition.

*They can be found.*

*And found, they can be given back to themselves.*

Below that, new ink, the first addition since she had written those sentences three months ago:

*The method is different each time. The principle is the same: meet the trial where it is. Not the corruption. The trial.*

She closed the case.

Cadis had recovered from the step. He was standing, his expression clear and professional, the look of someone who had been in difficult emotional territory and had come back from it with information. He would write his own account of what the Sympathy Chamber had done with his mother's voice. He would be precise. She trusted him.

Lessen was standing at the passage entrance to Level 5. Her face was dry now. Her notation cloth was in her hands and she was already re-rolling it, preparing it for the next section, the wax stylus tucked behind her ear. She did not look at Vor with expectation. She looked at the passage the way she looked at all things she was about to move through: with the attention of someone who had learned that attention was the only accurate response to the unknown.

Vor crossed the Sympathy Chamber one more time, looking at it. The chamber was quieter than it had been — not silent, not restored, the corruption still present and the crystal still dense with what it had stored and the voices still there for anyone who would trigger them. But the mechanism was less perfect. The weaponization had been disrupted, fractionally, by two people standing at the focal point and offering the honest version of what the chamber was trying to extract.

Two temples now. Two partial restorations. Two original trial rooms with the corruption receding from them, by increments, by degrees, because someone had gone in and looked and answered honestly and offered freely.

Eight remained.

She noted this in the margin of the formal map. Not as despair — she was not despairing. As a number. A precise accounting of where she was in the work.

Then she notated the surviving room's coordinates in her atlas — the living Compendium document she was building, dungeon by dungeon, floor by floor — and she wrote, beside the coordinates, in the notation she used for significant finds: *Trial room intact. Original question accessible. Corruption receded around trial perimeter. Partial restoration confirmed.*

She shouldered her map case.

"Level 5," she said.

They descended.
