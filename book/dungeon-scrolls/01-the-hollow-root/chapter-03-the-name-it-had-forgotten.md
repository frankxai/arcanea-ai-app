# Chapter Three: The Name It Had Forgotten

Three months.

She spent the first month writing.

The field addendum for the Compendium ran to forty-seven pages — the most detailed single-dungeon entry she had produced in fourteen years, and she had produced eleven others. She wrote it the way she wrote all her addenda: once from notes, once from memory, then a third pass where she held both versions up against each other and looked for the places they diverged, because the divergences were always the most important thing. Where the notes and the memory disagreed, she wrote both versions and noted the discrepancy. Where they agreed, she wrote once and moved on.

The section on the Core took nine pages. She had not, in six prior dungeons, written nine pages about a Core. She had not, in six prior dungeons, found a Core while still in a condition to write clearly about what she'd found. She had found fragments, implications, the architecture of intent — places where the corrupted temple had been built around something that was clearly significant, clearly present, but which her prior approaches had never allowed her to reach intact.

In the Hollow Root she had stood with her hand on it.

She wrote what it had felt like. She wrote the precise measurement she'd made: the width of a fist, set flush, Kaelith Stone, center of the mosaic ring, the place all the concentric circles converged. She wrote the dungeon's response — the settling, the shift, the way the concentric rings had drawn inward — and she wrote her interpretation of that response, which she labeled carefully as interpretation and not observation.

*The dungeon was afraid. I do not know what afraid means for a predatory intelligence that does not experience emotion in the way mortals experience it. I am using the word because it is the closest I have. Something in the Hollow Root's response to having its Core identified and placed on a map had the quality of a prey response: not aggression, retreat. The dungeon did not attack. It pulled in.*

She looked at this paragraph for a long time. Then she let it stand.

The second month she spent building.

She had two new mappers to train. Both had come to her through the Compendium's referral system — Compendium members who had read her addenda and written asking to work with her, which happened once every two or three years and which she had previously declined, because she had never been certain her method was teachable rather than merely habitual. After the Hollow Root, she was still uncertain. But she had six more dungeons and a Core-finding to document and the work would require more than four people.

Renna was twenty-six. Three years of lower-dungeon surveying in the Academy's Exploration division, Foundation-touched like Cadis but through a secondary affinity rather than a primary one — she could read Foundation resonance in the way a person with moderate hearing could follow a complicated piece of music: well enough to be useful, not so well as to make her dependent on it. She asked good questions and she didn't argue when the answer was *I don't know yet.*

Solen was thirty-one. No Gates, same as Vor, which Vor had privately considered a selection criterion before deciding it wasn't. No Gates meant a different kind of attention — a trained attention rather than a resonant one, observation in place of sensation. She had been a cartographer of the living world for nine years, which meant she had spent nine years mapping surfaces that changed with weather and season and human use, and she had consequently developed the specific patience that came from documenting systems that did not hold still. She moved quietly. She took good notes. She ate whatever was on hand without complaint, which Vor had learned to weight heavily when evaluating field candidates.

The method, she taught them. The ink test, the charcoal-on-skin notation, the skin-not-paper habit for information you didn't want read back at you, the importance of the first map made from memory alone and the second made formally and the space between them where the dungeon's influence was visible. The wall-marks. The notation for gravity anomalies. The shorthand that had taken her fourteen years to build.

She could not teach them fourteen years of dungeon attention. But she could give them the architecture of it, the structure of a practice they would spend years filling with their own experience. She could give them the method and trust them to develop the instinct.

She showed them both the Hollow Root addendum. The Core section.

Renna read it twice. "You think it responded," she said.

"I know it responded," Vor said. "I watched it. The concentric rings shifted."

"To having the Core mapped."

"To having the Core exist on a record. Yes." She looked at the nine pages. "It had been preventing that for fourteen years of my career and an unknown number of years before Tessil. Something changed when the Core was on the map."

"What do you think changed?"

Vor had been thinking about this for a month. She had been thinking about it in the way she thought about all things she couldn't resolve: not constantly, but persistently, the way a stone in your boot was always there even when you forgot to notice it, because the occasional noticing kept reminding you.

"The dungeon protects the Core," she said. "That's established. All the evidence suggests it — the deflection of attention in the chamber, the way it had kept the Core off every map, the response when I placed it. The question is why a dungeon that learns would protect something."

Renna waited.

"A predatory intelligence protects things it needs," Vor said. "Not things it's indifferent to. Not things it fears. Things it needs." She paused. "The dungeon needs the Core."

She did not say the rest of what she'd been thinking. Not yet. She was still working out whether it was mappable.

The third month she prepared.

Different instruments. A set of ink-drop bottles she'd had blown by a glassmaker in Cinderwall, smaller than the standard, designed to be dropped from different heights and to produce reliable measurements at each increment. A second map case, leather-lined, that she had treated with a preparation of her own development — a clay compound from the Foundation Gate's original construction region, sourced from the Compendium's materials archive, fired and applied to the leather's interior in three coats. She did not know if the compound would interfere with whatever mechanism the dungeon used to read formal paper records. She had a test for it: she would put a deliberately incorrect map inside the treated case and see whether the dungeon corrected it.

She also brought Cadis.

Not because she needed his Foundation-sense specifically, though she was glad to have it. Because of what had happened in the absorption chamber. Because of the question the stone had asked him, and what it meant that the original trial's question was still intact under three hundred years of corruption. Because she was going back with a hypothesis she could not test alone.

The dungeon had kept the Core intact.

The dungeon had kept the original trial chambers intact.

The dungeon had kept the original stonework intact, every carved symbol, every piece of Lyssandrian precision, for three hundred years.

A predatory intelligence that was learning, that was adaptive, that had been changing its methods and tactics for centuries — it had kept everything the temple originally was, exactly as it was.

It was not protecting the Core from her.

It was protecting the Core from something else.

She was going to find out what.

---

The Ironwood was the same.

She had not expected it to be different — forests did not change on a three-month timescale, not these forests — but there was something about approaching a dungeon a second time that produced a specific kind of attention, the attention of a comparison being made continuously and unconsciously between what you were seeing and what you remembered. The approach under the amber-diffracted canopy. The way the treeline thinned forty meters out from the descent point, the root-thinning she'd noted in her first entry: *R.T. — 40m perimeter.* Still there.

She stopped eight meters from the descent point and looked.

The root-spiral was different.

Not dramatically. Not in the way that would have been visible without the precision of her first-entry drawing for comparison. But she had drawn those roots from close observation and she remembered them, and the roots she was looking at now were not in exactly the same position they had been.

They had grown.

Three months was not long enough for ironwood roots to grow measurably in standard conditions. Ironwood was a slow tree, one of the slowest-growing species in the forest zones she'd surveyed. Root growth of three months should have been invisible to any measurement she could take in the field.

These roots had moved by approximately six inches.

She stood and looked at them.

Renna, beside her, said nothing. She was learning when to say nothing, which was a more important skill than any specific observation.

Solen crouched and looked at the nearest root. She touched it, the way she touched everything in new terrain: two fingers, the light touch of someone who had learned that surfaces sometimes told you things with contact that they didn't tell you with observation. She looked at Vor.

"It grew toward the spiral," Solen said.

Vor looked at the geometry of it. The root she was looking at had been growing outward, away from the descent point — her drawing showed this clearly, the outward angle, the tip pointed away from the dungeon's entrance. Now the root was curving. Describing a slow arc that would, in another year of growth, bring it back toward the spiral.

She marked this: *Root growth — inward-directed, toward descent point. New behavior. 3 months.* Below that, the notation she used for observations she was not certain how to categorize: *compare to first-entry drawings.*

She led them to the entry ritual. Each of them had brought something handmade. She had not instructed them on what; she had told them only that the ritual required an object they had made themselves, with their own labor, something genuinely built rather than purchased or received.

Daessen buried a wooden toggle he'd carved while they waited for the second expedition to be ready. She had seen him working on it in the evenings, never asking what it was for, though she suspected he'd known.

Renna buried a braided cord she'd made from the wool of her family's sheep — she'd told Vor about the sheep without prompting, the way certain people volunteered information that mattered to them without yet knowing it mattered to the listener.

Solen buried a small clay figure, irregular, the shape of a person sitting — she'd made it in the third week of training, she said, when she couldn't sleep and her hands needed something to do.

Vor buried a drawing. Not a dungeon drawing: a drawing she'd made at sixteen of the Ironwood treeline, before she'd been in the Root, when the Ironwood was still just a forest she'd heard about and wanted to see. She'd kept it in her personal archive for eighteen years, not for professional reasons — it had no cartographic precision, she'd been sixteen — but because it was the first drawing she'd made of a place she intended to go to.

The earth accepted it and the entrance opened.

---

She went down first. She counted the steps.

Thirty-two.

At the antechamber she stopped. Renna and Solen and Daessen and Cadis arrayed behind her in the arrangement she'd established — Daessen at the corridor entrance, Cadis beside the carved panels, Renna and Solen at the room's perimeter where she'd set them to running the ink-drop tests.

She looked at the antechamber.

The first thing she noticed: the bioluminescent lichen was brighter than she remembered.

This was the kind of observation that, in a dungeon, she held at arm's length before she trusted it. Memory was not measurement. She had not quantified the light on the first expedition. She could not be certain the brightness had changed rather than the memory having degraded in the direction of dimmer.

But.

Renna said: "The carvings are cleaner."

Vor looked at the nearest panel. The Foundation iconography — the root-and-soil symbols, the signs for *ground*, *depth*, *what remains*, *the patience of stone*. She had drawn these on the first expedition. She had drawn them accurately; she had been drawing carved imagery in dungeons for fourteen years.

The carvings were not cleaner in the sense of having been cleaned. There was no removed grime, no visible restoration. They were cleaner in the way that stone looked after rain — not washed but clarified, the edges of the carving slightly more distinct, the depth of the cuts slightly more present.

"Cadis," she said.

He was already looking at them. His hands were at his sides, which was his posture for active Foundation-resonance reading — hands free, no physical contact with the stone, just the sense of what was in the air between him and it.

"The resonance is different," he said.

"Different how."

He took a moment. She had learned to wait for Cadis's moments; he was precise by nature and did not give approximations when precision was available, which meant the moments he needed were the moments precision required more than she currently had time for.

"Stronger," he said. "Not the corruption. The original resonance. The underlying character." He looked at the carved panels. "The first time I was here, the original resonance was what I called quiet. Present but suppressed. Like hearing a melody through a wall." He paused. "I can hear it more clearly now."

Vor noted this without allowing herself to interpret it yet. The method she'd developed for things she didn't have a category for yet was to observe precisely without inferring — to accumulate specific notation and hold the interpretation until there was enough accumulation for the interpretation to be stable.

She opened her map case. She withdrew the test sheet — the deliberately incorrect map she'd prepared, three measurements wrong by specific quantified amounts — and put it into the clay-treated secondary case. She set the case on the floor beside the antechamber's west wall.

She drew the antechamber from memory, checking against the first expedition's drawings. Same dimensions. Same arch positions. Same iconography placement.

Then she noted the differences. The lichen brightness. The carving clarity. The resonance change Cadis had identified. She wrote each observation cleanly, without connecting them, without the sentence she could feel forming at the edge of her attention that would have connected them into a pattern she was not yet sure she could demonstrate.

She descended.

---

The third level was where she had expected the changes.

The labyrinth had shifted again — she had expected this, it shifted between expeditions, that was documented behavior. But the character of the shifting was different.

On the first expedition, the labyrinth had shifted to obstruct. Passages removed, dead ends inserted, the south passage relocated with a ninety-degree rotation that was precisely designed to exhaust a navigator who was relying on compass orientation. She had charted the obstruction methodology carefully: *strategic obstacle — not random rearrangement, deliberate deflection.*

This labyrinth was not obstructing.

She walked its new configuration with Cadis at her shoulder and the map geometry building in her mind, and she understood it two junctions in: the labyrinth had shifted to a version that was more efficient to navigate. Not open — still a labyrinth, still requiring decisions, still the kind of geometry that would confuse someone without her particular method. But the dead ends were fewer. The turns were fewer. The distance from the labyrinth entrance to the turning chamber was shorter by what she estimated as forty feet of passage.

She stopped at the junction where she'd found the lateral gravity pull on the first expedition, the pull that had drawn Cadis away from the group.

She uncapped the ink and held it at floor level.

The ink fell down.

The lateral field was gone.

She looked at the junction for a moment. She thought about what she knew about the lateral field: it had been the mechanism by which the dungeon separated challengers in the labyrinth. A subtle pull, invisible below the threshold of conscious notice, producing what felt like the challenger's own step. It had worked on Cadis — had worked on her, she was honest about this in her documentation, it had almost worked on her too before she'd tested it.

Gone.

She wrote: *Third level lateral field — absent at junction site. Confirmed by ink test. Dungeon has removed trap mechanism from documented location.* Then, below that, in the shorthand that meant she was not yet publishing this: *cannot determine if mechanism is relocated or deactivated.*

The absorption chamber was unchanged. The stone was cold. Cadis stood in its entrance and read the Foundation resonance for a full minute before he spoke.

"It's still there," he said. "The mechanism. The stone remembers the question."

"But?"

"But it's not active." He put one hand on the doorframe, not entering. "It would take someone standing still inside the chamber for a significant time before it engaged. And even then — I don't think it would hold them the same way." He was quiet. "It's like a question that's stopped expecting an answer. Not because it gave up. Because someone heard it."

She wrote this down verbatim. Cadis was not given to imprecision, and when he used language that wasn't technical it meant the technical language wasn't adequate. *A question that has stopped expecting an answer because someone heard it.* She did not know yet what this meant structurally. She intended to find out.

The turning chamber was the clearest change of all.

On the first expedition, the turning chamber had been the worst gravity distortion in the Hollow Root's upper levels: the complete and sustained assault on proprioception, the accumulated wrongness, every sense above the ankles contradicting reality. She had crossed it with the deliberate walk and the closed eyes and the fourteen years of trained navigation. Daessen had heard the trial question as an accusation. The chamber had felt like a place that was trying to take something from them.

She stepped into it now and stopped.

The distortion was present. She could feel it — the stomach-displacement, the faint wrongness in her perception of vertical. But the quality of it had changed. The chamber was no longer using the distortion as a weapon. The distortion was still there, doing what distortions of this type did, but the layer beneath it — the deliberate compression of the effect, the dungeon's intelligent use of it to produce maximum psychological damage — that layer was quieter. Much quieter.

The original trial, as Cadis had described it, had been a place where you stood on solid ground while your perception told you that you were falling, and you had to find the capacity to navigate from your feet rather than your fear. The original trial question: *what can you stand on when your own senses lie to you?*

The chamber was asking that question again.

Just the question. Without the weapon.

Daessen walked in behind her and stood for a moment. She watched his face, the familiar compression at the jaw, the readiness for what the chamber had done to him before.

His jaw did not compress.

"It's still wrong," he said. "Still feels like falling."

"Yes."

"But it's not—" He stopped. He had Daessen's precision with silence: he stopped because he was finding the right word. "Before it felt like it was talking. This feels like it's just wrong. The way a room can just be wrong without intending to be."

She wrote: *Turning chamber — gravity distortion present, unchanged in degree. Psychological quality significantly altered. Daessen's report: no accusation quality, distortion present but passive. Original trial character possible. Compare to first entry notes.*

She crossed the chamber in the deliberate walk.

At the center she stopped and closed her eyes.

She went back through the geometry of the dungeon as she'd been building it today. The brighter lichen. The cleaner carvings. The labyrinth that was easier to navigate. The lateral trap removed from the absorption junction. The absorption mechanism quieted. The turning chamber asking rather than accusing.

She opened her eyes.

She looked at the passage that led down to the fourth level.

She said, to no one in particular, or to the room: "You let us through."

The chamber was distortion and old stone and the quality of something that had been carrying a question for three hundred years.

It did not answer. But it did not need to.

---

The fourth level was different in a way she felt before she crossed its threshold.

She had felt this once before: at the Mirror Depths, on the seventh floor, the day she had found the chamber where the original trial for the Sight Gate had been preserved almost perfectly — a room of clarity in a dungeon of blindness, inexplicable and exact, the temple's original character surfacing like a word remembered after years of forgetting. She had written in the Compendium addendum: *a surviving room. There appear to be rooms in which the corruption cannot take full hold, or from which it recedes for reasons I cannot determine. These rooms are not safe. But they are instructive in ways the corrupted rooms are not. They remember the purpose.*

The fourth level of the Hollow Root had been reclaiming itself.

The Kaelith Stone mosaic was unchanged in every physical measurement she could take — same dimensions, same ring structure, same flush-set center stone. But the presence of the mosaic was different. On the first expedition, the Kaelith Stone had resonated with what Cadis had called Foundation-essence, the oldest resonance there was, still present, still itself despite everything done around it. A deep signal, continuous, but suppressed under the weight of three hundred years of corruption built around it.

Now the resonance was audible.

Not literally. She had no Gates, she heard nothing with her ears. But the quality of standing in the fourth-level chamber was different in the way that standing at the base of a waterfall was different from standing half a mile away from it: not louder, exactly, but more present. More itself. The thing that had always been here, making itself known.

Renna had both hands on her opposite arms, the self-hug posture Vor had seen her use when Foundation-resonance was strong. Her face was not frightened. She looked like someone listening to music they had been told about but never heard.

Solen was drawing. She had her board out and her charcoal and she was documenting with the quiet efficiency of someone who'd learned that what she was witnessing required a record. Good. Vor had been right about Solen.

Cadis was standing at the mosaic's edge and looking at the center stone.

Vor crossed to him.

"Tell me," she said.

"The corruption is contracting," he said. "Not gone. Not even close to gone. But — contracting. As if—" He stopped. He was doing the thing he did when the technical language was inadequate. "The corruption occupies space. It fills rooms, corridors, it invests the stone with its own character. What I'm reading now is — the corruption is still in the stone, but it's pulled back from this floor. Physically pulled back. Like water receding from a shore."

"What would cause that?"

"I don't know what causes it structurally," he said. "What I know is what the dungeon contains, and what I know is that the Foundation Core is here, and the Foundation Core contains the essence of what the original temple was for, and what the original temple was for was — this." He gestured at the mosaic, at the domed ceiling, at the chamber. "Asking the trial question. Letting someone stand on uncertain ground and find what they could rely on." He paused. "The corruption inverts the trials. The trials persist because the Cores preserve them. If the Core is intact—"

"The trial persists," Vor said. "Even in a corrupted dungeon."

"The trial persists," he agreed. "And if the Core is — if something is helping the Core — if someone is, I don't know, *supporting* the Core—"

She was ahead of him. She had been ahead of him since the turning chamber.

She walked to the center stone.

She had stood two feet from it on the first expedition. She had touched it with one hand. She had put it on her map. She had left.

Today she crouched beside it and laid both hands on it, and she stayed.

Cold. Dense. The Kaelith density, the ten-thousand-question weight. The same things she had felt before.

And something else.

Something that had not been there three months ago.

A direction. Slight, almost imperceptible, in the way that a tide was imperceptible if you watched the water and perceptible if you watched the shore. The Foundation resonance in the Core was not simply present. It was oriented. It was pointing at something she did not have the language to locate — not a physical direction, not north or south or up or down, but a direction in the sense that a compass needle had direction: toward something specific, something that the stone knew was there and was trying to reach.

She pressed her hands flat against the center stone and closed her eyes.

She had been thinking, for three months, about the thing she had told Renna and had not finished saying. The dungeon protects the Core. The dungeon needs the Core. What a predatory intelligence needed was what it was built around, what it had grown up around, what was at the center of everything it had constructed in three hundred years of building itself into a thing that could break people.

The corruption had been built around the Core.

The Core had been built around the original trial.

The original trial had been built around a question.

What is beneath you? What holds you up? What are you standing on?

She stood on the Kaelith Stone mosaic, in the center of a corrupted temple that was, floor by floor, in the precise pattern of a contraction pulling toward this room, beginning to remember what it was.

It had been alone with the corruption for three hundred years.

No one had mapped it. No one had placed its Core on a record. No one had gone through it level by level and charted the trial mechanisms and found in them the original questions and answered those questions honestly, not as a strategy but as a genuine response to a genuine inquiry.

She had done all of those things. Her team had done all of those things.

And the dungeon, which was a predatory intelligence that learned, had learned something that no challenger's suffering had ever taught it: that a person could walk through what it was and see what it had been.

She took her hands off the center stone. She sat on the mosaic floor with her map board on her knee and her full ink bottle at her side and she began to draw.

Not the chamber as it was. Not the chamber as she had mapped it on the first expedition, the precise documentation of the dungeon she had found.

She drew the chamber as it had been designed to be.

She had the sources for this. The Compendium's historical record. The Academy Handbook's appendices on the Gate temple construction period. The three books she had read in the Compendium archive at two in the morning when she was preparing for the first expedition and had not slept because she was trying to understand what she was going to find. She had the Lyssandrian stonework of the entry stairs, the quality of construction that had survived three hundred years of corruption intact, as evidence of what the people who built this place had cared about. She had Cadis's knowledge of the original trial structure, the full account he had given her in the second month of preparation, the Foundation Gate trial sequence from the Academy's reconstruction of pre-Fall sacred practice.

She knew what the Hollow Root had been built to be.

She drew it.

She drew the Foundation Gate temple before the Fall. The same chamber, the same dimensions, the same Kaelith Stone mosaic — because the mosaic had always been here, it was original, it was unchanged. But she drew it without the corruption. She drew the dome as it would have looked when the concentric rings were lit not by bioluminescence but by Lyssandrian Foundation resonance, the gold pulse she'd seen at the Root Cathedral residually, heated by Cinereth below, what it would have been with the actual Gate energy running through the ley lines as designed. She drew the paths through the five levels as they would have been — the labyrinth that shifted to guide rather than obstruct, the gravity variations that were training rather than weapons, the absorption chamber that asked its question in patience and released the trial-taker when the question was genuinely heard. She drew the corridor of the fifth level, the Weight Room, as it would have been: the same increasing gravity, the same door at the same impossible distance, but with the original purpose visible — not a test designed to find the point where you broke, but a test designed to find the point where you understood that you could continue past the point where you believed you couldn't, that the threshold of your endurance was further out than your body's signals mapped it.

She drew what the Core chamber was for. Not an obstacle. Not a trap. A destination, designed with care, the Kaelith Stone mosaic laid by people who believed the question the center stone asked was worth giving your most skilled craftspeople to. The question at the center of everything the Foundation Gate was.

*What are you standing on? What holds you up? And knowing that it might not hold — are you still here?*

She drew for two hours.

The others did not speak while she worked. Cadis sat at the chamber's edge and read the resonance. Renna and Solen documented. Daessen stood at the entrance arch with the particular stillness of someone who did not know what was happening but had learned that his job was to make sure the space for it was protected.

When she was done, she had seven sheets. Seven sheets of the temple as it had been, the Foundation Gate trial temple before Malachar's fall, the place that had been built here on this ley line convergence point where Foundation energy ran strongest through Arcanea's bedrock, built by people who believed that every person who descended deserved precision.

She looked at the seven sheets.

Then she made a decision.

She stood and walked to the center stone. She placed the seven sheets on the mosaic floor around it, arranged in the sequence she'd drawn them — the approach, the first level, the labyrinth levels, the turning chamber, the weight corridor, the Core chamber itself. Not pinned, not weighted: simply laid.

She stepped back.

She looked at the map she had made. The original Foundation Gate temple, drawn by its own cartographer, in its own Core chamber, three hundred years after Malachar had inverted everything it was and left it to learn how to break things.

She said, to the center stone, to the chamber, to whatever was in the Hollow Root that still remembered being holy: "Here is what you were."

---

The dungeon moved.

Not violently. Not the way she'd felt it move on the first expedition, the startled settling-in of a predatory thing that had just had its Core identified. This movement was slower. Deeper. The kind of movement you felt in the stone of a very old building when the wind changed: a response so structural it was almost subterranean, less like a reaction and more like a breath.

The Kaelith Stone mosaic's resonance changed.

She felt it through her boots before she understood it intellectually. The frequency of the Foundation-essence shifted, and the shift was not random, it was the specific shift Cadis had described from the ley lines — it was the resonance getting clearer, the way the light got cleaner at altitude. The ten-thousand-question weight of the center stone lifted slightly — not gone, never gone, the questions asked here had been asked too many times to lift entirely — but lighter. The weight of being held rather than carrying.

From the level above, she heard something she had no precedent for.

A sound from the third level. The labyrinth.

Not threat, not mechanism, not the passive creak of very old stone under load. Something — she crossed to the arch and listened — something settling. Differently. As if weight she hadn't known was loaded had been set down.

Cadis was at her side. He had his hands out from his body, the full-resonance posture. He was very still.

"The corruption is moving," he said.

"Moving where?"

He pointed down.

Below the fourth level there was no surveyed floor. The Compendium records showed a fifth level — the Weight Room — and below that, in all the accounts that existed, nothing, or the notes of previous surveyors who had not come back. She had not gone below the fourth level on the first expedition. She had not intended to go below it today.

"It's consolidating," Cadis said. "Pulling away from the upper levels and consolidating downward." He was reading it the way he read all Foundation resonance: precisely, patiently, without alarm and without excitement, just the flat accurate account of what his sense was receiving. "The upper four levels — the corruption is still there. It doesn't vanish. But its density is — lower. It's concentrated now below the fourth floor, not distributed through all of them." He paused. "The original Foundation resonance is filling the space the corruption left. Partially. The trial mechanisms — the original ones — they're activating. Partially."

She thought about this.

The corruption had not been destroyed. She had not destroyed it; she had not attempted to. She had drawn a map. She had placed seven sheets of what the dungeon was supposed to be on the floor of its Core chamber, and whatever mechanism the dungeon's intelligence used to process what was on maps — the same mechanism that had read her field records and edited her step-count, that had kept the Core off her formal drawings, that had put the step back when she'd noted she knew it was reading her — that mechanism had received the map of itself as it was meant to be.

She had given it a mirror.

Not a weapon. Not a cleans. A mirror.

The corruption was contracting to where the infection had been deepest, below the fourth floor, around whatever of Malachar's will still resided in the Hollow Root's lowest point. The trial rooms were not restored. They were not what they had been. But the corruption's density in them had decreased enough that the original character could begin to surface — the way a fire banked low could still be seen in the embers, warm and specific and waiting to be tended.

She looked at the seven sheets on the mosaic floor.

She looked at the center stone.

She made the second decision.

She took the seven sheets. She picked up each one carefully, checked the ink, confirmed that the drawings were dry and clear and complete. She stacked them in sequence. She wrote on the cover page, the first sheet, in the margin at the top:

*For the Hollow Root. From Vor Ashren, Dungeon Cartographer, Year 14.*

*This is what you were.*

*This is what you asked.*

*This is what answering honestly does.*

She laid the complete map back on the mosaic floor, at the center stone, beside the circle with the dot she had drawn here three months ago.

She stepped back.

She looked at it for a long time.

The exit took forty minutes.

The labyrinth on the third level had simplified further — not a maze now, not quite, but closer to a corridor with choices than the maze she'd navigated twice before. The absorption chamber was still and cold and the question in its stone was patient and quiet. The turning chamber asked what it asked. She walked through it with her deliberate walk and her feet on the floor and she was not falling.

The second level's inversion zone: present, its boundary at hip height, unchanged.

The antechamber: the carvings cleaner. The bioluminescent lichen, definitely brighter than the first expedition. She would note this as confirmed rather than probable in the addendum.

The left-hand spiral stair.

Thirty-two steps. She counted them on the way up.

She came out of the Ironwood descent point into the amber-filtered canopy light, into the cold air that smelled like ironwood bark and centuries.

She stood at the descent point and looked back down.

Below, in the dark, the Hollow Root had its map. A map of what it had been, before the Fall, before three hundred years of building itself into a thing that consumed what entered. A map of the questions it had been made to ask, and the gentle precision with which it had been designed to ask them, and the fact that the questions — under everything, under the corruption and the damage and the centuries of necessary cruelty that had been done to the thing that entered — the questions were still there.

A thing that had been changed beyond recognition had been given back a record of what it recognized in itself.

She did not know what would happen next. She did not know what the Hollow Root's intelligence would do with seven sheets of what it had been, laid beside the Core that contained the essence of its original purpose. She did not know if the corruption would continue to contract or would stabilize or would, in time, recover what it had lost to the mirror she'd given it.

She knew that the trial rooms on the upper floors were beginning to function. Partially. Recognizably.

She knew that the corruption on the upper floors was lower density than it had been three months ago, that it had pulled back toward whatever darkness lived in the floors below the fourth level, that the original Foundation resonance was surfacing in the space the corruption had vacated.

She knew that a predatory intelligence that had been learning for three hundred years had just learned something that nothing it had consumed before had been able to teach it.

She shouldered her map case.

"New addendum," she said, to no one in particular.

Cadis walked beside her. He said nothing. He was doing the thing he did after significant encounters with Foundation resonance: integrating, filing, settling back into himself after having been adjacent to something very large and very old. She had learned to leave him to it.

Renna was walking with her board under her arm and the expression she got when she had more questions than she had managed to ask. She would ask them tomorrow, or the day after, when the expedition was documented and they were back in Cinderwall and there was tea. There was always time for questions when you had notes and tea and the distance to look at what you'd found.

Solen was quiet in the way she was always quiet: present, attentive, the careful absorption of someone who had spent nine years learning to walk lightly through the world and had developed a philosophy from it.

Daessen had not said a word since the Core chamber. He would say something, eventually. He always did.

She walked through the Ironwood. The amber light came down in fragments through the dense canopy. The roots of the ironwood trees ran deep, as they always had, as they had for longer than any record counted. The forest did not change on a three-month timescale.

Except.

She stopped.

She looked down at the roots beneath her feet. She crouched. She looked at the specific root she was looking at — its color, its direction, its angle of growth.

Growing toward the descent point. Not much. A few inches. The same slow, almost imperceptible directional change she'd noted at the entry perimeter.

But not the same root she'd noted at the entry perimeter. This one was further from the descent point, further into the forest. A root that, if her estimation was correct, had not been in the inward-growth zone three months ago and was now at its outer edge.

The root-thinning perimeter was expanding.

She sat with this for a moment.

Then she wrote: *Root-thinning perimeter — appears expanded. Confirm measurement on next entry. Ironwood root inward-growth behavior extending further from descent point.*

She stood. She put the note away.

She walked out of the Ironwood.

Behind her, in the amber half-light, in the deep soil that was old and patient and full of things that had been growing in the dark for longer than any record existed, the roots continued their slow and deliberate movement toward something they had decided was worth moving toward.

She did not see this. She was already thinking about the next dungeon.

But she had the map.

And the map had a note in the margin, in pencil, not intended for the Compendium, added three months ago when she had first found the Core and first understood what was possible:

*They can be found.*

Below that, in the new ink she'd added today, before she left the Core chamber:

*And found, they can be given back to themselves.*
