# Chapter One: The Cartography of Wrong

The first rule Vor had learned — not from a teacher, from a dead colleague — was that you never name a dungeon before you enter it.

Names were a form of assumption. You named something according to what you expected it to be. And dungeons had been learning how to use expectations against you for three thousand years.

She called this one the Root, because that was what it was on the Compendium record. Not because she thought the name was accurate. Because it was efficient.

---

She was at the Ironwood's edge, drawing the treeline.

This was the part of her process that the rest of her team had long ago accepted as non-negotiable, the way you accepted a surgeon's insistence on washing their hands: not because you fully understood the necessity but because the surgeon had survived every operation and you hadn't. Vor drew the approach. Vor drew the descent point. Vor drew every transition, every level, every surface, as completely as she could while she was still in a position to draw with a steady hand.

Because later, sometimes, you weren't.

The Ironwood was the kind of forest you learned not to call beautiful after you'd been to it twice. The first time it looked beautiful — the ironwood trees genuinely were what their name described, trunks the color and density of hammered metal, bark that caught morning light and gave it back in fragments, canopy so dense it reduced daylight to a specific shade of blue-gray that fell like rain. The second time you knew what was beneath it, and the beauty acquired the quality of a warning.

She sketched the tree density. The way the canopy thinned in a rough circle forty meters from where the Compendium said the entrance was located. Root-thinning, which meant the dungeon's underground presence disturbed even the deep root systems of the Ironwood. She noted this with her shorthand: *R.T. — 40m perimeter.* She'd seen it before, at the Mirror Depths, though there the disturbance was more subtle: a quality to the shadows rather than the roots.

"Vor."

Daessen was standing eight feet behind her, his stance carrying the specific quality of patient readiness that made him genuinely excellent at his job and genuinely difficult to have a conversation with. He was forty, a Forge graduate, broad-faced, with the kind of stillness that came not from calm but from having made a professional decision to suppress everything but calm. She had worked with him on three jobs. He was the best fighter she'd ever contracted. He also had the conversational warmth of a load-bearing wall.

"I'm aware," she said, without looking up from the sketch.

"Sunrise was an hour ago."

"I'm aware."

"Lysse is asking when we're moving."

"When I'm done."

Lysse was the healer. Gate-Touched, two Gates open, Heart and Voice — a combination that made her exceptionally good at keeping people alive in situations that should have prevented it, and exceptionally opinionated about the methods of everyone around her. She'd asked the same question before the Mirror Depths and before the Tide Hollow and before the Crucible of Ash, and she'd also saved Vor's life twice at the Mirror Depths and once at the Crucible, so Vor had long ago categorized her impatience as a personality trait rather than a professional flaw.

The fourth team member was Cadis, who was sitting in the lower branches of the nearest Ironwood with his legs hanging down, eating dried fruit, watching the entrance point with the mild attentiveness of a man who'd chosen an interesting career and was having a fine time with it. Cadis was a Gate-specialist, Foundation-touched with a secondary Fire affinity — the former made him extraordinarily useful in a dungeon whose original function was Foundation Gate trials, the latter meant he'd been useful in four of the six jobs Vor had run before this one. He was twenty-five. He asked fewer questions than any of them and understood more than he asked.

Vor finished the treeline sketch and turned to the entrance.

The Compendium called it a descent point. In the external record it appeared as an irregularity in the Ironwood's root structure — a place where roots that should have run outward had turned downward instead, spiraling into the earth in a pattern that suggested not natural growth but architecture. Old architecture. Pre-fall architecture, which was the worst kind, because it had been designed by someone who understood what the structure was for, and that understanding had been corrupted but not erased, so what you got was a place that still remembered being holy and had forgotten what holiness required.

The root-spiral was thirty feet across at its widest point. Where the roots converged toward the center, the soil had given way — not a hole, exactly, but a threshold. The earth parted downward at an angle, like a cupped hand opening. At the bottom of the angle, in the dim morning light, you could see the first few steps.

They were carved from a single piece of deep stone. The carving was fine enough to have been done by someone who believed the person who would walk down these steps deserved precision.

"That's Lyssandrian stonework," Cadis said, dropping from the branch and coming to stand beside her. He was looking at the carving the way he always looked at the original Gate temple elements, which was with an expression she'd describe, if she were writing his professional assessment, as professional respect that he did not allow to become sentimentality.

"Foundation Gate construction style," she agreed. "Pre-Fall. The carving held."

"The carving always holds," he said. "The carving is the only part that holds."

This was true. She'd written it in the Compendium addendum after the Mirror Depths: *Original construction elements are structurally unchanged. The temple was built to last. What changed was the intention within the structure, not the structure itself.*

She opened her map case and took out a fresh sheet. Map board underneath, charcoal for roughwork, ink for the final lines. She labeled the sheet in the top-left corner the way she labeled every sheet: *Vol. 1 — The Hollow Root. Descent. Surface level. Vor Ashren, Year 14.*

Year fourteen of her career. Seventh dungeon.

She had started at twenty. The Mirror Depths at twenty, which had been a catastrophic beginning that had also established the fundamental skill of her professional life: the ability to map while the ground was trying to prevent it.

She tested the first step with the full weight of her left foot before committing.

Stone. Cold. Solid.

She went down.

---

The descent took four minutes.

The root-steps spiraled twice before they reached what the Compendium's previous surveyors — two of them, neither of whom had come back — had described as the primary antechamber. Vor noted the spiral's angle and count as she descended: thirty-two steps, seventeen-degree spiral bias, left-handed. Not architectural necessity. Design choice. Lyssandrian Foundation temples were built with left-handed spirals as a reminder that all paths that went inward eventually required you to change the hand you were relying on.

She'd read that in the Academy Handbook at sixteen. At sixteen she'd considered it poetic.

At thirty-four she considered it a warning.

The antechamber was large enough for twenty people. The ceiling was eight feet at its lowest, twelve at its central arch. The walls were the same precision-carved deep stone as the entry steps, and the carving here was more elaborate: patterns of root-and-soil that she recognized as the Foundation Gate's liturgical iconography, the symbols for *ground*, *depth*, *what remains*, *the patience of stone*. The carving was undamaged. Not a crack, not a chip. The dungeon did not damage the Foundation temple's architecture.

The dungeon used it.

The floor of the antechamber was flat and even and looked exactly like what it was: the floor of a sacred space that had been designed by skilled people who believed the floor mattered. It was, as far as her feet and her testing staff told her, solid. It felt solid. It registered as solid.

Vor uncapped the ink bottle she kept at her belt and held it horizontally over the floor, at knee height.

Then she let go.

The bottle fell down.

She noted this: *Floor — gravity standard. Antechamber.* She picked up the bottle, recapped it, and marked the position on her map.

"That's the tell," she said, for the benefit of the others, who had all descended behind her and were arrayed in their positions — Daessen at the room's single corridor entrance, Lysse at the wall, Cadis crouched beside the nearest carved panel. "Every gravity anomaly shows on the ink. If the bottle runs sideways, we're in a lateral shift zone. If it rises, we're in an inversion. If it falls in the wrong direction—"

"That can happen?" Lysse asked.

"It's happened to me twice," Vor said. "At the Mirror Depths, floor six. The ink fell toward the wall at a forty-five degree angle. That was a mixed-shift zone — floor gravity partially inverted, partially standard. The surface you were standing on was exerting pull from two directions simultaneously." She looked at the corridor. "I marked it on the map and we walked around."

"And if we can't walk around?"

"I mark it on the map and we figure something else out."

This was the philosophy, stripped to its minimum. She marked it. That was what she was for. She was not the one who knew how to fight what lived in the dungeon; that was Daessen. She was not the one who could keep Daessen alive when what lived in the dungeon had gotten close enough to leave its mark; that was Lysse. She was not the one who could read the Foundation-resonant architecture and tell them whether a floor was an original trial-space or a corruption-constructed trap; that was Cadis. She was the one who knew where they were and where they'd been and how to get back to where they'd started, which was the piece of information that, in six dungeons over fourteen years, had saved her life more than any weapon or any magic.

You could not fight your way home if you didn't know where home was.

She drew the antechamber. Quick, accurate, the lines going down with the confidence of repetition. She'd been drawing in low light and high stress for fourteen years and her hands had learned to do this without requiring her full attention, which meant her full attention could stay on the room while her hands made the record.

*Antechamber. Left-hand spiral entry, 32 steps. 8-12ft ceiling. Central arch. Original Foundation iconography, intact. Single corridor, north-facing (approximate). Gravity: standard, confirmed by ink-drop.*

She marked the ink-drop position with a small circle on the map.

"First level's always standard," Cadis said from beside the carved panel. He wasn't talking to anyone in particular. "Every Foundation dungeon that's been surveyed — the antechamber and first descent are standard. The dungeon is patient. It lets you get comfortable."

"That's cheering," Lysse said.

"It's useful," he said, without heat. "If you go into the second level expecting weirdness, you're ready for it. If you go in comfortable, you're ready for the antechamber."

Vor capped her ink. "Level two is where the rotation starts," she said. "According to the previous survey notes."

"Previous surveyors didn't come back."

"The notes did. They left them here." She patted her map case. "Survey rule. If you're not going to make it back, you leave the notes. Someone went to the trouble of getting these out."

She didn't say: *someone who knew they weren't coming back.* The room already knew.

---

The corridor to the second level was forty feet long and entirely unremarkable, which meant Vor drew it with the same precision as everything else. This was the discipline that the Mirror Depths had taught her: the boring passage between the door you'd opened and the door you were approaching was where the dungeon did its quietest work. Nothing happened in the corridor. But when you came back through it from the other side — if you came back — it was frequently not the corridor you'd walked through on the way in. Forty feet of nothing was forty feet of potential change, and potential change needed to be documented.

She took the corridor's measurements by stride-count and arm-span, noted the wall markings where they appeared — the Foundation iconography continuing here, less elaborate, the kind of carving you found in the secondary spaces of a temple, where the craftspeople had worked with skill but less ceremony — and marked the single anomaly she found: a section of wall, eight feet in, where the stone was the right color and texture but slightly too smooth. Machine-flat. Not carved. Not naturally formed.

She put her hand on it.

Cold. Solid. Flat. And wrong in the way that the Shadowfen's light was wrong, not through any specific quality she could measure but through the accumulation of small measurements that were each individually explainable and collectively impossible.

She inked a small star on her map at that position. *Texture anomaly — wall — smooth/non-original.* She would come back to it.

The second level opened ten feet below the corridor's end, down a short flight of un-spiraled stairs — also original stonework, also intact — into a chamber that was larger than the antechamber and differently shaped: not rectangular but approximate, its walls following a geometry that suggested organic growth rather than deliberate design. Root-shaped space. The chamber had grown into the shape of a root system, or the root system had grown into the shape of a chamber. At this depth, with the dungeon's corruption settled for three centuries, the distinction was probably irrelevant.

Vor stepped off the final stair and stood on the second level floor.

Cadis, immediately behind her, said: "Wait."

She waited.

"Don't move for a moment," he said. Not urgent. Careful.

She stood. Six seconds. Ten.

"Something's resonating wrong," he said. "Not the floor. The air."

She uncapped the ink and held the bottle at knee height.

The ink ran up.

Not fast. Not dramatically. A slow, steady upward pull, the ink running the inside of the bottle toward the cap rather than the floor.

She recapped it and took a step backward, up the stair.

Her foot came down on the stair in the right place and met the stair at the right angle. Her body registered nothing unusual. The pull on the ink had been above knee height. Below knee height, gravity was standard.

"Partial inversion," she said. "Upper-body zone." She stepped back down carefully and stood very still. The floor still felt like floor. The floor was still exerting normal downward pull on her feet. But from the knee up, something different was operating.

She put her hand out at waist height, palm up, and dropped a chip of charcoal from two feet above it.

The charcoal reached her palm and then continued. Upward. Slowly, like something uncertain of its own movement. It touched the ceiling fourteen feet up and rested there, a small dark mark against the stone.

Daessen said something under his breath that was not a word from any language she knew.

"Map it," Cadis said to her. "I can work with this."

"Mapping it," she said.

She mapped it. She took the measurements of the inversion boundary with the ink-bottle test, moving around the perimeter of the chamber and marking where the ink ran up and where it ran down, building the boundary's geometry on paper while Cadis stood in the center and did whatever he did with Foundation-resonance that she had never fully understood but had learned to trust absolutely.

The map grew. The boundary was irregular — not a clean horizontal line but a gradient, the inversion zone running higher at the room's center and lower at its walls, so that the effect was strongest where the room was most open and weakest near the stone. She noted this: *Inversion decreases near original stone. Resonance buffer?* A question for Cadis when there was time.

"The trials," Cadis said from the center of the room, "were about feeling the earth's truth. The trial-taker had to find the stable ground, the real ground, in a chamber where the ground was actively challenging you." He was looking at the carving on the walls — still intact, still the Foundation liturgical imagery, but here the imagery had changed. The root-and-soil symbols were upside down. Not damaged. Intentionally inverted. "Malachar inverted the trial. The earth that used to tell the truth now tells the lie. The ground that used to say 'I am here, I will hold you' — it says that, and it's wrong."

"Lovely," Lysse said.

"It's precise," Cadis said. "That's what makes it terrible. He didn't just corrupt it. He understood it well enough to invert it specifically." He looked at Vor. "The people who died here weren't killed. They were confused. They walked on the ceiling thinking it was the floor and then — when the gravity shifted — they fell up."

"Fell up," Daessen repeated.

"Ceiling is thirty feet. The fall would have been survivable if you hit it the right way." He looked up at the ceiling. The charcoal chip was still there, resting against the stone. "But the ceiling has traps too. And if you hit it wrong — or if you hit it having already been damaged—"

"Understood," Vor said.

She walked the chamber's perimeter with her staff down, testing the floor with each step. Standard gravity at floor level throughout. She took the full measurement. She drew the map. She marked the inversion zone, the boundary gradient, the inverted iconography, the ceiling height, the position of the charcoal chip, a note about the resonance buffer effect near original stone.

When she was done, she stood at the center of the chamber where Cadis was standing, in the zone where the inversion was strongest, and uncapped the ink.

The ink ran up.

She stood with the upside-down bottle and looked at the room around her: the carved walls with their inverted roots, the ceiling where a chip of charcoal was resting, the perfectly solid floor, the corridor back to the antechamber, the arch ahead of them into the third level, where the map from the previous surveyors' notes said things became more complex.

"Mark the entry point," she said. "Every team member, a mark on the wall at the stair entrance. Something you'll recognize."

"Why?" Lysse asked.

"Because I want to know if the marks move."

There was a pause.

"The dungeon rearranges itself," Daessen said.

"It has the capability. In theory. I want evidence before I plan for it, but I want evidence early." She put her own mark — a small careful V, the same mark she put on every dungeon wall she'd walked, in every dungeon for fourteen years — at eye level on the stone beside the entry arch. "Leave your marks where you can see them from the center of the room."

They left their marks. They moved forward.

---

The third level was a place where the ceiling and the floor were the same height.

Not a contradiction: the level had no vertical space. Or rather, the vertical space it had was populated entirely by stone, so that moving through it meant moving through passages barely wide enough for one person at a time, passages that turned and doubled back and opened into small chambers that were themselves barely large enough to stand in. Vor had read this description in the previous surveyors' notes and had not fully understood it until she was in it.

The Foundation trial — original, pre-Fall — had been a labyrinth. The trial-taker had to find solid ground when the labyrinth shifted. The corridors had rearranged themselves, and the trial-taker had to read the earth — the vibrations, the resonance, the language of stone — to find the path.

The corruption had left the labyrinth but removed the language.

The stone was there. The passages were there. The small chambers were there. But the earth-language that had guided trial-takers through, the resonance that told you *this way, not that way, this ground is stable, that passage leads back* — that was gone. The dungeon had eaten it. What remained was the physical maze and the silence where the guide had been.

Daessen moved first, because the passages required single file and Daessen at the front was the configuration Vor had used on every job.

She was second. She held her staff in her left hand and her map in her right, building the labyrinth's layout with each turn. She made marks on the passages with her heels — small scuffs, low on the wall, at every decision point — and noted each mark on the map with a corresponding symbol.

"Third turn, left," she said.

"Left," Daessen confirmed, and turned.

"Second chamber. Dead end?"

"Dead end," he said. She felt him stop ahead of her in the passage and she stopped too, her shoulder against the cold stone.

She noted the dead end. Added it to the map. Felt the labyrinth around her with the particular sense she'd developed over fourteen years, not magical — she had no Gates, had never opened one, had never wanted to — but experiential. The sense of a space that was organized rather than random. A space with memory.

"Back," she said.

"Back," he confirmed.

They were at the fourth junction when Lysse's voice came from behind her, and the quality of the voice was the specific quality that made Vor's hand tighten on her staff.

"Where's Cadis?"

She turned. In the narrow passage, turning meant shuffling — no room for a full turn, she had to press against the wall and slide. Lysse was there. Behind Lysse was wall.

"He was behind you," Vor said.

"He was behind me," Lysse said. "I heard him at the third junction. I felt him pass the second chamber."

"How long ago?"

"I don't—" Lysse's voice did something that Vor recognized as Lysse's version of controlling fear, which was the same as everyone else's version but compressed and professional. "Two minutes, maybe. I was watching the walls, I wasn't—"

"Which junction?" Vor asked.

"I don't know. I didn't—"

"From the beginning," Vor said. She was already moving back the way they'd come, pressing past Lysse in the passage. "Every choice you remember. First junction?"

"Left."

"Second?"

"Straight."

"That's wrong," Vor said. "We went right at the second." She stopped. She was at the second junction. She could see, in the dim bioluminescence the dungeon provided — a faint green-silver glow that emanated from no visible source — the low scuff marks she'd left on the walls. The correct passage had her mark. The passage Lysse was pointing to had nothing.

"Gravity fold," Daessen said, from behind her.

She looked at the unmarked passage. At the junction point, where the two passages met, there was a seam in the floor — a hairline crack where two stone slabs met. She crouched and held her ink bottle at floor level.

The ink ran sideways.

Not up. Not down. Sideways, along the crack, toward the unmarked passage.

A lateral shift. A pull. It would not have registered as a stumble — just a slight sense of being drawn one way, the kind of thing you'd attribute to your own movement, your own choice.

"He was pulled," she said. "He took the lateral pull for his own step."

"Can we go after him?" Lysse asked.

"We already are," Vor said, and moved into the unmarked passage.

---

The passage bent twice and opened into a chamber.

Cadis was in the wall.

Not inside the wall — not inside stone, the way Vor had seen in older records, the kinds of accounts she'd read late at night in the Compendium archive that she had categorized as probably exaggerated until this moment. He was part of the wall's surface: standing upright, or what had been standing upright, arms slightly away from his sides, feet together, the profile of a man embedded in stone as if the stone had been cast around him like a mold.

His face was uncovered. His eyes were closed.

His chest was moving.

Vor stood in the entrance of the chamber and watched his chest move for a full ten seconds before she allowed herself to do anything else.

She drew the chamber. She drew his position. She noted the wall's composition — original Foundation stone, the same precision carving, intact. She noted the depth of his embedding — his feet and the backs of his legs were gone into the stone, his hands and forearms, his shoulder blades. His face and the front surface of his body were exposed.

His chest was moving.

She walked to him and put her hand against the stone on either side of his head.

The stone was warm.

Not warm like stone in sunlight. Warm like a living thing in rest. The specific warmth of a body that had not lost its heat yet, maintained from within rather than acquired from without.

"Cadis," she said.

His eyes opened.

Not slowly, not groggily — immediately, fully, the way eyes opened when the person behind them had not been asleep but had been waiting.

"The Root asked me what I was standing on," he said.

His voice was entirely normal. Calm. The Foundation-specialist's tone, the one she'd heard from him across six jobs and twelve descents and more near-misses than she wanted to count: careful, observational, interested.

"What did you answer?" she asked.

"I didn't." He paused. "I didn't know."

She looked at the stone around him. At the embedding. At the warmth under her hands.

"The dungeon asked you what the trial asked," she said.

"Yes."

"And when you couldn't answer—"

"The trial suspended. The stone thinks I'm still in it." He looked at the wall beside him, at the stone that held his shoulder. "It's not hostile. It's just — patient. Waiting for an answer."

"What happens if you answer?"

"I don't know."

"What answers have you tried?"

"I tried 'stone.' I tried 'earth.' I tried 'the Foundation Gate's domain.'" He was silent for a moment. "Those are all correct answers to what I'm standing on. But they're Academy answers. Library answers."

Vor took her hand off the stone. She looked at the chamber — at the walls, at the floor, at the ceiling where the gravity was almost certainly wrong, at the precise carved imagery of Foundation Gate liturgy that surrounded them on every side. At the original temple that had been designed to teach this exact lesson, the lesson the trial was for: feel the earth's truth. Find solid ground when everything shifts. Know what you're standing on not by category but by certainty.

"Don't answer yet," she said.

"What?"

She uncapped her ink. Held it over the floor. The ink ran sideways — a stronger lateral field than the junction, directed toward the wall where Cadis was embedded.

She put the cap back on and reached into her map case for the piece of charcoal she kept there. She looked at Cadis and pressed it into his free hand, the one not embedded in the stone.

"When I tell you," she said.

She went back to the chamber entrance and marked it on her map: *DO NOT ANSWER.*

Below that, smaller, in the shorthand she'd developed over fourteen years for notes she intended to expand later: *trial mechanism active — Q: what is beneath you? — wrong answer: library. Correct answer: unknown. Must be experiential not categorical.*

She looked at the note for a moment.

Below it she wrote: *Do not stand still long enough to be asked.*

"Lysse," she said. "Daessen." They were in the passage behind her, and she heard them adjust their positions without being told — Daessen closing the distance, Lysse pulling out her kit. "I need the two of you to take his arms when he comes free. Don't touch the wall."

"What are you doing?" Lysse asked.

"I'm going to give the stone something else to think about." She moved to the wall beside Cadis, to the stone that held his right arm. She put her hand against it. Felt the warmth. Felt the deep, patient, sustained pulse of something that had been waiting for three centuries for someone to give it an answer. "Cadis. Tell it you don't know."

"I—" He stopped. "That's not—"

"The correct answer," she said. "I know. But 'I don't know' is not the same as a wrong answer. 'I don't know' is honest. The original trial was about finding the ground's truth. The honest answer to a question you can't answer is that you can't answer it. Tell it that. Exactly that."

A silence.

"I don't know," Cadis said. Not to her. To the stone.

The warmth under her hand shifted — not decreased, changed. The quality of patient waiting became something else, something she didn't have a word for: the quality of a question being heard rather than answered. Of a question finding its shape changed by someone acknowledging it rather than resolving it.

The stone released him.

Not violently. The stone released him the way water released something that had been submerged and had come back to its own buoyancy — a slow, complete separation, Cadis stepping forward off the wall's surface as if stepping out of a frame, his feet coming fully free, his arms dropping to his sides.

Lysse caught his right arm. Daessen caught his left.

Cadis stood in the chamber for a moment, breathing carefully, looking at the wall where he'd been.

Then he held up the charcoal she'd given him and looked at it. He'd been holding it the whole time.

"I didn't need this," he said.

"I know," she said. "I needed you to have something in your hand. Something that wasn't the wall."

She took it back and put it in her pocket. She would not throw it away — the charcoal had been in a room where the trial mechanism was active, and it had been in contact with someone who'd been partially absorbed. She didn't know what that meant. She would find out, later, when there was time to find out.

She made a note on the map: *charcoal sample — contact with absorption mechanism.*

She labeled the chamber fully: its dimensions, the embedding position, the lateral gravity field, the temperature of the stone, the mechanism description, the resolution.

She did not note whether the resolution would work a second time. She suspected it wouldn't. Dungeons learned.

She noted that too.

---

They camped at a stable junction — a four-way intersection where her ink-drops fell down in all four passages, where the wall temperature was standard, where Cadis confirmed the Foundation resonance was quiet. Not absent: quiet. The difference mattered. Absent resonance meant the dungeon had consumed it entirely. Quiet resonance meant the original temple's character was still present, suppressed, co-existing with the corruption in the way that two notes sounded simultaneously could each retain their own character if the ear was trained to separate them.

Cadis, in the two hours after his extraction, had become systematically quieter. She noted this without commenting on it. He would say what he needed to say when he had assembled it.

She worked on her map.

The map was extensive now — twelve surfaces of notation, the antechamber and the descent stairs and the gravity anomaly of the second level and the full labyrinth of the third level, as far as she'd charted it. She worked under the bioluminescent glow the dungeon provided, which was adequate for mapmaking if you positioned yourself correctly, and she worked with the focused attention that had been her primary professional skill for fourteen years, the ability to put everything that was not the paper out of her attention without losing awareness of the room.

She was inking the absorption chamber — drawing Cadis's position, the embedding geometry, the wall composition — when she noticed.

The antechamber entry. On the map. The first lines she'd drawn, up in the top left corner of the first sheet.

She'd drawn the entry stair at thirty-two steps. She remembered counting them as she descended. She remembered writing *32* in her shorthand at the margin.

The lines showed thirty-one.

She stared at the drawing.

She had not erased anything. Charcoal could be erased; she used charcoal for roughwork and ink for finals, and the entry stairs were in ink because she'd considered them final, because they were original stonework, because original stonework did not change.

Thirty-one steps in the drawing. Thirty-two in her memory.

She turned back to the entry page — she kept entry pages on top, so they were always accessible — and looked at the number she'd written in the margin. The shorthand note.

*32 steps.*

The note said thirty-two. The drawing showed thirty-one.

Her hand did not shake. She had trained it not to, specifically for moments like this one — moments when information came in that the nervous system responded to before the mind had finished evaluating it. Her hand was steady. The pen moved carefully to the bottom of the sheet, to the notes section, and she wrote: *Entry stair: note says 32, drawing shows 31. Discrepancy found at camp. Possible: error in drawing. Possible: dungeon has altered the number of steps. Test on exit.*

She looked at the note. Added: *Drawing altered, not note. Dungeon is reading the map.*

Then she crossed out that last sentence and wrote instead: *Dungeon may be able to alter map drawings. Ink-fixed drawings are not permanent record if subject to spatial field manipulation.*

She closed the map case.

She sat in the stable junction, in the quiet resonance of the original temple that was still present despite everything done to it, and she looked at the passages going out in four directions.

The dungeon knew she was mapping it. Or it didn't know — it had no consciousness in the way she understood consciousness — but its predatory instinct, the thing that had been learning for three hundred years how to use challengers against themselves, had found her specific method and begun to respond to it. She was a new kind of challenger. She didn't fight her way through and die. She drew the walls and came back. And the dungeon, which learned, had learned something new.

She opened the map case again and looked at the entry stair drawing for a long time.

Then she drew a new stair, on a fresh sheet, from memory alone. No measurements. No methodical stride-count. Just the shape of the descent as she remembered it, the curve of the left-hand spiral, the particular quality of the final six steps where the carving was finest, the dark opening at the bottom where the antechamber began.

She counted the steps in the drawing.

Thirty-two.

Memory drew thirty-two. The map showed thirty-one.

The dungeon had changed one drawing and left the other unchanged, which meant it was responding to the ink — to the formal record — and not to her mind.

She looked at that for a long time.

Then she took a blank sheet and wrote at the top: *FOR DUNGEON.*

Below that, she wrote: *I know you're reading this.*

She sat with the pen over the paper for a moment and then wrote the only honest thing she had to say to a three-hundred-year-old corrupted temple that had just demonstrated that it could alter her formal record:

*I'm going to finish the map anyway.*

She left the note on the floor of the stable junction, weighted by a stone, and went to sleep.

The dungeon made no alteration to that sheet.

In the morning, when she woke, she counted the steps in the original drawing again.

Thirty-two.

The dungeon had put the step back.

She noted this without comment, re-inked the date beside the drawing, and continued.
