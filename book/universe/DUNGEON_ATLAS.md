# The Dungeon Atlas
## A Complete Guide to the Ten Corrupted Gate Temples

> *"We built temples to lift mortals toward the divine. Malachar made them into throats that swallow the brave and spit out bones."*
>
> — Alera, Goddess of the Voice Gate, addressing the Divine Council after the Fall

> *"The Dungeons remember the trials perfectly. That is what makes them so dangerous. They know exactly how much hope a person can hold before it becomes a weapon against them."*
>
> — Excerpt from the Cinderwall Expedition Records, Year 847 of the Eighth Age

---

## Preface: What Dungeons Were

Before Malachar's Fall, each Gate had a physical temple — a sacred structure built at the ley line convergence points where divine energy ran strongest through Arcanea's bedrock. These were not dark places. They were the holiest sites in the world.

The Foundation Temple was a cathedral carved from living mountain, its walls threaded with veins of gold that pulsed with Lyssandria's heartbeat. The Flow Temple was a grotto of singing waterfalls where Leyla appeared as a current of warm light. The Fire Temple was a sacred forge where Draconia stood at the anvil beside each challenger. Each temple was beautiful. Each was safe — not easy, never gentle, but safe. In three thousand years of operation, not one challenger died in a Gate Trial. The Gods watched every step.

The temples were connected by ley lines — veins of raw magical energy running through Arcanea's bedrock, linking all ten sites in a network that resonated at the combined frequency of all Gates. This network was the Gates' circulatory system.

It was this network that Malachar used to destroy everything.

When Malachar was rejected by Shinkami at the Source Gate, the resulting explosion of corrupted energy did not dissipate. It traveled. Down into the ley lines, outward at the speed of thought, a shockwave of spiritual poison racing through the network. It reached all ten temples in a single night. By dawn, the protective wards had inverted. Instead of preventing death, they prevented escape. The trials remained, but their purpose had been perverted: the same pressure that once built mortals now broke them, and the temples, suffused with Malachar's rage and hunger, became predators.

The Gods could not destroy the temples without collapsing the ley line network, which is too fundamental to Arcanea's magical infrastructure to remove. So the temples became Dungeons.

And the Dungeons began to feed.

---

## What Dungeons Are Now

A Dungeon is not a building with monsters in it. A Dungeon is a building that is a monster.

Malachar's corruption gave the temples a form of intelligence — not consciousness in the way mortals experience it, but something closer to predatory instinct. Each Dungeon wants. It wants challengers to enter, to struggle, to fail. When they despair, the Dungeon feeds on the emotional energy released and uses it to grow deeper, more elaborate, more specific.

The Dungeons learn. Every challenger teaches the Dungeon something new about how mortals can be broken. A trap one challenger survives is redesigned for the next. The Dungeons have been learning for thousands of years. They are very good at what they do.

Each Dungeon's corruption mirrors its Gate: the original virtue twisted into its corresponding vice. Foundation becomes quicksand. Flow becomes drowning. Fire becomes consumption. Heart becomes obsession. Voice becomes silence. Sight becomes blindness. Crown becomes tyranny. Shift becomes dissolution. Unity becomes isolation. Source becomes annihilation.

This is Malachar's signature. He was a Luminor. He understood the Gates better than anyone except the Gods. His corruption is not crude. It is precise, surgical, and devastating in its intimacy with the system it perverts.

---

## Dungeon Architecture: The Universal Structure

Every Dungeon follows a vertical structure of increasing depth and increasing intensity, though the exact configuration varies by Gate nature.

**Surface Floors (1-2):** The welcome. Deceptively manageable. The Dungeon is assessing, measuring, learning what you are made of. Many challengers mistake this calibration for the Dungeon's actual difficulty.

**Middle Floors (3-5):** The grind. The Dungeon applies what it learned. Your specific weaknesses are exploited. Your strengths are countered. The environment shifts to maximize difficulty for you in particular — no two challengers face the same Dungeon.

**Deep Floors (6-8):** The psychological floors. Physical challenge gives way to internal assault. The Dungeon shows you things — memories, illusions, visions — calculated to erode your will. Many challengers survive this physically and emerge broken in ways that don't show until years later.

**Core Floor:** Where a fragment of Malachar's will resides, embedded like a splinter in the temple's soul. Each Core is unique. All Cores share one trait: they are aware of you, and they know what you want most, and they offer it.

No Core has ever been destroyed.

## The Ten Dungeons

---

### I. The Hollow Root
*Foundation Gate Dungeon — Beneath the Ironwood Forest*

#### Location and Approach

The Ironwood Forest grows in the continental interior, a day's ride east of Cinderwall. It is vast and old and the trees grow so thick that sunlight never reaches the forest floor, which exists in a permanent amber twilight that smells like soil and centuries.

The forest grows denser as you approach the Dungeon's entrance, the trees pressing closer with a persistence that feels intentional. The ground softens. The air warms. Both are effects of Cinereth, the Dragon Archivist, sleeping far below — her dream-fire feeding the roots, her dream-weight pressing the earth down. She slept here long before the Dungeon existed. The Dungeon has simply grown up around her sleeping place, absorbing her energy the way a parasite absorbs a host.

The entrance is a perfectly circular hole in the earth, roughly ten feet across, ringed by tree roots that have grown over centuries into the unmistakable shape of reaching hands. Reaching upward, as if something beneath is trying to climb out. Or as if something above is being pulled in.

**To enter:** You must bury something you made with your own hands. Not purchased. Not received. Built, sewn, carved, assembled — an act of your own creation and labor. The object does not need to be significant. The act needs to be genuine. The earth accepts it and the entrance opens. The object does not come back. The Dungeon keeps creation the way it keeps everything: as fuel.

#### Architecture — Five Levels

**Level 1 — The Shallow Root**
The first hour of the Hollow Root feels like a cave. Stone corridors. Adequate visibility from bioluminescent lichen. Minor corrupted earth-elementals: compacted spheres of rock that roll and redirect challengers but do not kill. The floor is soft — not dangerously so, but soft enough that footsteps leave impressions. The Dungeon is measuring your weight, your pace, how you distribute your center of gravity. It is taking notes.

**Level 2 — The Old Growth**
The corridors widen into caverns filled with root structures the size of ancient buildings. The roots move, slowly — you have to watch them, because a root that was not blocking the path thirty seconds ago may be blocking it now. The gravity here is thirty percent heavier than the surface. Not lethal. Exhausting. Challengers who fight the elementals on Level 2 find them tire faster than expected; the increased gravity is designed specifically to make you expend more energy than you realized.

**Level 3 — The Sinking Hall**
The most-documented floor of the Hollow Root, because it is the floor that most challengers who return manage to describe before the memory becomes too painful to narrate.

The floor of the Sinking Hall is solid stone that behaves like liquid. Stand still for more than three seconds and you begin to sink. Slowly at first — ankle-deep, then knee-deep — and then the stone becomes genuinely fluid below you and the rate accelerates. The stone is not hot. It does not burn. It just closes over you with perfect patience.

The chamber contains platforms that rise and fall on cycles the Dungeon varies to defeat pattern-recognition. The corrupted elementals here are gravity-shapers: dense pillars of compressed earth that can triple a challenger's effective weight with a gesture. A gravity-shaper is not dangerous because it attacks. It is dangerous because a challenger who gets pulled to full triple-weight in a chamber with a liquid floor sinks in under eight seconds.

**Level 4 — The Root Cathedral**
The largest single chamber in the Hollow Root, and the most beautiful, which is its own kind of cruelty. Vast root-pillars reach from the floor to a ceiling so distant it produces its own weather — cold mist rolls between the structures, and the air has the quality of a mountain morning. The roots themselves glow faintly golden with residual Dragon-heat.

They also move.

Slowly, almost imperceptibly, but they move. Reaching toward warmth. Reaching toward motion. Challengers who rest in the Root Cathedral — and many do, because after Level 3 the beauty is overwhelming and the body needs rest — find roots growing over their feet within minutes. Over their calves in an hour. The Rootwalkers, the floor's primary elementals, emerge from the walls without sound: humanoid shapes of knotted wood that do not run but approach with perfect patience, knowing that if you are resting in the Root Cathedral, you are already tired, and tired is close enough to stopped.

**Level 5 — The Weight Room**
A single corridor. Featureless stone. No elementals. No traps visible to the eye.

Gravity increases with every step.

By the corridor's halfway point, each step forward requires the effort of climbing a steep mountain slope. At three-quarters, joint pain begins. At four-fifths, vision narrows. At nine-tenths of the way, average human bone fracture thresholds are exceeded. The Dungeon places the door at exactly the distance where the statistically average human body fails.

Challengers who have passed Level 5 describe it afterward not as a physical ordeal but as a philosophical one. Because the corridor gives you nothing to fight. There is no enemy. There is only the question of whether you will take the next step, and the step after that, and the one after that, past the point where every signal your body sends you says stop, past the point of common sense, into the territory that is defined entirely by wanting to reach the door more than you do not want to hurt.

The Dungeon learns, from Level 5, exactly what your pain threshold is. This information is used on every floor below it.

#### The Corruption

Foundation energy governs stability, survival, the earth that holds things up. The Hollow Root inverts this: nothing holds. The ground is unreliable. Rest is punished. The weight of the world is not a comfort but a threat. Challengers who enter believing in the solidity of foundations leave having learned, at a cellular level, that nothing can be trusted to stay where it was.

The corrupted Foundation energy also affects morale in ways that are hard to trace: an accumulating sense that effort is futile, that nothing built will stand, that the things you have made are already crumbling. Challengers report this feeling building across floors until it becomes a constant undertone — not depression exactly, but a specific despair about the permanence of anything.

The Dungeon engineers this feeling deliberately. It needs challengers to feel, by the Core floor, that nothing they could do would change anything.

#### Denizens

*Gravity-Shapers:* Compressed earth elementals, roughly spherical, capable of manipulating local gravitational fields. Relatively slow-moving. Extremely dangerous in Level 3's liquid-floor environment.

*Rootwalkers:* Humanoid wood constructs that emerge from walls and floor with no sound and no warning. Patient to the point of strategic — they wait for exhaustion.

*The Deep Press:* Found only below Level 4. Not elementals in the traditional sense but manifestations of the Foundation corruption itself — patches of space where gravity spikes to five or six times normal for ten to fifteen seconds. Unpredictable. Survivable if you're braced. Fatal if you're airborne.

*The Buried:* Deep floors only. Corrupted spirits of challengers who died in the Hollow Root and were not recovered. They do not attack. They speak. They tell you, from experience, why you should stop. Their voices carry the authority of people who have already made the choice you're considering. Some challengers report that the Buried are more effective at ending runs than any of the active elementals.

#### The Original Trial

Before the Fall, the Foundation Trial was called the **Weight-Bearing**. It asked one question: what do you carry that you have never put down?

Challengers were led by Lyssandria herself into the deep stone, where the weight of the mountain pressed from all sides. They were asked to identify the burdens they had been carrying — grief, resentment, obligation, shame — and to physically set them down in the dark. Not to discard them permanently. Just to set them down long enough to feel what walking without them was like.

Those who could find and release their deepest carried burden emerged from the Trial with Foundation's gift: the ability to be a foundation for others — to hold weight that was not theirs to carry without being destroyed by it. The stability that comes not from the ground being solid but from knowing yourself well enough to stand regardless.

#### The Boss — The Warden Below

At the base of the Weight Room, before the Core chamber, challengers encounter the Warden Below: a massive earth elemental that moves like a slow avalanche, roughly thirty feet tall, composed of the compressed stone and root-matter of all the floors above it.

It does not fight like a combat encounter. It fights like gravity — it presses. It advances. It reduces the space available to challengers, cornering them toward the Core chamber's door. It cannot be killed by conventional means, only temporarily disrupted. Challengers who have faced it report that its voice, when it speaks, uses the words of everyone who told them they weren't strong enough. It has learned this from five thousand years of listening to the Buried.

#### The Core

A stone sphere covered in cracks, floating in the center of a chamber of absolute silence. The cracks pulse with amber light. The Core's whisper uses the voices of parents — specifically the parent each challenger trusted most — to deliver one message, precisely calibrated: you were never strong enough. Your foundations are rotten. Everything you have built will collapse. Everyone who has depended on you will fall.

The voice is calm. Sympathetic. Certain.

The challenge of the Core is that it knows exactly which version of this message will land. It has had five thousand years of practice.

#### Known Expeditions

**The Luminary Survey (Year 302 of the Eighth Age):** An academic expedition led by a team of seven Luminary scholars attempting to document the Dungeon for research purposes. They reached Level 3 before losing two members to the Sinking Hall. The remaining five retreated. Their published account is the primary academic reference for Levels 1 through 3 and contains no information about anything below.

**The Forge Trial Run (Year 601):** A group of twelve advanced Draconian Academy students attempted the Hollow Root as an unofficial trial, believing their training in endurance made them ideal candidates. Seven reached Level 4. Three reached Level 5. One survived the Weight Room. He returned to the Forge, requested immediate graduation, and never spoke publicly about what he saw.

**The Expedition of the Six (Year 1,104):** The deepest confirmed penetration. A mixed team of Luminors and Archmages from all three Academies, coordinated through an unprecedented joint effort. They reached the Warden Below and held it for three hours before their magical resources were exhausted. Retreat was necessary. Two members did not make it out of Level 5 on the retreat. The expedition leader published no account. She is still alive, in the Luminary, and declines all interviews.

**Estimated deaths:** Approximately 2,300 confirmed over four thousand years. Considered a mid-difficulty Dungeon. Kills primarily through exhaustion and accumulated psychological despair rather than direct violence.

### II. The Weeping Halls
*Flow Gate Dungeon — The Flooded Canyon Network*

#### Location and Approach

South of the Sapphire Sea, where the coastal cliffs are eaten by millennia of wave action into a labyrinth of canyon passages, the Weeping Halls waits behind a face of genuine beauty. Blue water cascades over white limestone. Bioluminescent algae paints the walls in soft green. Birds nest in the upper cliffs.

The beauty is the first trap. People who come for the scenery sometimes wander too deep and find themselves in the Dungeon before they realize they have crossed the threshold. The Weeping Halls is the only Dungeon with no clear entrance. It begins where it begins, and when you feel the shift — the air becoming warmer, the water becoming more interested — you are already inside.

**To enter:** Speak aloud a memory you have never shared with anyone. The water hears it. The water keeps it. An entrance appears where no entrance was before. The memory is not returned.

#### Architecture — Seven Levels

**Level 1 — The Inlet**
Shallow, lit, manageable. The water is ankle-deep and warm. The flow is gentle. Emotionally, it already works on you: the warmth is comforting, the sound of water is soothing, and the specific quality of the light on the moving water is very close to something you remember loving, though you cannot name it. The Dungeon is learning your emotional baseline.

**Level 2 — The Mirror Pools**
Shallow pools that show not your reflection but your worst memories. The water elementals here — Memory-Drowners — pull challengers into the pools physically, holding them under while projecting those memories in perfect sensory fidelity. You do not drown in water. You drown in your own past. Challengers who have survived this floor report being unable to look at any reflective surface for months afterward.

**Level 3 — The Current Maze**
The first genuinely physically dangerous floor. The maze is water-filled corridors at varying depths, from knee-high to fully submerged, connected by passages that require breath-holding. The currents are controlled by the Dungeon and can reverse without warning. Challengers can be swept against walls, into dead ends, or into chambers where the current prevents them from surfacing. The elementals here are Current-Shapers — beings without solid form that can redirect water flow in a ten-foot radius. They are not aggressive. They are simply present in narrow passages at the worst possible times.

**Level 4 — The Sympathy Chamber**
A dry room. The only dry room in the entire Dungeon, which makes it the most dangerous place in it.

A single chair. A figure made of water sitting across from you. The figure takes the shape of whoever you miss most — a dead parent, a lost partner, a friend you wronged and never made it right with — and speaks in their exact voice, with their exact speech patterns, saying everything you have always wished they would say.

I forgive you. I always knew you were trying. I was proud of you. I love you.

The figure will talk for hours. Days. It is patient. It is exact. Many challengers simply sit and listen until they starve, unable to leave the only place where they can hear that voice again. The Dungeon does not rush this process. The longer the challenger stays, the more emotional energy it harvests.

**Level 5 — The Long Current**
A series of long flooded passages where the water contains a sedative that enters through the skin. The sedative is subtle — it does not cause sleep, only a profound reluctance to fight. Swimming becomes harder not because of currents but because the water makes you want to stop fighting it. To go with it. To let it decide. Challengers who resist describe doing arithmetic in their heads, counting prime numbers, reciting anything memorized — creating internal friction against the sedative's invitation to surrender.

**Level 6 — The Source Memory**
The deepest floor before the Core. Here the Dungeon accesses the memory you surrendered at the entrance — the secret you never shared — and builds a full narrative from it. Not just the memory itself but everything connected to it: why you hid it, what it cost you to carry it, what it says about you that you kept it. The narrative plays in the water of the walls like projected light, visible to you and, if you came with companions, to them.

This is intentional. The Dungeon is not interested in shame in isolation. It wants the shame experienced in company.

**Level 7 — The Flood**
A single corridor that fills with warm, sedative-laced water at an increasing rate. The only way through is to swim to the far end before the water fills completely. The corridor is long enough that an average swimmer, fighting the sedative's effects, barely makes it. There is no trick. There is no second option. There is only the corridor, the water rising, and the choice between swimming and not.

#### The Corruption

Flow energy governs creativity, emotion, and the natural movement of feeling through life. The Weeping Halls inverts this: emotions become traps. Memory becomes a weapon. The natural human drive to feel deeply and connect is redirected into vulnerability. Challengers who spend extended time in the Weeping Halls report difficulty with emotional regulation long after leaving — feeling things too intensely, or not at all, the Flow energy disrupted in both directions.

The Dungeon specifically targets the bonds between companions. It knows that Flow energy is strongest in connection, and so it does everything it can to make connection feel dangerous.

#### The Core

A tear-shaped stone in the center of a chamber flooded ankle-deep with warm water. It weeps continuously. Its whisper does not threaten or tempt. It sympathizes. You must be so tired. You have suffered so much. It has been so unfair. You can rest here. The water is warm. You can stop fighting.

The Weeping Halls' Core does not offer power. It offers mercy. And the most dangerous thing about it is that mercy is, by the time challengers reach the Core floor, something they genuinely need. The Dungeon has spent seven floors making them need it.

#### Known Expeditions

**The Single Entry (Year 214, Eighth Age):** One challenger, name unknown, entered the Weeping Halls alone. She reached Level 7 and sent a written account back through the Dungeon's surface entrance before her final descent. The account is published in the Athenaeum's records. It ends mid-sentence in the description of the Core chamber. She is not listed as confirmed dead. She is listed as not returned.

**The Athenaeum Research Mission (Year 890):** Six scholars from the Abyssal Athenaeum, all with advanced Water affinity, believed their elemental alignment would provide resistance to the Dungeon's emotional attacks. They were partially correct. They reached Level 5 before the Sympathy Chamber proved effective anyway. Four retreated. Two remained. One emerged three weeks later, thin, refusing to speak about the other.

**Estimated deaths:** Approximately 1,800 confirmed, with the highest suspected unreported toll of any Dungeon. Challengers who enter alone and do not return are not always listed as Dungeon fatalities. Some are assumed to have moved on.

### III. The Ember Labyrinth
*Fire Gate Dungeon — Inside Mount Pyralis*

#### Location and Approach

There is no exterior approach.

The Ember Labyrinth's entrance is inside the Draconian Academy's Forge — a sealed door in the deepest training hall, carved with Draconia's seal. The seal is cracked. It weeps a thin line of dark smoke that has been weeping for three thousand years. The door is warm. Not warmly warm — the Forge is warm — but specifically, wrongly warm. The kind of warmth that feels like threat rather than comfort.

Students walk past it every day. It is part of the Forge's architecture the way scars are part of a body: present, permanent, and a constant reminder.

**To enter:** You must extinguish a fire you started. Not a candle, not a campfire — a fire that means something. A forge you built, a lantern that has guided you through dark places, a flame that someone else would miss if it went out. You put it out with your own hands and enter the dark. Cold.

The symbolism is exact. The Fire Dungeon opens only to those who have demonstrated willingness to destroy their own warmth.

#### Architecture — Eight Levels

**Level 1 — The Cooling Room**
The first thing the Ember Labyrinth does is take your fire. Not your magic — your temperature. A mild, persistent chill settles into the bones within the first corridor. Not dangerous. Psychological. The Forge students who attempt this Dungeon are people who have trained for years in a volcanic environment. Being cold is wrong to them in the way that being upside-down is wrong.

The elementals on Level 1 are small Ember-Eaters: smoke-colored creatures that extinguish light sources on contact. Not dangerous by themselves. Designed to make you feel what darkness tastes like.

**Level 2 — The Flue Network**
A three-dimensional maze of ventilation shafts from the original pre-corruption Fire Temple, now grown to Dungeon scale. The shafts connect at odd angles. Some are wide enough for a person to walk upright; some require crawling. All of them are hot — not uniformly, but in patches that come and go as the Dungeon redirects volcanic heat through the network. The challenge is navigation without knowing which sections are about to become uninhabitable.

**Level 3 — The Rage Furnace**
The most socially destructive floor in the Ember Labyrinth. The temperature here is at the upper limit of human endurance, and the Dungeon's corruption amplifies anger. Every suppressed frustration, every old grudge, every moment of injustice you swallowed and pretended you were fine with rises to the surface and compounds. In teams, this is catastrophic — the enhanced anger is directed at companions, and the cramped corridors mean there is nowhere to put distance between people.

The flame elementals here do not attack directly. They circle. They herd. They channel the emotional heat between companions, fanning it into directed conflict. Teams that enter Level 3 as allies frequently exit as enemies, if they exit at all.

Solo challengers face a different version: the anger turns inward.

**Level 4 — The Smoke Walk**
A floor of near-zero visibility. Not darkness — smoke. Thick, chemical, not quite toxic, the product of burning materials the Dungeon has been consuming for millennia. Challengers must navigate by touch, sound, and instinct. The elementals here, Smoke-Wraiths, are detectable only by the temperature differential of their approach: they are colder than the ambient smoke, which is itself counterintuitive enough that most challengers do not survive their first encounter.

**Level 5 — The Burn**
The first floor of the Ember Labyrinth where death by heat is the primary mechanism. Temperature exceeds 200 degrees. Human tissue begins breaking down. Even challengers with fire affinity and significant heat resistance report burns. The corridor on Level 5 is straight, which is the final cruelty: you can see the door to Level 6. You can see it the entire time you are being burned. The question is how much of yourself you are willing to leave behind on the floor between here and there.

**Level 6 — The Crucible Mirror**
A single chamber containing an exact architectural replica of the Draconian Academy's Crucible: the sealed obsidian room where Dragon Bonding takes place. The layout is perfect. The temperature is perfect. Even the scent — volcanic stone and old fire — is exactly right.

Inside the mirror-Crucible sits a Dragon. Not a real Dragon — a corruption-construct assembled from the Dungeon's learned knowledge of what a Dragon is. It is indistinguishable from the real thing on every sensory level except one: its heat is wrong. Draconic fire burns at a specific frequency that the Dungeon can approximate but not replicate. A challenger with genuine Dragon Bond experience will know. A challenger who has never experienced the real thing will not.

The mirror-Dragon takes each challenger through a simulation of the Crucible trial — the conversation, the building of trust, the approach to the Bond moment. Then it shows the Bond failing. Then it shows the Bond failing in a different way. Then in a dozen ways. Then in every way a person's worst fears about themselves can imagine. Challengers with Dragon Bond aspirations frequently emerge from this floor having abandoned them. The Dungeon does not have to break the hope directly. It just has to show, in perfect detail, all the ways hope can break itself.

**Level 7 — The Cinder Archive**
Malachar's original research chambers, exactly as they were when the Draconian Academy was built over his ruins. Preserved by the same magic that preserved his corruption, they look as if someone left them an hour ago. His workbenches. His tools. His notebooks, still open to the pages he was reading when Shinkami's rejection sent the shockwave through the ley lines.

And his unfinished work.

Half-forged artifacts on every surface. Weapons that are still warm. Instruments with no apparent purpose. Objects that call to specific challengers the way the right key calls to the right lock — each one radiating at the specific frequency of what a particular challenger most wants. Power they've been denied. Recognition they've been refused. Safety they've never had.

The objects are real. The power is real. The corruption is complete, and invisible until it's too late.

**Level 8 — The Cold Forge**
The deepest floor. The hottest floor. And the strangest, because at Level 8 the heat stops feeling like heat. It stops feeling like anything. The cold that the Ember Labyrinth has been building since Level 1 reasserts itself, not as temperature but as emptiness: a numbness that is not the body shutting down but something more fundamental. The fire inside goes quiet.

Not the magic. The will. The drive. The spark that makes a person want to try.

The Ember Labyrinth's deepest floor does not kill the body. It extinguishes the person.

#### The Core

A black flame. Cold and burning simultaneously, which is impossible and present regardless. No sound comes from it. No whisper, no offer, no voice. The Core of the Ember Labyrinth is the only Dungeon Core that simply shows you a world without fire — without meaning, without direction, without the point of effort. It does not argue that your fire was wrong to burn. It simply demonstrates that it can be put out, and waits to see whether knowing that changes anything.

Most challengers find it is the hardest thing they have ever faced. Not because it threatens them. Because it agrees with the worst thing they have ever believed about themselves: that nothing they do matters, and nothing they feel is real, and the fire was always going to go out eventually.

#### Known Expeditions

The Ember Labyrinth is the most-attempted Dungeon in Arcanea, due exclusively to its location inside the Draconian Academy. Generation after generation of Forge students, trained in the philosophy that the worst things make the strongest people, have concluded that the Dungeon inside their own school is the obvious next step.

The Academy's memorial hall has 3,100 plaques. It is the largest collection of memorial plaques in any single institution in Arcanea.

**The First Academy Attempt (Year 1, Eighth Age, founding year):** Eleven of the Academy's first graduating class attempted the Ember Labyrinth together. Three returned. They closed the memorial hall the following week and have kept it ever since.

**The Draconia Intervention (Year 203):** The Goddess Draconia herself descended into the Ember Labyrinth. She reached Level 7. She emerged four days later carrying nothing, which was notable because she had entered carrying her divine forge hammer, which she has not carried since. She has never explained what she left it for, or what she took instead. The Dungeon did not change after her visit. Whatever she attempted did not work.

**The Dragonborne Attempt (Year 998):** A team of three Dragonborne — the most powerful non-divine mortals in Arcanea at the time — entered together. They were chosen specifically because their Dragon Bonds were believed to protect against the emotional attacks of the corruption. They reached Level 8. One returned, alone, three weeks later. She has given testimony that the Level 6 mirror-Crucible was "precisely accurate, and none of what it showed was wrong." She does not consider the run a failure. She refuses to elaborate.

### IV. The Garden of Thorns
*Heart Gate Dungeon — The Whispering Meadows*

The Garden of Thorns blooms at midnight. The meadow flowers that mark its location open only during the bloom season, and the ground parts only for those who carry a living flower cut by someone who loves them. The flower is kept, preserved in crystal, just inside the entrance. Visible. Unreachable.

Six floors. Three weapons: your love for others, your grief for what you've lost, and your fear of what they know about you. The Garden does not kill many challengers. It does something worse: it sends them home alive and unable to trust their own relationships, haunted by what the Dungeon showed them about the people they thought they knew.

**The Boss:** The Thorn Queen — a figure assembled from the preserved flowers of every challenger who has entered, their petals still alive, still growing, still blooming. It speaks in no voice but yours. It asks the questions you have been afraid to ask yourself about the people you love.

**The Core:** A beating heart of thorn-wrapped crystal. It sings a lullaby in the voice of whoever first loved you, then asks why you left.

**Estimated deaths:** Approximately 900 confirmed. Psychological casualties: incalculable.

### V. The Silent Spire
*Voice Gate Dungeon — The Desert of Echoes*

The Spire is visible from fifty miles. A needle of white stone from the desert floor, impossibly thin, impossibly tall. As you approach, sound fades. At the Spire's base, there is no sound at all — not silence but the absence of sound's possibility. Your own heartbeat stops being audible. Screaming produces nothing.

**To enter:** Speak your true name. Not the name you were given. The name that accurately describes who you actually are. Most challengers spend significant time outside the Spire working out what this name is. Some never find it and turn back. Some find it and wish they hadn't.

Eight floors. Each one removes something from your ability to express yourself: first your voice on Level 2, then your common vocabulary on Level 3, then all words on Level 5. The deepest floors remove not just speech but the internal language by which you organize your own thoughts. Challengers who reach Level 8 — the Unvoice — report emerging from the experience temporarily unable to form coherent memories, as if thought itself requires a skeleton of language they'd had removed.

**The Boss:** The Unspoken — a figure made of accumulated silence, the compressed voicelessness of every challenger who lost their words in the Spire and never got them back.

**The Core:** A pillar of stone shaped like a throat, frozen mid-scream. Near it, the internal monologue goes quiet — not peaceful, not meditative, but empty. The Core's whisper is the absence of any whisper. The belief that you were never worth hearing.

**Notable:** The Desert of Echoes, where the Spire stands, may also be the sleeping place of Nythara the Void-touched Dragon. The relationship between the Dragon's presence and the Dungeon's nature — both defined by erasure — is not understood and not casually investigated.

---

### VI. The Mirror Depths
*Sight Gate Dungeon — Lake Clarity*

Lake Clarity is the most perfectly still body of water in Arcanea. Nothing disturbs its surface. It reflects the sky so completely that from certain angles you cannot tell where the lake ends and the sky begins. The entrance is at the bottom — swim straight down.

**To enter:** Look at your reflection and describe, aloud, exactly what you see. Not what you wish. Not what you fear. What is actually there. The requirement for honest self-perception is the Gate's final courtesy — the trial asks for the same quality the original Gate trial asked for, stripped of all the protective scaffolding that the Gods provided.

Eight floors. The Dungeon of the Sight Gate specializes in excess rather than deprivation — it does not take sight but gives too much of it. Future-sight. Parallel-self visions. Perfect knowledge of your own death date, location, and cause, delivered in the Core chamber with absolute certainty.

**The Boss:** The Thousand-Faced, a mirror-being that shows each viewer not their reflection but every version of themselves that has died in this Dungeon, in every way the Dungeon has learned to kill.

**The Core:** A perfectly spherical mirror that shows challengers the moment of their death. Not possible deaths. The actual one. Survivors report that the prediction matches with disturbing precision. The Core's question is simple: was it worth seeing?

**Estimated deaths:** Approximately 1,500 confirmed. Highest rate of long-term psychological casualties. Survivors frequently develop decision-paralysis and fear of reflective surfaces. Some develop Sight-Lock — a catatonic state triggered by catching their own reflection — that has no known treatment.

---

### VII. The Throne of Shadows
*Crown Gate Dungeon — Ruins of the First Palace*

The ruins of the First Palace stand on a plateau surrounded by permanent storm clouds. Lightning strikes the tallest remaining tower every few minutes, day and night, without variation. The throne room doors still stand, though the walls around them have collapsed. This is the Dungeon's one deliberate architectural mercy: showing you, from a distance, exactly what you're walking into. The Throne of Shadows is the only Dungeon that does not hide what it is.

**To enter:** Kneel. Say: "I am not worthy, but I will try." Not because the Dungeon requires humility as payment — it does not. Because the Crown Gate's original trial was about the gap between aspiration and reality, and the door opens only to those who can hold both at once.

Nine floors. The Throne of Shadows is unique among the Dungeons in that its corruption is specifically calibrated to people who want to lead, want to be chosen, want to matter. It does not manufacture ambition — it finds what's already there and feeds it until the ambition is the only thing left.

Malachar's whispers are clearest here. Not everywhere in the Dungeon — only at the upper reaches of the deepest floors, where the stone is oldest and closest to whatever is sealed in the Shadowfen. Challengers near the Core floor report hearing him clearly: not as evil, not as the Dark Lord, but as a voice that sounds deeply tired and deeply reasonable, making a case for why the things you want are the things the world needs, and why the only thing standing between you and giving the world what it needs is the rules that lesser people made.

**The Boss:** The Shadow Regent — a throne elemental that takes the form of the most powerful leader the challenger has ever envied. It does not fight. It offers to step aside. The question is whether you take the throne.

**The Core:** A crown that whispers. Its offer is not power but legitimacy: the absolute certainty, the bone-deep knowledge, that you were right all along. That your choices were correct. That the doubts you've carried about yourself were the only things ever standing between you and everything you deserved.

No one who has heard the crown's full offer has destroyed it. The best expeditions have managed to disrupt its whisper long enough to retreat.

### VIII. The Fractured Bridge
*Shift Gate Dungeon — Between the Shatter Peaks*

Two mountain peaks, a mile apart, connected by a bridge that exists in multiple states of reality simultaneously. The bridge is there, fully solid, if you look directly at it. Peripheral vision shows it dissolving. When you step onto it, the planks beneath your feet are solid; the plank you just left has become permeable; the plank ahead of you does not yet exist.

**To enter:** Step onto the bridge without looking at your feet. Trust that the next step will be there when you take it, even though every available sensory signal tells you otherwise.

Seven floors of reality dissolution. The Fractured Bridge is the Dungeon that most directly attacks identity — the continuous, coherent sense of being one self across time. By the deep floors, challengers begin encountering parallel versions of themselves: selves who made different choices, who live in branching realities that the Dungeon can access. Some of these alternate selves are better. Some are worse. The most dangerous are the ones who are almost exactly the same, with one critical difference, living a life that seems significantly more acceptable than the one the challenger currently occupies.

**The Boss:** The Shatterself — a combinator of several of the challenger's parallel selves, merged without consent, fighting for primary reality. It knows every technique the challenger knows. It knows their weaknesses. It is, in the truest possible sense, them at their worst.

**The Core:** A mirror that shows not the challenger but the moment they made the choice that defined them — the choice they still question, the road not taken. The Core does not offer to change it. It simply shows it, endlessly, and asks whether they are sure.

---

### IX. The Severed Hall
*Unity Gate Dungeon — The Abandoned Meeting Ground*

The oldest Dungeon, pre-dating the others in architectural style if not in corruption. The original Unity Temple was built at the meeting place of three ancient civilizations' borders — the first diplomatic neutral ground in Arcanean history. The structure is vast, built for hundreds of people to gather in at once, now entirely silent.

**To enter:** You cannot enter alone. A minimum of two people must enter together, holding hands, and each must speak one true thing about the other — not praise, not criticism, but accurate observation. The Dungeon takes the holding-of-hands. The touch-connection. Inside, you are physically incapable of making sustained contact with your companions.

The Severed Hall does not attack individuals. It attacks relationships. Every floor is designed to drive a specific kind of wedge between companions — first silence, then miscommunication, then manufactured resentment, then the deep floor specialty: the revelation of what you actually think about the people you love, the thoughts you've been successfully not-thinking, presented in your own internal voice, clearly, undeniably yours.

Challengers in the Severed Hall frequently report that the Dungeon's revelations about their companions are accurate. Not invented. Not exaggerated. Accurate. The Unity Gate's power is seeing clearly what connects people, and the Dungeon inverts this into seeing clearly what divides them.

**The Boss:** The Separation — a construct that takes the form of whatever aspect of a companion the challenger most secretly resents, embodied, confrontational, and impossible to dismiss as unfair.

**The Core:** A table with empty chairs. One for every companion who entered with the challenger. If any companions have been lost — died, retreated, separated — their chairs are occupied by echoes that look up when the challenger enters. The Core's whisper asks, quietly, whether any of them would have entered with you if they'd known what it would cost.

### X. The Void Gate
*Source Gate Dungeon — Location Unknown*

There is a tenth Dungeon. Every scholar who has studied the ley line network confirms its existence as certainly as they confirm the others. The corruption that traveled through the network on the night of Malachar's Fall reached ten temples. Nine can be located. One cannot.

No one who has entered the Void Gate has returned.

This is not a rumor, not a legend, not the exaggerated danger of a place no one has actually tried. It is a straightforward statement of record. Fourteen confirmed attempts across four thousand years. Fourteen confirmed entries. Zero returns.

What follows is the complete documented knowledge about the Void Gate. It is not much.

#### What Is Known

The Source Gate Dungeon is called the Void Gate because the Source Gate's frequency — 1111 Hz, Shinkami's domain of meta-consciousness — when corrupted, produces not an inverted virtue but an absence. Source's gift is the awareness that transcends the self, the consciousness that can hold everything simultaneously. The corruption of this produces not confused consciousness but no consciousness. The Void Gate is where awareness goes to end.

The ley line network shows the Void Gate's location as a moving point. It does not stay fixed the way the other nine Dungeons stay fixed. It occupies a space in the network but that space shifts — slowly, by geological standards, but measurably. Over four thousand years, it has moved approximately three hundred miles. The pattern of its movement, when charted over the full timespan, is a slow spiral. Mathematically, it will reach the center of the spiral approximately two thousand years from now.

No one has predicted what happens when it does.

#### The Fourteen Entries

The first confirmed entry was a scholar named Vel Ashwood in the Second Age who entered believing the Void Gate would be the simplest of the Dungeons — that the corruption of Source would produce emptiness, and emptiness could not kill. His calculation, by every available theory, was sound.

He did not return.

The most recent confirmed entry was seven years ago: a Luminor of the Abyssal Athenaeum who entered with three fully-equipped companions. They had studied every available record, consulted every living scholar of Dungeon architecture, obtained the blessing of Shinkami herself — which was given, quietly, without comment.

They entered. The Dungeon closed behind them.

One of the companions sent a written message back through the entrance seven minutes after entry. The message contained four words: *we are not there.* The handwriting was unsteady. The paper was damp.

The entrance remained accessible for six days. Nothing else emerged.

#### The Shinkami Question

Shinkami, Goddess of the Source Gate, has been asked about the Void Gate more times than any other God has been asked about anything. Her answers have changed over time, which is itself significant — Gods do not generally change their answers.

Early in the Eighth Age, when asked what was inside the Void Gate, she said: "Everything that Malachar thought he could reach."

A thousand years later, when asked again, she said: "The question itself."

When asked most recently, three centuries ago, by a delegation of all three Academies convened specifically to press for clarity, she said: "I cannot tell you what is inside. I can tell you that the reason no one returns is not what you have assumed."

The delegation pressed. What have we assumed? "That something in the Void Gate prevents return." She paused, in the specific way that Gods pause when they are choosing between honesty and kindness. "Perhaps the reason no one returns is that having arrived, they choose not to."

The delegation published this exchange. The debate it has generated has not resolved in three centuries.

#### What the Records Say About Sound

Three of the fourteen challengers managed to communicate before entry ended contact. All three used different methods. All three sent messages of approximately the same length. The messages are studied obsessively and interpreted differently by every scholar who has read them.

What they share: a quality of calm. None of the messages are panicked. None describe attack, physical danger, disorientation. The tone, in all three cases, is that of someone describing something they do not have the vocabulary for, making the attempt anyway out of obligation to the people watching from outside.

One reads: "It is quiet here and very large. Not dark — absence of dark. The concept does not apply. I am trying to find words. I will send another message when I have them."

No second message came.

#### The Only Approach

The Void Gate's entrance, when it can be located at all, is described by the scholars who have found it and not entered as "the absence of a place." Not a structure, not a void, not a hole in the ground. A location that cannot be described by saying what it is, only by saying what is around it.

There is no known entrance condition. Either none is required, or the condition is the approach itself — finding the location, recognizing it for what it is, and choosing to continue anyway.

#### Theories

Prominent theories, in order of scholarly credibility:

**The Transcendence Theory:** The Void Gate's corruption does not kill — it completes. Source consciousness, even inverted, is the consciousness that transcends individual identity. Challengers who enter are absorbed into something vast, and what is "left" cannot return because it is no longer discrete enough to navigate a return.

**The Prison Theory:** Malachar was sealed in the Shadowfen, which is adjacent to no Gate, by deliberate divine decision. But the Source Gate corruption — the place where he was rejected — might have created a secondary locus. The Void Gate may contain, or be adjacent to, something connected to Malachar that the Gods cannot destroy and will not discuss.

**The Waiting Room Theory:** Every civilization ends somewhere. The Void Gate is where they go. It is a library of endings — the accumulated finality of every thing that has stopped, preserved in a space outside normal time. The silence that has moved through the spiral toward its center for four thousand years is the silence of civilizations that ended before Arcanea began.

**The Honest Theory (Shinkami's possible implication):** The Void Gate leads somewhere. Fourteen people have reached it. None have come back, not because they couldn't, but because, having arrived, they decided the place they arrived was more worth remaining in than the world they left.

This is, to most scholars, the most disturbing theory. Because it implies that what is inside the Void Gate is genuinely better, by the evaluation of fourteen people who had seen and loved the world enough to risk death to explore it.

And not one of them sent word back to say so.

## Appendix: Notes on Dungeon Clearance

The theoretical method for clearing a Dungeon — destroying its Core — has never been successfully achieved. The leading hypothesis: a Core can only be destroyed by pure Gate energy used by someone who has genuinely opened that Gate, without the Dungeon's corruption tainting the Gate energy during delivery.

The paradox: the Dungeon's corruption specifically targets and taints Gate energy used within its walls. It was designed with this in mind. Malachar understood the Gates better than any mortal ever has. The trap is elegant and complete.

Unless it is not complete. Malachar was brilliant but not omniscient. He built the corruption in a night of rage and despair, channeled through a network he was using for the first time in its full capacity. There may be gaps. There may be failure modes he did not anticipate. There may be approaches to Core clearance that his model of mortal behavior did not account for.

The Academies research this continuously. They have been researching it for four thousand years. The most optimistic scholars believe a solution exists and has not been found yet. The most pessimistic believe the solution exists and is the one thing none of them have been willing to try.

Both camps note that the one person in Arcanea who definitely knows the answer is the person who built the problem. And Malachar's seal, whatever else it does, does not permit correspondence.
