# Chapter One: What We Chose to Lose

The morning smelled like salt and oleander, same as every morning.

Nereth stood on her high balcony in the Speaker's Tower and let the city happen to her one more time.

Thal'Maris at dawn was a thing that could not be adequately described to someone who had not seen it. She knew this because she had tried, many times, in the formal records she kept for the Archive — and always the sentences fell apart under the weight of what they were trying to hold. She had written *the white towers catch the first light and hold it differently than stone has any right to* and then struck it through. She had written *the harbor fills from dark water to copper to gold in the span of twenty minutes and this happens every single morning without ever becoming ordinary* and struck that through too. Language was a tool for pointing at things. The thing itself remained itself, indifferent to how well you pointed.

So she stood on the balcony and received it without translation.

The harbor. The three tiers of the city rising from the waterfront to the Speaker's Tower at the crest, each tier its own small world of markets and workshops and libraries and the long public gardens where children ran between the fountains in the afternoon heat. The Pillar Bridge, spanning the harbor's mouth, its white columns catching the dawn at an angle that made the whole structure appear momentarily transparent — as if you were looking through something rather than at it. The fishing boats already out on the flat morning water, their wakes cutting the harbor's reflection into long silver rags.

She had lived in this city for sixty years. She had been born in the second tier, in a room that smelled of her father's woodwork and her mother's dye-vats. She had been elected Speaker at forty-three, the youngest in Thal'Maris's history, and had spent seventeen years in that work with more love for it than she'd expected and less certainty than she'd projected to the people she governed.

She looked at it and felt the grief already, the grief that had been building for three days, since Seranthi had come out of the upper room and sat on the floor of the corridor without speaking.

The vote had been yesterday.

31,247 to sink.

8,753 to remain.

She had announced the result from the Speaker's Dais, her voice steady because she'd had seventeen years of practice keeping it steady, and then she had come directly here and stood on the balcony and watched the city run its evening business below her as if nothing had been decided, and had stood there until her legs ached and then stood there longer.

The wind off the harbor was warm. It was always warm here. That was one of the things.

---

She went to see Seranthi an hour after dawn.

The upper room occupied the Tower's quietest corner, where two walls met to form an alcove facing away from the harbor — facing inland, toward the mountains that Thal'Maris had kept at its back for six hundred years. The alcove had a single window, narrow, and Seranthi sat below it in the way she had been sitting for three days: folded inward, her arms wrapped around her own ribs, her eyes open and fixed on something that was not the room.

She had been the city's seer for eleven years. Before that she had been a fisherman's daughter from the lowest tier who had come to the Speaker's door at nineteen with water in her lungs and a voice that said everything twice — once in the world and once from somewhere underneath the world. Nereth had sent for the best practitioners she knew and none of them had a name for what Seranthi was. They had eventually stopped trying to name it and started listening instead, which was usually the correct progression.

In eleven years, Seranthi had spoken thirty-two visions. Twenty-eight had been true. Three had been conditional — the things they'd seen had been averted by choice, which the practitioners argued about endlessly: were conditional visions true visions or warnings? Seranthi herself had no opinion on this. She saw what she saw and said what she said and seemed genuinely baffled by the idea that she ought to have a philosophy about it.

The thirty-second vision had come nine days ago.

She had been standing in the public garden, watching the children in the fountains. Then she had not been standing. She had been on the ground with her hands over her mouth and her body shaking, and the two practitioners who were always assigned to accompany her in public had held her while she shook, and when the shaking stopped she had looked at them with an expression they had never seen on her face before — not the dazed drift of a recent vision, not the careful steadiness of someone containing what she'd seen. Something they could not name.

She had spoken seventeen words.

The practitioners had written them down. Those seventeen words had gone from the practitioners to the Counselors and from the Counselors to the full city assembly in sealed form, each councilor reading in private, no one permitted to speak the words aloud. And then the assembly had voted on whether to release the vision to the city, and the assembly had voted no, and then the assembly had voted on whether to call the question, and the assembly had voted yes, and then the full city had gathered in the harbor square and voted on whether to know and whether to remain.

The full city had voted not to know.

Nereth did not know the seventeen words. She had chosen not to read them. She was possibly the only Counselor who had declined — there had been a long silence when she'd announced this decision to the assembly, and Counselor Tava had looked at her with something very close to pity and said: *Nereth, you have to know what you're asking us to give up.* And Nereth had said: *I know what we're giving up. I don't need to know why.*

She had meant it. She still did. Mostly.

"Seranthi," she said softly, stepping into the alcove.

The seer's eyes moved to her. Focused, but only slowly — as if coming back from a long distance.

"They voted," Nereth said. Not a question.

"I know." Seranthi's voice was very quiet. It had always been quiet, which had required Nereth to become a better listener. "I could feel the shape of it changing. Yesterday afternoon. Like a current turning." She paused. "I'm sorry."

"Don't be sorry."

"I am, though." Seranthi looked at her hands in her lap. "I've been sitting here for three days trying to make myself not say the words. They want out. I can feel them in my throat. Like something alive that wants to move." She swallowed. "I won't say them. I want you to know that I won't. But I wanted you to know it isn't nothing — it isn't just staying quiet because the vote said so. It's harder than that."

Nereth sat on the floor of the alcove, back against the cool stone, at the same level as Seranthi. This was something she had learned over seventeen years: that the conversations that mattered happened at floor level or at kitchen tables. Elevated furniture was for governing. The rest was just being human together.

"How long have you known?" Nereth asked. "That this was what the vision would require."

"About two days after it came." Seranthi didn't look at her, but her hands stilled. "There was a moment when I understood that the only way the words couldn't spread was if no one who carried them was above the water. The words want to be spoken. They've wanted to be spoken since I said them inside myself and they changed shape in there and became something that knows it exists." She exhaled. "I don't entirely understand what I'm telling you. I understand that it's true."

"I believe you."

Silence.

Below the Tower, Thal'Maris was doing its morning. The market bells in the second tier had started their rhythm. Somewhere in the distance, a child was laughing — the particular high-note laugh of a child being chased, running in delight.

"The ones who chose the boats," Seranthi said. "Will they be all right?"

"I don't know." Nereth was careful to be honest when the honest answer was *I don't know.* It was the easiest lie to tell — the comfortable certainty — and she had refused it seventeen years running and wasn't going to start now. "They'll be alive. They'll have the boats and the supply maps and the star charts. Where they go from there is theirs to decide."

"They'll carry some of it with them," Seranthi said. "The feeling of this place. The shape of it." She pressed one palm flat against the floor. "Thal'Maris gets into you. I've met people who left years ago and they still sleep toward the harbor. They still know what time the morning bells ring even when they're nowhere near bells." She paused. "I hope that's enough for them. What they carry without knowing."

Nereth did not say: *It will have to be.* She did not say anything. Sometimes silence was the only honest response to the impossible.

She sat with Seranthi on the floor of the alcove while the morning expanded below them, and neither of them spoke about the words, and neither of them needed to.

---

The speech was scheduled for midday.

She had been writing it and discarding it and rewriting it for three days, filling pages with attempts that were each wrong in a different specific way: too formal, too bare, too full of the abstractions she'd learned to mistrust. She wanted to give the city something true. Not comfort — she had learned that there was a kind of comfort that was a small lie, a smooth surface over a rough truth, and people deserved the rough truth even when it was heavier. Especially then.

She stood on the Speaker's Dais in the harbor square and waited while the city gathered.

40,000 people. Or near enough. The ones who had voted to remain were all here; the 8,753 who had voted otherwise were permitted to come too, and most of them had. They stood in the square and on the tier walls and along the Pillar Bridge and crowded the balconies of the harbor-facing buildings. Some of them had brought their children. The youngest ones rode on their parents' shoulders, high enough to see over the crowd, blinking in the midday light and unaware that this was not an ordinary gathering.

The market bells had stopped. The harbor was quiet — the fishing boats had all come in.

Nereth stood at the Dais and let herself look at it. All of it.

The faces.

She was sixty years old and she had been looking at these faces for all sixty of those years and she knew them in the way you knew a place you had grown in: not every individual name, but the grain and texture of the whole, the family resemblances that ran through generations, the particular quality of how Thal'Maris looked at things it loved and things it feared, which was with the same open steadiness. They had built a city of people who looked at things steadily. She was proud of that. She would always be proud of it.

She did not tell them it was going to be all right.

She said: "You know what we decided. I'm not here to explain the vote. You were there. You made it, each of you, which is how decisions of this weight must be made — not by a Speaker, not by the Counselors, but by the whole city speaking at once."

The square was silent in the way that large crowds are only silent when they are genuinely listening.

"I want to tell you what I believe is true," she said, "because I have always believed that is the only real use of this Dais. Not ceremony. Not the performance of leadership. The honest accounting of what is and what it costs."

She had spoken at this Dais hundreds of times. She had never once prepared for silence like this.

"What is true: the vision was real. I have known Seranthi for eleven years and I have watched thirty-two visions resolve, and I will stand before you and say that what she carries is real, and that what it means is real, and that the choice we made together is the only choice that meets the scale of what it means." She paused. "I know some of you voted the other way. I know some of you believe we are choosing dissolution over courage — that we could face what is coming rather than removing ourselves from the path of it. I will not tell you that you're wrong to feel that way. I will tell you that I voted to sink, and I will try to say why as plainly as I know how."

She looked at the harbor. At the Pillar Bridge catching the noon light.

"We are not dying," she said. "I want that to be clear. What Leyla has agreed to do for us — what I have asked her to do, and what she has accepted, which is not nothing; she loves this city and I know what we are asking costs her something — is a transformation. The water will change us. We will breathe differently. We will remember differently. Some of us, especially the youngest, may not remember at all in the way we think of remembering — not the sharp recall of mornings and faces, but something older and deeper, the kind of memory that lives in the body, in the hands, in the way you move through water." She swallowed. "I don't know everything it will mean. I won't lie to you and say I do."

A child somewhere in the crowd began to cry — a small, bewildered sound — and was hushed.

"What we are doing," she said, "is choosing to lose ourselves so that what we know cannot be used."

She let that sit.

"The words Seranthi carries are not simply words. I have not read them. I chose not to, and I would make that choice again. But I know what words of that kind do, from the histories: they move. They find mouths. They pass from speaker to speaker like a seed looking for soil, and every time they're spoken they grow a little, accrete a little, gain a little of the shape of whoever speaks them, until the original thing and the current thing are both real and both different and the consequences ramify in ways that no single decision-maker can follow." She paused. "We are a people who have built our civilization on the principle that knowledge belongs to everyone. I believe that still. I will believe it on the other side of this, in whatever form believing takes. But there is a category of knowledge that is not a gift. There is a category that is a weapon that does not care who it harms. And the only way we know to make a weapon harmless is to bury it somewhere nothing else can reach."

The harbor.

The city.

The faces.

"To the 8,753 who voted to remain," she said, and she looked at them specifically, found them in the crowd, the people who were standing with a different quality of stillness — grief-still rather than acceptance-still, the body braced against a thing it hadn't chosen. "The boats are ready. The charts are complete. There are maps of six viable coastlines, notes on the seasons, letters of introduction to the settlements at the river mouths who have agreed to receive you. You carry Thal'Maris in you. Not as memory only — as character. The way this city asks questions and makes decisions and sits with difficulty. That is not lost when the city sinks. That goes where you go."

She held that.

"To everyone," she said. "To all 40,000 of us. What we built here was worth building. The six hundred years of this city are worth exactly as much as they were yesterday, before the vote. That doesn't change. We are not being punished. We are not failing. We are doing the last and hardest thing a city can do, which is to choose rightly at great cost. That is not defeat. That is what we are made of."

She stopped. She had more she could have said — there were seventeen drafts, she could have filled another hour — but she knew when a thing was complete. The words that needed saying had been said. Everything else would only be addition for its own sake.

She stepped back from the Dais.

Afterward, she could not have said with certainty that the crowd was silent or loud. Both, she thought. The kind of both that happened when sound and silence occupied the same moment because neither was sufficient.

---

Leyla came at evening.

Not in glory. Nereth had half-expected glory — the goddess of the Flow Gate arriving in light and transformation and cosmic announcement. Leyla was old as the world and present in every current of every water, and Nereth had been braced, in some part of herself, for the overwhelming register of a God appearing.

What arrived was a woman, approximately Nereth's age, in a dress the color of seawater at depth, sitting on the harbor steps with her feet in the water and her eyes on the boats being loaded in the middle distance.

Nereth sat beside her.

"You built a beautiful city," Leyla said.

"I know."

"I've watched it for six hundred years." Leyla's voice was not what Nereth had expected. Not vast. Not resonant with ancient power. Just a voice, with all the texture of something that had spoken for a very long time and still found the speaking worth doing. "I watched the founders lay the Pillar Bridge in forty days with twenty families working in relay because no single family could hold the engineering knowledge of the whole thing — it had to live in all of them at once." She was quiet. "I've thought about that bridge every day for six hundred years. What it meant that they built it that way."

Nereth looked at the bridge. The last light was touching the columns from the west.

"Does it hurt you?" she asked. "What we're asking."

Leyla was quiet for a moment that had weight in it. "Yes," she said. "I want you to know that it does. I want you to know that I am not doing this because it is easy or because I can do it without grieving what it costs. I'm doing it because you asked me, and because I've had nine days to sit with the reason you're asking, and because the reason is real." She paused. "And because I know what happens to prophecies that find no silence to lie in. I've seen it before, in other ages, in other places. What Seranthi carries is a specific kind of truth — the kind that doesn't want to be true, that tries to make itself not-true by propagating, by becoming so widely known that it becomes instead of just predicting." She was quiet. "Thal'Maris is choosing against that. I can honor that choice by doing the hard part of it."

"Will they remember?" Nereth asked. "The youngest ones."

"Not in the way you're asking. Not their names or their neighbors or this morning's light on the harbor." Leyla lifted one foot from the water and looked at the water running off it, silver in the evening. "But the city will be there. The shape of it. Six hundred years of this place being this way — that doesn't vanish. It becomes what they are. The way they move. What they're drawn toward. What feels like home." She set her foot back in the water. "Memory is older than language. You can lose the language and keep the memory."

Nereth thought about Seranthi, sitting in the alcove with her hands around her own ribs.

"The seer," she said. "Seranthi. She said the words want out. That she can feel them trying."

"I know." Leyla's voice was gentle and precise. "I'll take her last, if you'll allow it. I want to speak with her first, before the water. I want her to understand that I will carry them — the words, in the place where language goes when it becomes water. The Athenaeum will hold them in the deepest vault. Carved in stone that nothing alive will be able to read."

"Will that be enough?"

"I don't know," Leyla said. "I'm going to try."

They sat in silence for a long time, a woman and a goddess, while the harbor darkened and the loading of the boats continued in the middle distance, lanterns appearing one by one on the water.

---

The water rose slowly. That was the mercy of it — the time.

They had expected something abrupt, a decision made in one catastrophic moment, and instead what came was incremental, which was perhaps the right way for a civilization to end: not all at once, but in gradual accumulation, each stage providing a brief still point in which to witness what was being given up before the next stage claimed it.

The sea came in through the harbor first, the water level rising against the pier steps, then covering the lowest market stalls, then reaching the base of the Pillar Bridge's landward approach. It was cold — Nereth had expected the sea to feel like the sea, familiar, the water she'd swum in her whole life, but it had a different quality now, a intention to it, the sense of something that was moving with purpose rather than simply following the physics of water moving through a gap.

She stood in the harbor square, thigh-deep, and watched the change.

The people around her were changing. Slowly. With the same incremental quality as the water, which Nereth suspected was not accidental — the change was paced to match the rising, so that by the time the water reached your chest you had already become something that could breathe it.

The gills appeared first, a faint rippling along the sides of the neck, the way gills looked on the small fish the children caught in the shallows. Then the fingers: the skin between them stretching, translucent, the webbing that deepwater things had and which her people would need. There were sounds that were not quite crying — the in-between vocalization of a body discovering its own alteration and uncertain how to interpret it.

Then the water reached faces.

And the crying — the uncertain not-quite-crying — changed. It became something else. Not silence, but a different register of sound, the deep sub-vocal resonance of beings that spoke through water, and it was strange and it was beautiful and it was nothing she had words for.

A small girl beside her, someone's daughter whose name Nereth did not know, had gone from terror to wonder in the span of ten seconds. She was laughing. The water was warm at depth — that surprised Nereth, who had known the harbor water her whole life, it was never warm — and the small girl was laughing because the warmth was remarkable and she was three years old and had not yet learned that remarkable things required something more complicated than joy.

Nereth thought: *good. Let that be the last thing I see above the waterline. Let that be what I carry.*

She was the last.

It had been agreed. The Speaker goes last. Thal'Maris's final dry-air words would be hers, which was fitting, which was the kind of fitting that only became visible in the last moments of things when you couldn't do anything about it anymore.

The water was at her shoulders.

She could feel the change beginning — the faint pressure at her throat, the cool alien shift of a body that knew something her mind did not yet know. She had been afraid of this. She was surprised to discover that she was not afraid anymore, that the fear had been replaced by something she didn't have a word for, something that was neither courage nor resignation but a third thing that contained both and was fuller than either.

She looked at the Pillar Bridge, visible above the water. Only the tops of the columns now.

"Thal'Maris," she said.

Her voice carried across the water the way voices carried on still mornings. Not far — far enough.

"We were a city that built with its whole knowledge distributed so that no single part of it could be lost. We were a city that decided by assembling and listening until the whole spoke at once. We were a city that stood on the Pillar Bridge and watched the first light take the harbor and did not become accustomed to it, not once in six hundred years, not once."

The water was at her chin.

"We are choosing to lose ourselves so that what we know cannot be used. This is not surrender. This is the highest form of keeping."

She let it reach her.

The last thing that remained was the sound of the small girl laughing, somewhere below the surface, and the warmth that Leyla had promised, and the sense — which was perhaps only Nereth's understanding of what was happening to her, perhaps something more — of the city intact and entire, six hundred years of it, present in the water around her not as memory but as a kind of permanent fact, the way a building's foundations remain even when the building is gone, invisible and true.

The words were still in Seranthi somewhere. Nereth would never know them now.

She was glad.

*Three hundred years later.*

*Four hundred. Five.*

*The Athenaeum rose where the city had been, built by Leyla's direction and the slow labor of those who had become what Thal'Maris had made them, the memory-keepers who dove through the architecture of the past as easily as through water, who taught in voices that resonated at depths that kept certain sounds safe.*

*In the lowest vault, past the sealing doors that required all three sitting Heads to open and which had not been opened in the memory of anyone alive, there was a wall of dark stone.*

*Carved into it: seventeen glyphs in the old Atlantian script, the one that had not been written since the city went under, that no living scholar could parse, that the most dedicated linguistic archaeologists in Arcanea had spent careers attempting to reconstruct without reaching a confirmed reading.*

*The wall was warm to the touch.*

*Not the ambient warmth of a building long occupied, but something specific and interior, as if the stone were alive, as if something in it were still in motion.*

*Every Head of the Athenaeum, at their installation, was brought to the vault and left alone with the wall for one hour. None of them ever reported what happened in that hour. It was not required. What was required was that they emerged knowing something they could not articulate, carrying a heaviness that was not grief and not knowledge but something between them, and that they governed accordingly: carefully, with immense respect for the things that needed to stay buried, and with a permanent understanding that the deepest mercy was sometimes the one that required you to lose yourself.*

*The small girl who had laughed in the rising water had a granddaughter who was head of the Athenaeum's memory archive, and a great-granddaughter who was learning the current-shaping that would let her reach the oldest impressions.*

*The girl did not know her name had been Nereth's last witnessed joy.*

*The warmth in the wall did not require her to know.*

*It only required the wall to remain, and the glyphs to remain, and the vault to stay sealed.*

*Which it did.*

*Which it always did.*

*Which it would.*
