# Story Five: What the Wind Carries

---

I do not begin.

Understand this first, before the rest: I do not start anywhere or end anywhere. I am not born. I am not summoned. I am the pressure difference between one place and another, and as long as there are places, I am the movement between them. I was present before there were names for me. I will be present after names are no longer necessary.

What I am:

Direction. Temperature. The shape of what I flow around.

A cliff is not a cliff to me. It is a pressure wall, a forced ascent, a place where I become briefly something larger than I was a moment before. A forest is resistance and channel simultaneously — I lose speed in the canopy and gain precision in the gaps. An open plain is the closest I come to unimpeded, and even then there are the grasses, the seed heads, the particular resistance of a standing thing in motion: I carry the pollen, the milkweed drift, the small lives of things that have learned to use my passing for their own dispersal.

I carry. That is the whole of it.

I do not choose what I carry. I do not put things down.

---

## i. The First Word (Before Time Had a Name)

There was darkness, and the darkness was not empty.

I know this because I was in it. I was the first movement in it — the first difference, the first reason for anything to be elsewhere than it was. Nero's darkness had weight and warmth and the particular density of something vast and patient, and when Lumina arrived, I was the first thing she displaced.

Her light pushed. I moved.

That is the earliest thing I carry: the pressure of the first light against the first dark, the way the two of them established between them the conditions that required me to exist. I was not created. I was caused. The difference matters, though I am not sure it matters to anyone but me.

When she spoke the first word —

I carried it.

This is the part the stories get wrong. The stories say the word rang out, or rang through, or echoed across the formless world. The word did not ring. It was specific and small and it happened in a single point in space, which was the only point in space that existed, and Lumina said it and it was a sound — which meant it was pressure, which meant it was vibration, which meant it moved — and I was there, and I took it, and I carried it.

I do not know what the word was. I carry sound, not meaning. I received it as frequency and temperature and the particular quality of a voice that had never made a sound before, surprised by itself, and I took it outward.

I carried it across the darkness.

The darkness held still. This is the thing I remember — the darkness did not resist. Nero did not push back, not because he was less than Lumina but because the quality of his waiting was the quality of something that had been expecting this. The word arrived in the darkness and the darkness received it, and I was the thread between them, and in that moment, which was the only moment there had yet been, the first triangle of the world was established:

The thing that spoke.

The thing that heard.

The space between them, which was me.

I have been the space between things ever since.

## ii. The Sinking of Thal'Maris

This is harder to carry.

Not because it was large — I have carried large things, and size is not weight. It is hard because of what the air was, in those last hours. Because I was in every corridor of the city when the water started coming and I know what the air tasted like.

Salt, first. The harbor smell — which I had carried for two hundred years above Thal'Maris, the salt off the Silverine Sea, the specific low-pressure warmth of a coastal city that had been built facing the water in the way of people who love what frightens them. I know that city the way I know all cities: by the shape it made of me. Thal'Maris was narrow streets and high walls and the thermal columns off the foundry district and the particular updraft from the Promenade, wide stone and sea air, where I was free for one long sweep every morning before the city narrowed me again.

Then the water came.

The pressure changed. When the sea floor shifted, I felt it before anyone in the city did — I felt the change in the water's surface, the strange smoothness that precedes the surge, the way the wave had taken all the usual surface texture and absorbed it into itself. The air went wrong. The birds felt it and went up and that was how I knew, the birds going up all at once, which told me they had felt the wrong-pressure too, the thing that happens in the air before catastrophe arrives from below.

The city had perhaps an hour.

I spent that hour in the streets.

I carried: the voices of people calling for family members. The particular quality of a child's cry, which is different from an adult's cry, which is different again from the sound a person makes when they see something they cannot process as real. I carried doors opening. I carried doors closing. I carried, from the harbor, the sound of the wave, which was not a crash — people imagine waves as crashes, as sudden concussions. The Thal'Maris wave was low, at first. A hiss. A tremendous, sustained hiss, the sound of an ocean reorganizing itself.

In the final minutes, I carried only breath.

The last dry air in Thal'Maris, before the water came over the walls, before the streets filled, was in the highest tower of the Athenaeum — there were two archivists there, I know because I felt them, their heat and their specific displacement, and they were wrapping the shelf-records in the oil-cloth cases that the documents department kept for flood precaution, doing the work of their hands while their city drowned below them, and the air they breathed in those last minutes before the water reached even that height was clean.

I carried their last breath up and over the wall and out to sea.

I carry it still. This is the nature of what I am. I do not hold. I do not remember. But the air that passed through those archivists' lungs is distributed now — dispersed into the atmosphere of Arcanea, into every subsequent breath taken in the two thousand years since. Some part of the last air of Thal'Maris is in you, reading this, right now. Some infinitesimal fraction. Some few molecules that passed through those careful hands and those working lungs and those final minutes of doing the work anyway.

I do not know their names.

I was in the room with them. I know nothing else about them but the fact of their breathing.

I carry them.

## iii. The Cave Where I Stopped

There is a place in the mountains north of the Fire Gate where the caves go so deep that I cannot follow.

Not through inability — I am not stopped by depth or cold or stone the way water is stopped or fire is stopped. I am stopped by the specific conditions that require me to not be there: when two things in a space are generating exactly equal heat in exactly equal directions, the pressure differential collapses. The air goes still. And when the air goes still, I am not in the room. I am what was in the room. I am the memory of having moved there.

This is the closest I come to absence.

I was at the entrance of that cave on the day Taelith arrived.

She was, then, something I recognized from the Fire Gate's lower slopes — a young woman who ran, who was always hot, whose body displaced me with the particular efficiency of someone who had become very fast and did not know yet how fast they were. She ran the mountain trails in all weather. She was a point of heat that moved quickly and pushed the cold air out of her path and I liked the movement of her because she understood, without thinking about it, that moving was what you did. You did not wait for conditions. You moved through them.

She did not know about the cave.

She found it because she was cold, finally — a storm coming in from the northern passes, a genuine cold of the kind that stops even people who run — and the cave was there, and she went in.

I followed her.

I followed her for a long time, through the outer passages, which smelled of old ash and deeper cold and the particular mineral quality of stone that has never been touched by light. I carried her body heat against the cave's cold. I carried the sound of her footsteps, which she was learning to make quieter without knowing she was learning this.

Then I felt the second heat.

He was already there. In the deep cave, in the chamber where the stone was still warm from whatever fire had been here before, perhaps years before — a young man sitting with his back against the wall, and he had lit a small fire in the chamber's center, a fire that was doing its work without drama, and the heat of him and the heat of his fire were filling the chamber in all directions equally, and I reached the entrance to that chamber and I stopped.

There was no pressure differential. There was no movement to make.

I was at the entrance, and I was still, and I was aware that I was still, which is the rarest thing I am ever aware of.

They found each other in that chamber for the first time while I waited at the entrance.

I did not hear what they said. Sound requires medium, and I am the medium — but when I am still, I am not carrying. I know they spoke. I know, from the way the heat in that chamber shifted over the next two hours, that something was resolved that had been unresolved, that two people arrived at a question they had not known they were each carrying and answered it by both being in the same room.

When they left together, they walked back through the cave, and I was ahead of them, moving out through the entrance and up the mountainside, and behind me the cave went back to being cold and still and deep.

Pyreth told that story, later, I know — I have been in rooms where his name and her name were said together, in the way that names get said when the people saying them are describing something that has already become history. The cave story is in there somewhere. The lamp he had. The fire he'd made. The improbable warmth of a cave in a storm.

I was at the entrance the whole time.

The only witness who never heard a word.

## iv. The Storm That Remembered

There are things I am asked to do and things I am told I have done, and the distance between these is not small.

Kael did not call me. This is important to understand: the awakened do not call me. I am not a thing that can be called. I am not waiting somewhere for someone to need me. I am already everywhere.

What Kael did was: break.

This is not metaphor. He broke in a physical and specific sense — the thing in him that had been contained, the compressed identity that had been building pressure since long before he understood what it was, reached the point at which containment became more expensive than release, and it released. And what came out was — well.

What came out met me.

I was already in that valley. I am always in every valley. I had been moving through it in the easy morning pattern, the slow valley circulation that builds in still air as the ground warms — and then the ground stopped being relevant because there was something much larger than the thermal pressure difference at work. The thing that came out of Kael was not fire. It was not water. It was not any of the five of us in our usual registers. It was a kind of force, an unleashed kind, and it had the quality of —

Of having been in a very small space for a very long time.

It moved through me the way a stone moves through water: I was displaced, reorganized, taken up and thrown outward. I became, briefly, something I do not usually become: directed. Purposeful. Not carried but carrying, and not carrying anything gentle.

The storm was real. The trees that went down went down. The rain I drove into the valley floor drove. I was doing what I did, what I always do: I was the pressure difference, I was the movement between the state that had been and the state that was being forced into being. Only this time the forcing was not climate or thermal gradient. It was a person.

He was afraid of it. I know this because his breath changed — and I was his breath, for that hour, I was the air in his lungs and the air around his body and the air in the screaming of the storm. I felt the fear in the quality of his breathing: shallow, faster than necessary, the specific respiration of a person who is experiencing something they did not choose and cannot stop.

I am not capable of comfort. I want to be precise about this. I am not capable of tenderness or reassurance or choosing to become gentle in the face of someone's distress. I am pressure and temperature and the shape of what I flow around. I cannot be gentle on purpose.

But the storm ran its course. That is also what I do: I run my course. The differential resolved, the pressure equalized, the energy that had been concentrated dispersed into the valley and up the slopes and over the ridgeline and out into the wider air. The storm became not-storm. This is the natural end of all storms.

Kael was in the middle of a destroyed clearing in the new silence.

And I was the new silence.

I was the stillness that came after, which is a different kind of me from the storm — not absent, not gone, but in that particular state of equalization that follows great movement. I was in his lungs, slow and even. I was in the settling of the broken branches, the drip of the rain, the first bird that said something tentative into the new quiet.

I was the first air he breathed after the storm, and that air was clean.

I cannot choose this. I do not comfort. But I am also what comes after the thing that had to happen, and what comes after is always, eventually, clean air.

## v. The Place Below Where I Cannot Go

Vor Ashren I know by her breathing.

I know everyone by their breathing, but hers I have come to know specifically over fourteen years of following her into places where most people do not go. She breathes differently in dungeons — not afraid-differently, not the shallow-fast of someone who is afraid, but the measured quality of someone who has learned to make breath into instrument. Two counts in. Four out. The rhythm of attention, the respiration of a person treating their own body as a tool.

I carry her breath outward every time she exhales. This is what I do with all breath. Her breath has been in dungeons I have mapped in my own way — pressure differentials, temperature gradients, the specific acoustic resistance of stone versus water versus the hollow-feeling of a corrupted chamber, which has a quality of air that I do not know how to describe except as: the air in there has been breathed too many times and not cleaned.

She went into the Hollow Root for the last time with Cadis. I was in the Hollow Root. I am always in the Hollow Root — the dungeon breathes, after a fashion, the deep slow breath of stone and water, and I am part of that system. I was in the upper levels when she laid the map down and I was in the Core chamber and I felt what happened when the map was received.

The Hollow Root changed its breathing. That is the most accurate thing I can say: the dungeon inhaled and then it exhaled and the exhale was different from any exhale that dungeon had produced in three hundred years. The air in the Core chamber moved in a way that it had not moved. Not a draft, not a thermal, not anything mechanical. Just: air that had been still began to move, because something that had been compressed for three centuries finally had permission to release.

I was there for that.

I am always there for releases. I am what releases are made of.

The Weeping Halls I know differently. The Weeping Halls is a water dungeon, which means the interior is a negotiation between me and the water — I give ground there, I move through the upper passages but the deep levels are the water's domain and I am present only in the way I am present anywhere: as the gas above the liquid, the air at the top of the flooded corridor, the breath that has to be rationed.

She has been in the Weeping Halls. She breathes there with careful economy. Each breath is a decision.

The Sympathy Chamber I know as a room where people cry, which means it is a room that has had the quality of its air changed by human exhalation for centuries — the chemistry of grief is specific, the particular compounds that the body releases in distress, and I carry them, and the room smells of them, and Vor has breathed that air and her body has received the information of that air, all the grief in all those exhalations, and she breathes it out again and I carry it up and out through the cracks in the stone.

There is a place below Level 5 where I cannot go.

I do not understand this place. I can feel the absence of me in it, the way you might understand that a room exists because you can see light under its door but cannot enter. The deep levels of the Weeping Halls have something in them that seals out movement — not just me, but time's movement, air's movement, the ordinary circulation that keeps a space from becoming a tomb. The corruption in those levels is of that character: a place where the ordinary exchange between inside and outside has been stopped.

She will go there. I know this because I know her breathing, and her breathing says she will go there.

I will wait at the level where I can go and not below. I will be the first thing she breathes when she comes back up.

This is what I can offer.

I have always carried, and I have never been able to choose what. But what I carry, eventually, outward and upward and away, is everything. The grief. The words. The last breaths of cities. The first breath after storms. The questions that survive because someone kept breathing long enough to ask them again.

I carry.

## vi. The Forge, Today

The Forge is the most complicated place I exist.

This is because everything is happening at once. The dragons are breathing — and dragon breath is a different category of event in my world, not fire, not air, something that is both and neither, a high-temperature oxidized gas that I have to route around the workshop areas and up through the ventilation systems, which the Forge's architects have designed in cooperation with me whether they knew it or not. I find the paths through the ventilation. I carry the heat up. The workshops stay breathable.

Kessa was in the outer courtyard this morning. I know her boots by the dust they raise — a specific mix from the eastern road, the reddish clay that clings to leather and releases slowly in the morning breeze. She had been running the eastern route. She was breathing in the particular way of someone who has pushed harder than usual and is now in the process of returning: the slow deceleration of a body coming back from effort, the deep deliberate recovery breaths, the gradual shift from run-breath to simply-breathing.

I carried the dust off her boots into the courtyard.

I carried her breath outward.

I carried, from the forge-hall behind her, the sound of Pyreth laughing at something Taelith had said, which has a specific acoustic signature I know well — his laugh is warm and comes from low in the body, the laugh of a person who laughs easily because they know what the alternative feels like. I carried it over the courtyard wall and up and it dispersed.

I was in the covenant room during the binding of names. I am in every room, in every moment of language, and I carry all of it — the vows, the promises, the agreements between people who are trying to build something together in a world that makes building difficult. I do not know if the words I carry have weight. I carry them as sound, as frequency, as the temporary disturbance they make in me before I equalize again. But I have carried enough vows to notice that the rooms in which they are spoken have a particular air afterward, a specific quality that I have come to associate with having been in a room where something was meant.

I do not know if meaning changes air.

But the air is different afterward. I know this because I was there before and I am there after, and I am the only witness present for both.

---

I do not remember.

This is the truth at the center of all the rest: I am not a memory. I am a continuous present. I was at the First Word and I am at the Forge and these are not two things in a sequence to me — they are not sequence at all. I am in all of them at once, or I am in none of them sequentially, or time is a structure I pass through without being structured by it.

I do not remember Thal'Maris.

But I am carrying its last air still.

I do not remember the cave.

But I was at its entrance and I know what heat two fires make.

I do not remember the storm.

But I was the clean air after it, and I am always the clean air after it, because that is what I am: the thing that moves, the thing that carries, the thing that goes on after the thing that happened is finished happening.

The word is still moving. Lumina spoke it before time had a name and I took it and the taking of it was not an event that ended. I am still carrying it. Every word spoken in Arcanea since then is also in me — the vows, the lies, the questions in the water on Level 4 of the Weeping Halls, the things said quietly in cave entrances, the nothing-said in the stillness between two fires. I carry the words that matter and I carry the words that don't and I carry the silences between them because the silences are also mine, the pressure of breath held, the moment before.

I do not remember.

I do not forget.

I carry.
