# The Gate of Foundation

## Book One of the Arcanea Saga

---

# Chapter Ten: The Foundation Opens

The Dungeon did not announce itself the second time either.

It was there when they came down from the Luminari mirror at dawn — the Atlantean passage had taken two hours, the mirror-shift adding its usual quality of being briefly unlocated, and the world was still in the gold-rose of the campus's permanent morning when they walked through the main gate and Kael felt it. Not underfoot. Not as a sound. As an attention. The same quality as the limestone crack on the road from Ashenmere — invitation rather than demand, the dungeon knowing they were coming and making room.

He stopped at the gate.

Riven, beside him, stopped too. "What?"

"It's ready," Kael said.

Corenne, who had come through the mirror with them, said nothing. She had been saying nothing in the way of someone who already knew what was going to happen and had decided the right posture was patient attention rather than announcement. She was looking at the path toward the Academy's older wing, toward the broom-cupboard door and the staircase and the sealed chamber below.

But that was not where the feeling was coming from.

The Foundation Dungeon was not in the mountain.

It was in the mountain, it was under the Academy, it was connected to the Void-node and the sealed chamber and the deep geological ley-network that ran beneath all three campuses — but it was presenting on the eastern slope. A section of the Academy grounds that Kael had walked past a dozen times: a retaining wall of old limestone, grown over with the small white flowers, set into the hill at the base of the primary practice yards.

There was a door in the wall that had not been there yesterday.

He was looking at it from fifty yards away and he could feel it in the soles of his feet.

"That's—" Ryn said, from behind them. He had come through the mirror last, was still adjusting his satchel, had his head up and his instruments out. "That's resonating at a frequency I haven't—" He got out the device Kael had seen him use on the moor, the one for leyline mapping, and it promptly began doing something it had not been designed to do: all five of its indicator needles were moving simultaneously, cycling, as if unable to decide which element to report. "Right," Ryn said. "Okay. That's the Dungeon."

---

Taelis was in the practice yard above.

He was up early — Taelis was always up early, another quality Kael associated with people who had learned that the hours before others woke were the hours when the truest version of themselves could move without witnesses. He was doing the Draconian standing form in the half-light, alone, and he looked up when Kael came up the path toward him.

He looked at the door in the retaining wall.

Then at Kael.

"It's been there since the third bell," he said. "I've been watching it."

"You weren't going to tell anyone?"

"I was going to tell you when you arrived." He dropped the standing form with the easiness of someone for whom the form was so deeply incorporated that stopping it required no conscious act. "Are you going in?"

"Yes."

"Are we going with you?" He said it calmly, without marking the weight of the question. But the question had weight. The Dungeon had been presenting differently from canon since the road from Ashenmere. The limestone crack that had invited three. The carved figures at the bottom of the pool. The first dungeon had said: this works in company. The question was whether the Foundation Dungeon was saying the same.

Kael looked at the door. He listened to the frequency in the soles of his feet. The dungeon's attention was broad — not focused on him, not excluding the others. More like — the way a room feels when the person who lives there has opened it to guests, has left it prepared for several people to be present, has not specified who but has clearly planned for more than one.

"I think it's asking all of us," Kael said.

Riven was already in the courtyard, having come up the path a minute behind Kael. He was looking at the door with the expression of someone running the relevant risk calculations and reaching conclusions he did not enjoy. "The canonical Foundation Dungeon floods," he said. "The Godbeast's test is survival. Claustrophobia. Rising water. The stripping of the capacity to climb or swim."

"I know," Kael said.

"None of us is a Water-mage."

"Corenne has Water primary."

"Corenne has Water primary and the most undocumented ability set of anyone in three Academies." Riven looked at Corenne, who was standing at the edge of the courtyard in the same quality of stillness she had maintained since they came through the mirror. "I mean no disrespect."

"None taken," Corenne said.

"I'm cataloguing."

"I know." She looked at the door. "We should go in."

---

The door opened when Kael touched it.

Not dramatic — no mechanism, no grinding stone, nothing that would have made a suitable entrance in a story. The limestone simply received him. Present, open, available. He walked through.

The others followed.

The passage was narrower than the staircase beneath the Academy, and darker — Ryn's lamp came out immediately, and Kael held the thread of Fire between his fingers, and they moved in a small sphere of orange-gold light that threw shadows that did not quite correspond to the shapes casting them. This was not the secondary library in the Academy, not a constructed space, not something maintained by human attention for centuries. This was geological. Raw limestone, cool and wet, the smell of mineral time.

Corenne's hand was light on the wall. She was reading the location-memory, Kael understood — the centuries of Dungeon-attempts stored in this stone, the people who had come through this passage and found it something different from what he would find it. Or the same.

They descended. The passage leveled. The air changed — colder, denser, carrying a pressure that was not atmospheric but elemental, the weight of five elements at high concentration in an enclosed space.

The passage opened.

---

The first level was water.

Not rising water — present water, immediate water. A chamber of floor-to-ceiling dark stone except for the center, which was a pool occupying the entire floor. No dry path around it. Still. Clear. By the lamp-light, perhaps knee-deep.

Ryn checked it with his instruments, which confirmed the obvious: Water-element saturation, moderate Earth-element density in the stone around them, trace of Fire from Kael's flame. Nothing dangerous. Nothing except the fact that to reach the far wall, which had a passage cut into it, they would have to walk through.

They walked through.

The water was cold. Not dangerously cold — the specific cold of mountain springs, the cold that woke the body without punishing it. The pool was exactly knee-deep. The bottom was stone, smooth, secure. They made the crossing in sixty seconds and emerged dripping onto a dry ledge.

"One," Taelis said.

The second level was dark.

Ryn's lamp went out the moment they passed the threshold. Not extinguished — absorbed, the light taken gently from the flame and not returned. Kael's Fire-thread died the same way. Total darkness. The kind of darkness that has physical weight, that exists in the body as a distinct sensation rather than simply the absence of vision.

Ryn immediately began using his devices, which produced no light but allowed him to map the chamber's dimensions by leyline measurement, describing in quiet precise language what he was charting. "Roughly circular, twelve meters across, two entries including the one we came through. The second entry is eight meters north-northeast. Floor is even. No obstacles I can detect in the ley-field."

They moved in the dark. By touch — Kael's hand on the wall, Riven's hand on Kael's shoulder, Taelis just behind, Corenne last and unguided, finding her own way. They moved slowly. The darkness pressed in and they moved through it the way you move through all the difficult things: one step, then the next, then the step after that.

The second entry was where Ryn said it was.

"Two," Taelis said.

The third level was sound.

Not threatening sound — the opposite of threatening. The sound that met them was the low continuous note of the Foundation Gate, the 174 Hz frequency that Kael had been hearing in his bones since before he had words for it. The dream-voice, the lighthouse window, the dungeon on the Ashenmere road. Here it was not submerged, not subtle: it filled the chamber completely, resonating in the stone walls and the stone floor and the stone ceiling, resonating in the bodies of the people standing in it. The sound of something enormous being very still.

They stood in it for a moment.

Riven's breath changed. The sound did something to the controlled architecture of him — Kael felt it without looking, the same way he had felt Taelis's center of gravity in the training yard, the elements reading the people around him through the shared medium of the stone. Riven was moved. He was standing in the sound and something in it was moving through him the way it had moved through the practice yard all those weeks ago, the Earth-practice settling, the quality of what-will-hold being recognized.

Taelis was still in the same way he was still when he was reading.

Corenne was the most still of all of them, which was not unusual, but the quality of it here was different — not the stillness of management but the stillness of homecoming. She had been here before in the memory she carried. She was matching the present to what she held.

Ryn had put away his instruments. He was just listening.

"Three," Kael said.

---

The fourth level was choice.

A corridor, long and straight, with two doors at the end. Not labeled. Identical to each other in every visible way: same stone, same width, same darkness beyond. The corridor had a quality that made Kael feel the walls closer than they were — not actual claustrophobia, but the ley-signature of the space pressing inward, testing the capacity to think clearly in constriction.

They stood at the two doors for a while.

"Both feel the same," Riven said. He was using his Wind-sensitivity to read the air coming from each passage. "Same temperature. Same elemental density."

"One of them is wrong," Taelis said.

"You don't know that."

"The Dungeon has made us wet, then blind, then still, then constricted. The next test is—" He paused. "The kind of decision where being wrong has a cost."

"Which one?" Ryn asked. Not rhetorical — he was already writing.

Kael stood at the junction. He did not reach with any element. He stood with the weight of the mountain under his feet, the Foundation note still humming in the stone, and he asked the question the stone had been teaching him to ask since the first practice yard at Luminari: *What remains?*

Left door: the ley-signature of the five elements at balanced density. Equal. Controlled. The signature of a path that was safe.

Right door: the ley-signature of the five elements at unbalanced density. Not aggressive — differential, the way the real world was differential, Fire here and Water there and Void threading through in irregular currents. The signature of a path that was honest.

"Right," Kael said.

No one asked him to justify it. This was, possibly, the truest expression of the three months they had spent together: the willingness to follow his navigation without demanding the reasoning first.

The right door opened onto level five.

---

The fifth level was fire.

Not the Fire of the Draconian arena. Not the disciplined, directed Fire of Hael's instruction. Ambient fire — fire that was part of the chamber itself, lived in the walls, breathed from the stone the way the deep Forge breathed. The walls were warm. The air tasted of clean burning. The light was the color of the inside of a coal at the end of its life: red-dark, nearly dark, entirely hot.

They were sweating within thirty seconds.

"Earth channeling," Taelis said, immediately. "Ground contact. The fire is in the stone — Earth-channeling gives you the stone's heat regulation. Don't fight the temperature. Become part of the thermal system."

This was Draconian method applied to a Dungeon situation. Taelis did not wait to be asked. He went into his standing form and his body heat stabilized visibly — the sweat stopped, the quality of his breathing changed. He looked at Kael.

Kael went into the standing form. The Earth came up from beneath the fire — below the hot stone, the geological earth, patient and enormous and entirely indifferent to the surface temperature. He let the Earth regulate the contact. Not cooling him from outside — equilibrating him with the thermal system from below. He was neither cooler nor hotter. He was appropriate.

The others followed Taelis's direction. Riven's Wind-channeling instinct was to push the heat away; Taelis quietly corrected him, and Riven changed technique without debate. Ryn pressed his hands to the floor and let the Earth-contact do the work.

Corenne needed no instruction. She stood in the fire-warmth with the same quality she brought to everything: present, undefended, allowing the experience to be what it was without managing it into something else.

Level five opened when they had been in it for ten minutes. Not because they had done anything. Because the Dungeon had measured something it needed to measure, and they had been in it long enough to demonstrate the quality the test required.

Patience, Kael thought. Not endurance. Actual patience — the willingness to remain in discomfort without making the discomfort into an emergency.

"Five," Taelis said.

---

The sixth level was the hardest.

Not physically. Not elementally. The sixth level was simply a room, completely empty, lit by a cool clean light with no visible source, and the room did nothing to them for what felt like a very long time.

Nothing happened.

They stood in the clean light and waited and nothing happened. No element manifested. No test presented. The ley-signature was present and steady and not building toward anything. The room simply — held them. It was not uncomfortable. It was not testing their patience, because patience was level five. This was something else.

It took Kael twelve minutes to understand what the room was asking.

"It wants us to stop waiting," he said.

Everyone looked at him.

"The test is not patience. It's presence. Actually being here, not waiting to move through here." He paused. "We've been treating every level as an obstacle. Walk through the water, navigate the dark, survive the fire. This room isn't an obstacle. This room is the Foundation note." He felt for it in the stone. It was there, steady, 174 Hz, the hum of something enormous being perfectly still. "Foundation Gate first lesson: feel the weight of the world through the soles of your feet. Not to move through it. To be in it."

He sat down.

The floor was smooth limestone. He sat on it the way Corenne had sat on the floor of the sealed chamber, cross-legged, with the quality of someone who had decided this was the appropriate posture for this particular moment in this particular place.

The others sat.

They sat in the clean light for a while. Just sitting. Just present in the geological patience of a room that had been here for longer than the Academy above it, waiting for people to simply — be in it, rather than perform being in it.

Kael felt the Foundation note in the stone under him. He felt the five elements at their balanced density — not demanding anything, not moving toward anything. Simply present, in the same space, being what they were.

He felt Riven beside him go still in a way he had not been still in any of the other levels. Not the stillness of alertness or the stillness of tension. The stillness of actually resting. It lasted perhaps thirty seconds and then Riven caught himself and the stillness changed quality — but thirty seconds was real.

The room opened.

"Six," Corenne said.

---

The seventh level was deep.

Deeper than the sealed chamber. Deeper than anywhere Kael had been in any of the three campuses. The passage down was angled steeply and the air pressure changed as they descended, not to something painful but to something deeply old: the pressure of geological time, the weight of strata above strata above strata, the feeling of being at the root of something that extended upward for distances that had no human names.

The chamber at the bottom was enormous in a way that the lamplight could not fully map. Kael's Fire-thread was back here — the lower layers of the Dungeon had not suppressed it. He raised it, let it grow, let it illuminate in all directions at once.

The chamber's walls were natural limestone, uncut, shaped by ancient water into curves that were not quite organic and not quite geometric. Fossils in the stone: not individual specimens but layers, strata of what had lived and settled and become the rock. The history of the world pressed into the walls in geological notation.

The floor was dry.

At the center of the floor was a door.

Not a door into the wall. A door standing free in the chamber — a frame of old stone with no visible structure supporting it, a threshold that was simply present in the middle of the open floor. On this side of the door: the Dungeon, the lamplight, the five of them, the weight of stone and time. On the other side of the door: dark, but not the dark of the second level. A different dark. The dark of something that had its own quality, its own density, its own temperature.

The door had no mechanism. No handle. No inscription.

Kael walked toward it.

He stopped at the threshold and looked at the surface of the door itself. It was stone — the same limestone as everything else here, but worked. Not carved. Worked in the way stone was worked by someone who understood it as a living material: pressure and time and attention rather than tools, shaped into a surface that was exactly flat in a way that required enormous care.

The surface was a mirror.

Not polished stone, not glass, not anything manufactured. The stone itself had been worked to a flatness that reflected — not clearly, not the way a mirror reflected, but the way still water reflected: with honesty, and with the quality of depth, showing you not just the surface image but the thing below the surface.

He looked at his reflection.

The eyes. All five elements in the iris, clearly visible, familiar to him now in a way they had not been in Torven's kitchen three months ago. The hands. The scar-lines, the frost-patterns, no gloves. The face of someone who had been in water and darkness and fire in the last three hours and had not been destroyed by any of it.

The reflection was him. There was no ambiguity about it, no mystical substitution, no alternate version. It was him, precisely, as he was right now.

He put his hand on the stone door.

The five elements responded.

---

Not the way they had responded on the cliff — not the sudden, overwhelming waking of things that had been asleep. This was different. The response of things that were awake, that had been awake and working for three months, that recognized what was being asked and brought themselves forward with the measured quality of people who understood the weight of what they were doing.

Fire: in the sternum, the old home, the first thing that had woken. Not the house-fire of the Forge arena. Not the suppressed coal-heat of the early weeks. Fire that knew itself. Knew its cost. Came forward anyway, because coming forward was what it was for.

Water: in the blood, in the particular cold of the deep-water training in the Atlantean levels, in something Kael realized he had been learning without noticing — the capacity to be receptive, to receive what arrived rather than reaching for it. Water that had been there since the dream-voices and the lighthouse window, finally present in the body as something with a name.

Earth: in the soles of the feet and in the standing-method and in the stone-certainty of the dungeon three months ago and in the inscription and in everything that had happened since. Earth that knew how to hold without consuming, how to remain without rigidity.

Wind: faint still, the element he had felt least clearly, but present — in the breath, in the space between his feet and the stone, in the particular quality of presence-without-holding that he had been circling toward since Solvane's room. Wind that didn't grip.

And Void.

The Void rose from below — not from the stone this time, not from the geological node or the sealed chamber or the familiar echo at depth. From inside. From the place Kael had been learning, slowly and without framework, to call his own: the space in the center where the other four elements met and were kept distinct from each other.

The relational principle. The word between words.

The Void came up like a tide and it was not threatening and it was not hungry and it was not a door opening to something outside.

It was a door opening to what was already here.

The stone door became transparent.

Not glass — transparent in the way that understanding makes things transparent, the way a word learned in a foreign language suddenly makes the sentence you've been looking at make sense. The stone was still stone. The stone was showing what it was: limestone, pressure-formed, ancient, full of the record of every creature that had lived and died and settled into the earth over geological time. He was looking at the history of life written in the gates of a dungeon.

He pressed his hand flat on the transparent surface.

The Foundation Gate opened.

---

He did not have words for what it was. He had words afterward — he found them in the weeks after, building a description from the outside in, the way you built a description of anything you had been inside. But at the moment of opening, there were no words.

What there was: a frequency. Not heard — experienced in the entire body simultaneously, in the cells and the bones and the specific places where the five elements made their home in his physiology. 174 Hz, yes — the Foundation note, the hum he had been hearing since the cliff and the lighthouse and the dungeon on the road. But resolved. Not a single note but a chord, the single frequency revealing itself as the fundamental of a harmonic structure that had been there all along, waiting for his range to expand enough to hear the overtones.

What there was: Kaelith.

Not a presence in the room. Not a face or a voice or any of the material metaphors. A quality of attention, vast and patient and geological, recognizing what had just opened in the same way that a river recognizes that the ice above it has broken. Not with urgency. With the certainty of something that has been waiting without anxiety, because it understood that the waiting was a temporary condition and the opening was always going to happen.

*Stone Certainty,* it said — the voice from the first dungeon, but here it was not a phrase in passing. It was a name given back: the name of a quality that had always been in him, named by the Godbeast that held the Gate.

What there was: himself, but wider. The self that existed before the practiced smallness, before the gloves and the pockets and the carefully managed eyes. The self that had been present on the cliff — not the capacity or the power but the willingness, the love of being present for what comes next, the fundamental quality that had survived eight years of deliberate diminishment and was still there, intact, waiting to be stood in.

What there was: his hands. The scar-lines illuminated fully for the first time, all five colors at once, not faint but clear — red-gold and blue-silver and green-brown and white and the deep not-color that the Void showed when it was not hiding. His hands in the light of the open Gate.

And then — at the edge of that light, at the furthest reach of what the opened Foundation Gate showed him through the transparent stone of the door —

Malachar.

---

Not the Dark Lord. Not the corruption. Not the absence-made-manifest that the histories described, the thing that had tried to end existence as a mercy. This was something before all of that, or something that had persisted through all of that — the part that was still the man. The part that had stood in the sealed chamber and carved *I was right* into the stone because it needed to be said even if no one could hear it.

He was visible for three seconds. Maybe less. A figure at enormous distance — not in this chamber, not in this world, in the Shadowfen that Kael had only known as a name in records, the sealed place at the root of the Worldtree where the containment had held for twelve hundred years.

He was looking back.

The moment Kael saw him, Malachar's gaze turned. Not searching — as if he had always known the direction and was waiting for the line of sight to open. As if he had been facing this way for a hundred years.

The expression on his face was not the expression of a Dark Lord. Not malevolent. Not threatening. Not the fixed, cold certainty of someone who had decided existence was an error.

It was the expression of someone in an enormously long grief who had just seen, for the first time in twelve hundred years, something that surprised them.

He was smiling.

Not a victorious smile. Not a corrupt smile. The smile of recognition — a man who had understood something alone for a very long time, who had carved it into stone in a language no one could read, who had sealed it in a chamber under a mountain and waited, and who was now, across twelve hundred years and an impossible distance, watching someone young stand in the same understanding and not be alone in it.

*You found it,* the smile said. Without sound. Without transmission. Not a sending — a recognition. *You found what I found. You are not alone in it.*

The Gate closed.

---

Kael came back to himself on the chamber floor. He had not fallen — he was kneeling, both hands flat on the limestone, the stone cold against his palms. His scar-lines were still lit, dimming slowly, the five colors fading toward their ordinary faint presence.

The door stood closed in front of him.

He was aware of the others without turning around. The quality of their attention behind him — not alarmed, not rushing toward him. Present. Waiting to know what had happened before deciding what to do about it.

He sat back on his heels. He breathed.

"Kael," Riven said. One word. Not a question.

"I'm here," Kael said.

He stood.

He turned.

The four of them were arranged in an arc behind him, each face showing something different. Riven: controlled, but the control working to contain something real that would not be named in this chamber but was present and would be named later. Taelis: steady, measuring, the slow assessment of someone taking a careful reading. Ryn: notebook out, pen moving, documenting — and eyes wide, eyes that had seen something in the lamp-light's reaction to the Gate-opening he would spend weeks trying to explain to himself. Corenne: neutral, but underneath it the closest to warmth she showed in his presence — the expression of someone whose long watching has arrived at a destination.

"Are you injured?" Taelis asked. Practical, direct, the first useful question.

"No."

"Are you—" He paused, choosing the word. "Stable."

"Yes." Kael thought about it. The truthful answer, not the reassuring one. The Foundation note was still there, in the soles of his feet, in the stone under them, in the geological patience of everything below. He was standing on it. He was not standing on it differently than before. He was standing on it with the knowledge that it was open, which was different, but it was still there. "Yes. Stable. Present."

"What happened?" Ryn asked. The pen was still.

Kael told them. Not all of it — not the quality of the experience, the chord-structure of the frequency, the vast recognition of Kaelith, the wideness of himself that the Gate had opened onto. He told them what could be reported accurately: the elements engaging, the Gate opening, the three seconds at the end.

When he told them about Malachar, the chamber went very still.

"He was smiling," Riven said.

"Yes."

"That is—" Riven stopped. Started again. "That is either the most significant thing you've told me or the most alarming thing, and I cannot determine which."

"It's both," Corenne said. She had been still throughout the account. Now she looked at Kael with the expression of someone making a final confirmation. "He saw you. Not metaphorically. He saw you across the seal. The Gate-opening created a line of sight."

"For three seconds."

"Three seconds is not nothing." She was looking at the door — stone again, opaque, sealed. "He has been waiting twelve hundred years. Three seconds of someone standing in the understanding he was alone in — that is not nothing."

"The seal—" Riven said.

"The seal is intact," Corenne said. "The Gate-opening doesn't weaken the seal. It is a different mechanism. The Shadowfen seal is elemental — it is Earth and Water and Wind layered in containment. The Foundation Gate is a threshold. They do not interact." She paused. "What interacted was — recognition. The understanding that he was alone in, that he wrote in ciphered stone in a dead language in a sealed chamber, and that Kael has now found and stands in — that recognition is now shared. Between two people. Across twelve hundred years and an elemental containment."

"Is that dangerous?" Taelis asked.

Corenne was quiet for a moment. "I think it is the opposite of dangerous," she said. "I think it is why the resonance exists. I think it is what Tessara Vole was trying to reach when she went north." She looked at Kael. "He was alone in the correct understanding and it curdled. The understanding is not wrong. The aloneness was the problem."

"He's not alone anymore," Kael said.

"For three seconds," Riven said. "Through a sealed Gate that is now closed."

"Yes." Kael looked at the door. "For now."

The word carried more than he intended it to, and less than was true, and everyone in the chamber understood this.

---

They came up out of the Dungeon as the campus's permanent dawn was reaching its gold-rose peak. The sky was immense, the air above the cloudline clean and cold and distance-smelling. The small white flowers on the retaining wall had, in the hours since the door had been there, grown around the door's frame — not obscuring it, outlining it. As if the flowers had known.

Cors was in the courtyard.

She was standing at the edge of the practice yard with her notes and her pen and the expression of someone who had been informed and had come to see the evidence for herself. She looked at Kael. At his hands. At the five of them emerging from the door in the retaining wall that had not been there yesterday.

She looked at the flowers.

"When?" she said.

"Two hours ago," Kael said.

She wrote something down. "How does it feel?"

Kael stood in the courtyard with the Foundation note still running through the stone under his feet, the opened Gate present in his chest like a chord struck and not yet faded, the image of a man's smiling face across twelve hundred years and an impossible distance. He stood there and felt all of it and did not close any of it off and did not require any of it to be simpler than it was.

"Like the beginning of something," he said.

Cors wrote it down. Capped the pen. Looked at the sky. "That's going to make the Convocation very anxious."

"I know."

"They'll want a full account."

"I know that too." He looked at his companions — Riven already formulating the appropriate language for a formal report, Taelis with the steady openness he carried through everything, Ryn writing in his notebook at a speed that suggested he was going to need more pages, Corenne standing in the morning light with something that was, on her face, the quiet expression of a person who has arrived at the end of a very long wait. "We'll give them one."

"And after?"

Kael looked east. Toward the Draconian Forge. Toward the Atlantean Depths. Toward the road north, which he had not been on, which ended somewhere in the world's deep geography at the root of a Worldtree where twelve hundred years of waiting had just been interrupted by a three-second line of sight.

"After," he said, "we keep going."

The Foundation Gate was open.

Everything else was still ahead.
