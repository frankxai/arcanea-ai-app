# The Gate of Foundation

## Book One of the Arcanea Saga

---

# Chapter Eight: The Tide Archives

The ocean was not what Kael expected.

He had grown up with the sea. He had spent four years at the lighthouse above Ashenmere's harbor, watching it in all its moods — the flat silver of calm mornings, the iron gray of coming gales, the rare moments of green clarity when the light came down at just the right angle and you could see, for a few seconds, the shapes of things far below the surface. He had thought he knew it.

He had known its surface.

The Atlantean mirror deposited them in a descending chamber — not underwater yet, just deep, a limestone shaft that went down through the ocean floor the way a well goes down through a field, except the well shaft here was a hundred yards across and lined with the coral formations that the Atlantean architects had been growing into structural purpose for centuries. The air was different. Not the clean salt smell of open sea but something older and more mineral: the particular cold of depth, of pressure, of water that had been the same temperature for longer than civilizations.

Sela was waiting at the bottom of the shaft.

She looked as though she had been waiting for some time, which meant she had probably been waiting for exactly the right amount of time. She had three notebooks already open and ink on her left hand. Ryn was beside her, looking up the shaft with the expression of someone who had just understood a geological process he had been theorizing about and was now adjusting the theory for material contact.

"You look like the Forge treated you interestingly," Sela said.

"Yes," Kael said.

She looked at his hands. At the absence of gloves. Something moved in her expression — brief, not quite a smile, the register she used when a development had confirmed a prediction she had not been certain of. "Good," she said. "The Depths will require both hands."

"That's not ominous at all," Ryn said, still looking up.

"It's a literal statement. The passage to the first level requires channeled contact on both sides. You can't enter with gloves."

"Right," Ryn said. "Definitely not ominous."

---

The descent happened in stages.

The first stage was the Entry Dome — a chamber the size of the Luminari atrium, air-filled and lit by the bioluminescence of the coral walls, where new students received the Atlantean breathing modification: a gentle Water-channeling technique that Sela administered with the same precision she brought to everything, a pattern drawn in the air just above Kael's sternum that settled into the body like a key into a lock and made the lungs able to process the deep-water air. Not underwater breathing — nothing so dramatic. Simply the capacity to function at pressures that would otherwise produce the symptoms of surfacing-too-fast in reverse.

"This will last a week," she said. "After that you will need to reapply it or ascend to the upper levels before sleep."

"How many levels are there?"

"Seven accessible ones. The eighth level is—" She paused. "The eighth level has not been fully mapped. We know it exists."

"How do you know it exists if it hasn't been mapped?"

She looked at him. "Because the maps stop before the depth does."

Stage two was the descent proper — a glass passage that extended from the Entry Dome's floor downward through the ocean floor itself, angled at forty-five degrees, dropping through layers of rock and sediment and then, suddenly, into open water. The glass was not glass. It was compressed water, held in shape by a continuous channeling technique so old that its originator's name had been lost, but which had been running without interruption for eleven hundred years. Walking through it was like walking through something that was being a wall very hard, by choice, and was prepared to continue being a wall for as long as necessary, but was fundamentally still water.

Kael felt it as he passed the boundary. The Void stirred — not intrusively, just noting the quality of the construct, the way you note a piece of fine craftsmanship without intending to touch it.

Below the ocean floor, the world changed.

---

The Atlantean Depths were a city the way the ocean was water — technically accurate and inadequate in every important way.

The Depths occupied a section of the Meridian Trench that nature had, over geological time, produced as a series of enormous interconnected caverns in the seafloor limestone. The Atlanteans had been living there for twelve hundred years, and the twelve hundred years of habitation showed not as construction but as growth: the coral architecture that made up the building stock was alive, continually growing, shaped by generations of Water-mages into forms that served human needs while remaining functionally oceanic. The walls breathed. The floors grew. The light — cold blue and silver and the deep teal that Kael would later understand was bioluminescence from a species of coral cultivated for nothing else — moved with the slow pulsation of living things.

The first level was the widest. Commerce, housing, the ordinary infrastructure of a community of several thousand people who had collectively decided the middle of the ocean floor was an appropriate place to live. Kael had expected the oppressive quality of deep enclosed spaces. He had not expected the openness of it — the Atlantean architects had been solving the problem of limited space for twelve hundred years, and their solutions produced the paradox of an underground city that felt lighter than the sky: high ceilings shaped to bounce light upward, corridors that opened onto sudden drops revealing deeper levels, vistas through the glass walls to the open water beyond where the trench extended beyond sight into dark.

Things moved in the dark beyond the glass. Very large things, moving slowly, lights traveling along their edges like lamps carried by something vast and unhurried.

"What are those?" Ryn asked.

"The greater whales," Sela said. "The ones that have been here since before the Academy was built. They're not hostile. They're not friendly either. They're—" She considered. "Contemporary. They were here when we arrived, and they understand that we're here, and they have decided this is an acceptable arrangement."

One of the lights paused at the glass wall. An eye resolved out of the dark — enormous, pale, ancient in the way that living things rarely were. It looked at Kael.

Or it looked at the space where Kael was. Kael had the sense that whatever the whale was perceiving, it was not exactly him in the way that most observers perceived him.

The eye moved away, back into the dark, carrying its small light.

"It felt the Void," Corenne said. She had been silent since the descent, which was her normal register, but she had the quality here of someone reading a book they had already read — turning pages she knew by heart, waiting for the passage that would be different this time. "The whales are Void-sensitive. They navigate by it, partly — they read the absence-qualities in the water. The empty spaces. They know when something changes in the elemental density."

"Did it decide something?" Kael asked.

"It noticed something," Corenne said. "What it decided, I can't tell you."

---

Corenne guided them to the third level, which was where the Archives began.

The Tide Archives were the Atlantean Depths' primary reason for existing, which was its own kind of institutional logic: the city had grown around the library rather than the other way. The building stock of the third level was entirely devoted to storage and scholarship. The coral walls here were dry — or as dry as anything got at this depth, which was considerably dryer than the upper levels, maintained by a continuous low-level channeling that pulled moisture out of the air and directed it into the floor drainage. The effect was a dryness that felt unnatural in context, like finding a room-temperature space inside a glacier.

The Archives themselves were not arranged by subject, which had struck every outside scholar who had ever visited as a significant organizational failure, and which the Atlanteans had been defending as a deliberate philosophical position for six hundred years. "Knowledge is not a tree," Tidecaller Pelagius had written, in the official response to the last Luminari request for a categorical index. "It is a current. You cannot separate a current into labeled segments without killing it. You can only learn to navigate."

Navigation, Sela explained as she led them through corridors that branched and merged without obvious pattern, was a skill taught to Atlantean students in their second year. In their first year, they got lost. The getting-lost was part of the curriculum.

"So are we meant to get lost?" Ryn asked.

"Not this time. I know where I'm going." She turned a corner into a section of corridor that was narrower, the coral walls pressing closer, lit by a string of bioluminescent organisms that pulsed in slow sequence, a living light-rope. "The historical records on multi-element channelers are in the fourth section of the deep wing. Third level, east face. Normally restricted to fellows and above."

"You have access," Kael said.

"I requested it for this visit specifically. After the Forge." She turned again. "The Warden wrote a supporting letter. Ashcroft."

"Ashcroft wrote—"

"He wrote three pages on why the research was necessary. It was—" She paused in the way she paused when a thing had surprised her and she was deciding whether to characterize the surprise. "Very thorough."

The corridor opened into a room that was not a room so much as a collection of niches in the coral wall, each niche containing a sealed water-vellum scroll or a bound archive-book or a flat tablet of compressed stone-memory, each one labeled in the Atlantean shorthand script that took two years to read fluently and looked, to the uninitiated, like someone had written in two languages simultaneously while experiencing turbulence.

Sela went directly to a lower set of niches without consulting any index. She pulled three bound archives and a stone-memory tablet and laid them on the reading shelf that ran the length of the room like a keel.

She looked at Kael.

"Sit," she said. "This will take time."

---

The historical record of multi-element channelers was longer than Kael had expected.

He had been told — by Sela's voice on the lighthouse steps, by the careful absence of information in the Convocation meeting, by the way people looked at him when they thought he wasn't watching — that he and Malachar were the only cases. The only two. The singular anomaly, twelve hundred years apart, with nothing between.

That was not what the Archives said.

"There were eight," Sela said, moving through the records with a systematic speed. "That we know of. Possibly more — the pre-Siege records are incomplete for obvious reasons, and there's a gap in the second century of the current age that suggests a deliberate purge of certain documentation." She pressed a flat hand to the stone-memory tablet, which was the Atlantean equivalent of reading a text at high speed: direct impression of the contents through the Water-sensitivity in the palm. "Eight confirmed multi-element channelers in the twelve hundred years between Malachar's fall and the present. None of them Five-Fold. All of them three or four elements."

"What happened to them?" Ryn asked. He had positioned himself at the other end of the reading shelf with a notebook and was taking notes in the three-column format he used for research he considered significant.

Sela was quiet for a moment, her hand still on the tablet. "Four died young. Two were killed in what the records describe as 'containment incidents' — which is Archives language for things that cannot be called accidents without being misleading." She moved her hand. "One reached Archmage rank and died in the Shadowfen Breach at age thirty-four. One—" She stopped.

Kael was watching her. "What about the one?"

"One was a woman named Tessara Vole. Water, Earth, and Void affinity. Born in the coastal provinces, two hundred and seventy years ago. She reached Master rank at twenty-one, which was the fastest in three generations." Sela opened a different archive. "She disappeared at twenty-three. Not died — disappeared. The record here is — marked." She tilted the page. In the margin of the water-vellum, something had been written in a different hand than the rest of the entry. A later addition, by someone who had access to the archive and had chosen to annotate rather than alter.

Kael read it. His ability to read Atlantean shorthand was three weeks old and rough, but the annotation was in the older form, slower and more letter-by-letter, as if the person writing had been choosing words from an unfamiliar alphabet.

*She asked not to be followed. We did not follow. — N.P.*

"Nerissa Pelagius," he said.

Sela looked at the annotation. Looked at him. "Yes."

"She annotated someone's record two hundred and seventy years ago."

"She was the Tidecaller then too. She has been the Tidecaller for four hundred years." Sela closed the archive. "Tessara Vole asked not to be followed and Nerissa Pelagius respected that and made a note of having respected it. Which means Nerissa Pelagius knew what happened to her and is the only person who does and has kept that knowledge for two hundred and seventy years."

The room was very quiet. The bioluminescent light pulsed slow and cold.

"I should ask her," Kael said.

"Probably." Sela's tone carried something careful in it. "Though I would note that Nerissa Pelagius has had four hundred years to share that information with anyone she chose to share it with, and she has not."

"Which means she's been waiting for someone to ask."

"Or she's been waiting to be asked in the right way, by the right person." Sela pulled the third archive. "Let me show you the others first. I want you to understand the pattern before you go to her."

---

The pattern was not what he had hoped.

The eight documented multi-element channelers had, between them, produced four consistent outcomes:

Containment. This was the one that appeared most often — a decision, made either by the channeler themselves or by the Academies around them, that the full expression of their ability was too dangerous to pursue. Four of the eight had been contained. Three of the four had died, the Archives noted, in ways that involved the contained ability eventually expressing anyway, catastrophically, because suppression was not stability.

Destruction. Two of the eight. Both in the context of combat, both cases where the multi-element capacity had been used as a weapon and had performed exactly as well as a weapon, including the aspects of weapons that were not selective about what they destroyed.

Disappearance. One. Tessara Vole, with Nerissa's annotation and no further record.

Attempt. One. The last one. A boy named Aldric Voss, three hundred and ten years ago, Water and Fire and Earth, who had attempted to open a Gate and had made it to the third level of the Foundation Dungeon before the attempt killed him. Not the Dungeon — the attempt itself. The combination of three elements simultaneously moving through a Gate-threshold had produced a feedback event. His instructors had described it afterward, in their Convocation report, as watching someone try to carry a river in cupped hands.

"The feedback," Kael said. He had been reading the Convocation report for ten minutes while the others moved through other records. "The elements moving in the same channel simultaneously — they don't combine the way you'd expect."

"No." Sela was reading over his shoulder, which she did without ceremony. "In a single-element channeler, the Gate-opening is a negotiation: the channeler's will meets the Gate's threshold, the element passes through, the Gate opens. Sequential. In a multi-element channeler, if more than one element is active in the channel at once — "

"They don't negotiate. They compete." Kael looked at the Convocation report. "Each element is trying to open a different Gate simultaneously. The body can't arbitrate between them fast enough."

"That's the current theory."

"Was it his theory too?"

Sela was quiet for a moment. "Aldric Voss wrote a paper," she said. "It's in the restricted section — I have temporary access to the summary." She moved to the stone-memory tablet again and pressed her hand to it. Something in her posture changed — the slight stiffening that meant she had found something she had been looking for and was not entirely prepared for what it felt like to find it. "He thought the elements didn't compete in the channel. He thought they competed for the channel because there was only one. He thought—" She stopped.

"What did he think?"

"He thought the solution was not to sequence them or suppress them but to make the channel wider." She took her hand from the tablet. "He died trying to test the hypothesis."

Kael sat with this for a moment. The light pulsed around them. In the water beyond the corridor's glass wall, something large and slow moved.

"He was right," Kael said.

Sela looked at him.

"On the cliff," he said. "In Ashenmere. The five elements didn't sequence. They didn't compete. They all came through at once and the channel held because — because it wasn't a channel in the ordinary sense. It was something I don't have a name for yet." He looked at his hands. The scar-lines, the frost-patterns, the faint illumination that the depth here was making slightly more visible. "Something that is wide enough."

"That is a significant claim."

"I know." He looked at the archive in front of him. At the eight names, the four outcomes. "None of them had the Void element."

Sela said nothing for a moment. Then: "No. None of them had Void. The strongest was four elements — Fire, Water, Earth, Wind. But not Void."

"What does Void do to the channel?"

"We don't know." She said it with the directness she used when she had looked for an answer and genuinely not found it. "We know what Void does in isolation — the Atlantean theory on Void-channeling is actually better than either of the other Academies', because we've studied it longer. But Void in combination with the other four elements simultaneously is — we don't have a model for it." She paused. "Malachar had all five. You have all five. There is a twelve-hundred-year gap in the evidence set, which means we have two data points and one of them ended catastrophically."

"And the one you can still study," Kael said, "keeps not dying."

"Yes." Something moved in her expression — not quite humor, more the quality of someone who has been waiting for a fact to be stated clearly and is relieved to hear it. "You keep not dying. This is, professionally speaking, very useful."

---

Corenne had been silent for the better part of an hour. She was in the far corner of the reading room, her hand flat against the coral wall, her eyes open but not reading anything visible to the others. This was, Kael was learning, one of her ways of accessing the location-memory: direct contact with a surface that had held high-intensity elemental events, letting the residue come to her rather than reaching for it.

He waited until she came back.

She took her hand from the wall and looked at it for a moment, then at Kael. "Tessara Vole was here," she said. "In this room."

"When?"

"Two hundred and seventy years ago. Shortly before she disappeared." She was doing the thing with her voice that she did when the location-memory was recent enough to carry emotion: a slight flatness, as though the language was being filtered through someone else's feeling before arriving at words. "She was reading these same archives. The multi-element records." A pause. "She wrote something. Not in the archive — on the wall."

Everyone looked at the wall.

"There," Corenne said.

The coral grew in patterns here, as it did throughout the Depths — not random growth, shaped by centuries of Water-channeling into structures that served the room's purpose. Along the lower section of the east wall, the coral had been allowed to grow in tighter formation, a denser section that served as a kind of low shelf or bench. It was in the back of this formation, where the coral was thickest and least often touched, that Kael saw it.

Not carved. Channeled. Someone with Water-affinity had moved the coral itself, infinitely slowly over some period of time, guiding its growth into a pattern rather than simply growing. The effect was that the words were not cut into the surface — they were the surface. Grown into it, part of the living structure.

Kael could read Atlantean shorthand. He had been reading it for three weeks with Sela's instruction. He could not read this. The script was not Atlantean.

It was the old Arcanean. The same script as the inscription in the sealed chamber beneath the Luminari campus.

He read it.

*The Void does not add. It relates. Take that to the Source and give it back.*

He read it twice. Three times.

"What does it say?" Ryn asked.

Kael told him.

The room was very quiet.

"*Give it back,*" Sela said. "Give what back? To the Source Gate?"

"I don't know yet." He was still looking at the words in the coral. A woman had grown them here, over what must have been months — Water-mages could shape coral but not quickly. She had stood in this room, two hundred and seventy years ago, and channeled this into the wall, one cell at a time, in a language that the living Atlantean scholars would not read.

"Did she know someone would be able to read it?" Ryn asked.

"She knew Nerissa Pelagius could read it," Corenne said. "Nerissa is one of the few people alive — or alive then — who reads the old script." She paused. "She annotated Tessara's record. She knew what Tessara wrote here."

"And she left it," Sela said.

"She left it," Corenne confirmed. "For four hundred years."

Kael reached out and put his hand on the coral, beside the words but not touching them. He did not channel anything. He just felt the material reality of a message left in a dead language, grown into living stone, in the deepest library in the world, two hundred and seventy years ago by a woman who had three of his five elements and had asked not to be followed and had gone somewhere no one in the Archives could document.

The whale outside the glass wall passed again. Its light was dimmer here, deeper in the rock, barely visible. But present.

"I need to speak with Nerissa Pelagius," he said.

"I know," Sela said.

"Will she see me?"

"I think," Sela said carefully, "that she has been waiting for you to be ready to ask."
