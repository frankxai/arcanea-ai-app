# PROLOGUE: BEFORE THE FLOOD

*Thalassara — The Atlantean Depths*
*The First Year of the Fourth Age*
*Two Thousand Years Before*

---

The order came on a Tuesday in the late season, when the ocean above was in its autumn rhythm and the bioluminescent tide-organisms that lit the primary corridors had shifted from their summer blue-white to the deeper amber that meant the year was ending. Veradis had always thought the amber light made the Archive look like it was burning. She had never found this as ominous as she did now.

The order was written in the Headmaster's formal hand on the Archive's official vellum, which meant it had been reviewed by the chancellery staff and sealed by three senior faculty members before it reached her desk. It was, in the language of institutional authority, final. The kind of document that did not invite response, only compliance.

She read it twice, which was two times more than most archivists would have needed to understand it. Then she set it face-down on her desk and looked at the wall for a long time.

The wall was coral, old coral, the deep arterial red of sections that had been growing since the Academy's founding. The Archive's oldest section. In the way of old coral, it had a surface texture like compressed time — centuries of growth laid down in the same space, each year's work invisible in the whole but accumulated there nonetheless. Veradis had spent twenty-two years in this building. She knew which wall sections were oldest without looking at the growth charts. She knew which alcoves smelled of specific decades. She knew the Archive better, she sometimes thought, than she knew herself, which was probably what the Academy had intended when they trained her.

She turned the order face-up again.

*The Veltherys Collection* — this was its formal catalogue designation, though the name had never appeared in any document a student would encounter — *is to be removed from controlled access and transferred to the Preservation Vault pending destruction. As per Headmaster Directive Seven, issued this day in the first year of the Fourth Age. Reason: the information contained in the Collection is accurate and therefore dangerous in the present circumstance. The current circumstance does not permit the luxury of accurate information about multi-element channeling. Compliance is required within thirty days.*

Accurate and therefore dangerous.

She had been an archivist for twenty-two years. She had catalogued documents she disagreed with, documents she found repugnant, documents that described events she wished had not occurred. She had overseen the careful preservation of records that argued against her own scholarly positions. She had, twice, restricted access to materials she believed would cause harm to unprepared readers — not destroying them, restricting them, which was the distinction the Atlantean tradition maintained between preservation and censorship. The tradition held that memory was territory. That it was sacred. That the Archive's function was to remember, and the archivist's function was to serve that remembering even when what was remembered was uncomfortable.

Accurate and therefore dangerous was the opposite of how the tradition worked.

She put the order back on her desk. Then she got up and went to the Collection.

---

The Veltherys Collection occupied seventeen bound volumes in the controlled-access section of the deep wing, in a shelf bracket whose coral-growth suggested it had stood in this specific alcove since the early Third Age. The volumes were written in the old formal scholarly style — dense, specific, cross-referenced, occasionally footnoted with the self-aware precision of archivists who knew they were creating primary sources. The covers were sea-calfskin, waterproofed with a coating that had been reapplied regularly for centuries, the successive layers creating a texture like geological strata under the fingertip.

Veradis knew every volume. She had read the Collection in its entirety three times: once during her initial inventory when she took over the controlled-access section twelve years ago, once when a junior archivist had requested guidance on a historical question that the Collection was the definitive source for, and once — two years ago, in the months after the battle at the Shadowfen, after Malachar's fall and the sealing and the inventory of what the world had lost — simply because she needed to understand what the Headmaster's new directive was trying to prevent.

She understood it now. She had understood it then, too. She disagreed.

The first volume, opening with the slight resistance of a book that had been opened many times and resisted gently because it had learned to expect handling: the founding documents of the Veltherys research tradition, which predated the Atlantean Depths by several hundred years and had been moved here for safekeeping in the second century of the Third Age, when the Depths became the world's primary repository of historical scholarship. The original archivist who had brought the collection here had noted in his transfer documentation: *these are the most complete records of multi-element channeling in existence. They should be protected rather than hidden, which is why I am bringing them here rather than elsewhere.*

Veradis stood in the amber light and held the first volume and thought about the difference between protection and hiding, and whether what she was about to do could be called either.

---

She did not return to her office. She went instead to the deep alcove at the back of the controlled-access section — the one that had been used, before her tenure, for overflow storage, and which she had cleared out and repurposed as a working space because she spent long enough in the Archive that having somewhere to sit without going back to the office was a practical necessity. She brought all seventeen volumes with her, in three trips, which left her slightly breathless from the exertion and the pressure-weight at this depth. She set them on the working table. She lit the reading lamp — a different organism from the corridor lighting, producing a cooler, whiter light suitable for close work. She sat down.

Then she began to read.

Not because she needed to refresh her memory. Because she was saying goodbye.

---

*Volume Three, page 47: Lyssara of the Western Coast, Second Age, whose records described her as a woman of unremarkable appearance and extraordinary generosity, who had worked as a healer in the fishing communities near what is now Solmere for fifty years before the documented accounts of her elemental range became part of the Veltherys records. Her students had noted that she channeled Earth-steady, Fire-precise, Water-immediate — three affinities held simultaneously and deployed in clinical rotation. The accounts described the way she worked: the Earth affinity first, establishing stability; the Water to diagnose, to feel the condition from inside; the Fire to correct, to burn away what should not be there. "She did not understand herself as exceptional," her primary student wrote. "She understood herself as someone who had found the right tools for the work."*

Veradis paused at the student's note. She had thought about it often since her first reading. The right tools for the work. As if the five elements were simply capabilities in service of a purpose that existed independent of them. As if what mattered was not the elements but the healer.

*Volume Seven, page 112: A practitioner known only by the title he was given posthumously, the First Cartographer, who had spent a generation mapping the World-Tree's root system and discovered, in the process, that the five elements were not discrete. That they were, in their deepest substrate, expressions of a single tendency — what he had described as the tendency of existence to be itself fully. His four-element range (Earth, Water, Wind, Void; notably not Fire) had let him perceive the substrate in a way no single-element cartographer could have. His maps, of which six partial copies had survived into the Veltherys Collection through the preservation work of four intervening archivists, were the most accurate depictions of the elemental geography of the world in existence.*

She thought about the First Cartographer's maps. They were incomparable. Irreproducible, because no one with his range of perception had existed since, and the Headmaster's directive would ensure that no one who came after would know his work had existed.

*Volume Twelve, page 203: Aelith of the Mountain Communities, whose records span fourteen documents from three different archivists who had encountered her across sixty years of her documented life, who appears to have opened all five Gates in her forty-third year and spent the subsequent fifty years as a practitioner of what the records consistently described as "holding all of existence without grasping at any of it."*

Veradis stopped at this entry. As she always stopped at this entry. Fourteen documents. Sixty years of consistent observation. The oldest of the fourteen dated from before the founding of the three-academy system; the youngest postdated it by a generation. Aelith had been real enough and present enough in the world that four different archivists across six decades had found her worth documenting. She had opened five Gates. She had not fallen. She had not become catastrophic. She had lived to ninety-three and died, the final document noted, in the early autumn, at her own house, surrounded by students.

That was the whole record. Ninety-three years of life. A peaceful death. Students at the bedside.

And the Academy was going to destroy it.

---

Veradis closed Volume Twelve and held it in her lap for a long time.

The amber light shifted. The organisms that produced it were tied to the tidal rhythm, and the tide was turning. The great seasonal change that the Academy called the Great Turning, when the ocean's circulation pattern shifted for winter — the water above moved differently, slower and heavier, and the city felt it. The pressure changed, slightly but perceptibly, the way a room changes when the people in it decide something collectively without speaking.

She thought about the Headmaster's reasoning, which she had been turning over since the order arrived. The reasoning was sound, on its own terms. Malachar had fallen three months ago. The world was in the specific chaos of a catastrophe that had been prevented but only barely, and prevention had cost lives that could not be replaced and had left scars in the elemental substrate that the archivists were still cataloguing. In that context, the existence of records demonstrating that multi-element channeling was a real and observable tradition — that it had produced forty-one documented practitioners over two Ages, most of them apparently functional and none of them catastrophic in the way Malachar had been — was, from the Headmaster's perspective, a problem. The academies were trying to establish what had caused Malachar's fall. The narrative they had settled on — that multi-element channeling was inherently dangerous, that Malachar's fall was the inevitable consequence of a nature that should never have been permitted to develop unchecked — required that Malachar be unprecedented.

The Veltherys Collection made him not unprecedented.

Therefore, the Veltherys Collection had to go.

The reasoning was sound. It was also, in the specific way that sound reasoning in service of a dishonest premise was always wrong, completely wrong. The Headmaster was trying to prevent the next Malachar. But the records — if Veradis had learned anything from twenty-two years in this Archive — suggested that what had produced Malachar was not his five-element range. It was what happened to him after the range was discovered: the isolation, the institutional fear, the systematic message that what he was could not be integrated into a legitimate life. That message, encoded in every interaction he had with the systems that were supposed to support him, was what had pushed him toward the only path that seemed to offer resolution.

And the Headmaster was now preparing to give that exact same message to every multi-element practitioner who would ever exist.

She put Volume Twelve back on the stack. She looked at the seventeen volumes.

Accurate and therefore dangerous. She turned the phrase over again. Accurate and therefore to be destroyed, because the accurate information contradicted a narrative that someone very powerful needed to be true. The narrative would persist, unchallenged, because the evidence against it would be gone. Any practitioner who discovered their multi-element range in the next generation, or the next ten, or the next hundred, would be told what Malachar had been told: you are unprecedented, you are terrifying, there is no record of anyone like you surviving intact.

It would be a lie. A clean, well-maintained lie, with all the evidence of its falseness removed.

She sat in the amber light for a long time.

Then she got up and went to the supply room.

---

The supply room was three alcoves back, accessible through a low connecting passage that required ducking — a design artifact of the original coral growth, never corrected because the archivists who used this section were, as a professional cohort, not particularly concerned with architectural comfort. Veradis had ducked through this passage ten thousand times in twelve years and would have noticed if it changed by a millimeter.

The supply room held the Archive's working materials: blank vellum in several weights, binding materials, the solutions for treating old documents, the instruments for reading Depth Scrolls without disturbing their emotional layers. And, on the bottom shelf: blank books. Plain coral-cover, sea-calfskin binding, the standard Archive binding that the Academy had used for a thousand years because it worked and there was no reason to change something that worked.

She took one blank book. She went back to the working alcove.

She sat down with the seventeen volumes, the blank book, and the question she had been turning over since the order arrived. Not whether to destroy the collection. She had settled that. She would not destroy it.

The question was where to put it.

The problem with hiding something in the Archive was the Archive. It remembered. Everything in the Tide Archives was cross-referenced, catalogued, indexed, and tied into the living memory network that the Academy's Water-mages maintained. Concealment in the Archive was, eventually, discovery in the Archive. Something could be moved to restricted access. Something could be given a misleading title. But the Archive itself knew what it contained, and the Archive told, given the right question asked by the right practitioner.

She could take the collection out of the Archive entirely. Hide it somewhere in Thalassara, or above the surface in a location she controlled. But then she was creating a single point of failure: if she died, the collection was lost to anyone who didn't know where she had put it.

What she needed was a hiding place that would persist past her death, that the Archive itself would not reveal to casual search, but that the right person could find given the right key.

She had been working on this problem for the three hours since the order arrived. She thought she had it. It was technically possible; she had verified the theory while walking to the supply room and back. Whether she could execute it alone, in thirty days, without being caught in the process — those were the practical questions, and they had practical answers.

She opened the blank book.

She reached for her pen.

She thought about Aelith of the Mountain Communities, who had opened five Gates at forty-three and spent fifty more years holding all of existence without grasping at it, and she began to write.

---

The cipher took three weeks.

Veradis worked at night, in the hours after the Archive's administrative staff went to their quarters and before the early-shift maintenance practitioners began their morning rounds. She had four hours each night. She used all of them. The cipher required two things: a concealment layer, which she built from the Void-sensitive notation she had learned as a Water-Void practitioner herself, and a retrieval mechanism, which was the harder problem. The information had to be findable. Not by anyone who happened to look. By someone specific. By the only kind of person for whom the information would matter.

A five-element channeler.

She built the cipher so that its Water layer could only be read by someone who had opened the Flow Gate — not just touched the Water element, but genuinely opened, genuinely surrendered, genuinely received. The Void layer was visible to any Void-sensitive practitioner; she left it visible deliberately, because she wanted the cipher to be found, and Void sensitivity was rare enough that an accidental discovery was unlikely but not so rare that it was impossible across two thousand years. The Void layer would show the blank book as dense with content, not empty. Someone with the right sensitivity would see it and come back. What they would find when they came back would depend on whether they could read Water.

Three weeks. Four hours a night. Seventeen volumes encoded.

On the twenty-second night, she encoded the last entry. Aelith of the Mountain Communities. Ninety-three years. A peaceful death. Students at the bedside.

She looked at the last page for a long time.

Then she wrote, in the margin, in her own hand and not in cipher: *tell him: still water runs deepest. Begin there.*

She did not know who him would be. She thought she knew the shape of the person — someone who had found their way to the blank book by perceiving the Void layer, and had been patient enough to wait for the Water, which meant someone who had learned patience after being, probably, congenitally impatient. A person who controlled things because controlling them was the only protection they had known. A person who would need to be reminded that water moved not by force but by yielding.

She closed the blank book. She placed it on the shelf in the deep alcove.

She stood back and looked at it.

It looked like nothing. It looked like a blank book that had not been catalogued yet, sitting on a shelf waiting for use. The coral growth would accumulate around its bracket and after a generation no one would think twice about why it was there.

The Archive knew what it contained. The Archive would not say, because what she had built was not a record in the Archive's living memory network. It was an absence — a structural gap in the form of a present object, which was the specific technique the Archive had no counter-measure for. The blank book was real and it was in the Archive and it contained seventeen volumes of irreplaceable history and the Archive knew none of this, because the Archive only knew what had been registered and the blank book had never been registered. It was outside the taxonomy. The network would find it only if someone specifically looked for something registered by those specific words and there were no specific words because the book had no title.

She had hidden the collection inside the collection's own absence.

The order specified destruction. She was providing a different kind of preservation.

She went back to her desk. She put the seventeen volumes in the transfer crate, sealed it with the Archive's official sealing notation, and set it by the door for the Preservation Vault intake team to collect in the morning.

In the morning, they would take seventeen empty shells. The content would be gone — not destroyed, which was what they had asked for, which was not the same thing. The Headmaster had asked for destruction. The tradition that had trained her asked for memory. She had found the gap between the two.

She sat at her desk in the amber light.

She was very afraid. She had been afraid for three weeks, steadily, at a level she had learned to work around rather than through. She was afraid of being caught. She was afraid of being wrong about the cipher's technical execution. She was afraid, most specifically, that she would die before anyone found the blank book and that all of this would become exactly the outcome she had been trying to prevent: the records of the forty-one, lost anyway, simply lost more slowly.

She was afraid. She had done it anyway.

She picked up her pen and opened her private journal — not an Archive document, a personal record, bound in different leather, kept in the desk's locked bottom drawer.

She wrote: *I am placing these records in the care of a practitioner who does not yet exist. Everything I know about what they will be, I have encoded into the retrieval mechanism, which means the cipher itself is a description of the person I am hoping will read it. Someone who perceives the Void and has learned to receive the Water. Someone who has been told they are unprecedented and has discovered that this was a lie. Someone who will be angry about the lie, which is the right response, but who will know what to do with the anger. I am writing this in the hope that they are better at the last part than I am. I am not sure I am doing the right thing. I am sure that the other thing is wrong.*

She closed the journal. She put it in the drawer.

She sat in the amber light until the early maintenance shift began to move in the corridor outside, and then she stood up, straightened her coat, and went back to work.

---

The Archive settled around her, enormous, present, breathing with the ocean above in its autumn rhythm. Every document ever committed to its keeping. Every memory donated, every experience dissolved and preserved and waiting for the right reader.

She had added one more.

The blank book sat on the shelf in the deep alcove. The cipher held, invisible to everything but the right perception. Two thousand years of ocean would move overhead before anyone came looking.

She hoped they would be patient. She hoped they would know that still water ran deepest.

She hoped, in the way she had always hoped, in the specific and particular way of a person who does small careful things in the dark against the larger carelessness of the world: that it would be enough.

*Outside, in the waters above Thalassara, the Great Turning continued. The seasonal current shifted direction. Something in the deep substrate, far below where even the Dreaming Pools could perceive, began — in the way of all deep things — to wait.*

---

*Two thousand years later, a boy from a burned village put his hands flat on the coral floor of a practice yard in the earliest hour of the morning, and the water came.*

---

*END PROLOGUE*
