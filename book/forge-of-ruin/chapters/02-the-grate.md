# THE FORGE OF RUIN

## Chapter Two: The Grate

---

The temperature rod had cracked down its middle seam sometime in the night.

Ashe noticed it when they lifted it from the instrument case — the hairline split running from the brass collar down through the cedarwood grip, fine as a spider's thread but there. Three years of use. The case was padded but the cellar stairs were steep and Ashe had dropped it once, two winters back, and perhaps the damage had been waiting inside the wood ever since, patient, working itself open by degrees.

They set it on the workbench and pressed both thumbs along the seam.

It held.

Good enough. They wrapped it in a strip of cloth — tight, two passes around the collar — and placed it back in the case beside the light-stone and the logbook and the spare chalk for marking the door-frame measurements. Erivar's chains were measured weekly. Height in a crouch. Reach at full extension. The Ashpriest order had developed the protocol over decades of managing berserkers, and the protocol assumed that a Fury in latency would still, slowly, be changing — building density in the muscle, thickening through the joints, the body preparing for what the will had not yet permitted. You measured the chains because eventually the chains would not be enough, and it was better to know that before it became true.

Ashe had been making the measurements for three years.

Erivar's reach had not changed by more than two inches.

They closed the instrument case and picked up the logbook. Outside the narrow window of the Ashpriest chapter house, Galdheim was waking up in the way garrison fortresses always woke — loud, abrupt, without ceremony. Someone was shouting something about the south granary. A hammer was ringing on stone. The Thornwall stretched east and west beyond the window's frame, its dark bulk cutting the pale morning sky, and somewhere beyond it was the Wraith territory, which Ashe had never seen and did not particularly want to. The Thornwall was there so that people like Ashe did not have to think about what lay past it. Ashe preferred to think about the observation schedule.

Half-hour until the morning round. Time enough for tea.

---

The guards stationed at the chapter house entrance were named Corran and Pell, and they had the look of men who had drawn the assignment and made their peace with it. They stood a little apart from the door. Ashe had noticed, three years ago, that guards assigned to the cellar level always stood a little apart from the door. Not enough to be derelict. Just enough.

"Morning," Ashe said.

"Morning, Priest." This was Corran. He had a broad, weathered face and he looked at the instrument case the way most people looked at the stairs to the cellar — aware of it, choosing not to engage with it directly.

"Quiet night?"

"Quiet enough."

Pell said nothing. He was newer — six months at the posting — and he had not yet developed the practiced normalcy that Corran wore. Pell's eyes went to the door and then away again, the way they always did.

"Good," Ashe said, and went through.

The stairs were stone, twelve steps to the lower landing, and the cold came up them like water. This was the thing about the cellar that no one who had not been down there could quite understand from description alone: it was not the temperature. It was not even the dark. It was the quality of the air, which was different from the air in the rest of the fortress in a way that Ashe had never been able to precisely articulate in three years of private log entries. The best attempt, written on a night six months in when Ashe had still been trying to find language for everything, was this: *like breathing in a room where someone has been very afraid for a very long time.* Which was not scientific. Which was why it went in the private log and not the official one.

The official log said: *cellar atmosphere: cold, dry, no notable change in quality.*

Ashe carried the lamp low and walked to the end of the corridor.

The grate was iron, set into a door of iron-banded oak, two feet square and positioned at eye height. Ashe had measured once, in an idle moment: the center of the grate was exactly sixty-two inches from the floor, which was neither Ashe's eye height nor Erivar's, but somewhere in between. An averaging, perhaps. A compromise the architects had made for the general category of prisoner, the general category of observer. There was something slightly absurd about that, Ashe had always thought — this one window between two specific people, built for no one in particular.

Ashe set the lamp on the hook and looked through.

---

Erivar was awake.

This was not unusual. Erivar was almost always awake when Ashe arrived for the morning observation, though he did not sleep in the human sense of the word — not fully, not restoratively. The Ashpriest texts described a Fury-state called *threshing*, a kind of shallow rest that was not sleep and not waking, the body conserving itself by cycling down its non-essential functions while the underlying fury-mass remained active at a low hum. Three years ago, when Ashe had first been assigned to this post, Senior Priest Doveth had explained threshing in a lecture that lasted ninety minutes and used the phrase *like banking a fire* no fewer than four times. Ashe had written it down. Ashe had looked for evidence of it in every morning observation since.

What Ashe had found, instead of the textbook signs, was that Erivar simply sat.

He was sitting now. Far wall, back against the stone, chains pooled in his lap like a question he had stopped trying to answer. His eyes were open. In the lamplight through the grate they looked dark, the irises carrying that deep-brown coloration that was normal for him in dormancy — no bleeding at the edges, no corona of amber, no sign of the vein-glow that preceded a state shift. His hands rested on his knees with a precision that was either deliberate or habitual; after three years, Ashe was not always certain which.

"Good morning," Ashe said.

"Morning." A pause. "You're six minutes early."

"The rod needs replacing. I wanted time to prep before the round."

Erivar looked at the instrument case. Or looked at the grate in a way that suggested he was aware of the case behind it. "Cracked?"

"Down the seam. I wrapped it."

"Hmm." He looked away. Not dismissively — there was a quality to the way Erivar directed his attention that was more like turning a lens than breaking contact. He looked at the wall to his left and seemed to be thinking about the rod.

"It'll hold for today," Ashe said.

"I'm not worried."

"I know." Ashe opened the logbook to the morning column and uncapped the writing stick. "Ready?"

"Yes."

This was the ritual. Ashe had not invented it; it had come with the post, outlined in the protocol manual in a section titled *Dormancy Verification Procedure, Cellar Level*. The manual had been written by people who had never met Erivar specifically, but who had met enough men like him to know that the verbal component of the observation served a dual purpose: it assessed speech coherence and response latency while also maintaining what the manual delicately called *subject cooperation through normalized social contact*. Which was a careful way of saying: talk to them like a person and they are more likely to remain one.

Ashe had read that section three times in the first month. Then had stopped reading it, because what it described as methodology had already become something else.

"Name?"

"Erivar sal Veth."

"Date of your own reckoning?"

"Eight of the Cold Month. Or thereabouts." The *or thereabouts* was new, two years in — a small joke, delivered deadpan, acknowledging that time in the cellar did not move the way time moved elsewhere.

"Present state?"

"Dormant."

Ashe made the note. "Any changes since yesterday's observation?"

"No."

"Dreams?" This was not in the official protocol. Ashe had added it fourteen months ago, after Erivar had mentioned in passing that the dreams had shifted in character — less fire, more distance, a quality he had described as *watching the shore from the water* — and the note had been in the private log and then in the official log and now in the protocol, because the dreams, it turned out, were a leading indicator that none of the manuals had bothered to document.

Erivar was quiet for a moment. "Shore again."

"Same distance?"

"Further, maybe." Another pause. "Hard to say."

Ashe wrote it down.

---

The temperature rod held.

Ashe slid it through the grate — this was the part of the procedure that required the most care, the moment where protocol and physical reality had to agree — and Erivar accepted it without comment and pressed the brass collar to the inside of his wrist for a count of thirty. This was also practiced. In the first weeks, three years ago, Erivar had refused the contact. Not aggressively. Simply: *no.* And Ashe had stood at the grate and thought about what the manual said about *normalized social contact* and had said, quietly, *the reading only works on skin, and I need the reading to know whether you're safe today, and I'd rather know.* Erivar had looked at the grate for a long moment. Then he had said *fine* and extended his wrist.

He had not refused since.

The rod's indicator grain read within normal range. Ashe noted the figure. Then came the light-stone — smaller procedure, less contact, just Ashe asking Erivar to hold his eyes open and angle toward the grate while the pale glow checked the pupil response. The irises were deep brown. No amber bleeding. The pupils contracted correctly. Ashe noted this too.

"Anything else?" Erivar said.

"Not officially."

He nodded, once, and shifted against the wall to a slightly different position. The chains moved with him, heavy and slow. Ashe had noticed a long time ago that Erivar never pulled against the chains — not even the small unconscious resistances that other berserkers showed, the constant micro-tension between body and restraint. He wore them the way he wore his own skin. Which was either acceptance, Ashe had thought, or something deeper and more troubling: the understanding that the chains were not the real containment, and so they were not worth fighting.

"Can I ask you something?" Ashe said.

"You ask me things every day."

"Off the record."

Erivar looked at the grate. "Go ahead."

"Last night." Ashe kept the voice even. "What number did you stop at?"

Silence.

"I wasn't counting," Erivar said.

"I was listening through the wall. You stopped." Ashe paused. "You always go to forty-seven. You stopped at forty-five."

The silence that followed was longer than the question warranted. Ashe had learned to read silences the way the protocol read vein-glow and temperature — as data, as signal, as the space between what was said and what was true. This silence was not avoidance. It was not aggression. It was the silence of a man deciding how honest to be, which was also something Ashe had learned to recognize, because it looked different from the silence of a man deciding how to lie.

"Forty-five is where the Thornwatch names begin," Erivar said finally.

Ashe said nothing.

"They're soldiers. They knew what they were doing when they came for me." He looked at the wall to his left. "Or they should have."

"But?"

"But they were young." His voice had not changed in register or texture. That was the thing that made the back of Ashe's neck go cold — not the words, but the absence of anything surrounding them. "I noticed that at the time."

Ashe wrote nothing. This was the kind of thing that went in the private log, if it went anywhere.

"Thank you for telling me," Ashe said.

Erivar looked back at the grate. His eyes, in the lamplight, were dark and steady and entirely unreadable. "You're still six minutes early."

"I know."

"You could stay."

---

They talked about bread.

This was not strange. The bread at Galdheim had worsened sharply since the autumn rains ruined the eastern grain stores, and the garrison had opinions about it the way garrisons always had opinions about food — with great volume and exhaustive specificity. Ashe had opinions too, as it turned out, having subsisted on the same three meals per day for three years, and Erivar listened to them with what appeared to be genuine interest.

"The sourness is worse at the far end of the loaf," Ashe said.

"The far end goes stale first."

"Yes, but it's not stale-sour. It's different."

"They're undertiming it."

"How would you know?"

"My mother baked bread." He said it the way he said most things about before — flatly, as a statement of fact, without the inflation of sentiment and without the deflation of distance. Just the thing itself. "When she underproofed it, the far end went sour differently."

Ashe thought about this. "I'll tell the cook."

"Don't mention where you heard it."

"Obviously."

Erivar almost smiled. Ashe had learned to read Erivar's almost-smiles the way navigators read stars — as orientation, as indication of position. They appeared rarely and they meant something about the state of the man behind the chains that no instrument could measure.

"I saw a bird this morning," Ashe said. "On the wall. Just sitting there."

"What kind?"

"I don't know birds."

"Describe it."

Ashe thought. "Brown. Fat. Very confident."

"Reed bunting, maybe."

"Do they come this far north?"

"In the Cold Month?" Erivar considered. "Sometimes. If it was warm enough early." He looked at the ceiling of the cell — stone, low, damp-stained. "They follow the warmth. When there isn't enough, they follow what warmth remains."

Ashe looked at him.

"I'm not being poetic," he said.

"I know," Ashe said. "I'm writing it down anyway."

He did not respond to this. But the not-smile was there again, briefly, and then gone.

---

Ashe wrote the official log at the small desk outside the cellar corridor, in the lower anteroom where the lamp brackets were and the protocol manual sat in its lacquered box. The official log was precise and complete and honest in the way that official logs were required to be — which was to say honest about the measurements and not about everything else.

*Subject: Erivar sal Veth. Day 1,102 of continuous observation.*

*Dormancy metrics within established range. Temperature stable. Pupil response normal, no amber presentation. Speech coherence uncompromised. Response latency within normal parameters. Dream content: consistent with previous entries (shore imagery, increasing distance). No behavioral indicators of state-shift precursor.*

*Assessment: Stable. Recommendation: Continue observation schedule.*

*Signed: A. Ceth, Ashpriest Third Rank.*

Ashe looked at it for a moment. Then closed the official log and opened the other one.

The private log was not regulation. Doveth knew about it and had never formally objected, which Ashe had come to understand as a kind of permission given in the space between permission and its absence. It was a narrow journal, dark-covered, and it had Ashe's real handwriting in it rather than the careful formation of the official entries.

*He recited the names tonight. I listened through the wall as I always do. He paused at forty-five.*

*When I asked about it this morning, he told me why. Forty-five is where the Thornwatch names begin — soldiers. Young ones. He did not say this with guilt, or with grief, or with anything I have a name for. He said it the way you say something you have been carrying in your hands for a long time and have finally set down, just for a moment, to look at. Not to show anyone. Just to look.*

*I am writing it down because I have learned that the things I cannot explain are the things that matter most.*

*The official log says stable. The official log is not wrong. His temperatures are within range. His pupils are fine. By every instrument I carry, by every protocol I was trained to apply, Erivar sal Veth is exactly where he has been for three years.*

*But there is something in the eyes today that I cannot log.*

*I have been looking at his eyes through that grate for one thousand one hundred and two days. I know what Fury-dormant looks like in him. I know what the edges of the irises do when the state is building, and I know how his hands sit differently on his knees when something is moving under the surface. None of those signs are present.*

*What is present is something else. Something that is not the Fury.*

*He is thinking about the forty-five names and he is thinking about what young means and he is thinking about something I cannot follow him into, and the instruments I carry cannot measure thought. I was trained to assess the Fury. I was not trained to assess the man.*

*I stayed longer than protocol requires. We talked about bread and a bird I saw.*

*This is the part I do not know how to log: I believe he was glad I stayed.*

*I believe I was glad to stay.*

*That is all. I do not know what it means that I am uncertain and I am writing it down anyway.*

---

Ashe closed the private log and sat for a moment in the cold of the lower anteroom.

Outside, through the stone above, the fortress was louder now — the full morning movement of four hundred soldiers going about the work of a garrison day, all the industry and small purpose of lives arranged around a wall. The hammer was still going. The granary argument had resolved, or relocated. Somewhere a dog was barking at something.

Down the corridor, past the lamp brackets and the lacquered box, beyond the iron door and the grate and the fifty-two inches of cold stone and darkness between —

Ashe stood up.

Tucked the private log inside the coat, against the body, where the warmth was. Picked up the instrument case. Went back up the stairs, twelve steps, into the light and noise of the fortress proper, where Corran was still at his post and the morning smelled of coal smoke and porridge and the particular salt of the Thornwall's old stone, and the word that sat in Ashe's chest all the way up the stairs and kept sitting there — through Corran's nod and Pell's careful looking-away and the long corridor back to the chapter house — was not one of the words in the protocol manual, and was not a measurement, and would not go in the official log.

*Uncertain.*

Like a stone. Right in the center of the chest, where the warmth was.

Ashe walked back to the chapter house in the thin morning light and went to write a request for a new temperature rod.
