# THE FORGE OF RUIN

## Chapter One: The Forty-Seven Names

The ritual began the way it always began.

Erivar sat cross-legged on the stone, wrists resting on his knees, chains pooled in his lap like sleeping iron snakes. He had learned, in the first months, not to fight the weight of them. The chains were eight feet long. He had paced that circuit so many times he could feel it in his bones — the slight slickness near the wall where condensation gathered, the rough patch two steps from the floor-bolt where the stone had been chipped by some previous occupant's fury or boredom. He was not that man anymore. He had become something quieter.

The Ashpriest blessing hummed in the iron. Not a sound, precisely. More like pressure — the memory of a sound, felt in the teeth.

He breathed out. Began.

*Tormund Skaldson.*

His brother's face. Unchanged, the way the dead stayed unchanged, locked in the moment before the moment. Tormund's expression had been confused more than afraid. That was the part Erivar carried. Not screaming. Not accusation. Just the bewilderment of a man whose body had stopped working and hadn't yet understood why.

*Yrsa Halvörn.*

Shield-sister. Seven years of practice grounds, seven years of her elbow finding his ribs in sparring, her voice finding his ear in the moments before a line held or broke. She had not run. That was the thing about Yrsa. She had recognized something in his eyes — recognized the Fury rising, understood what was coming — and she had planted her feet.

She had not run.

He held her name for three breaths. That was the rule he had made for himself. Three breaths per name. The Fury's killings had not been quick things, most of them. The least he owed was time.

*Bjarne Keldsson.* Three breaths.

*Runa of the Holm.* Three breaths.

*Sven the Fuller, his wife Astrid, their son Pieter.* Three breaths each.

The names moved through him like water through a known channel. He had recited them every night for three years. One thousand and ninety-five recitations, give or take the first weeks when he had been too broken to count. The names had worn grooves. They came without effort, the way breathing came without effort, the way the circuit of eight feet came without thought.

This was what he had built in the dark. Not peace. Something else. A practice. A refusal to let the Fury's work become abstraction.

Forty-seven people. He had read that number off the surviving witnesses' accounts, which Thane Halla had thrown into the cell with him on his second day, the parchment landing in a puddle of his own blood. *Know what you are,* she had said through the grate, her iron-grey eye filling that rectangle of dim light. *Know what I'm keeping.*

He had read the accounts until they fell apart in his hands. He had memorized forty-seven names. He had attached each name to a face, a detail, a breath of life, scraping them from the parchment's spare accounting: *woman, thirty years, brown hair, weaver's marks on her hands; boy, approximately six years, found near the millpond.*

He had made them real because the accounts would not.

*Olla Magnusdóttir.* Three breaths.

*Tvar the Ferryman.* Three breaths.

The children of Aske. He came to them the way you came to a wound you had stopped expecting to close. Eleven names, eleven small faces assembled from the descriptions of survivors, from the shapes of what the Fury had left behind. He said each one carefully. He would not rush through them.

*Sigrid Keldasdóttir, seven years.*

*Finn Olasson, four years.*

*Britta, family name unknown —* and here, as always, the small snarl of grief, the failure of the parchment, the nameless girl given half her due.

He moved through them. Britta to Leif to Amma to Rorik to the four Vansen children. He knew each name by position. The forty-first name was always Tomas Aske — the village's own boy, the irony of that. The forty-second was his older sister Freyja.

*Tomas Aske.* Three breaths.

*Freyja Aske.* Three breaths.

He drew in for the forty-third name.

It was not there.

---

He sat for a moment without thinking. The way you sat for a moment when a step expected solid ground and found nothing — not fallen yet, simply suspended in the knowledge that falling was coming.

The forty-third name.

He knew its position. He knew its shape — the particular rhythm of it, three syllables, ending soft. A woman. She had been — she had been near the —

He went back. Started at forty-one.

*Tomas Aske.*

*Freyja Aske.*

And then the nothing.

His hands closed on the chains in his lap. Not gripping. Just closing. The iron was cool against his blackened veins, and the hum of the Ashpriest blessing pressed into the dark lines of his skin like a thumb pressing a bruise.

The woman. Three syllables. She had been — he had a fragment. Hair the color of — no. That was Runa of the Holm, the thirty-first name, Runa he knew cold, Runa was not this. He was confusing them. He had never confused them.

In three years, he had never confused them.

He breathed. Methodical. He would not panic in this cell. He had decided that in the first month, when panic had been the only thing available to him and he had exhausted himself against it until there was nothing left to burn. Panic was the Fury's weather. He lived in something colder.

The forty-third name. A woman. The accounts — he closed his eyes and tried to see the parchment, tried to remember the ink, the precise way the scribe's hand had shaped the letters.

He could not see it.

He could see the parchment. He could see that a name had been on it. He could not see the name.

*Think.* The village of Aske. The Fury's path through it. He knew the accounts, he had read them until the parchment dissolved, he had built a map of what happened in that village based on the positions of the dead and the testimonies of those who survived and the pattern of destruction, which had its own logic, the Fury's logic, the way a fire moved through a structure.

The forty-third name had been a woman found near the north well.

He knew that. He held that.

Her name was —

Nothing. A hole in the record of the world. A gap in the architecture he had spent three years constructing stone by stone, name by name, three breaths at a time.

He went to forty-four, forty-five, forty-six, forty-seven, recited those, found them intact, and came back.

The forty-third name was gone.

He sat with that for longer than three breaths.

---

He tried to reconstruct her.

Brown hair — no, that was assumption. The accounts hadn't recorded hair for most of them, only the children's had been noted with any particularity, the survivors' descriptions had been grief-blurred, approximate. *A woman, forty years or so, callused hands, wore an apron.* Was that her? He thought — he thought that was her, something about an apron, something about the hands. But he had known her name. He had read her name off parchment. He had said it one thousand and ninety-five times.

The blacksmith's wife, he thought suddenly. Was she the blacksmith's wife?

He went through the names looking for the blacksmith. Sven the Fuller, Bjarne Keldsson, Orik of the South Bend — no blacksmith named anywhere in the forty-seven. The blacksmith of Aske had survived, then. Or the blacksmith's wife had another name in the accounts, not listed by trade.

He was inventing now. He recognized the feeling. The mind's desire to fill a hole with something, anything, rather than hold the empty shape of it.

*She might have been called Marta,* a voice in him said. Not the Fury's voice — the Fury did not speak, had never spoken to him in words, only in heat and pressure and the red edge of things. This was his own voice, and he hated it. *She might have been called Solveig. Women in the Aske Valley are often called Solveig.*

He pressed his fingers against the stone floor until the cold edge of it registered through the deadened patches on his palm. The sensation — limited, flattened by three years of Fury's presence — grounded him.

He could not know her name by reasoning. The name was gone. The parchment was gone. There were no surviving witnesses to ask. There was no record available to him in this cell, in this dark, in this fortress where Thane Halla kept him like a loaded crossbow.

The name was gone.

He felt the shape of that for a long time.

---

Then he found the forty-fourth and forty-fifth names were not where he expected them.

He recited them — *Ketil Olasson, Maris of the River* — and they came, but they came strange, too smooth, with the particular smoothness of a word said so many times it loses meaning and becomes only sound. They were there but he could not feel the weight behind them. He could not see their faces clearly.

He went cold inside.

How long? How long had this been happening?

He thought of the last weeks. The ritual, every night. Unbroken — he was certain of that, he had not skipped a night, he had not been sick enough or beaten badly enough to lose a night, the ritual was inviolable. He had said the names. He had said all forty-seven.

He had said *something* in the positions where forty-three and forty-four and forty-five lived.

Had they been the right somethings?

He could not remember last week's recitation. He had a general memory of it — *the ritual, performed, complete* — but no specific memory of any individual name, which was how it usually worked, the names so practiced they passed through him without registering, only the notable ones, only Tormund and Yrsa, only the children, ever really landing. He had assumed the others were intact beneath the routine of them.

He had assumed the Fury was dormant.

Behind his left eye, very distantly, something shifted.

Not waking. Not hunger. Something that had been fed moving in its satisfaction. The slight reorientation of a creature turning in sleep.

The iron taste hit the back of his throat.

He swallowed it down.

---

He sat in the dark for a long time, listening to the cell breathe. Stone settled somewhere above him with a sound like a compressed sigh. The chains' hum shifted frequency, infinitesimally, then returned. From the corridor beyond the iron door: nothing. The guards had learned not to walk close to his cell. Thane Halla had given that order in the early months when the Fury's proximity, even caged and chained and Ashpriest-blessed, had made men's hands shake.

He thought about what he knew.

He knew the Fury had killed forty-seven people while wearing his body. He knew this because he had read it from accounts compiled by the survivors and the Thane's own clerks. He knew he had memorized those names. He knew that somewhere in the past weeks or months, something had eaten three of those names from his memory while he was performing a ritual specifically designed to prevent their loss.

He thought about that word: *eaten.*

He had chosen it instinctively, and instinct in this cell was information.

The Fury was not dormant. He had believed it dormant because it was not killing, not pressing against him with that particular heat, not fragmenting his sleep with the red dreams it sent sometimes, less often now than in the first year. He had interpreted the quiet as absence.

He had been wrong before about what inhabited silence.

---

He made a decision. He was not certain it was a good decision.

He would finish the ritual.

He would fill the three gaps with names. They would not be the right names — he understood that, held it clearly, did not allow himself any comfort about it. They would be names that sounded like the names might have sounded. They would be placeholders. *This woman who was killed and whose name I have lost.* The name he gave her would be a monument to the name he had failed to keep.

He breathed out slowly.

*Marta of Aske,* he said, and his voice was nearly steady. *North well.*

Three breaths. He held the shape of a woman he had not known and could not name, and he gave her the wrong name carefully, the way you close a dead man's eyes even when the gesture means nothing to the dead man.

*Ketil Olasson.* The name came, still too smooth, uncertain underneath. He held it until it had weight.

Three breaths.

*Maris of the River.* He tasted the falseness of his certainty about it. He held it anyway.

Three breaths.

He finished the ritual. Forty-seven names, three of them now counterfeit, currency debased in the dark without a mint mark to say when or why.

---

He lay down on the stone.

The chains settled around him with the small, familiar music of iron on rock. He put his darkened left arm under his head. The cold of the stone came up through him, through his back and his hips and his legs, the cell's particular cold that never left no matter what the season, a cold that belonged to the stone itself, to the underground, to the geography of being buried.

He stared at the dark.

In three years, he had learned to see in this dark. Not truly — it was not light, his eyes were not finding shapes where shapes weren't visible. But the dark had texture, and he had learned its texture. The slight gradation near the grate where something close to light persisted even at night. The specific black near the floor-bolt, heavier somehow. The almost-color of the stone walls when he looked at them from the side of his vision.

He held three names that were not names.

He held forty-four names that were.

He would not lose the forty-four. He would say them louder inside himself. He would press them into the walls. He would find some way — in this cell, with no parchment and no ink, with nothing but time and chains and the hum of iron — to make them permanent.

He would not think about what it meant that the Fury had been eating while he believed it sleeping. He would not follow that thought to its end tonight. There were nights for hard truths and there were nights for lying still and waiting for morning, and he had learned, in this cell, to know which night he was inside.

He closed his eyes.

Behind his left eye, very deep, in whatever territory the Fury occupied inside him — that tangled root-space where his bloodline and the old parasite had grown so interwoven that cutting one would kill the other — something turned again.

Slow. Content.

Dreaming, perhaps.

He lay on the stone and understood, with the cold clarity of a man counting coins in the dark, that he did not know how long the Fury had been hungry.

Only that tonight, it was not.
