# THE FORGE OF RUIN

---

## Prologue: The Chronicle of Erivar Skaldson, Final Entry

*Recorded by Odre Flachmark, Chronicler of the Third Compact*
*Harrow's Rest, the Coldwatch Inn, upper room*
*Fourteenth day of the Frost Month, Year 1,203 of the Calend*

---

The candle has burned three times down since I sat at this desk. I have refilled the ink twice. The page before this one — the one I was going to use as the final entry, the one with the heading already written in my best hand — is crumpled beside the fire, blackened at one corner where I threw it and it landed wrong.

I am a trained observer. I have documented wars, successions, executions, and one occasion where a man tried to eat his own boots for reasons that remain, professionally speaking, unclear. I know how to end a chronicle. You state the final facts. You note what endures and what does not. You close the record.

I cannot close this record.

I am going to try anyway. That is what this entry is. A chronicler at the very edge of her competence, trying to put words to something that has burned through three languages already and come out the other side still smoking.

---

Begin with the known.

Erivar Skaldson is alive. He sits in a room seventeen paces from where I write this, a room I could reach in forty steps through the cold corridor. He has eaten. He has slept, which for a long time he could not do without — well. That is later in the record. He is alive, and he is still, and the stillness is the most frightening thing I have ever witnessed from a safe distance, which is saying something, because I was at the Cairn of Ashe when she died.

I have written that sentence in six different ways over the past three candles. That is the one I am keeping, because it is accurate and because accuracy is all I have left.

---

Eleven months.

I was twenty-four years old when the Compact assigned me to this chronicle. I was, in the assessment of my supervising archivist, "detached enough to be useful." She meant it as a compliment. She thought detachment was the quality the work required.

She was not wrong about the work. She was wrong about me.

I arrived in the border camp at Thresk with two satchels of blank-paged ledgers and a very firm theory about observation. The theory was: I would watch. I would record. I would not participate.

The theory did not survive Erivar Skaldson at close range.

He was not what I expected. The reports described a berserker — and the word conjures something specific, something loud and singular, a weapon shaped like a man. What I found was a person who moved through the world with extreme deliberateness until he did not, who counted everything, who knew the names of the camp's horses and addressed them individually, who, every morning without exception, turned to face east and spoke forty-seven names into the silence before he did anything else.

I will not write the names here. They are in the main body of the record, transcribed on the fifteenth day of the second month of my assignment, when I finally asked him what he was doing. He told me. He told me everything about the names, and I wrote it down, and then I closed the ledger and sat very still for a long time because some information lands differently than other information. Some information enters you like a change in climate, and after it, you are never quite the same temperature.

The names matter. They explain everything that comes after. The reader will understand them when the time comes.

---

I should describe the violence, because this is a chronicle and the violence is part of the record.

I watched Erivar Skaldson kill nine men in the pass at Verrath in less than four minutes, in terrain that should have made four minutes impossible. I watched him take a wound that would have ended a lesser person three times over and keep moving, not through will exactly — through something that does not have a name in any of the three languages I have tried. I watched him weep afterward, which is not in the official account, and I have included it in this one because I am the chronicler and I decide what is true.

The violence is not the story. I thought it was, at first. The Compact thought it was. We were all looking at the wrong thing.

The story is what he carries. The story is what he does with what he carries. The story is a woman named Bryda who forged her own chains — I understand now why that matters, why the forging is the point and not the wearing — and a god the locals call the Mawfather, who on the thirty-second day of the ninth month wore my friend like a glove.

That sentence is precise. I have considered other words. They are all less accurate. The Mawfather put itself into Erivar as a hand goes into a glove, and Erivar — the part of Erivar that is Erivar — was still there, which was the worst part. Still there, looking out, aware. I watched it happen from forty feet away and I did not run, and I will not claim that was bravery, because the honest accounting is that my legs simply did not work.

The record of the Mawfather's manifestation occupies the longest entries in this chronicle. I have read them back three times. They are accurate, and they are not enough.

---

There is a night that is not in the record.

I will not put it in the record here, either. A chronicle is not a private document, and that night was private. What I will say — what I find I cannot not say, which is a grammatical construction my archivist would circle in red ink — is that we were in the pass at Verrath, the night before the nine men, and it was very cold, and there was a fire, and Erivar told me something I have not told anyone else, and I told him something I had not told anyone else, and the specific quality of that exchange has not left me, and I suspect it will not.

The reader does not need to know what was said. The reader will understand, by the time they reach that point in the record, what it meant that anything was said at all.

---

I keep returning to the door.

This is where I break as a chronicler. This is the fact I cannot process into clean language, which is why I have been at this desk since before midnight and it is now some hour that does not have a reasonable name.

When I left the room seventeen paces from here, three hours ago, the door was open.

He could leave. The people who might stop him are gone, or changed, or dead — Ashe gone at the cairn in a way I will not describe here because some events deserve a proper account and not a prologue's shorthand. The chains are Bryda's to decide about now, and Bryda is no longer the person she was when she forged them. The Mawfather has retreated, or been pushed, or made an agreement, and I do not know which because there are things Erivar has not told me and I have chosen to trust that.

The door is open.

He is sitting on the floor beside the narrow bed with his back against the wall, and the door to the corridor is open, and he has not moved toward it.

I do not know what to do with this information. I have stared at it from multiple angles. I have written three entries trying to explain it, and every explanation I arrive at is too clean, too resolved, a chronicler's lie by omission, a way of making a complicated truth into a satisfying sentence.

The truth is: I do not know if he is staying because he has chosen to stay, or because he does not know yet how to want anything, or because he is waiting for something — someone — to give him a reason to move.

The truth is: I do not know if I am the reason he has not moved.

The truth is: I have been a trained observer for four years, and I am very good at it, and I have sat in the room with Erivar Skaldson in extreme circumstances and I have watched him with professional precision, and at some point, I do not know which point, the watching changed into something that professional precision does not have a word for.

I became part of the record.

---

My supervising archivist would tell me to seal the ledger and leave. She would say that a chronicler who enters the chronicle is no longer a chronicler. She would not be wrong.

I am going to seal the ledger.

I am going to put my coat on and walk the seventeen paces to his door.

Everything after that is —

---

*Here the entry ends. The final word is incomplete, the pen line trailing off in a direction that suggests not exhaustion but interruption. The inkblot at the terminus of the line has dried in the shape of nothing in particular.*

*What follows is the chronicle itself, beginning eleven months earlier, in a border camp at Thresk, on the first day Odre Flachmark saw Erivar Skaldson turn east at dawn and speak forty-seven names to the silence.*

*It is all here. Every detail. The chronicler was very thorough.*

*Reader, make of it what you will.*
