# THE TIDES OF SILENCE

## Chapter Three: Midwater Market

---

The descent-line dropped Sael through the hull like a swallowed stone.

One moment: the Surface's white stone plazas, the flat grey light of morning, the smell of salt and civic order. Then the descent-line's harness tightened across their chest, and the living hull opened — a dilating aperture of coral and muscle that the city expanded and contracted like a held breath — and Sael fell.

Not fell. Descended. The distinction mattered to the descent operators who ran the lines, a guild of their own with strong opinions about language.

The hull closed above them, and for three seconds there was nothing but the cable singing in Sael's grip and the rush of cold water along their neck and the city's underside scrolling past: an inverted geography of root structures and waste channels and the slow mineral accretion of decades, organisms that had attached themselves to the hull and built upward, reaching toward warmth they'd never touch. Sael had made this descent two hundred times, maybe more, and the three seconds still stopped thought. The passage between worlds always did.

Then the harness unlocked, and Sael stepped off onto the landing platform of the Midwater Market, and the noise hit them like a wave.

---

People called it a market because it was where buying and selling happened, but the name was insufficient. The Midwater Market was an argument. It was forty conversations happening at once in the space of three hundred hanging stalls. It was the smell of cooking fish and wet rope and mineral tang and something sweet from the coral-honey brewers three platforms over — a smell that had no name but that Sael associated with coming home, or with the particular ease of being in a place that made no effort to impress you.

The market occupied the great open gap between the city's hull above and the ring of hanging habitats around the perimeter — structures that dangled from the hull's underside like clusters of barnacles, connected by rope bridges that swayed and by current-tubes that hummed with the pressure of diverted ocean flow. The whole arrangement formed a rough cylinder of commerce and noise, open on all sides to the ocean. When the weather ran strong, swells moved through the market and everyone held their lines without comment and waited for the water to settle. This was considered normal.

Light came from two directions. Above: filtered through the hull in slow pulses, greenish and diffuse, like sunlight through deep water. Below: the bioluminescent organisms that gathered beneath the market in drifting clouds, drawn by warmth from the thermal vent networks that the Midwater ran on. Between these two light sources the market existed in a permanent blue-green glow that made everyone look like they were already underwater, which, Sael often thought, was accurate.

They took the east walkway toward the crystal stalls, past the fish traders.

---

The fish traders occupied their usual section: a long row of suspension platforms connected by planking that flexed underfoot, with tanks of circulating seawater where live specimens swam in slow circuits, and above them the gutted and salted rows of the already-taken hanging on lines, glittering. The smell here was layered — fresh catch over brine over something faintly organic and decaying, which was just the smell of food being honest about itself.

Sael had a habit of checking the fish traders before buying crystals. Not for any professional reason. Habit. The composition of the catch told you things.

Morvau ran the stall at the end, a heavy-set woman with arms like suspension cable who'd been selling deep-water fish since before Sael had learned to swim. She was arranging the morning's take when Sael stopped, and the arrangement looked thinner than usual.

"Slow season?" Sael said.

Morvau looked up. She had a way of looking at people that felt like measurement. "You're the fourth one who's asked me that today. Are you comparing notes, you Tide-Speakers, or does the water tell you to say the same thing?"

"I walked past. There's less."

"There's less," Morvau agreed. She didn't sound alarmed. She sounded tired in a specific way — the tiredness of a person who has been explaining something to themselves for long enough that the explanation no longer quite satisfies them. "The deep-fish moved. Seasonal variation. They'll be back."

The tanks held brightfin and scaled-mirror, both surface-range species. Good fish. Not what Morvau usually led with. Her usual lead was the longtail and the cave-lurker, both deep-range, both requiring the long-net drops into water the descent-lines couldn't reach.

"When did you last pull longtail?" Sael asked.

A pause. "Twelve days."

"That's not seasonal variation."

"No," Morvau said. "It's not." She went back to her arrangement. Her hands were steady, but they moved with more deliberation than the task required — the deliberateness of someone doing something uncomplicated because the complicated thing was sitting next to them uninvited. "They'll be back."

Sael said: "Yes." And moved on, because what else was there to say? The fish had moved because the currents had moved, and the currents had moved because something in the deep architecture of the water had shifted, and Sael could feel it in the back of their throat like a word they couldn't quite remember. But saying so would not bring Morvau's longtail back. And saying so in the wrong room, to the wrong person, would bring other problems.

There were three other fish traders with the same story in their arrangement. Less deep-range. More surface-catch to compensate. The nervous laughter of people making do. Sael noted it and kept walking.

---

The crystal stalls clustered near the market's north rim, where the habitat structures created a partial windbreak and the bioluminescence gathered thickest, providing good light for close examination of stone. Resonance crystals needed light to read well — the fracture patterns only showed under direct illumination, and buying a cracked crystal was the professional equivalent of picking up a speaking-trumpet with a hole in the bell. It would work, after a fashion, until it didn't.

Sael worked through the first two stalls without finding anything worth the price. Veldau's stock was the usual quality — serviceable, uninspired, the crystals cut to maximize visual impact rather than acoustic resonance, which was a common compromise and a frustrating one. The second stall was a seasonal trader Sael didn't recognize, selling crystals sourced from the northern reefs, which ran smaller and clearer but lacked the mineral density that gave a good resonance hold.

The third stall was new. Or rather: not new, but recently expanded, taking up the space where old Penrick had sold rope fittings before his hands got too stiff for the work. The expansion was made of salvaged planking and current-tube frames, and it held, arranged on a slanted display board covered in dark cloth, a set of current-harnesses — the small mechanical rigs that the Midwater ran on, pulling energy from the ocean's flow the way a mill wheel pulled energy from a river's.

Tucked in the corner of the same stall, almost as an afterthought, was a small tray of resonance crystals.

Sael moved toward them.

"If you're looking at the third one from the left," said a voice from under the stall, "don't."

Sael looked. Someone was lying under the display board, visible only as a pair of boots and the lower half of arms working on something mechanical, something that made a periodic clicking sound. One of the harness units, taken apart for repair.

"Why not?" Sael asked.

"Because it's cracked."

The crystal in question looked fine. Sael picked it up and held it to the bioluminescent light from below, angling it until the interior showed. There: a hairline fracture, running the full diameter, nearly invisible against the mineral grain unless you knew exactly where to look.

"You can see that from down there?"

"I could see it when I bought the lot, which is why I paid less than market rate and why I'm not charging you market rate either." The person under the stall slid out. She was late twenties, dark-skinned, wearing a work apron whose original color was genuinely impossible to determine, now being primarily the deep blue-green of bioluminescent residue worked into every fiber. Her hands, when she stood and wiped them on a cloth, were the same color to the wrist — not dirty, exactly, but permanently stained, the mark of someone who worked with the light-bearing organisms long enough that the chemical signature had become part of them. She looked at Sael with the expression of someone accustomed to having their assessments doubted. "It'll hold resonance for maybe three uses before the crack propagates. You'd know something was wrong before it failed entirely, but you'd lose whatever you were listening to."

Sael set the cracked crystal down. "That's the best one in the tray."

"Correct." She leaned against the display board. "The others are worse. I keep the tray out because people stop when they see crystals and sometimes they're actually interested in the harnesses, which is what I actually sell."

"You're using crystals as bait."

"I'm using crystals as discovery mechanism. There's a philosophical difference." She tilted her head. "You're a Tide-Speaker."

It wasn't a question. Sael got used to this. "The gloves give me away?"

"The gloves and the way you held the crystal. Tide-Speakers hold them like someone holding a glass they're afraid is already broken." She crossed her arms. The bioluminescent stain on her forearms caught the upward light and seemed to glow faintly. "You planning to find me another tray somewhere?"

"I might." Sael looked at the harness units on the display board. Six of them, varying sizes, each one representing a different trade in Midwater design — the large ones for stationary habitat power, the smaller for personal-use mechanical rigs, the smallest no bigger than a fist, built for precision applications. They were well-made. Better than well-made: the casework was cleaner than it needed to be, the flow channels cut with a precision that implied someone measuring rather than estimating. "These are yours?"

"Built and tuned." Something in her voice changed slightly when she said this. Not pride exactly. More like the flat statement of fact that comes from having done something correctly enough times that qualification feels dishonest. "Lira. And you're — "

"Sael."

"Right. You're the one who argued with Councilor Veth at the Resonance Accounting last season. My neighbor was in the gallery."

Sael felt the familiar small contraction that came with being known for that particular event. "Argued is generous. I submitted a formal counterpoint."

"Your neighbor described it differently." Lira's expression was readable: she found this mildly entertaining and was not going to pretend otherwise. "She said you told him his acoustic models were built on thirty-year-old current maps."

"They were. They are."

"And?"

"And the Council accepted the models and declined the counterpoint."

Lira looked at them for a moment with an expression that Sael couldn't entirely categorize — skeptical, but not dismissive. Something more considered than dismissal. "What would you actually use the crystal for? If you found one."

"Deep resonance sessions." Sael sat on the edge of the dock planking where there was room. "Long work, below the platform. The city's hull attenuates the current-voice significantly — the crystals compensate for that, give you a cleaner channel."

"Compensate how?"

"Mineral resonance. The crystal lattice vibrates sympathetically with specific current frequencies — it's essentially a filter and amplifier combined. You hold it, the signal clarifies."

Lira's expression shifted toward something Sael recognized from very specific people — engineers, mostly, when they heard an explanation that almost made sense but not quite. "The lattice isn't doing the amplifying. The lattice is creating a local pressure differential by its acoustic properties. The amplification comes from the pressure differential focusing incoming current."

Sael considered this. "That's a different description of the same effect."

"It's the correct description of the mechanism rather than the felt experience of the result." She paused. "I don't mean that as an insult. I'm saying if you understand the mechanism you know what'll break it."

"The crack breaks it."

"The crack breaks it because it disrupts the pressure differential. Which is also why you can't fix a cracked crystal by filling the fracture — the fill material has different acoustic properties than the stone, so you get interference instead of amplification." She said this with the ease of someone who has worked out the reasoning from first principles and now finds the working-out itself more interesting than the fact. "Did your teachers explain it that way?"

"My teachers explained it as a matter of attunement."

"Which means?"

"Which means the Tide-Speaker needs to be properly present to the crystal's voice, and then the current-voice comes through."

Lira looked at them. "That sounds like the same mechanism stated mystically."

"It might be," Sael said.

This seemed to surprise Lira slightly — the concession, or perhaps the ease of it. She unfolded her arms. "I've been trying to work out if there's a non-mystical explanation for why some Tide-Speakers pull more signal than others with identical equipment. I have three who've borrowed my test harnesses and the signal quality varies by about forty percent between them."

"The 'attunement' explanation covers that."

"I know it covers it. I want to know what the attunement is actually doing physiologically." She held up one stained hand. "I listen to currents. I read their flow patterns, track variance, model the pulls and eddies. I built a whole mapping system for the currents through this section of the Midwater so I could tune my harnesses to pull maximum efficiency. The harnesses perform about twenty percent better than anything else in the market because of it." She lowered the hand. "I don't call that 'listening to the current-voice.' I call it measurement and engineering. But I'm not sure the difference between what I do and what you do is as significant as either of us is supposed to believe."

The noise of the market moved around them — a vendor three platforms over calling prices for processed seaweed, the creak of a rope bridge taking weight, the deep background rushing of water through the suspension cables that held the whole Midwater up, a sound so constant it existed below hearing, felt more than perceived. Somewhere deeper than the market, far enough down that sound distorted, something moved through the water in a low repeating call that might have been a whale or might have been current through a geological feature or might have been something else.

Sael heard it differently than Lira would hear it, probably. Or experienced it differently. The mechanism might be the same.

"Your harnesses have been pulling less lately," Sael said.

Lira looked at them. "How did you — "

"The current-harnesses on the northern rim were running quiet when I came in on the descent-line. Low-frequency hum instead of the usual mid-range. That's reduced flow volume."

"Yes." The word was flat. "About eight percent down over the last six weeks, which is outside normal seasonal variance for this section. My models say it should have come back up after the cold-current exchange in the seventh week of this season, and it hasn't." She looked at the harnesses on the display board with the expression Morvau had worn over her fish tanks — the tiredness of an explanation that had stopped satisfying. "I've checked my equipment. The harnesses are calibrated correctly. The current is just weaker."

"It's not a local variance."

"No." She paused. "You know something about it."

"I know it's not local. That's most of what I know." Sael stood, brushed the market off their hands. "I should find crystals."

"Veldau on the west side has a new lot from the southern mineral beds. They're not cut well but the raw acoustic density is higher than anything she usually stocks — I bought two for my own current-reading work and they're genuinely better than they look." She said this with the slightly aggrieved tone of someone sharing useful information and being slightly annoyed at themselves for it. "Tell her I sent you and she'll let you see the uncut pieces before she marks them up."

Sael looked at her. "You don't like Tide-Speakers."

"I don't have strong opinions about Tide-Speakers as a category. I have strong opinions about people who mistake the description of experience for an explanation of mechanism." She reached under the display board and came back up with the harness unit she'd been repairing, turning it in the upward bioluminescent light to check the flow channels. "But you were right about the current being weaker, and you were right about the crack, and you didn't tell me I was wrong about the pressure differential mechanism, so." A small, precise gesture that dismissed the conversation without hostility. "Veldau. West side. Tell her Lira."

---

Sael bought two crystals from Veldau, who was exactly as Lira had described: ungracious but not dishonest, with a new lot of southern stone that ran rough and dense and sang when Sael held them close to the water's surface in a way that the polished crystals rarely did anymore.

On the way back to the descent-line, Sael stopped at Morvau's stall again. Not to buy. Morvau was alone for a moment, the afternoon crowd having thinned.

"The longtail," Sael said. "Twelve days. Before that?"

Morvau set down the knife she was cleaning. "Fifteen days before that. Three weeks before that, three days." She looked at Sael with the directness of someone who has been not-saying a thing for long enough that saying it is a relief. "I've been fishing these waters for twenty-two years. The longtail has never gone this long. Not sick-time, not storm-time, not even the bad current season fourteen years ago." She picked the knife back up. Her hands were steady. "What's happening down there?"

"I don't know yet," Sael said.

"You're going to find out."

"I'm going to try."

Morvau nodded once, the nod of someone confirming a transaction, and went back to work.

---

The ascent was slower than the descent. The descent-line motors ran at half-pull on the way up to save on current-draw, which meant two minutes of slow rise through the hull rather than three seconds of drop. Sael hung in the harness and watched the Midwater diminish below them — the market's blue-green glow, the rope bridges swaying in the perpetual motion of water under everything, the harness units on the habitat walls turning slowly, and turning slowly, and turning slowly, pulling their reduced current from a water that was moving differently than it had moved six weeks ago.

Eight percent down in six weeks. Morvau's longtail gone for twelve days. The dissonance this morning at dawn, the quality of wrongness in the deep current that Sael had carried since waking and had not been able to put down.

The hull opened above them, dilating, and the grey Surface light came down.

Below: the market's noise, the creak of rope bridges, the distant whale-call or geological voice or whatever it was that moved through deep water in long slow repetitions, carrying information no one was reading.

Sael emerged into the Surface and the hull closed beneath them, and the sound of the Midwater went immediately muffled, and then the descent platform's familiar smell of salt and civic maintenance replaced it, and the Surface reasserted itself: polished, ordered, facing in the wrong direction.

The two crystals in Sael's pocket were warm from handling. They would hold signal well — Lira had been right about the density. Good tools for the work ahead, whatever shape that work took.

Sael stood on the platform for a moment before stepping off, letting the Midwater's sound finish fading.

Then walked back into the Surface, carrying what they knew.
