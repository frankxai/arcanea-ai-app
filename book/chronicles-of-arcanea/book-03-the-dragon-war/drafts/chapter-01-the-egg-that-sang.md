# Chapter One: The Egg That Sang

The Forge announced itself three days before Kael reached it.

He felt it through his boots first — a low harmonic in the stone of the mountain road, steady as a held breath, rising from somewhere far below the path. Then through his palms when he rested against a waymarker at the second switchback. Then through his sternum when he slept, fitfully, at the traveler's shelter on the high pass, waking at intervals to press his hand against the wall and feel the mountain's fever.

Mount Pyralis was hot. Not the surface warmth of volcanic activity that the geological surveys attributed to the magma chambers — he'd read those surveys on the three-day ride from the Luminary, and they were thorough and useless. This heat had structure. It moved in patterns that his Foundation sense read the way a mason reads stress fractures: organized, intentional, and getting stronger.

By the time the Forge's outer gates came into view — black iron set into obsidian walls that caught the afternoon light and threw it back as something darker — the vibration in the stone had become a frequency he could feel in his molars. Not painful. Insistent. The kind of thing you couldn't ignore once you'd noticed it, the way you can't un-hear a rhythm once your body has started keeping time.

He dismounted at the gate and pressed both palms flat against the road.

There. Below the volcanic tremors, below the geothermal activity, below the Forge's furnaces and training grounds and the thousand students who walked its halls — a sound that was not a sound. A heat-pattern that his Foundation Gate translated into something almost like language, the way a current of water translates into direction if you know how to feel it.

The egg was singing.

---

The Forge's receiving hall was not designed for diplomacy.

Obsidian floors. Walls that radiated heat from the magma channels threaded through the building's architecture like blood vessels. Torches that burned without fuel — fire drawn directly from the volcano's throat — casting shadows that moved in ways shadows shouldn't, following the ambient heat patterns rather than the light. The air tasted of iron and ozone and something underneath both that reminded Kael, incongruously, of the smell of his father's forge at the end of a long day: hot metal cooling, spent energy, the particular warmth of work that mattered.

He was sweating. The Forge maintained an internal temperature that its residents had adapted to and its visitors had not. Kael's Luminary envoy robes — light weave, designed for the Crystalpeak's crystalline cool — were catastrophically wrong for this environment. He resisted the urge to roll his sleeves. An envoy did not roll his sleeves.

"Thornfield." Commandant Ashcroft descended the entrance stair with the deliberate pace of a man who arranged his arrivals for maximum effect. He was tall, iron-haired, and built like the volcanic rock that surrounded them: dense, angular, radiating something that wasn't quite heat and wasn't quite authority but occupied the same space in a room. "The Luminary's youngest diplomatic appointment in sixty years. Celestine must be either very confident or very desperate."

"Headmistress Celestine sends her regards," Kael said. "And her concern. We've been detecting seismic anomalies originating from this location for three weeks."

"The mountain is a volcano. Seismic anomalies are its default state."

"These aren't volcanic. They're harmonic. Regular frequency, increasing amplitude, centered not on the magma chambers but on the caldera rim." He paused. "On the Dragon Roosts."

Ashcroft's expression did not change. Kael's Foundation sense read the micro-shift in the man's weight distribution — a tightening in the left knee, the kind of thing a body did when it was preparing to stand firm against pressure it had been expecting.

"The Roosts are under restricted access," Ashcroft said. "Standard protocol for Dragon activity."

"It's not standard Dragon activity. Your own monitoring stations have been reporting readings they can't classify. I felt it from three days' ride out." Kael let the statement settle. He was not good at politics. He was good at stone. Stone did not negotiate. "Something is happening in those Roosts that your instruments can't measure. My Gate can."

Ashcroft studied him. The torches shifted, following the heat of the Commandant's attention the way iron filings follow a magnet.

"Your Gate," Ashcroft said. "Foundation."

"Yes."

"Foundation doesn't read fire."

"It reads structure. Fire has structure, if you go deep enough. Whatever is happening in the Roosts is reorganizing something at a level below temperature. Below molecular. I can't describe it more precisely than that because I've never felt anything like it before."

A long silence. The obsidian walls hummed — the mountain's permanent song, which Kael was beginning to separate from the other frequency, the one that rose from the caldera's rim like heat from a sleeping body.

"You'll have supervised access to the Roosts," Ashcroft said. "Supervised. One escort, my choosing. You report your findings to me before you report them to Celestine. Those are my terms."

Kael wanted to argue. The envoy protocols were clear: diplomatic observers maintained independent reporting lines. But the Forge was not the Luminary, and Ashcroft was not Celestine, and the thing singing from the caldera rim was getting louder by the hour and he needed to be close enough to hear it properly.

"Accepted," he said.

Ashcroft turned toward the staircase. "Your escort will meet you at the seventh bell. I suggest you change into something that won't catch fire."

---

His escort was not what he expected.

She was waiting outside the Roost access corridor when he arrived — tall, sharp-angled, wearing Forge training blacks with the sleeves pushed up to reveal forearms marked with pale burn scars that she made no attempt to cover. Her posture was simultaneously at ease and on guard, the way a drawn bowstring is at ease. Her eyes found him and assessed him in approximately the time it took him to realize he was being assessed.

"Vesper," she said. "Third-year. Void primary, Fire secondary. I'm your babysitter."

"Kael. Luminary envoy."

"I know who you are." She started walking. "Thornfield. Foundation. The one who broke Ironhold."

He felt it land — the old weight, settling between his shoulders like a slab of granite he'd been carrying so long he sometimes forgot to notice it. But something in the way she said it was different from how most people said it. No pity. No careful avoidance. Just a fact she'd learned and filed and moved past, the way you'd note a structural feature of a building without commenting on whether it was attractive.

"I didn't break Ironhold," he said. The correction was automatic and inadequate, but it was the only version of the response he'd learned to give.

"Fine. You broke six walls in Ironhold and the eastern quarter subsided and the mason's guild has been rebuilding it for two years. The distinction matters to you. I'm not arguing." She turned a corner and the corridor temperature jumped ten degrees. "What I care about is whether your Foundation sense is actually doing what Ashcroft says it's doing, or whether I'm escorting a Luminary diplomat through a restricted area so Ashcroft can claim inter-Academy cooperation in his report to the Council."

Kael almost smiled. He suppressed it, because smiling at someone who was essentially asking whether he was genuine or theatrical felt like it would answer the wrong question.

"Take me to the egg," he said. "I'll show you."

---

The Dragon Roosts occupied the caldera rim of Mount Pyralis — the shattered crown of the volcano's ancient peak, open to the sky, with walls of black basalt that radiated the mountain's deep heat in visible waves. The floor was natural rock, worn smooth by centuries of draconic occupation: grooves where tails had rested, depressions where bodies had curled, scorch marks that were old enough to have fossilized.

Most of the alcoves were empty. The Forge maintained the Roosts for the four Awake Dragons who used them — Embera primarily, Obsidius occasionally, and two Fire-blooded whose names Kael didn't know. Tonight, only one alcove was occupied.

The egg sat on a pedestal of volcanic glass in the largest alcove, surrounded by a heat-containment field that the Forge's best artificers maintained in rotating shifts. It was the size of a wine barrel, mottled dark red and black, and it should have been cold. Seraphine's unkindled eggs had been cold for two hundred and fifty years — warm enough to remain viable, too cold to spark, tended by a mother Dragon's stubborn, patient fire that every expert agreed was a vigil rather than a gestation.

This egg was not cold.

Kael felt it from the corridor entrance — thirty paces away, through stone and warding fields and the insulation of the heat-containment system. A pulse. Not heat. Not vibration. Something that his Foundation Gate received the way his ears received sound: a pressure that carried information.

He stopped walking.

Vesper, two steps ahead, turned. Looked at his face. "You feel it."

"Yes."

"What does it feel like? Through Foundation?"

He tried to find the right words. Foundation dealt in structure — in the deep language of matter, the way things were arranged at their most fundamental level. What the egg was doing wasn't structural in any way he'd been trained to understand. It was more like —

"Composition," he said. "It's composing itself. Not growing — there's nothing biological about this, not in the way eggs usually develop. It's rearranging its own internal structure. Rebuilding from the inside according to a pattern I've never —" He pressed his hand against the basalt wall and closed his eyes. "It's a frequency. A harmonic. Every few seconds the pattern shifts, adds a new element, integrates it. Like watching someone build a chord, note by note, except the notes are made of — I don't have a word. The notes are made of the thing that heat becomes when it goes deeper than temperature."

He opened his eyes. Vesper was watching him with an expression he couldn't read. Not skepticism. Not the performative interest of someone humoring a visiting dignitary. Something rawer. Something that looked, if he was honest, like recognition.

"I feel it too," she said. "Through Void. It feels like — absence with intention. A space where something is choosing to become. I've felt it since the first night. Through three floors of volcanic rock. Through sleep." She paused. "Ashcroft knows. That's why I'm your escort. He wants to know if what you feel and what I feel are the same thing."

They looked at each other in the corridor. The egg's frequency pulsed — a wave of structured energy that moved through the basalt, through the floor, up through the soles of Kael's boots and into the bones of his legs and his spine and his chest. Not painful. Not warm. Insistent in the way that something alive is insistent: a heartbeat, a breath, the particular pressure of a thing that exists and intends to continue existing.

"It's not the same thing," Kael said slowly. "You're feeling the space it's building in. I'm feeling the structure it's building."

"So between us —"

"Between us we're hearing two parts of the same song."

The egg pulsed again. The heat-containment field flickered — a momentary dropout, like a candle flame caught in a draft — and for one second the raw frequency hit Kael unmediated, and he felt it clearly.

Not just structure. Not just composition. Language. The egg was arranging itself according to a grammar that preceded the Gates, preceded the Gods, preceded the distinction between fire and void and every element that came after. A grammar from the time when the universe was deciding what it was going to be and hadn't finished yet.

He took a step toward the alcove. Then another. Vesper didn't stop him.

The egg was warm. Not fire-warm — warm the way a living thing is warm, with the specific heat of something that has an interior and an outside and knows the difference. He crouched beside the pedestal. He placed both hands on the volcanic glass base.

The world went deep.

Not vision. Not sound. A comprehension that bypassed his senses entirely and arrived in the part of his mind where Foundation lived — the part that understood how things held together, the structural grammar of the real. He felt the egg's interior: not a developing Dragon, not a biological organism gestating toward hatching. A *pattern*, assembling itself from frequencies that should not be able to coexist in the same space. Fire and void and water and wind and earth and light and every other elemental force he'd ever encountered and some he hadn't, woven together in an arrangement so complex that his mind could only hold the edges of it.

The pattern pulsed. And in the pulse, something looked back at him.

Not a consciousness. Not yet. Something pre-conscious — the thing that consciousness would become when the pattern finished composing itself. A vast, patient, unspeakably old potential, curled inside a shell the size of a wine barrel, waiting with the particular patience of something that had been waiting for longer than the mountain it sat inside.

It recognized him. He felt that clearly. Not his name or his face or his history — it recognized his *frequency*. The Foundation note he carried. As if it had been listening for that specific note among all the notes in the world, and now that it had found it, it could continue its composition with a piece it had been missing.

It added his frequency to the chord it was building. He felt himself become part of the song.

And then it released him, gently, the way you'd set down a valuable thing you'd been holding carefully, and Kael was crouching beside an egg in a volcanic nest with tears on his face that he didn't remember producing and Vesper standing behind him with her arms wrapped around herself and her own tears falling and her voice saying, very quietly:

"It knows us."

---

He didn't sleep that night.

He lay in the Forge's guest quarters — an obsidian-walled room that maintained a temperature optimized for volcanic natives and not for Luminary diplomats — and stared at the ceiling and felt the egg's frequency through four floors of stone. Faint now, at this distance. Steady. Present, the way a heartbeat is present when you're lying still enough to feel it.

The Forge went about its business around him. Students in the corridor below — late-night training session sounds, the particular thrum of fire being channeled under instruction. A Dragon's distant rumble from the Roosts, which might have been Embera or might have been the mountain itself, the distinction blurred at this depth.

He should write to Celestine. The envoy protocol was clear: initial impressions within twenty-four hours, formatted according to the diplomatic observation template, transmitted via the Luminary's crystal-relay network. He had the template. He had the relay access code. He had approximately none of the ability to reduce what he'd felt in the Roosts to a formatted observation.

He thought about what the egg had done — the way it had added his frequency to whatever it was building inside itself. Not taken. Added. The way a musician adds a note to a chord: because the chord needed it, because the composition was incomplete without it.

He thought about Ironhold. The eastern quarter. The walls that fell because the stone answered his fear and his anger and his un-grounded chaos with the only response stone knows: release. Cracking along the lines of least resistance. Becoming rubble because the thing moving through it had no structure of its own.

The egg had structure. The egg was nothing but structure — the most intricate, intentional pattern he had ever felt, building itself with a precision that made the Luminary's finest stone-work look like a child's first attempt at stacking blocks.

And it had recognized his frequency as something it needed.

This was the thought he couldn't set down. Not that the egg was remarkable — it was, but remarkable things existed and you catalogued them and moved on. The thought that wouldn't release him was simpler and more destabilizing: something of staggering complexity and beauty had examined his fundamental nature and found it *useful*.

Not dangerous. Not something to be managed or contained or sent away to an Academy before it broke anything else.

Useful.

He pressed his hand against the wall. The frequency pulsed, distant and steady, through the stone.

"What are you?" he whispered.

The egg did not answer. It was busy composing. But the pulse shifted, slightly — a variation in the pattern that his Foundation sense read as acknowledgment. As if the thing inside the egg had heard his question and filed it under topics to be addressed later, when it had finished becoming whatever it was becoming.

He lay in the dark and listened to something ancient build itself, note by note, in a language that preceded words.

In the morning, he would write to Celestine. He would format his observations according to the template. He would use the approved terminology: *anomalous harmonic activity*, *unprecedented structural reorganization*, *recommend continued observation*.

He would not mention the tears.

He would not mention that for the first time since Ironhold — the first time in three years — he had felt something that was not colored by what he had broken. A clean frequency. A song without guilt in it. Something vast and patient and uninterested in his mistakes, reaching toward what he was instead of what he'd done.

He would not mention this because he did not know how to put it in a report, and he did not know whether it was the egg's doing or his own need doing what need does: finding hope in whatever it can reach.

But he thought about it. In the dark, with the mountain humming around him and the egg singing below, he thought about it.

The Forge burned through the night. The egg composed. And Kael Thornfield lay awake in his borrowed room and felt, for the first time in a very long time, as if the ground beneath him might be solid enough to stand on.
