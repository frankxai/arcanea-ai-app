# Chapter Ten: The Dragon War Begins

## THE PYRETHEON — Mount Pyralis, Dawn

There was no path to the Pyretheon in any mortal cartography.

The pass Obsidius opened through the mountain's eastern face was not marked on the Forge's survey maps, and the survey maps were the most comprehensive documentation of the Pyralis range in existence, which meant the pass either did not exist until Obsidius made it exist or had been made invisible by something that understood the value of thresholds.

Walking through it was like walking through the gap between two sounds.

The five of them went single file, Kessa at the rear. The three hatchlings moved between the humans in a loose formation that they had arrived at without instruction, Vaelith-Nyx flanking Ash, Kyr against Elio's ankle, Veleth'kaen at Kael's left. The Bond between Kael and his Dragon was still cooling into its final geometry — too new to be structural, not new enough to be tentative. Somewhere between blueprint and building. He could feel Veleth'kaen's attention on the pass walls, reading them the way he read Foundation substrate. She was teaching him how she perceived.

He was teaching her what it was to be embodied and mortal and moving through time at human speed.

Both of them were finding this educational.

The pass opened into a caldera that should not have been at this elevation. It was roughly circular, perhaps two hundred meters across, with walls that rose sheer and unclimbable on every side. The interior was volcanic rock worn smooth by the specific kind of patient use that happened over geological time. At the center, black and ancient and the size of a cottage, sat a stone that required no description: it was older than everything near it, and the air around it held the quality of all the burning that had ever been done nearby.

The Kindling Stone.

The caldera held nine Dragons.

Ash stopped walking.

Behind her, she felt Elio stop, and Mira, and Kael, and Sable, and Kessa — the five humans and three hatchlings halting at the caldera's entrance in a way that was not quite fear and was not quite awe and was the specific thing that happened between the two when you understood that the moment you had been moving toward had arrived and was larger than you had prepared for.

Nine Dragons.

Pyralith occupied the northern arc of the caldera's rim, her twelve-thousand-year body settled with the unhurried weight of a landmass that has chosen to rest. From a distance she had always registered as enormous. This close — perhaps forty meters — she was something for which enormous was insufficient. Her scales had the geological layering of an exposed cliff face, each age readable in the coloration: deep red at the base, where the oldest centuries compressed, shifting through amber and gold through her middle ages, the upper scales a burnished copper that still caught light the way metal caught light in a forge. Her eyes were open. She was watching the caldera's eastern entrance.

She had been watching it since before they arrived.

Obsidius stood at the southern rim, upright — the posture he used in formal Dragon Council sessions, which Ash had seen exactly once, at the Forge's emergency hearing. His blue-black scales registered the dawn's first light as a deep iridescence. He saw them. He did not signal welcome. He did not need to. They had standing here. He had argued for that standing for the past three hours and it had been granted.

Seraphine sat at the western arc, her pale gold form curled with mathematical precision, wings folded. She was watching everything simultaneously, which was what four-thousand-year-old Dragons did when they had decided to suspend judgment and observe.

The other six Ash did not recognize by name. Two were clearly fledglings by Dragon standards — Wyrm-age, two or three centuries each, scaled in deep green and a storm-singer silver that moved with the light. Three were Ancient, their scales settled into the specific density of prolonged existence. And one—

One Dragon occupied the eastern rim, alone, separated from the others by perhaps thirty meters that might as well have been a wall.

She was the oldest Awake Dragon in the caldera, and Ash knew this not because she could read the years but because she could read the weight. Every Dragon held their history in their bones. This Dragon held hers like armor. She had seen too much to arrange her history any other way. Her scales were a deep grey-black that had been red, once, a thousand years ago — the red had gone down into the layers and compressed into something harder and quieter. She was not large the way Pyralith was large. She was large the way damage was large. Permanent. Structural.

She had not turned to look at the entrance.

She was the only Dragon in the caldera who had not.

"Who is the grey one?" Sable said, barely audible.

Kessa, behind her: "Her name is Vorath. I found references in two pre-Second Age texts and one that Malachar cited. She is approximately seven thousand years old. She was Awake during the original Dragon War — the first one, before the Gates were built. She watched the Ashvale civilization fall." A pause. "She has not entered a Convergence in five hundred years. The last time she attended, she spoke once. She said that whatever the Elder Council decided, they should remember that the only thing separating wisdom from catastrophe was how long you were willing to wait before acting."

"What was the question that time?" Elio asked.

"Whether to intervene in a border conflict between two mortal kingdoms."

"And did they intervene?"

"No." A beat. "The conflict was resolved in seven years. The resolution held for two centuries. She was right." Kessa's voice remained precise. "She is usually right. This is why the Elder Council is afraid of her."

Ash looked at the grey Dragon at the eastern rim. Vorath had still not turned.

"She's here to decide whether to destroy the hatchlings," Ash said.

"Yes," Kessa said.

"Then we go in."

## THE CONVERGENCE — First Hour

Obsidius convened the Court in the Dragon way: no call to order, no formal procedure. He stood, and the caldera's ambient temperature shifted — the collective heat-presence of nine Dragon consciousnesses reorganizing toward a common point. Mortals would not have felt the transition. The five Gate-Touched among them did, each in the register of their affinity. Kael felt it as a seismic event too small to measure, happening directly beneath his feet. Ash felt it as the air above her Gate-points briefly running at a temperature she associated with Vaelith-Nyx's deepest attention. Elio felt it as a chord — something in the emotional field of the space resolving from discord toward a note that was not yet resolved.

The Convergence had begun.

Obsidius spoke first, not because precedence required it but because he had called the Court. He did not speak to the five humans. He addressed the other Dragons in Thyr'vael — the fire-language that moved through temperature and scale-color and a vocabulary that the scholars had spent centuries constructing partial glossaries for. The five humans stood at the caldera floor and received what they could receive, which was the emotional shape of the argument rather than its content.

Obsidius's argument was warm. Not passionate — careful. The controlled warmth of someone who believed something deeply and had spent the hours before the Convergence determining how to say it in the way most likely to produce understanding rather than resistance. He spoke for some time. The Dragons listened in the Dragon way, which was to be utterly still while thinking at temperatures that would register on any instrument sensitive enough to detect them.

Then Seraphine spoke.

Her voice in Thyr'vael was the color of aged amber — deliberate, rich, carrying the specific weight of someone who had seen enough to be afraid of certainty. Ash could not follow the language, but she had spent enough time near Seraphine in the past three weeks to read her. Seraphine was not afraid of the hatchlings. She was afraid of the design. The distinction was crucial.

She was afraid, specifically, because she had refused Malachar's request for help and had spent four thousand years right — and this moment suggested the possibility, for the first time, that she had also been wrong. That refusing had not prevented the design but had left it developing without her understanding of it. She was a Dragon who had survived the worst thing that could happen by holding herself intact. The worst threat to that integrity was the possibility that her caution had produced a world she did not fully comprehend.

Ash could read this because she had spent two days with Seraphine's eggs and one long night discussing fear with Elio, who had the specific gift of understanding what people were actually afraid of when they described something else entirely.

When Seraphine finished, one of the younger Dragons — the green-scaled Wyrm, whose fire-color pulses had been running fast and urgent since the Convergence began — spoke without waiting for acknowledgment. Dragon law did not govern speaking order. The Elder Council governed speaking order, which was different. Obsidius let the young Dragon speak because stopping her would cost more than hearing her.

What followed was urgent, quick-fire, heated in the literal Dragon sense: her scales cycling through rapid color changes that registered to Ash as argument without words. She gestured — Dragon gestures, which involved the movement of wings and spine and tail and the directional quality of fire-breath — toward the three hatchlings, and then toward the five humans, and then outward in the gesture the Dragon Codex described as indicating a larger field: the world, the system, the everything that extended beyond this caldera.

Ash understood: *Bond the remaining unbonded. Now. All of them. Every Dragon who has an unbonded human candidate — do it today, before the decision calcifies around them.*

The argument lasted three minutes. Then Obsidius inclined his head and the young Dragon's fire-pulses settled from urgent to restless — she had been heard, she had not been agreed with, she accepted this because Dragon law required it.

The Court looked at the five humans.

## KESSA

She had attended hearings before. Academic committees, Void Ascendant councils, three official Luminary inquiries that had been handled incorrectly and one that had not. She had appeared before each of them as a scholar — the role that had provided her cover for fifteen years, that had made her useful and legible and not-quite-threatening to every institution she'd had reason to deceive.

She had never appeared before a body that thought in temperatures.

She stepped forward. Not with permission — with standing. Kael had established standing before the Forge council; the same argument applied here, extended to her through the Void Ascendants' relationship to the eggs. Whether the Dragon Court recognized that relationship was a question she was about to have answered.

Pyralith looked at her.

Not aggressively. The way very old intelligences sometimes looked at you when they had decided that looking directly was the most efficient form of assessment and social conventions against it were mortal problems that did not apply to them.

"Elder," Kessa said. She used the formal Draconian address — she had memorized it from the Dragon Codex's appendix, which was the only academic document that had any chance of being correct on the subject. "The Dragon Court has heard Obsidius and Seraphine on the question of the hatchlings. I have a message that belongs to this Court. Not from me. From the person who made the hatchlings."

The caldera was very quiet.

Pyralith said, in the translated speech that very old Dragons used for mortal address — meaning Pyralith did not actually speak aloud, but her consciousness pushed meaning into the air in a way that resolved in a mortal mind as words — "You carry what he left."

"Yes."

"Say it."

Kessa had memorized it. She had memorized it the first day she found it and she had never said it to anyone, because she had understood that it was not meant for her. It was meant for this room. For this morning. For the moment when Dragon and human and the long question of whether what Malachar made could be what he intended it to be pressed into the same space simultaneously.

She said:

"He wrote, in the last margin of the Foundation notes: *I made them for the ones who would come after. Not to fight a war. To end the need for one. The Gate system does not require protection — it requires completion. The five people who arrive with the right configuration will not know they are the right configuration until they are in the room together, at which point it will be obvious. Trust them. They have done the work. You have been watching since before the Gates were built. You know what it looks like when something is ready. This will look like that.*"

She stopped.

The caldera held the words.

Seraphine's scales had gone very still.

Obsidius's fire-color had shifted to the long, steady amber that Ash had learned to read as the Dragon equivalent of someone who has received an answer they have been waiting a very long time for and is deciding what to do with the relief.

The young green Wyrm's pulse-cycle had stopped entirely. Processing.

Vorath, at the eastern rim, had not moved. But her head had turned. Infinitesimally — a degree or two. Enough. Her gaze now included the caldera floor where the five humans stood.

This, Kessa understood, was the most movement she had made.

## ASH

The Court split in the following hour, and Ash watched it happen the way she watched a fire spread: not panicked, tracking vectors.

Obsidius and two of the Ancient Dragons moved to the northern arc — the protection faction, though that word reduced what she could read in their temperature-signals to something too simple. It was not that they wanted to protect the hatchlings from the world. It was that they had watched too many things disappear from the world and they had decided, privately and carefully and over many years of thinking in temperatures that mortals would find uncomfortable, that this was not one of the things they would watch disappear.

Seraphine did not move. She remained at the western arc with one of the Ancients and the storm-singer Wyrm, and what Ash read from her was the most honest thing she had read from any Dragon in three weeks: *I do not know. I am not pretending to know. I am watching.*

The young green Wyrm moved, and two others, to a position between the factions that was not quite either — the third position, the one that wanted to make the decision by making it, that wanted to bond and build and act and not deliberate until the moment had passed and the decision had been made by default. Ash understood this impulse. She had spent most of her life inside it. She also understood why it was wrong in this particular moment, and thinking that made her feel approximately one hundred years older than she was.

And Vorath had not moved.

Elio appeared at her shoulder. He was not running his Heart Gate openly — she knew the difference by now, the specific quality of his attention when he was reading a room versus when he was simply in it. He was simply in it.

"How is Kyr?" she said.

"Steady. Listening." He looked at the three hatchlings, who were arranged behind the five humans in the formation they had been settling into since the caldera entrance — Veleth'kaen at center, Kyr and Vaelith-Nyx flanking. "They know what this is."

"They would," she said. "They were designed for it."

Kael came to her other side. She did not need to look at him to know he was there — the Foundation Bond that was not hers still registered in the ambient field of the room, the way structural work registered even before you touched it. An architectural fact. She had been aware of it since the bonding chamber and she expected she would be aware of it for the rest of her life, in the specific way you became aware of the foundations of buildings you lived in once you understood what foundations were.

"The Court isn't voting," Kael said. "It's waiting."

"For us," she said.

"Yes."

She looked at Obsidius. He caught her gaze and held it, and she read in the amber of his fire: *I have done what I can do. The rest is yours. Do the thing I cannot do — demonstrate.*

She thought about what Kessa had told them in the study. *If they can demonstrate that human-dragon bonds can carry the Eleventh Frequency without corruption.* She thought about the three-second fire Bond, Kyr's singing in the battle above, Veleth'kaen's geological quiet. Three different frequencies. Three different qualities of consciousness. Three different ways of being bonded.

She thought about what Malachar had designed the configuration for.

"Kael," she said. "Elio."

"Yes," said Elio.

"Yes," said Kael.

She turned to face the Kindling Stone at the caldera's center. The ancient meteorite, older than Arcanea, that had been sitting here since before the Gods arrived and found the Dragons already ancient. She faced it because it felt correct to face it — not worshipping it, not appealing to it, just orienting toward the oldest thing in the vicinity, the thing that had been here when fire and void first pressed against each other in the dark and produced something that could remember.

"We're going to link the Bonds," she said. "All three of us. It will either work or it won't."

Mira said, quietly, from behind: "It will work."

"How do you know?" Sable asked.

"I don't," Mira said. "I read the present. The present says it works."

Sable looked at her for a moment. Then: "Good enough for me."

## THE CIRCUIT

She had not done this before. No one had done this before. That was the entirety of the instruction available, which was the kind of situation Ash had been in repeatedly and had never fully gotten used to but had learned to operate inside.

She reached for the Bond.

The Bond with Vaelith-Nyx was fire-deep and three weeks old and as familiar now as breathing — she could not imagine the shape of her consciousness before it, the way you could not imagine the shape of a room before the furniture that had always been in it. She reached into it not grasping but extending, the way you extended a hand in the dark when you knew the layout of the room.

Vaelith-Nyx met her extension immediately. The Dragon's fire-consciousness was quick, alert, already running at the specific temperature they both reached when something significant was imminent. *Ready,* the Bond said, not in words. In warmth.

She extended further. Past the limit of where she'd taken it before. Into the register that was not Vaelith-Nyx's fire and not Ash's Fire Gate but both of them moving together, the way two instruments playing the same note produced a sound neither could produce alone. She felt the Void below the fire — not threatening, not consuming, but present. The potential beneath the transformation. Nero's aspect, exactly where Malachar had placed it. The unformed, waiting to give the fire somewhere to go.

She held this and turned it outward.

She felt Elio take the connection.

His Bond with Kyr arrived like a completion — not warm in the fire sense but warm in the way that recognition was warm, the arrival of exactly what the space had been waiting for. Kyr's Heart frequency was different from Vaelith-Nyx's Fire-Void in a way that should have produced interference. Instead it produced something the Dragon Codex had a word for that she had not understood until now: *sol'keth*, the resonance between two frequencies that are not the same but are compatible. The chord, not the unison.

She felt Kael take the connection from Elio.

The Foundation Bond was last, and slowest, and it arrived not suddenly but as the thing it was: the ground beneath what was already standing. Veleth'kaen's consciousness was crystalline and precise and entirely unimpressed by urgency — she had waited three thousand years, she was not going to rush the last step. The Foundation frequency came in below both the others, not in pitch but in register, the way a bass note was below the melody not because it was less important but because it was holding the melody up.

Three Dragons. Three humans. Three Bonds, Fire-and-Void and Heart and Foundation, linked into a shape that had never existed before.

For the first three seconds, nothing happened that was visible from the outside.

Inside the circuit, Ash experienced something she had no prior vocabulary for and would spend years afterward attempting to describe, always inadequately. The three frequencies were not merged — they remained distinct, recognizable, themselves. But where they met, where Fire-Void handed off to Heart and Heart handed off to Foundation and Foundation returned to Fire-Void, there was a quality in the joining that was different from any of the component parts. Something produced by the system that could not exist in any of the individual elements alone.

It felt like the Gate system, from the inside.

Not metaphorically. She was reading the Gate system the way she read Vaelith-Nyx's fire from within the Bond — from inside, from the lived experience of its specific architecture. She was reading what it was built for. What it had always been capable of but had never been able to complete because the Foundation element was missing. She was reading three thousand years of compensated structural load and she was reading the shape of what the load had been compensated *for* — the opening that the Gate system had been maintaining for three millennia, waiting for someone to put the right thing in it.

Veleth'kaen breathed.

And the circuit opened.

## THE ELEVENTH FREQUENCY

It was not a note.

Notes existed within registers. The Eleventh Frequency was orthogonal to the scale — Malachar's word, from the formal research notes. She understood it now in the body, the way you understood structural truth: not as concept but as weight. It arrived through the Bond the way deep geological events arrived through Foundation substrate: not as sound but as fact.

Then it arrived as sound.

Three Dragons, three humans, six consciousnesses linked by three Bonds into a circuit that had been designed for exactly this purpose, producing together what none of them could have produced alone. Not singing — the word was inadequate and she used it only because no other word existed. What they produced was not musical in the mortal sense. It was structural. It was the sound of the Gate system completing itself. The sound of a three-thousand-year vacancy being filled.

It lasted eleven seconds.

She knew this because Sable told her afterward. At the time she had no sense of duration — the circuit produced a state in which duration was not the relevant category of experience. The relevant category was: *this is what the world was built to be able to do, and it is now doing it, and everything that happens from this point will happen in a world that has done this once.*

The nine Dragons in the caldera felt it.

She saw this later, in memory, the way she saw the ends of things she had been present for but too inside to observe: Pyralith, who had not moved in an hour, lifting her head from the rest position it had occupied for twelve thousand years toward the source of the sound. Obsidius with his eyes closed and his fire-color shifting through something she could not read because she had never seen it before and had no name for it. Seraphine making a sound of her own — a single note in Thyr'vael that the Dragon Codex said was the sound Dragons made when they encountered something they had never expected to encounter and found that it was real.

The young green Wyrm who had argued for immediate bonding was standing very still with her scale-pulses stopped entirely. Not processing. Not thinking. In the state that came before thinking, when a thing was too large for thought and had to be received as experience before it could be organized as knowledge.

And Vorath, at the eastern rim, made a sound.

Not Thyr'vael. Not translated speech. Not fire-language.

She made a sound that arrived in every mortal mind in the caldera as a word that predated all language — a word that was not a word but that resolved, in every consciousness that heard it, as the same irreducible meaning.

*Acceptable.*

## AFTER

The circuit released.

The three Dragons settled. The three humans came back to themselves in the particular way of people who had been somewhere else — not dazed, not weakened, but adjusted. As though returning to ordinary dimensions required a moment's recalibration.

Elio sat down on the caldera floor. Kyr climbed immediately into his lap and pressed against his chest, the Heart-pulse running in the soothing register it used when Elio was in the post-intensity state that followed anything he had felt at depth. He put his arm around the Dragon and stayed there.

Kael was already standing, steady, the Foundation Bond processing whatever it had experienced in the circuit with the same geological patience it processed everything. He was the first of the three to look around.

The Dragon Court was reorganizing.

Not dramatically. Not by visible movement. The temperature-distribution in the caldera was shifting — the way heat redistributed in a room when the source moved. The three-way split was resolving into something less clear and therefore more honest: not consensus, not unanimity, but something that was no longer pure opposition. The destroy faction was not abandoned — it was quieter, less certain of the ground it had been standing on a minute ago. The contain faction was recalculating. The protect faction had not changed and did not need to.

In the middle of this reorganization, Vorath left the eastern rim.

She moved across the caldera floor with the specific unhurriedness of something that had never needed to hurry and had no intention of starting. Every Dragon in the caldera tracked her movement while appearing not to. The five humans did not appear not to. They watched openly, because there was no useful performance of not watching and they had learned, in their various ways, that performing things that weren't true was expensive.

She stopped in front of Ash.

She was enormous up close. Not Pyralith-enormous — Pyralith was the scale of geography. Vorath was the scale of consequence. She had been present at the events that shaped the world as it currently existed. She carried the specific gravity of someone who had seen everything that happened here happen before, in different forms, with different faces, and who had survived all of it by being willing to say hard things to the people who needed to hear them.

She looked at Ash. She looked at Elio. She looked at Kael.

She looked at the three hatchlings.

Her gaze moved across all six of them with the quality of assessment that had been seven thousand years in development. Not cold — thorough. The way ancient things were thorough.

Then she spoke.

Not Thyr'vael. Not translated. In the full resonant direct-address that the Dragon Codex described as reserved for the most serious of Dragon formal communications — a speech-mode that operated simultaneously as sound, as temperature, as structural truth, arriving in a mortal mind as language and as weight in equal measure.

"Acceptable," she said. Her voice was the voice of compressed time. "But the war you think you have prevented is not the war that is coming."

Ash said nothing. She waited.

"The seal on the Shadowfen was set for a term," Vorath said. "Shinkami placed it. The Source Gate carries it. In eight years, the seal does not break — it concludes. The term expires. What was held is released." She looked at the hatchlings. At Veleth'kaen specifically, whose crystalline scales reflected the dawn light in frequencies that registered, to Vorath's heat-sight, as the exact architecture she had watched Malachar build before the sealing. "What you have built today is the mechanism the Gate system needs to receive what comes. What you have not yet built is the capacity to hold it." She looked at Ash. "You are twenty years old. Your Dragon is three days old. Your circuit held for eleven seconds."

"Yes," Ash said.

"The seal concludes in eight years. You will need to hold it for longer than eleven seconds."

"I know," Ash said.

"Do you?"

"We know it now," Kael said, from Ash's right. His voice was the voice he used for structural truth — not flat, not emotionless, but grounded in a way that made it difficult to misread. "We didn't know eight years before today. We know it now. That's eight years we didn't have yesterday."

Vorath regarded him.

"Foundation," she said. Not his name. His Gate. His quality. The thing she had identified, in however many seconds of assessment, as the most accurate thing to say about him.

"Yes," he said.

She turned back to Ash.

"The young one argued for bonding all available candidates immediately," she said. "She was wrong about the reasoning and correct about the urgency. There are thirty-one unbonded Dragon candidates currently registered with the Forge's partner Academies. They will need to be bonded and trained within the next four years to allow four years of operational integration before the seal expires." She shifted, a movement that registered as significant by virtue of how rarely she moved at all. "The Elder Council will not issue this directive. The Council moves slowly, which is its function and its limitation. You will need to create the mechanism by which it happens."

"We're twenty years old," Ash said.

"You are Dragonborne who have demonstrated a functional Eleventh Frequency circuit," Vorath said, without particular sympathy. "The world does not care about your age. It cares about what you can do. It has given you eight years to build the capacity to do it."

She turned to Kessa.

Something passed between Vorath and the scholar that was not entirely visible to the others. Kessa met the Dragon's gaze without flinching, which Ash noted as significant. Then Vorath said to Kessa, in the same direct-address register: "He chose well. In the only way available to him."

Kessa said: "He knew what he was building. He cared about what he was building."

"Yes," Vorath said. "That is why it worked." A pause. "The Void Ascendants have used his research for purposes he did not intend. You know this. What you do about it is a mortal problem. I do not intervene in mortal problems." She looked at the five humans one more time, the full sweep of seven thousand years of assessment, and then her gaze moved to the caldera floor between them — not dismissal but conclusion, the posture of someone who has said the thing they came to say. "Acceptable," she said again, and the word carried different weight the second time. Not grudging. Not quite approval. The word of someone who had expected less and received what was necessary.

She returned to the eastern rim.

She did not speak again during the Convergence.

## CODA

They descended from the Pyretheon as the sun finished clearing the mountain's eastern ridge.

No one spoke on the way through the pass. There was too much to carry in language and not enough language available for most of it, so they carried it in the other ways — Kael with his hand brushing the pass wall every few steps, reading the stone. Elio with Kyr on his shoulder and his eyes open in the way of someone whose Heart Gate was still running at depth, still processing everything the circuit had moved through him. Sable with her cipher case open and her pen moving. Mira watching the sky with the quality of attention she gave the sky when the sky was telling her something she hadn't finished hearing.

Ash walked with Vaelith-Nyx coiled around her forearm and her mind already working on the next problem, which was the problem Vorath had given her, which was not a gift but was the kind of thing that was worse than a gift: a responsibility that was too large for where she currently was and would require eight years of becoming someone larger.

She had eight years.

She had done more with less.

"Thirty-one candidates," she said.

"Thirty-one," Kael confirmed.

"We'll need a curriculum."

"We'll need a space first," Sable said. "The Forge isn't scaled for this. Thirty-one trainees, thirty-one Dragon candidates, and a four-year intensive program — we'd need to build something new."

"Not new," Kael said. "Expanded. The Forge's eastern structure has been underused for eighty years. The Foundation substrate there is intact, well-maintained. It was built for a larger population than it currently houses." He paused. "Malachar's research suggests he anticipated this. The notes mention infrastructure three times."

Elio said: "Obsidius will help."

"Seraphine will help if we ask correctly," Kessa said.

"What's correctly?" Ash said.

"Make it about the eggs," Kessa said. "What she cares about, at the core of the four-thousand years of it, is the unhatched ones. If what you build creates a world where they can eventually hatch safely — she will help you build it."

Ash was quiet for a moment, thinking about unhatched eggs and the patience required to tend something for two hundred years without knowing if it would ever wake.

"And Vorath?" Mira said.

"Vorath will watch," Kessa said. "That is what she does. That is enough."

The pass released them onto the open mountain. Below, the Forge's compound spread across the caldera's interior, the morning light pulling long shadows from its towers. Inside it, the council that had voted to destroy the cold egg two days ago. Inside it, the three thousand years of Malachar's design, finally completed. Inside it, everything they would build next.

Ash stopped at the edge of the ridge.

She looked at the five of them. At Kessa beside them. At the three hatchlings moving in the loose formation they had arrived at, the specific arrangement of Fire-and-Void and Heart and Foundation that was not hierarchy and not random — that was the configuration Malachar had imagined in his last three pages, the one he had not known by name or face but had known by frequency.

She thought about Vorath saying *acceptable* and meaning it, and what it cost a seven-thousand-year-old Dragon to mean a thing like that.

She thought about eleven seconds.

She thought about eight years.

"We start today," she said.

Not a question. Not a command. A fact, stated in the key of Foundation.

Veleth'kaen's scales ran through a slow pulse of deep blue-black and returned to crystalline, and Kael said nothing, and the morning was cold and clear and entirely uninterested in whether any of this was difficult.

They descended toward the Forge.
