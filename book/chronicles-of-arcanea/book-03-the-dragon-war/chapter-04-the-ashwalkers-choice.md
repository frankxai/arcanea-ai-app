# Chapter Four: The Ashwalker's Choice

The girl was walking southeast again.

Orin watched her from the ridgeline, thirty paces back, the way he'd watched her every morning for the past eleven days. Nim stood at the edge of camp with her pack on her shoulders and her face turned toward a point on the horizon that corresponded, on Orin's map, to the Ember Wastes and beyond them Mount Pyralis. The air around her shimmered. It did this when she was focused on something — the heat leaking from her skin in visible waves, bending the light, making the landscape behind her waver as if reality itself was reconsidering its commitment to solidity.

She was fourteen. She was also, by every metric Orin had for measuring danger, the most potentially destructive person in his group. Her Fire affinity had been growing since the egg started singing — whatever the egg was doing, Nim's body was resonating with it the way a tuning fork resonates with a struck bell. The air temperature around her had risen four degrees in eleven days. She set things on fire in her sleep. Two nights ago, her blanket had ignited at three in the morning and she hadn't woken up.

Orin had put the fire out. He'd put out the one the night before that, too, and the one three nights before that. He carried a water-skin specifically for this purpose now, strapped to his hip where his Shadowwatch patrol knife used to be.

Nim turned. She had a face that was all angles and no softness — dark skin, close-cropped hair, narrow eyes that looked at the world with the particular watchfulness of someone who had learned very young that the world did not watch back with equivalent care. She looked at Orin the way she looked at everything: measuring the distance, calculating whether it was safe.

"I need to go," she said.

"I know."

"Today."

"I know that too."

She waited. She had learned, in the weeks since Orin had pulled her out of the Registration holding facility in the Contested Territories, that Orin's pauses were not refusals. They were the spaces where he assembled the thing he was going to say from the several things he was considering.

"The Forge is nine days' walk," Orin said. "Through open terrain for the first three, then the Ember Wastes, which are called that for a reason, and then the mountain approach, which the Forge controls and monitors and does not generally welcome unannounced visitors to."

"I know."

"You're fourteen."

"I know that too."

"And you're proposing to walk into the most heavily fortified Academy in Arcanea — an institution that is currently in political lockdown over a Dragon hatching that has every power on the continent paying attention — and present yourself at the gate as a fourteen-year-old unregistered Fire-touched refugee with no documentation and no institutional affiliation."

Nim's jaw set. The air around her went from shimmering to visibly hot — the kind of heat that made Orin's face prickle from ten feet away. "I'm not proposing anything. I'm going. You can come or you can stay."

She said this the way she said most things: flat, certain, with a quality of having made the decision in a place below words and simply waiting for the rest of the world to catch up.

Orin looked at her. He looked at the camp behind them — twelve people, seven adults and five children, all Gate-touched, all unregistered, all traveling under Orin's protection through the Contested Territories toward the Sapphire Coast, where contacts could get them passage to the Archipelago and something that might qualify as safety.

He looked southeast, toward a mountain he couldn't see from here but could feel — not through any Gate sense, because he didn't have one, but through the simple animal awareness that something enormous was happening in that direction and everything with a pulse was orienting toward it.

"Darin," he called.

---

Darin Solace was sitting at the edge of camp, cleaning a water-skin with the methodical attention he gave to every task. He had been with Orin's group for three weeks. He had been useful for two of them — his Registry training included logistics, route planning, and a working knowledge of the patrol schedules that the enforcement teams used in the Contested Territories. The first week he had been nearly useless: a man walking through the aftermath of a belief system that had collapsed under him, putting one foot in front of the other because the alternative was standing still and Orin did not let people stand still in territory where standing still got you caught.

He came when called. He moved the way he always moved — controlled, measured, the residual muscle memory of institutional training overwriting whatever his body would have done naturally.

"The girl wants to go to the Forge," Orin said.

"I know. I've been watching her compass readings." Darin paused. "Metaphorical compass. She doesn't own a compass."

"She doesn't need one."

"No." Darin set the water-skin down. "The egg's frequency has been intensifying for eleven days. If what I've been reading about Dragon birth cycles is correct — and the Registry's Dragon intelligence files are not comprehensive — the hatching is imminent. Days, not weeks." He hesitated. "I can feel it too. Through the ground. Faint, but it's there. A vibration that doesn't correspond to seismic activity."

Orin looked at him. Darin met the look and held it — he had gotten better at that, in three weeks. The first few days he'd defaulted to the Registry officer's response: eyes down when challenged, deference to the person with authority, the body language of a man accustomed to hierarchy. Orin had no hierarchy. He had people who needed protection and a direction they were moving and the increasingly complicated problem of what to do when the people he was protecting needed to go somewhere he didn't want to take them.

"You think she should go," Orin said.

"I think she's going to go whether we think she should or not. The question is whether she goes alone or with us."

"Not us. Her. You're not part of this."

Darin was quiet for a moment. The kind of quiet that Orin recognized from years of border service — the quiet of someone working through the thing they needed to say before they said it.

"I put a tracking sigil on a girl her age," Darin said. "Six months ago. In a processing facility in the Contested Territories. The sigil was presented as a monitoring tool. Standard registration protocol. I'd placed dozens of them." He paused. "This girl — her name was Caris, she was thirteen, she had Fire affinity that she couldn't control and she was terrified — she screamed when I placed it. Not a pain sound. A different sound. And I heard it. Through my Voice secondary, which I'd been ignoring for years because the Registry's policy on dual-affinity officers was to suppress the secondary and report any manifestations."

He looked at Nim, who was standing at the camp's edge with her face turned southeast.

"The sigil didn't track her. It dampened her. I heard the dampening happen — heard her Fire frequency drop by half in the moment the sigil activated. The Registry wasn't monitoring the Gate-touched. It was muting them."

"You told me this."

"I'm telling you again because I want you to understand what it means when I say: I will not let another institution get its hands on a Fire-touched child without that child's consent. Not the Registry. Not the Forge. Not anyone. If Nim goes to the Forge, I go with her. Not as a handler. Not as an escort. As a witness. Someone who can testify to what she chooses and what she doesn't."

The camp was quiet around them. The other refugees — adults and children who had learned, in weeks of shared travel, to read the emotional weather between Orin and his decisions — were watching without obviously watching.

Orin looked at Darin for a long time.

He did not like Darin. This was not entirely fair — Darin was competent, reliable, and had demonstrated a moral clarity in his defection from the Registry that Orin respected in the abstract. But Orin had spent eleven years watching the Shadowwatch operate, watching the institutions of Arcanea run on the fuel of people who followed orders because the orders came from above, and Darin was, in his bones, a person who followed orders. He had followed better orders now — his own conscience, his own horror at what the Registry was doing — but the instinct was the same. Darin did not act from self-direction. He acted from conviction, and convictions could be redirected.

Then again, so could anyone's. Orin had redirected his own when he let those refugees through his checkpoint. The difference between a convert and a liar was what they did when the conversion was tested.

"The rest of the group continues to the Coast," Orin said. "Mareth takes lead." Mareth was his second — a former teacher, Earth-touched, steady as bedrock and approximately as charismatic. She could get seven people to the Sapphire Coast. She'd done harder things.

"You, me, and Nim to the Forge."

"Three people approaching a military Academy in political lockdown. Two adults, one child. No credentials, no invitation, no institutional standing."

"I have my former Registry identification," Darin said. "Modified, but functional enough to get us through a gate check."

"Modified."

"I was a systems officer. Modifying identification protocols was part of my training."

Orin almost smiled. He didn't, because Nim was watching and he had learned that Nim read adult expressions with the precision of a survival skill, and a smile here could be mistaken for reassurance when what he was offering was something less comfortable than that. He was offering his presence. His attention. His willingness to walk into a place he distrusted because a child he'd accepted responsibility for needed to go there.

"Pack light," he said. "We leave in an hour."

---

They traveled for nine days.

The first three were open territory — the grasslands south of the Contested Territories, where the population thinned and the ley lines ran close to the surface and the ground hummed with a low, steady frequency that Darin said was the Sealing's ambient resonance. Orin couldn't feel it. He felt the grass under his feet and the weight of his pack and the temperature drop as they moved south toward the Ember Wastes, and that was enough. He did not need to feel the magical architecture of the world to understand that the world was changing. He had eyes and ears and eleven years of watching borders, and what his eyes and ears told him was sufficient: the world was orienting toward something, and the something was south.

Nim walked ahead. She always walked ahead. Her pace was steady — faster than a comfortable adult walk, the pace of someone being pulled — and the air around her never stopped shimmering. At night she slept between Orin and Darin, and the fires she started in her sleep were small and getting smaller, as if the closer she got to the egg's source the less her fire needed to escape randomly and the more it could organize itself around the signal it was following.

On the fourth day they reached the Ember Wastes.

The Wastes were the borderland between the civilized provinces and the Forge's territory — a landscape of dark rock and thermal vents and scrubby vegetation that survived by being too stubborn to die. The air tasted of sulfur. The ground was warm enough in places to feel through boot soles. In the distance, Mount Pyralis rose from the wasteland like a fist thrust upward through the earth's crust, trailing a plume of smoke that caught the afternoon light and turned it amber.

Nim stopped when she saw it. She stood at the Wastes' northern edge and looked at the mountain and something happened to her face that Orin had not seen before.

Relief. Pure, uncomplicated, physical relief — the expression of someone who has been holding their breath for so long they forgot they were holding it, and who has just been given permission to exhale.

"That's it," she said. Her voice was different. Not flat. Not guarded. Open, in a way she had not been open since Orin had found her in the holding facility, crouched in a corner with her arms around her knees and the air around her hot enough to blister paint. "That's where it is."

"The Forge," Orin said.

"Not the Forge. The egg. It's up there. At the top." She pressed her hand against her chest. "I can feel it here. Like a heartbeat that isn't mine."

Darin moved to stand beside her. His face had changed too — not relief but attention, the focused quality of someone hearing something they had been straining to hear.

"I can hear it," he said quietly. "Through the ground. Through the — the air. Both. Foundation and Voice at the same time. It's a frequency. It's saying — " He stopped. "It's not saying anything. It's doing something. Building something. Like listening to someone construct a building by feel, in the dark, and you can't see it but you can hear the pieces coming together."

Orin watched them both — the fourteen-year-old with fire in her blood and the twenty-one-year-old with a shattered belief system and a dual affinity he was learning to trust — and he thought about what he was bringing them to.

The Forge. An Academy. An institution built on the philosophy that strength was the answer to every question, that fire was the medium through which the world was shaped, that the measure of a person was what they could endure.

He did not believe this. He had spent his adult life on the borders where strength meant the ability to survive, which was different from the ability to conquer, and the difference mattered more than anyone inside the walls seemed to understand.

But Nim needed what was inside those walls. Not the philosophy. Not the institution. The egg. The thing singing from the caldera's rim that had reached across a continent and found a fourteen-year-old girl in a holding facility and called to her with a voice that spoke through heat and bone and the particular ache of being something the world hadn't decided it wanted yet.

He couldn't give her that. He could give her his presence while she went to get it herself.

"Five more days to the mountain," Orin said. "We stay off the main roads. Registry patrols are thicker near Academy territory — they'll have checkpoints on the approaches."

"I know the checkpoint schedules," Darin said. "They rotate on a five-day cycle. We time our approach for the shift change on day three of the cycle and we come in from the eastern ridge, which has the weakest coverage because the terrain makes it hard to maintain stationary posts."

Orin looked at him.

"I was a systems officer," Darin said. "I designed some of those checkpoint schedules."

"And now you're using them to get around the system you designed."

"Yes."

"How does that feel?"

Darin considered the question with the same methodical attention he gave to everything. "Like finding out the house you built has a door you forgot to lock. And then walking through it."

They crossed into the Ember Wastes. The ground grew warmer with every mile. Nim walked ahead, steady, certain, the heat around her indistinguishable now from the Wastes' ambient temperature. She had stopped shimmering. Or the world had started.

---

On the ninth day they reached the Forge's outer approach.

The mountain road climbed in switchbacks through basalt cliffs that radiated the volcano's deep heat. Security wards pulsed in the stone — Orin couldn't feel them but Darin could, his Foundation sense picking up the resonance patterns embedded in the road's construction. "Tripwires," Darin said. "Pressure-sensitive. They can tell the difference between foot traffic and hooved animals. The Forge knows we're here."

"Good," Orin said. "I'd rather be expected than discovered."

The outer gate materialized from the black stone as they rounded the final switchback — iron and obsidian, twenty feet high, radiating heat from the volcanic channels that ran through the Forge's architecture. Two guards stood at the gate. They wore Forge blacks and carried staffs that glowed at the tips with channeled fire. They did not look welcoming.

"State your business," the lead guard said.

"Refugee passage," Orin said. "Three individuals seeking emergency shelter under the Contested Territories Protection Accord."

The guard's expression did not change. "The Protection Accord guarantees shelter at designated way stations. The Forge is not a way station."

"The Forge is the closest protected structure to our current position. We've been traveling for nine days through the Ember Wastes with a minor. The Accord's emergency provision applies."

He was citing the Accord accurately. He had memorized the relevant sections during the journey because this moment was always going to arrive and he intended to arrive at it prepared.

The guard looked at Darin. "Identification."

Darin produced his modified credentials. The guard examined them with the portable verification device standard to Forge security — a crystal that changed color based on the credentials' legitimacy. The crystal turned amber: valid but flagged. Darin's modification was good but not perfect. The guard's hand tightened on his staff.

"Former Registry," the guard said. "Flagged for status review. You're not currently authorized."

"I am a Gate-touched individual who has left the Registry's employment. The Accord does not distinguish between Gate-touched refugees and former institutional personnel."

This was the moment Orin had been preparing for — the moment where the gate either opened or didn't, where the institution's machinery decided which of its own rules it was going to honor and which it was going to discard.

The gate didn't open.

What happened instead was that someone spoke from the wall above them — a voice Orin didn't recognize, young, male, carrying the particular flatness of someone who had grown up in places where emotion was a liability.

"Let them in."

Orin looked up. A figure on the wall's parapet — lean, dark-haired, wearing Forge training blacks with the sleeves pushed up. Burn scars on the right forearm. Amber-brown eyes that looked down at the three of them with the assessing directness of someone who located exits before he sat down.

The figure's eyes had moved from Orin to Darin to Nim and stopped on Nim.

"She's one of us," the figure said. Not explaining. Not asking. Stating a fact with the certainty of someone who recognized a thing on sight because he had been that thing. "Let them in."

The guard hesitated. The figure on the wall didn't move, didn't raise his voice, didn't produce credentials or invoke authority. He simply waited, with the patient immobility of someone who had learned that walls were not about what they kept out but about what they chose to admit.

The gate opened.

Orin walked through it with Nim on his left and Darin on his right and the full weight of the Draconis Forge — its heat, its ambition, its institutional mass — pressing down on him like the volcanic atmosphere that filled its corridors.

He did not like this place. The stone was wrong — arranged for dominance rather than shelter, built to impress rather than protect. The heat was wrong — maintained at a level that communicated *adapt to us* rather than *we accommodate you*. The guards were wrong — trained for obedience rather than judgment.

But the figure on the wall had looked at Nim and said *she's one of us*, and the gate had opened, and sometimes the way into a system was through the people inside it who remembered what the system was supposed to be for.

Orin followed Nim into the Forge and did not let go of the door he had walked through and did not trust the walls around him and did not stop watching.

He had eleven years of watching. It was the only qualification that mattered here.
