# Chapter Six: The Cold Egg

## THE FORGE — The Joint Academy Council Session

The council had been arguing for four hours when Sable stood up and stopped arguing back.

Not from defeat. From a decision: the argument had reached the point where continued engagement was indistinguishable from complicity. Each rebuttal she offered was being used as scaffolding for the next dismissal. She recognized the structure. She had spent three years in the Crown Gate's archive reading formal proceedings and she knew what it looked like when a council had already made its decision and was using debate as a means of generating the appearance of process.

She sat down. She opened the cipher case. She began writing.

The council chamber was the Forge's largest formal space, a room of volcanic stone and high windows that caught the caldera's ambient heat and turned it into something almost comfortable. Twenty-three chairs arranged around a table of black basalt, each representing an institutional voice: the four Academies, the Starlight Corps, the three regional authority bodies, the Dragon Elder Council's observer — Obsidius, sitting in the far corner with the particular stillness of a twelve-hundred-year-old Dragon who had already decided what the outcome of this meeting would be and was waiting for the mortals to arrive at it.

Eleven people had spoken since the session opened. Sable had been one of them, presenting for forty minutes, laying out the frequency analysis with the thoroughness that years of Crown Gate training had made second nature to her. The Crown Gate was the gate of Enlightenment — the 741 Hz that the Goddess Aiyami governed, the frequency of pattern recognition at depth. You saw through things. Not past them; through them. When Sable read a problem, she was reading its architecture. When she presented her findings, she was building a model in the air above the table and showing people how to see it.

They had seen it. Most of them. The problem was that seeing it and believing what you saw were two different capacities, and for several of the people in this room, belief had been organized into structures that could not accommodate this particular fact.

The fact was this: the cold egg resonated at a frequency that did not exist in the canonical Gate system. Not between gates. Not below Foundation or above Source. Orthogonal to the entire scale — an Eleventh Frequency, occupying the same relationship to the existing ten that a perpendicular line occupied to a plane. Not part of the system. Not outside the system. Operating at an angle to it that made the usual categories irrelevant.

Sable had detected it three days ago using an instrument calibration that was, technically, not approved for use on Dragon artifacts. She had used it because approved instruments were designed to find things within the known parameter set, and she had suspected for a week that what she was looking for was not within that set.

She had been correct.

"This brings us to a vote," said Curator Vellan, the Athenaeum's senior delegate — a grey-haired woman with the eyes of someone who had spent thirty years deciding which knowledge to preserve and which to quarantine, and who had learned to treat the latter with the particular neutrality of a surgeon.

"We do not need a vote," Sable said, without looking up from the cipher case. "We need more time."

"What we need," said Headmaster Ashcroft, from the seat that faced the caldera windows, "is a decision. The Void Ascendant factions have been escalating. Three incidents in the past week. Two at outer perimeter posts. One in the village below the mountain." He paused. "They are not observing. They are testing."

"I know what they're doing," Sable said. "I'm telling you the implications of acting before we understand what we're acting on."

"The implications of inaction—"

"Are also mine to have analyzed, Headmaster. I have." She looked up. "If the cold egg carries the Eleventh Frequency at the resonance level I've measured — if it functions as an Eleventh Gate crystallized — then it exists in structural relationship to the other ten Gates the way a keystone exists to an arch. Not decorative. Load-bearing. Destroy it and you are not removing an anomaly. You are removing a structural member from a system that was designed to include it."

The room was quiet.

"That is an extraordinary claim," said Curator Vellan.

"Yes. I make it on the basis of extraordinary measurement."

"Your instrument was not approved."

"My instrument was not constrained to find only what we already knew existed. That is a different thing."

Ashcroft said: "The Void Ascendants believe the egg is a weapon."

"The Void Ascendants believe many things that are not accurate, and at least one thing that is. The Eleventh Frequency's existence is not in question. Malachar documented it. The original proposal to the Gods included forty years of research. What I've found in the cold egg is consistent with what his notes describe." She closed the cipher case. "The question is not whether the egg is dangerous. The question is whether it is dangerous in the way a weapon is dangerous, or in the way the foundation of a building is dangerous when you try to remove it."

Obsidius, from the far corner, said nothing. His scales had not moved. His attention had not shifted. He was a twelve-hundred-year-old Dragon and his stillness was a specific kind of stillness — the stillness of something that already knew the answer and was watching the mortals find it.

Vellan said: "Then what is your recommendation?"

"More time. Forty-eight hours of additional measurement. Let me establish the full resonance map."

The vote was 7-3.

Destroy it.

---

Sable did not leave the chamber immediately.

She sat with the cipher case open and wrote while the delegations dispersed — while the Luminary's envoy exchanged brief, satisfied words with the Athenaeum's curator, while the Starlight Corps Stellarch left without speaking to anyone, which Sable read as disagreement expressed through withdrawal rather than argument. She wrote the full record: the vote, the speakers, the arguments made, the arguments not made. The Crown Gate trained her to see patterns, and the pattern here was one she wanted documented precisely.

Obsidius remained seated in the far corner while the rest of the room emptied.

When only the two of them were left, he said: "Your frequency map is correct."

"I know," Sable said.

"Your argument was correct."

"I know that too."

The Dragon's stillness remained unchanged. Twelve hundred years of deliberate stillness — not the stillness of a body at rest but the stillness of a mind that had decided to receive rather than project, that had been doing so long enough that it had become the default orientation. "They are afraid," he said. "Fear produces decisions of this quality. Not foolish decisions — fear-decisions can be precise, technically sophisticated, and entirely wrong simultaneously."

"Will the Elder Council formally object?"

A pause. Long enough that she understood the pause was the answer to a different question than the one she had asked — specifically, the question of whether Obsidius was going to be honest with her about the constraints he was operating under.

"The Elder Council's formal objection requires a request from a party with standing," he said. "I cannot request it myself. I can receive the request and act on it immediately." He looked at her with the Dragon attention that was never simply visual — the heat-sense behind the eyes, reading temperature rather than reflected light. "You understand what I'm telling you."

"Someone with standing has to ask."

"Before dawn," he said. "After which the Forge's enforcement order becomes active and the Elder Council's objection becomes a different kind of conversation — one that involves terms like 'irreversible' and 'breach of Compact.'"

"Who has standing?"

He looked at her for a long moment.

"The people the eggs chose," he said. "Sol'vathyn was not granted to the Forge. It was not granted to the council. It was not granted to the institutional structure that has been debating the eggs for the past week." He stood — not the simple movement of a human rising, but the full expression of twelve hundred years of Dragon body achieving vertical, a transition that the human mind registered as a spatial rearrangement of the room's available atmosphere. "Dragon permission is personal. The five of you have it. The five of you have standing."

He walked to the chamber door. Stopped.

"The egg has been listening to every conversation in that hatching chamber since it was placed here," he said, without turning. "I know this because I can feel its attention when I stand near it. I have been able to feel it for three hundred years, since I arrived at this facility as a newly appointed Elder Council observer and walked through the Roost alcoves for the first time and felt the specific quality of something very intelligent and very patient orienting toward me." He paused. "It knows the argument being made in its behalf. It has known every argument made in its behalf and against it for three thousand years." Another pause. "It has been patient. Do not ask it to be patient past dawn."

He left.

Sable closed the cipher case and sat for a moment in the empty council chamber, in the quality of the room that remained after a decision had been made — not silence, not peace, but the particular atmospheric weight of a conclusion that has not yet resolved into consequence. She sat with it. The Crown Gate trained for this as well: not just the recognition of patterns but the willingness to remain with what you recognized after everyone else had left.

## KAEL

He watched Sable walk out of the council chamber into the corridor, and he watched the particular quality of her composure — she had the Crown Gate's training, which meant she had the deep architecture of someone who had learned to hold pattern-recognition in the body rather than letting it live only in the mind, and the pattern she had recognized was the one he was recognizing now through Foundation — and he understood, because he had been pressing his awareness into the basalt floor throughout the entire session, that the decision that had just been made had not been made from analysis.

It had been made from fear.

The Foundation substrate was a precise instrument. Kael had spent four years learning to read it with the granular attention that the Goddess Lyssandria's 174 Hz trained — not just geological composition but structural truth, the load-bearing reality beneath surfaces. He could feel the weight of objects. He could feel the weight of intentions. What he had been feeling throughout the session was the particular density of a room full of people who had organized their institutional identities around certainties that the cold egg was threatening, and who had resolved the threat by voting to eliminate it.

That was fear's math. Sable's argument had been correct. They had understood it was correct. They had voted 7-3 to ignore it.

"The vote will be implemented at dawn," said Mira, who appeared from the side corridor where she had been standing outside the chamber door throughout the proceedings. Her eyes were tired. She had been holding the emotional field for the past four hours — not influencing it, not pushing, just holding it the way you held a complicated structure while it was being assembled, keeping the weight of everyone's feelings from collapsing into the kind of reactive decision that ended badly. She had not succeeded. "They didn't even ask for a second opinion from the Dragon Council."

"Obsidius didn't offer one," Kael said.

"Obsidius knows better than to offer things to rooms that have already decided." She looked toward the chamber door. "He was there because the Elder Council has an observer right. He observed." A pause. "He didn't agree."

"Do you know that or feel it?"

"Both," she said. "When Vellan called the vote, his scales did something. Very small. The Heart Gate reads it as—" She considered. "As the specific quality of a person who is watching a mistake being made for the eighth time and has stopped arguing with the mistake and started calculating its consequences."

Kael put his hand against the corridor wall.

The Foundation substrate ran through the entire mountain — through the council chamber's floor, through the outer corridors, through the caldera's support structure, down through Mount Pyralis's volcanic core to the layer of Luminor substrate beneath. He could feel the egg from here. Not its frequency — that was Sable's domain — but its architecture. The physical structure of the thing, radiating up through the stone the way a tuning fork's vibration traveled through a table it was resting on.

He had felt it since the first night at the Forge.

He had not yet told anyone exactly what he felt.

---

The hatching chamber sat in the mountain's eastern flank, an excavated natural cavity that the Forge had expanded and formalized over centuries until it was something between a geological formation and a deliberately designed space. The Roost alcoves lined the upper walls. The floor was volcanic stone, natural, worn smooth by decades of Dragon contact. The ambient heat was maintained by the mountain itself — the caldera's warmth bleeding through the rock at a temperature that kept eggs viable without artificial intervention.

The cold egg was in the third alcove from the left.

Not cold because of temperature. The chamber's heat reached it the same way it reached everything else. Cold because of what its presence did to the ambient frequency — the way a window open onto winter cooled a room not by introducing cold but by creating a space through which warmth escaped. Near the cold egg, the Foundation substrate's standard hum dropped below Kael's ability to hear it. Not because it stopped. Because something was drawing its attention elsewhere.

He had come here at the second bell past midnight.

Not because he had a plan. Because he had been lying awake since the vote and the Foundation substrate had been pulling at him with the specific quality of unfinished structural business — the sensation of a load-bearing question that had not been asked yet, and that needed to be asked from close proximity.

He crossed the chamber floor in the dark. Not full dark — the mountain's ambient heat had an infrared quality that Foundation-sense could navigate, the thermal texture of stone different from air different from alcove walls. He knew where his feet were. He knew where the egg was.

He sat down on the floor in front of the third alcove and put both hands flat on the volcanic stone.

The Foundation substrate moved through him the way it always did: a full-body resonance at 174 Hz, the Goddess Lyssandria's frequency, the frequency of survival and structure and the deep bedrock truth of things. He had opened this Gate at seventeen, in circumstances he did not revisit unnecessarily. He had been working with it for six years. He thought he understood its grammar.

He did not understand what happened next.

The substrate shifted under his hands. Not physically — the stone did not move. But the pattern of it changed, the way a room changed when someone else entered it. A second awareness came into contact with his — not a human awareness, not a Dragon awareness in any form he had encountered — something structural, something that thought in architecture the way he thought in architecture, that understood load and support and the distribution of force the way he had been trained to understand it.

The egg was listening.

Not had started listening. Not woke up to listen. Had been listening. The specific quality of the attention he was feeling was the attention of something that had been present, collecting, organizing — for as long as it had been in this chamber. Which was — he worked through the substrate's timeline, the geological record pressed into the stone — three thousand years.

His hands went very still.

The egg showed him.

Not showed him — there were no images, no words, no narrative. What passed from the egg's frequency into his Foundation awareness was structural information. Architecture. The most complete structural rendering he had ever received, compressed into a sensory impression that lasted perhaps four seconds and that he would spend weeks working out the full implications of.

He saw the Gate system.

From the inside.

---

Mortals and scholars studied the Gates from the outside: the experience of opening them, the effects they produced, the frequencies they emitted. Sable's Crown Gate gave her the ability to see through the system's structure from above — the pattern-recognition of enlightenment, the bird's-eye view. Kael's Foundation Gate gave him the ability to read structural truth from below — the ground-level awareness of what things were built on.

Neither of these was what the cold egg showed him.

The egg had been built by Malachar — not laid, built, the Codex's distinction between Dragon genesis and engineered creation now clarified into something Kael could feel in his bones — built to carry the Eleventh Frequency, which existed in relationship to the other ten the way a perpendicular axis existed to a plane. And from that perpendicular position, it had a view of the Gate system that no Gate-Touched, no Dragon, no scholar had ever had access to.

The Gates were not what their official framework described them as.

The official framework: ten progressive frequencies, each opening the next, each level of attainment unlocking access to higher resonance. A ladder. An hierarchy of development, with Source at the top and Foundation at the bottom and the implication, in the language of every Academy handbook Kael had ever read, that Foundation was the first rung and Source the last and progress moved in one direction.

What the cold egg showed him was not a ladder.

It was a structure.

Load-bearing walls.

The Gates were not progressive levels. They were interdependent structural members, each one holding the others in place the way the walls of a building held the ceiling, the way the foundation held the walls. Foundation was not lower than Source. Foundation was what Source stood on. Source was not higher than Foundation. Source was what gave Foundation its purpose. Remove any one Gate and the system did not lose one tenth of its function.

It collapsed.

The cold egg was not the Eleventh Gate. It was the keystone. The structural member that closed the arch, that distributed the load that all ten Gates were collectively bearing, that kept the whole system from spreading outward under its own pressure. Malachar had not built the eggs as weapons or as demonstrations or even as proof of the Eleventh Frequency's existence.

He had built them as the element the system required to hold itself together.

And the council had just voted to destroy one.

Kael sat on the chamber floor with his hands pressed to the stone and understood that he was the only person who could make this argument — not because he was smarter than Sable, not because Foundation gave him access to knowledge that Crown did not, but because he was the person who had been sitting next to the substrate for three weeks, the person who had been feeling the cold baseline beneath the mountain, the person the egg had been waiting for, in its patient, architectural way, since before any of them were born.

The weight of this did not feel like responsibility. It felt like load.

He knew how to work with load.

He took his hands off the stone, sat back, and began composing his argument.

## SABLE

She came to the hatching chamber at the third bell because she could not sleep and because the cipher case was full of analyses that the council had refused to receive and because Crown Gate enlightenment, at its worst, was the experience of seeing clearly what could not be changed by seeing clearly.

She had been sitting with the cold egg for two hours, working through the full frequency map with the concentration of someone who had decided that understanding the thing was more valuable than acting on it, when she heard Kael's footsteps.

Not unusual. She had assumed one of them would end up here. They were all spending too much time near the eggs — the pull of the three hatchlings, the warm one and the red-gold one and the one that hadn't bonded yet, was becoming part of the mountain's ambient texture. Being near them was easier than not being near them. This was not in any Dragon Codex she had read and she intended to research it properly when the current crisis permitted.

The cold egg did not pull the same way.

The cold egg did something different. It was not warm — it sat in the alcove with the quality of deep stone, impervious, old, self-contained. But near it, the Crown Gate's pattern-recognition operated differently. She could see further. Not physically — her physical vision was the same. But her ability to perceive relational structure, the architecture of how things connected to each other, became sharper, more granular, more complete. Near the cold egg, she could see the shape of what she didn't know.

That was the feeling she had been sitting with for two hours. Not insight. The precise outline of the absence of insight. All the places where her model of the Gate system had gaps it had never needed to acknowledge before.

Kael sat down across the alcove from her and put his hands on the floor and was quiet for a long time.

She waited. She was good at waiting. The Crown Gate trained for it.

"I know what it is," he said finally.

She looked at him.

"I mean I felt it. What it is structurally. What it does." He paused. "What happens if they destroy it."

She closed the cipher case. "Tell me."

He told her.

She listened with the full attention of someone whose Crown Gate was already two steps ahead of the narrative, already recognizing the implications before he finished describing them — the pattern recognition doing what it did, filling in the architecture of the argument from the first structural members he laid down. By the time he finished she had been sitting with the complete picture for ninety seconds and was doing the thing that required doing next: figuring out who in that council chamber was persuadable, and what language would reach them.

"The problem," she said, "is the measurement."

"What do you mean?"

"What you felt is structural truth. I believe it. Completely." She looked at the alcove. "The council doesn't feel structural truth. They evaluate arguments, and arguments require evidence that can be formally presented and peer-reviewed. Your impression from the Foundation substrate—"

"It's not an impression."

"To them it is. To them the Foundation sense is a capability that produces interpretations, not facts." She turned to look at him. "What I have is measurements. Instrument readings, resonance maps, frequency analyses. That's the language they accept." She paused. "But you have something I don't."

"What?"

"Mira."

## MIRA

She came when Sable sent for her and she sat at the alcove's edge and she put her hands not on the floor but on her own knees and she breathed and she opened the Flow Gate — 285 Hz, the Goddess Leyla's frequency, the frequency of emotion and creativity and the deep-current truth of feeling — and she read the cold egg.

Not the way Kael read it. Not structurally. The way she read everything: as a field of feeling, a composite of what a thing felt, had felt, was in the process of feeling. The Flow Gate read the emotional truth of things. Stones had emotional truth. Mountains had it. Three-thousand-year-old artifacts engineered by a scholar who had been building toward a proposal that the Gods would reject — absolutely had it.

"It's not afraid," she said. "That's the first thing. Most things in a situation like this — contained, with a destruction order pending, not yet hatched — most things in that situation would be producing fear. Fight-or-flight frequency. This isn't." She breathed. "It feels like—" She went quiet for a moment. "Like a proof that has not yet been presented. Like something that knows it's correct and has been waiting for the right person to read it."

"That's what I got," Kael said. "But structurally."

"The emotional and structural truth are the same thing." She opened her eyes. "It's been holding that frequency — the one Sable measured — since it arrived here. It's not waiting to hatch. It's not waiting to bond with anyone. It's waiting to be understood." She paused. "That's not a metaphor. That's the actual thing I'm reading. It wants to be read the way a document wants to be read. It believes the information it carries is necessary."

Sable was already writing.

"Good," she said. "That's the argument. Kael gives the structural truth: destroying it destabilizes the Gate architecture. I give the frequency measurements: removing a keystone frequency from a closed resonance system causes cascading failure. And you give the emotional truth: this is not a weapon, not a threat, and not a risk that containment serves — it is a specific kind of knowledge that has been waiting three thousand years for someone capable of receiving it."

Kael looked at her. "They voted 7-3."

"Yes. And Obsidius was in that room and said nothing."

"That's—"

"Significant," Sable said. "Obsidius doesn't observe silently when the outcome is acceptable. He observes silently when the outcome is inevitable — when he has decided that the process needs to run, and that the correction will come from inside rather than outside." She looked at them both. "He was watching us. I thought he was watching the council. He was watching us."

She told them what the Dragon had said after the chamber emptied.

Kael listened with his hand still not quite touching the stone. Mira listened with her eyes closing briefly halfway through — not sleep, the beginning of the Water Gate's read on the emotional weight of the information arriving. When Sable finished, the three of them sat with it for a moment — the specific moment of people who have received new information and are integrating it into the architecture of what they already knew.

"Before dawn," Kael said.

"Before dawn," Sable confirmed.

"The request to the Elder Council has to come from all five of us," Mira said. "Not three. All five."

"Ash and Elio—"

"Are already awake," Mira said. The Flow Gate's read on the building's emotional field had been producing data for the past hour. "Ash has been awake since the vote. Elio has been up since the second bell. Kyr woke him." She opened her eyes. "The Dragon knows. Whatever the eggs carry, they feel what the building feels. Kyr felt the vote and she woke Elio and he's been sitting with her in the upper dormitory trying to figure out why his heart is going too fast." She looked at Sable. "He doesn't know why. He doesn't need to. He'll come when we ask."

The cold egg sat in the alcove.

Patient.

Three thousand years of patience. The same patience Mira had felt in its emotional field — not the patience of resignation, not the patience of endurance. The patience of a correct proof that has not been read yet, holding itself in a state of readiness, waiting for the specific moment when the right person with the right preparation is standing in front of it with enough time and enough silence to finally hear.

"We go back to the council at first light," Sable said. "All five of us. With everything. Not to change the vote."

"Then why?" Kael said.

"Because Obsidius said the Elder Council's objection becomes a different conversation after the enforcement order activates. The conversation we want is the one before. Which means we need to give Obsidius the request — formally, on record — before dawn." She stood, tucking the cipher case under her arm. "The council's vote is institutional. The Dragon Compact is older than the council, older than the Forge, older than the current Academy structure. If we make the formal request and Obsidius acts on it before the enforcement team moves, the cold egg is protected by law that none of them have the authority to override."

"Malachar filed his research in a Forge archive three thousand years ago," Kael said. "Because he understood that someone would eventually read it."

"Yes," Sable said. "He was working with the systems available to him. So are we."

## THE HATCHING CHAMBER — THE FOURTH BELL

Ash's dragon — Vaelith-Nyx, copper-black and Void-threaded, three weeks old and less sleeping than its general appearance suggested — was on the Roost's upper tier when it went rigid.

Not with fear. With attention. The quality of attention a very young consciousness gave to something that was interrupting its entire sensory field simultaneously.

The cold egg pulsed.

Not the single pulse Kael had felt at the end of the council's first gathering — not the brief, measured beat of something marking time. This was a sustained shift, a frequency change that moved through the Foundation substrate of the entire mountain and that the three people currently in the hatching chamber felt in their bones before they understood what they were feeling.

The cold egg was awake.

Not in the way the other eggs had awakened — not the building internal temperature of a consciousness preparing to hatch, not the crystalline crack-sequence of a shell preparing to shatter. Different. More like a building turning on its lights than a living thing waking up. Purposeful. Deliberate. Something had been activated that had been in a state of waiting, and the state of waiting was now over.

The chamber door hit the wall.

Four grey-uniformed figures. Void Ascendant extraction team — not the observers from the council, not the diplomats who had been maintaining the faction's presence in the formal proceedings. These were different. Movement efficiency and the silence of professional entry. One carried a containment device — an artifact cylinder that Sable recognized from the classified catalog as Void-dampening resonance technology, adapted from original Third Age research.

Original Third Age research.

Malachar's research.

They were going to take the egg using instruments built from the notes of the man who designed it.

Vaelith-Nyx came off the upper tier.

The hatchling was three weeks old and the size of a large wolfhound and it had not yet produced fire. This was unusual — Fire-blooded hatchlings typically manifested breath in the first week. Obsidius had noted this without comment. Seraphine had noted it with the expression she wore when something was developing according to a schedule she had not set and did not intend to rush.

The hatchling's scales went copper, then black, then the Void-threads surfaced — not the usual cycling, not the periodic shift that Ash had learned to read as emotional temperature. All at once. Black all the way through, the scales losing their copper base entirely, and in the darkness of them the Void-threads going bright — not gold, not amber, the specific cold-light white of the Eleventh Frequency.

Vaelith-Nyx opened its mouth.

Fire came out.

Not the standard expression — not the involuntary first-breath, not even the controlled projection the Codex described for early manifestation. This fire was Void-threaded: white at the core, black at the edges, the specific inversion of standard draconic fire that the Dragon Codex mentioned in exactly one paragraph near the back of the breeds section with the annotation *theoretical, unconfirmed, referenced in pre-Third Age texts as Null-fire.*

It hit the containment device.

The device shattered.

The cylinder did not break the way physical objects broke — it did not crack, did not fall in pieces. The frequency structure inside it, the Void-dampening resonance technology, came apart at the wave level, the contained harmonics releasing simultaneously as a sound that was below hearing but above sensation, a pressure that the three scholars felt in their chests at the same moment that Sable's instrument readings spiked beyond their calibration limits.

The Void Ascendant team stopped.

The hatchling stood in front of the cold egg's alcove.

Still. Very still. The Null-fire cooling in the air in front of it, leaving no burn mark on the stone, no ash, no residue — only the brief temperature signature of fire that had passed through the Void and returned as something that was not quite fire anymore.

Fire and Void in a single living consciousness.

Malachar's proof.

The Void Ascendants had come to take what their patron had built. What they had found instead was what his patron had built in defense of what he had made.

From upstairs, through the Forge's stone corridors, came the sound of running feet.

Kael's hand found the floor.

The Foundation substrate moved.

The ground rose.
