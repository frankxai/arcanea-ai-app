# Chapter Three: The Current Changes

The provisional Keeper credential was a piece of treated sharkskin the size of a playing card, stamped with the Athenaeum's deep-water seal and Thessaly Mar's personal authorization glyph. It granted access to the restricted archives — not the Luminary's politely locked Restricted Provenance Collection, but the Athenaeum's actively guarded deep stacks, where the water pressure kept casual visitors away and the current-wards kept everyone else.

Sable had earned it with the Remediation files. Not as a reward — the Athenaeum did not reward the discovery of institutional crimes. As a containment measure. Thessaly had looked at Sable's documentation, had read the seven case files with the careful blankness of someone processing something they had long suspected and never confirmed, and had said: "You have found something that the Council would prefer to remain unfound. The choice is to restrict your access further, which would invite exactly the kind of determined investigation that your surname suggests you're capable of, or to bring you inside the wall and trust that the view from here will give you perspective you currently lack."

"And if the view doesn't change my mind?"

"Then we'll have that conversation when it arrives."

Thessaly was not a warm woman. She was fair. The distinction mattered more than most people realized.

Sable had been inside the wall for six weeks. In that time she had read more than she had read in three years at the Luminary, which was a significant quantity. The deep stacks held things the standard archives did not: unredacted prophecy translations, pre-Fall scholarship, the unedited correspondence between the Academy founders, and — sealed in a chamber that required two Keeper credentials and a current-ward password that changed with the tides — the original Atlantian prophecy texts.

She was reading the prophecy texts when the water-message arrived.

---

The technique was hers. She had invented it during Book 2's final weeks — a method of encoding information into water's molecular structure using Void compression, such that the message would dissolve on contact with any surface except the intended recipient's skin. It required the sender to know the recipient's exact physical signature — their particular frequency, the way their Gate power inflected the world around them. For Foundation, this meant the recipient's specific relationship with gravity, the way their weight met the ground. Sable had catalogued Kael's Foundation signature during their work together at the Luminary. She knew how he stood.

The message reformed on her left forearm — Void-compressed text emerging from water droplets like ink rising through paper, visible only to her, dissolving after a single reading. It was the most secure communication method currently in use in Arcanea, and it was also, she acknowledged privately, unnecessary for most of what she and Kael discussed. But precision was its own satisfaction, and the technique's elegance pleased her in a way she did not examine closely.

Kael's message:

*S — Something is happening in the Forge's Dragon Roosts. An egg. Singing. Not sound — frequency. My Foundation reads it as structural composition at a sub-molecular level. A Void/Fire student here reads the same event through different channels. Between us we're perceiving two aspects of a single phenomenon. The egg is building a chord. It took my Foundation frequency and incorporated it. Whatever is inside that egg is assembling itself from Gate harmonics. I don't know what it's assembling into. The Forge doesn't know either, or they do and they're not sharing. Come if you can. Bring what you know about Dragons as frequencies. — K*

Sable read the message. It dissolved. She sat in the prophecy chamber's ambient blue light — bioluminescent coral casting the kind of illumination that made everything look like it existed underwater, which it did — and she thought.

Dragons as frequencies.

She had been reading the prophecy's Second Stanza for three weeks. *Each fracture holds a thread the others lack. / The first shall ground what cannot land. / The second shall feel what will not be felt. / The third shall burn what refuses to change.* The standard interpretation assigned each line to a member of the Five: Foundation, Flow, Fire, Void, Heart. Neat. Orderly. The kind of reading that satisfied because it was simple.

Sable distrusted simple readings. Simple readings were what happened when the reader decided in advance what the text should say and then found evidence to support the decision. She had been working on the Second Stanza from the other direction: what did the text say before she decided what it meant?

*The third shall burn what refuses to change.*

Fire. Obviously. Ash's line, in the standard reading. But the prophecy was written in Old Atlantian, and Old Atlantian did not have a single word for *burn*. It had seven. The word used in this stanza was *kael-thir*, which meant specifically: *to apply heat until the internal structure transforms*. Not destruction. Refinement. The word a smith would use for tempering, not the word a soldier would use for razing.

*Kael-thir.* To change the internal structure through applied fire.

She had been thinking about this in the context of Gate-touched individuals — mortals whose internal structure was already undergoing transformation. But Kael's message shifted the frame. What if the stanza wasn't about a person at all?

What if it was about a Dragon?

---

She worked for four hours without stopping. The deep stacks held three texts that referenced Dragons in terms of frequency rather than biology — two of them academic treatises from the Second Age, one of them a fragment of something much older that the Athenaeum's cataloguers had labeled *Origin Unknown, Pre-Fourth Age, Language: Unclassified*.

The unclassified fragment was eleven words long. Sable read it in the original — the glyphs were not Old Atlantian but something earlier, carved into a piece of volcanic obsidian that the Athenaeum had mounted behind glass. The translation offered by the cataloguers was tentative: *When fire remembers itself across all frequencies, the Gate walks.*

The Gate walks. Not *opens*. Walks.

She turned to the Second Age treatises. The first, by a scholar named Avessian, argued that Dragons were not creatures but "living harmonic structures — fire organized into consciousness by a pattern that precedes the Gate system and operates independently of it." Avessian had been writing a thousand years before the Malachar Wars. His work was theoretical, dismissed by his contemporaries as speculative, preserved in the Athenaeum because the Athenaeum preserved everything.

The second treatise referenced a term Sable had never encountered: *Luminarch*.

The treatise defined it carefully, with the kind of hedging that indicated the author knew the concept was dangerous: *A Luminarch is a theoretical Dragon-state in which fire achieves simultaneous resonance with all ten Gate frequencies. Such a being would function not as a channeler of Gate power but as a living expression of the Gate system itself — a mobile, conscious structure through which all ten frequencies could be accessed without the mediation of the divine Gatekeepers. The Luminarch theory predicts that such a being would emerge naturally when the conditions are met: specifically, when all ten frequencies are present in a single location at sufficient intensity for fire to organize itself around them.*

The conditions are met. Sable read that phrase three times.

All ten frequencies, present in a single location. She thought about the Sealing — the harmonic prison woven from all ten Gate frequencies. She thought about the fracture lines — the cracks where those frequencies leaked through. She thought about Seraphine's egg, sitting in the Dragon Roosts of the Draconis Forge, which was built on the caldera of Mount Pyralis, which was built on the ruins of Malachar's first fortress, which sat on a convergence of ley lines that connected directly to the Sealing.

An egg that had been unkindled for two hundred and fifty years. Listening. Absorbing. Building a chord from the frequencies leaking through the cracks in Malachar's prison.

She put down the treatise. She picked up her pen.

She wrote to Kael:

*K — The prophecy mentions Dragons. Not as creatures. As a frequency. What if Dragon fire is the missing element that makes Luminarch possible? The word appears in a Second Age treatise as a theoretical Dragon-state: all ten Gate frequencies organized into a single consciousness. The conditions for Luminarch emergence require all ten frequencies present at sufficient intensity. The Sealing leaks all ten through its fractures. The egg has been absorbing them for 250 years. I think you're feeling what happens when a Dragon builds itself out of the Sealing's overflow.*

She paused. The next line required committing to something she had been circling for weeks.

*Also: the prophecy's third stanza. "The third shall burn what refuses to change." The standard reading assigns this to Ash. The Old Atlantian word is kael-thir — structural transformation through applied fire. What if it describes not a person but a process? What if the third fracture is not one of us? What if it's a Dragon?*

She sealed the message in water and sent it through the current-tube network to the Forge. The Athenaeum's tubes connected to the continental system — the message would travel through underground water channels, reform only on Kael's skin, and dissolve after one reading. Secure. Precise.

Insufficient, probably. But it was what she had.

---

Scholar Ondine found her in the deep stacks at the eleventh bell. This was not unusual — Ondine had a habit of appearing when Sable was working on something that the Athenaeum's institutional comfort required her not to find.

Ondine was seventy-three years old and looked fifty, the way long-term exposure to the Athenaeum's pressurized environment preserved its residents. Her hair was white and worn short. Her eyes were the particular dark of someone who had spent decades reading things that changed what the world looked like and had not found a way to un-read them.

"You've been accessing the pre-Fourth Age collection," Ondine said, sitting without invitation in the chair opposite Sable's. "Torva notes these things. She's noted you specifically."

"The provisional credential gives me access."

"It gives you access to the restricted archives. The pre-Fourth Age collection is restricted-within-restricted. The fact that the shelving isn't physically separated doesn't mean the authorization is the same." Ondine held up a hand before Sable could respond. "I'm not here to revoke your access. I'm here to tell you what you've found, because you're going to find it anyway and I'd rather you have context than come to conclusions without it."

Sable closed her notebook. She recognized this — the particular quality of an intelligence that was tired of watching someone approach a dangerous truth at the speed of scholarship when the truth could be delivered faster with a conversation.

"Luminarch," Sable said.

Ondine's expression did not change. "Yes."

"The egg in the Forge is building itself into a Luminarch. It's been absorbing the Sealing's leaked frequencies for two and a half centuries. A contact at the Forge confirms it — the egg is composing a harmonic structure that incorporates all ten Gate frequencies."

"Not all ten. Not yet." Ondine's voice was careful. "Seven, at last count. It's missing three. It added Foundation recently — within the last few days, I'd guess, based on the timing of your correspondence."

Sable went still. "You're monitoring my messages."

"I'm monitoring the frequency fluctuations in the continental water-current network, which is my job as a senior scholar of the Deep Translation department. Your Void-compressed messages create a measurable displacement in the current's baseline frequency. I can't read them. I can count them and track their destinations." Ondine's expression softened by a degree. "I am not your enemy, Sable. I am someone who has spent thirty years studying the same texts you are now reading, and who arrived at the same conclusions, and who has been waiting for someone else to arrive at them because the Athenaeum's Council does not listen to old women who say alarming things about Dragon theory."

"They listen to young women who say alarming things about Remediation files?"

"They listened to you because you brought evidence and because your surname carries weight that mine doesn't. Use both. But use them knowing what you're walking into."

Ondine leaned forward. The bioluminescent coral dimmed slightly — a response to the emotional pressure in the room, the way the Athenaeum's living architecture responded to the currents of feeling that moved through its residents.

"The Athenaeum has not sent observers to the Forge's hatching," Ondine said. "You've noticed this."

"I've noticed."

"The Council's position is that Dragon births are Draconic Academy matters and do not require Athenaeum involvement. This is the official reason. It is also a lie. The actual reason is that Thessaly Mar read the same Second Age treatise you just read, seven years ago, and she understood what it meant, and she has been quietly ensuring that the Athenaeum's institutional knowledge of Luminarch theory remains inaccessible to the other Academies." Ondine paused. "Not to hide it. To protect it. Because the last person who developed a complete understanding of Luminarch theory was Malachar Lumenbright, and his understanding led directly to his attempt to force-create a Luminarch by channeling all ten Gate frequencies through himself, and that attempt led directly to the Hungry Void, and the Hungry Void led to everything else."

The deep stacks were silent. The water pressure at this depth was immense — enough to compress sound, to make the silence feel heavy, physical, a weight you wore on your chest.

"Thessaly believes that knowing about the Luminarch creates the temptation to build one," Sable said.

"Thessaly believes that Malachar's theory was correct. The mathematics work. The frequency model is sound. A Luminarch can be created. The problem is that Malachar tried to create one from the outside — assembling the frequencies by force, controlling the process, directing the outcome. And the frequencies cannot be forced. They have to choose to combine. The fire has to decide to remember itself across all ten registers. And fire that is forced to remember does not remember. It hallucinates. It produces something that looks like a Luminarch and is actually a weapon."

Ondine stood. She placed a hand briefly on the table — a gesture that might have been steadying, might have been emphasis.

"The egg is not being forced," she said. "The egg has been listening to the Sealing's music for two hundred and fifty years and composing its own response. That is different. That may be the difference between what Malachar tried and what is actually supposed to happen. But the Athenaeum cannot say this publicly because saying it requires admitting that Malachar's theory was right, and admitting that Malachar was right about anything is the one thing the Academy system has spent three thousand years refusing to do."

She walked toward the stacks' exit, then stopped.

"Come with me," she said. "There's something you should see. Below this level."

"There's nothing below this level. This is the deep stacks' lowest floor."

Ondine looked at her. The expression on her face was complicated — amusement and sadness and something that might have been the relief of someone who had been carrying a thing alone for too long and was about to set half the weight on someone else's shoulders.

"That," Ondine said, "is what the Athenaeum's architecture wants you to believe."

---

They descended.

Ondine used a current-ward Sable had never seen — a sequence of pressure modulations that opened a passage in the deep stacks' floor, a vertical shaft of water held in shape by wards old enough that the coral framing them had fossilized around the ward-anchors. They dropped through the shaft — current-shaping for propulsion, the water warm at this depth, warm enough that Sable's Void sense registered it as unusual — and emerged in a chamber that should not have existed.

The Athenaeum was built on the sunken city of Thal'Maris. Sable knew this. Below the Athenaeum's lowest level lay four hundred fathoms of cold, dark, pressurized ocean and then the ruins of the original Atlantian civilization.

This chamber was not four hundred fathoms down. It was perhaps fifty feet below the deep stacks. And it was dry — an air pocket maintained by wards so old that they hummed at a frequency Sable's Void sense classified as *pre-linguistic*. Before the current system of magical grammar. Before the Gates, possibly. Old enough that the walls were not carved coral but natural stone — volcanic basalt, the same material that made up the Forge's architecture.

Volcanic stone in an underwater library. The wrong material in the wrong place, which meant it had been here first. The Athenaeum had been built on top of it.

The walls were carved. Glyphs, dense and precise, covering every surface. And in the center of the far wall, larger than the rest, illuminated by bioluminescent organisms that had colonized the stone and were glowing with the particular quality of life that had adapted to something extraordinary:

A mural.

Five figures. Standing in a circle. In the center of the circle: a Dragon. Around the Dragon: ten concentric rings, each inscribed with a frequency notation that Sable recognized from the Gate harmonic tables.

The Dragon in the mural was dark. Its scales were rendered in a technique that used the absence of paint rather than its presence — negative space shaped into a form, so that the Dragon appeared to be made of the stone showing through. Its eyes were two points of light: one warm, one cold.

Above the mural, in script that Sable's Deep Translation training identified as *hand-carved, individual, not institutional* — someone had written this with their own hands, not a committee's — a single line:

*"They will come after me. They will be better. Let them be better."*

She knew the handwriting.

She had seen it in the Remediation files — the marginal notes, the cross-references, the particular way the author formed their consonants with a leftward lean that the graphological analysis in the files had attributed to *habitual urgency, consistent with a writer who thought faster than they could inscribe*.

Malachar's handwriting.

Sable stood in a chamber beneath the Athenaeum and looked at a mural painted by the Dark Lord of Arcanea and felt, for the first time in her carefully managed life, something she could not file or categorize or hold at analytical distance.

It was not fear. It was not the thrill of discovery or the satisfaction of confirmed theory.

It was grief.

Grief for a man she had never met, who had stood in this room — before the Athenaeum, before the Fall, before the Hungry Void consumed his trust and his curiosity and left behind the thing the world called the Dark Lord — and painted a picture of hope. Five figures. A Dragon. The theory that would destroy him, rendered not as a schematic but as a wish.

*They will come after me. They will be better.*

He had known. Standing in this room, painting this wall, Malachar Lumenbright had known that he was going to fail. And he had left this behind — not a weapon, not a trap, not a plan for resurrection or revenge. A letter. To the people who would come after him. To the five fractures that the Sealing he hadn't yet created would one day produce.

To her.

Sable pressed her hand against the volcanic stone. It was warm. Not the ambient warmth of the deep ocean or the Athenaeum's heating systems. A different warmth. The kind that remained in stone long after the fire that heated it had gone.

"How long have you known about this?" she asked Ondine.

"Seven years. Since Thessaly brought me here."

"And Thessaly?"

"Since her predecessor brought her here. And that predecessor's predecessor, back through a chain of Keepers that stretches to the Athenaeum's founding. This room predates the institution. It predates the city. Whatever Malachar was doing here, he was doing it before Thal'Maris existed."

Sable looked at the mural. The five figures. The Dragon. The ten rings.

"He was right," she said. "About the Luminarch. About the frequencies. About the possibility of a Dragon born from all ten Gates."

"Yes."

"And being right destroyed him."

"Being right and trying to force the outcome destroyed him. The distinction—"

"I understand the distinction." Sable's voice was sharper than she intended. She modulated it. "The distinction is the most important thing in this room. More important than the mural. More important than the theory." She turned to Ondine. "He tried to build it. The egg is building itself. He forced the frequencies. The egg is listening for them. He worked alone. The egg is reaching out — taking Foundation from Kael, something else from the Void/Fire student at the Forge. Collecting. Composing."

"Yes."

"The difference between Malachar and the egg is consent. The egg asks. He demanded."

Ondine said nothing. She watched Sable the way Thessaly watched Sable: with the particular attention of someone evaluating whether the person in front of them could carry what they were being given.

Sable turned back to the mural. The five figures. The Dragon made of negative space. The ten rings, incomplete — the tenth ring, Source, was drawn but not filled.

*Let them be better.*

"I need to go to the Forge," Sable said.

"I know."

"Not as an observer. Not as a diplomat. As myself."

"I know that too." Ondine moved toward the chamber's exit shaft. "Thessaly will arrange it. Academic exchange — the Athenaeum's provisional response to the Forge's hatching event. Delayed, which the Council will call measured and which you will correctly identify as strategic." She paused at the shaft's entrance. "Sable. The man who painted that mural hoped for exactly what is happening. He also fell into the darkest place in existence trying to make it happen faster. Be careful with hope that burns."

Sable looked at the mural one more time. The Dragon's two-toned eyes — one warm, one cold — stared back at her from the negative space of the stone.

"I'm always careful," she said.

It was true. It was also, she suspected, going to be insufficient.

She followed Ondine up through the water shaft, through the deep stacks, through the Athenaeum's familiar architecture of coral and glass and bioluminescent light. She walked to her quarters. She sat at her desk. She wrote to Kael:

*K — I'm coming to the Forge. The Athenaeum knows what the egg is. They've known for years. Also: Malachar was there first. Before the Athenaeum. Before everything. He left a message. I'll tell you when I arrive. — S*

*Also: I need to speak with the Void/Fire student. Her frequency perception may be the key to understanding what the egg still needs.*

She sealed the message and sent it. She began packing. She packed the way she did everything: precisely, economically, with the particular care of someone who had learned that the weight you carry shapes the distance you can travel.

She packed light.

The distance she needed to travel was considerable.
