# Chapter One: Embera

## ASH

Six months of silence had not made her quieter.

If anything — and she had the bruises to prove it — the opposite. The Forge's advanced training didn't teach patience. It taught precision, which was different. Precision was patience with something at stake. Precision was knowing when to hold and when to release and making that distinction fast enough that the choice became reflex rather than decision.

She had spent six months turning choices into reflexes.

The deepest arena — Sublevel Four, carved so far into Mount Pyralis that the obsidian walls held their own heat independent of the torches — was where the third-years went when they'd exhausted the second-year drills and weren't yet cleared for the upper-rank training sequences. It was also, she had found, where instructors didn't come to observe at the sixth bell because the sixth bell was when the sixth bell was, which was to say: before anyone important was awake.

She had the arena to herself.

The sequence she was running was called the Sunder-and-Return, and it was exactly as violent as its name suggested. You took something with your fire — a target, a configuration, a pattern you'd built — and you destroyed it cleanly, completely, without residue, and then you rebuilt the same thing from nothing in the same space in under ten seconds. Not restoration. Rebuilding. The point was to prove that your fire understood the thing's structure well enough to reconstruct it from first principles. That you weren't holding on. That you could let go so completely that the letting-go was itself a form of mastery.

She was on her thirty-eighth repetition.

The targeting post — a practice construct of treated volcanic glass, standard issue, durable enough to survive a hundred cycles — caught fire on her exhale and came apart at the molecular level in approximately two seconds. The fire wasn't hot. That was the part instructors kept finding difficult to explain to new students: the most precise fire wasn't the most intense fire. The most precise fire was the fire that knew exactly what it was doing and did exactly that and nothing else.

She called the construct back.

It assembled — glass particles drawn together by the structure of her fire's memory of the thing it had just been, re-knitted, re-formed, standing again in the center of the sand.

She had it down to seven seconds.

She was going for five.

---

The thirty-ninth repetition ended in the target's destruction and the beginning of the rebuild when the mountain moved.

Not an earthquake. She knew earthquakes. She'd grown up in the Ember Quarter, where the mountain was a constant low presence beneath the city's feet, occasionally reminding the neighborhood that volcanoes were not in fact foundations so much as threats that had agreed to wait. Earthquakes had a particular quality: random, rising, unorganized. The vibration of a thing that had no opinion about what it was doing.

This had an opinion.

The vibration moved through the arena floor in a directed wave — not spreading outward from an epicenter but arriving, the way a sound arrived at an ear rather than an earthquake arriving everywhere at once. She felt it through her boot soles, through her knees, into the fire in her chest.

The fire responded.

Not her fire. The fire in her chest that was her Fire Gate — the 396 Hz that was Draconia's frequency, that her instructors had been working with for six months to sharpen from instinct into technique. It resonated. The way a string resonates when its pitch is played on another string across the room: not struck, not touched, just responding to the fact of another frequency that was close enough to its own.

The targeting construct she'd been rebuilding collapsed. She let it.

She crossed the arena in eight strides, hit the access corridor at a run, and did not stop moving.

The Roosts were four levels up. She took the inside stairs.

---

The access corridor to the caldera level was restricted.

She knew this. She knew also that the restriction operated on an authorization cipher that the Forge reset every morning at the fifth bell, and that the third-year training master — who supervised the advanced sequence that technically authorized Roost observation access — had given her the previous day's cipher to retrieve a piece of equipment three days ago and the reset schedule had been disrupted because there was something happening in the Roosts that had the Forge's facilities team running on six hours' sleep and making errors.

The cipher worked.

She told herself she would put it in writing if anyone asked.

No one asked. No one was in the corridor. The Forge, she had learned in six months of living inside it, could absorb an enormous amount of concentrated attention in one location and simultaneously leave the adjacent corridors completely empty, because emergencies were selfish that way. They demanded all of what they demanded and left nothing for the spaces in between.

She went through the cipher gate at a walk — running attracted the wrong kind of attention, and she had learned from watching Elio that the most effective way to go unnoticed was to look as though you belonged exactly where you were. Confidence was better camouflage than invisibility.

The Roost level opened above her.

---

The caldera air hit her face first.

Even after six months the Forge's outdoor spaces surprised her — the scale of them, the volcanic sky above the crater rim, the way the orange light that filtered down through the mountain's interior was a different quality than the copper light that scattered off the caldera's walls from above. Here, at the top, the sky was the actual sky: grey-blue pre-dawn, the eastern horizon just beginning to think about getting lighter, and beneath it the crater floor and the Dragon Roost alcoves carved into the inner wall of the caldera rim.

There were people ahead of her.

More than there should have been. A lot more.

Delegations — she had been learning to read delegations in the six months of the Forge's political education, which was less formal than the Luminary's and more practical. You learned by watching who stood together and who stood apart and how the air between them carried the specific quality of managed tension that existed between people who needed something from each other and intended to hide that fact as long as possible. Three groups, each keeping several careful paces of distance from the others. Forge masters in their training blacks. Luminary envoys in the light-weave robes that meant someone was sweating through something expensive. And in the third cluster — standing slightly further from the alcove, at an angle that allowed observation while maintaining plausible distance — figures in deep grey who were doing something much more interesting than all of them.

She did not know the grey uniforms. She filed them for later.

The designated candidates — six students, all third-year and above, including Vesper Ashcroft's daughter whose name she had never learned to remember because the girl had never spoken to her directly — stood inside the rope barrier that marked the official observation perimeter. Ash was standing outside it.

She stopped. Thought about the options. Decided she was inside the perimeter and walked forward until she was.

No one stopped her. Everyone was looking at the egg.

---

She had been this close to Seraphine's clutch twice — once during the formal Roost orientation in her first month, once during a training assignment that had required her to map the caldera's geothermal flow. Both times the eggs had been what they had been: warm volcanic rock, tended and still, the steady patient warmth of a mother Dragon's centuries-long vigil.

The egg at the center of the alcove was not that.

The egg was moving.

Not the whole egg — not rocking, not shifting in position. The shell itself. Hairline fractures mapped and re-mapped its surface in patterns that appeared and disappeared too quickly to follow, the dark red-black mottling splitting and seaming and briefly — she was not imagining this — briefly glowing from inside before the light withdrew and the fracture sealed. As if something inside was testing the shell's surface. Finding the weak points. Learning the structure before committing to a direction.

The fire in her chest tuned to it without her permission.

Not mimicking it — the frequency of what was happening in that egg was not 396 Hz, was not the clean standard Fire Gate resonance she had been training with for months. It was something that contained 396 Hz the way a chord contained individual notes: present, recognizable, but held inside something larger that changed its meaning.

She knew the other note inside the chord.

She knew it the way you knew a sound you had heard once in the dark and had spent months since identifying in daylight: not because you had heard it again, but because you had learned enough about sounds to recognize the shape of the one you were looking for.

Void.

Not the corrupted version — not the cold architectural absence she had touched in the arena eight months ago and had spent six months learning to understand. Nero's Void. The real kind. The potential, the unformed, the space where things could be. The dark that was not absence but the ground that presence grew from.

The egg carried both.

Fire and Void, together, in a ratio that her Fire Gate read as unusual and her fingers told her to put her hands out toward, which she did not do, because she had some restraint left.

"First candidate," the Forge's chief Dragon-keeper said. His voice was measured with the particular quality of professional calm that people used when they were managing their own fear by organizing other people. "Approach the observation line."

The first candidate — a fourth-year she recognized by reputation, Fire-primary, strong record — stepped forward. He stood at the observation line for twenty seconds while the egg's shell patterns shifted, then stepped back. The Dragon-keeper noted something in his ledger.

No response from the egg.

Second candidate. Third. The pattern was the same.

The egg was present, doing what it was doing, and completely indifferent to the designated observers.

Ash stood three feet back from the observation line behind the designated candidates and felt the fire in her chest pull toward the alcove the way a compass needle pulled toward north, which was not a direction she had requested and was also not something she was going to manage by standing still.

She stepped forward.

Someone's hand came down on her shoulder. Forge uniform. She knew without turning who it was — the master responsible for candidate management, whose specific job in this moment was to prevent exactly what she was doing.

"Cinder," the master said. Low. Controlled. "Step back."

She did not step back.

She did not step forward either. She stood at the observation line and let the fire in her chest do what it was doing, which was to stretch toward the egg as if the egg were a wick and she were the match, and she pressed her hands flat against her thighs and did not reach out, and she breathed.

The egg stopped.

The hairline fractures stilled. The internal light steadied. The patterns on the shell surface locked into a configuration and held.

She realized that the Forge's chief Dragon-keeper had also stopped speaking.

Everyone had stopped moving. The sort of collective stillness that happens when a room full of people all understand simultaneously that something is occurring that none of their plans accounted for.

---

The shell split down the center.

Not gradually. Not the way she had read about in the Codex — the incremental cracking over hours, the progressive widening as the hatchling inside worked at the seams. One fracture line ran from the egg's apex to its base, fast and certain, and from that line the shell opened in two halves as if it had always been designed to do exactly this.

The fire came first.

Gold-white, a burst that would have been fatal at close range and was merely blinding at ten feet. She did not flinch. The fire rolled off her skin at the surface level the way fire always rolled off her skin, which was to say: it recognized her and found nothing to catch.

The hatchling emerged into the light.

She had read the descriptions. The Dragon Codex was thorough: small by Dragon standards, the size of a large wardrobe, immediately and completely conscious. The fire in the first days was involuntary, uncontrolled, everything within six feet a fire risk.

The Codex was both accurate and completely inadequate.

The hatchling that stood in the ruins of its shell was the size she'd expected. Its scales were the color she had not expected: not the deep red-black of a standard Fire-blooded, not the fire-orange of a young Fire/Earth like she'd read about in the case files. The hatchling's scales shifted. The base color was something between copper and black, but across them — across the surface, in the same places where she had watched the shell's fractures pattern and re-pattern — ran threads of darkness. Not dark scales. The absence of light, structured into something visible. Void made color. Made form.

The hatchling shook the shell fragments from its wings.

It was beautiful the way a storm was beautiful: the kind of beauty that doesn't ask your permission.

The designated candidates were pressed against the back of the observation perimeter. The masters had not moved. The Luminary delegation had. The grey-uniformed figures she had not recognized were three steps closer than they had been.

The hatchling looked at each of them in turn.

She could not explain how she knew this: that the looking was deliberate, cataloguing, the inventory of a consciousness that had been formed and was now taking its first survey of the world it was in. The yellow-orange eyes moved from master to master, from delegation to delegation, with the particular quality of attention that was not curiosity — not the shallow wanting of a thing that didn't know — but the deeper assessment of a thing that already understood that the world contained both what it needed and what it would have to defend against, and was beginning the work of sorting the two categories.

The eyes reached Ash.

And stopped.

Not the assessment. The assessment was still there — the same quality, the same depth. But the speed changed. The inventory that had moved from face to face in seconds settled on her and slowed into something that had duration. The hatchling looked at her for what she estimated was a full three seconds, though her sense of time had recently become unreliable in situations like this.

She felt it in the fire in her chest. The same recognition she had felt from the egg — the Fire and Void frequency together, the chord that contained both notes. Looking at her. Reading her. Recognizing in her what it recognized in itself.

She did not move. She did not reach toward it. She stood at the observation line with her hands flat against her thighs and she let herself be read, and she thought: *I know what you are. I have known since before you existed. I am not afraid of you.*

She thought: *Not yet. Not here. Not with all these people watching.*

The hatchling turned away.

One clean motion, deliberate as a period at the end of a sentence, and its attention moved to the Forge's Dragon-keeper, who was doing the admirable work of maintaining professional composure while standing six feet from a newly hatched multi-element Dragon whose second element was Void, which had last appeared in a Dragon of record three thousand years ago in the case file that was sealed in the classified archive on the restricted level.

The Dragon-keeper cleared his throat. "Embera's hatching is confirmed," he said. His voice was steady. Not a tremble in it. "All observers, please —"

From very far away — not sound, not vibration, not any of the standard carriers of sensation — something moved.

She felt it in the same place she felt the fire. Below the fire. In the architecture of her own elements, the layered resonance of the Gates she carried. An awareness, vast and old, moving through the ley lines with the absolute unhurried certainty of something that had not moved in a very long time and was moving now because it had chosen to.

Not toward the Forge. Not toward the hatching.

Aware of the hatching. The way a mountain is aware of a fire on its slopes: not threatened, not responding, simply incorporating the new information into a consciousness that had been running for twelve thousand years and would continue running long after the fire was ash.

The masters knew it too. She could see it in them — the micro-expressions, the minute adjustments of posture that people made when something moved through them that they hadn't expected. The Dragon-keeper's pen had stopped moving. The Forge's head instructor, standing at the perimeter's edge, had put one hand against the caldera wall the way Kael put a hand against a wall when the Foundation substrate was telling him something.

The awareness moved through the Forge.

It moved through the mountain.

It moved in the direction of distant things.

And somewhere deep in Mount Pyralis, far below the Roost level, far below the training arenas, far below Malachar's sealed research chambers, further than any survey had reached, something shifted. Not structurally. Not geologically. The shift of a consciousness that had been turned inward for a hundred years, ambient heat its only presence in the world, now turning outward again toward something it had recognized.

One moment of recognition.

One word.

It was not a sound. It was fire-language — Thyr'vael, the Dragon tongue, modulated through volcanic rock and ley line and the physical structure of a mountain that had been, for longer than the current civilization had a name for itself, the sleeping place of the oldest thing alive. It arrived through her Fire Gate rather than her ears, and she understood it the way she had understood things since the Hollow Root: not in translation but in the feeling beneath the translation, the heat-pattern of it, the thermal impression of meaning carried in something older than language.

The word was: *Again.*

She stood at the observation perimeter in the caldera light while the designated candidates regrouped and the delegations murmured and the Forge's masters exchanged looks that carried the specific quality of people being professional about discovering that an emergency they had been managing had just become a different and larger emergency.

The Dragon-keeper's pen was still not moving.

The grey-uniformed figures were two steps closer than they'd been a moment ago.

The hatchling — the one with Void in its scales, the one that had looked at her for three full seconds and then turned deliberately away — was investigating the shattered remains of its shell with the focused attention of a consciousness that had just arrived in the world and was already taking inventory.

Ash looked at her hands.

Clean. The fire in her chest steady. The Void below it, present and quiet, the way it had been since she had named it and chosen not to be afraid of it.

She thought: Pyralith said *again.* Which meant this had happened before.

She thought: The masters have gone pale.

She thought: I need to find the others.

She thought, last of all — and this one she did not think in words but in something that was only barely language, a thing she had learned from six months of pressing her palms to the volcanic substrate of the mountain that held Malachar's sealed research chambers, a thing she had learned from the Void below the Fire that she had not asked for and had decided to understand: the hatchling and I carry the same thing. The same frequency. Fire and Void together, in the same organism. And whatever that means — in the ancient politics of dragonkind, in the sealed histories that the classified archive holds, in the Thyr'vael word that a twelve-thousand-year-old Primordial just spoke for the first time in a century — it means something that none of the people managing this morning's observation roster were prepared for.

The hatchling looked up from the shell fragments.

It looked at her one more time.

Not the three-second inventory. Something shorter. A single beat of acknowledgment, the kind you gave someone across a crowded room when words weren't possible: *I see you. We will talk. Not now.*

Then it returned to its work.

Ash unclenched her hands, turned, and walked back through the caldera access corridor at a pace that she hoped looked like a student who had seen something remarkable and was appropriately overwhelmed, rather than a student who had just understood something that the masters in charge of managing today's hatching had not understood yet, and who was planning on finding her people as fast as her legs would carry her.

## CODA: DEEP PYRALIS

No mortal record holds what happened next.

There is no witness to give account. There is only what the Draconian Academy's seismology instruments recorded in the forty minutes following the hatching — an anomalous subsurface event, not volcanic, originating from a depth the survey instruments could not locate precisely but estimated at twelve hundred meters below the caldera floor. A thermal signature that did not match any known geological phenomenon. A shift in the mountain's background resonance that the instruments logged and the duty officer flagged and the department head sealed under restricted access before the morning shift was half over.

The shift was the feeling of something old becoming current.

The shift was twelve thousand years of memory re-engaging with the present.

The shift was Pyralith opening one eye.

The Primordial had been sleeping for a hundred and seven years. Not deeply — not the crystalline withdrawal of a Sleeper, scales hardening into gemstone, consciousness withdrawing from the world like heat from cooled metal. A light sleep. The sleep of something that had not decided to stop watching so much as decided that the watching was, for a time, unnecessary.

The hatching had been felt at twelve hundred meters the way a tuning fork feels another tuning fork struck in the same room.

Not the hatching itself. What was in the hatching.

The old Dragon's eye — gold, slit-pupilled, the size of a wagon wheel, carrying in its iris a heat-pattern that encompassed twelve thousand years of memory — opened in the dark of its chamber and considered what it had felt.

Void in a Dragon's fire.

The last time this had happened, the world had called it the Third Age's end. The histories that survived from that era were incomplete. Most had burned in the thing that ended the Third Age. What remained were fragments — heat-memory, buried in the strata of the mountain, still warm if you pressed the right stone.

Pyralith knew what had burned the rest.

It also knew what had ended the burning.

It lay in the dark of its chamber, twelve hundred meters below the caldera where a two-hundred-year-old fledgling with Void in its scales was examining its own hatching and taking its first inventory of the world, and the Primordial thought in the slow complete way that twelve thousand years of thought had made it think — not fast, not urgent, because urgency was a mortal's way of processing threat, and a Primordial did not process threat the way mortals did. It processed threat the way stone processed pressure: completely, deeply, through its entire structure, over whatever time the processing required.

The new Dragon carried Void.

The new Dragon lived in the world that was.

Something in the ley lines — in the Foundation substrate that the Three Academies had been pressing their elemental work into for three thousand years, in the particular quality of the containment that held what it held beneath the Crystalpeak and Mount Pyralis and the Sunken City — had loosened, slightly, in the months since the Hollow Root's awakening. Not broken. Not even weakened. But different. The way a chord was different when one instrument in the orchestra changed its tuning.

The combination of what was in the Foundation substrate and what had just hatched was a combination Pyralith had felt once before, in an age when the world had a different name.

*Again.*

The word moved through the mountain.

It was not a warning.

It was not an alarm.

It was what the oldest things said when they recognized the shape of something they had seen before: not with dread, not with certainty, but with the absolute animal knowledge of a pattern that the universe had decided to run again.

The masters above heard it as a subsurface thermal anomaly.

One student understood it as a word.

Pyralith felt her understand it, very briefly, through the ley lines — the specific Fire/Void frequency of her, the particular chord of a young woman who had touched the Void six months ago and had spent every day since deciding what that meant. The Primordial filed this information in the heat-stratum of its memory, and added it to the pattern, and continued thinking.

When it had thought enough, it would speak.

For now: one eye open. The world receiving.

The world, as always, running.
