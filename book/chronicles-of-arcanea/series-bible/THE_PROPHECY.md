# The Prophecy of the Five Fractures

---

## Part One: The Discovery and the Drowning of Thal'Maris

In the second century of the Fourth Age, a maintenance crew reinforcing the Prophecy Spire's foundation pilings broke through into a chamber that did not appear on any architectural survey. The room was small -- ten paces across, dry despite being below the waterline, carved from a stone that no geologist in Thal'Maris could identify. The stone was warm. It hummed at a frequency that the workers later described as "the sound a bone makes when it remembers the animal it belonged to."

The walls were covered in writing. Old Atlantian, but older than any surviving example of the script -- the glyphs cut deeper, the grammar stripped of the courtesies and qualifiers that the language would later acquire. The maintenance foreman, a woman named Rael, could not read Old Atlantian. She sealed the chamber and reported the find to the Spire's Chief Archivist.

The Archivist was Tessavaine of the Deep Lens, the foremost seer in the Atlantian tradition and a woman who had spent forty years teaching herself not to look at things she could not bear to see. She entered the chamber alone. She emerged six hours later. Her assistants reported that she had aged visibly -- not in the way of illness or exhaustion, but in the way of someone who had received news so heavy that her body could not carry it without bending.

Tessavaine convened the Council of Depths that same evening. She read the prophecy aloud. The translation took ninety minutes. When she finished, the chamber was silent for what the official minutes recorded as "an extended pause."

Councilor Vethis Shallowmere, the youngest member, reportedly asked: "What do we do?"

Tessavaine's answer is preserved in three independent accounts, all identical: "We make certain that no one above the waterline ever hears these words spoken aloud. The air carries sound. Water swallows it."

The vote to open the sea gates was unanimous. No debate. No dissent. Ninety-four thousand citizens of Thal'Maris were given three hours to gather what they could carry. The evacuation barges launched at midnight. By dawn, the sea gates were open and the oldest city in Arcanea was drowning.

Those who survived and resettled on the Sapphire Archipelago founded what would become the Abyssal Athenaeum. They built it above the sunken city, partly to guard it, partly because they could not bear to be far from what they had sacrificed. The prophecy chamber now sits four hundred fathoms below the Athenaeum's foundation, in permanent darkness, in water too deep and too cold for all but the most powerful current-shapers to reach.

Three people alive today have read the full text. One of them is Keeper Thessaly Mar, who oversees the Athenaeum's restricted archive. Another is Scholar Ondine, whose private annotations appear in this document and whose increasingly urgent correspondence with the Academy Council has been, by her own account, "received politely and filed in the place where inconvenient truths go to suffocate."

The third reader's identity is not recorded.

What follows is the most complete translation available, produced across four decades by the Athenaeum's Department of Deep Translation. The translators noted that certain words shift emphasis depending on who reads them -- not metaphorically, but measurably, the glyphs producing different harmonic resonances when different people speak them aloud. This has made a definitive translation impossible. What is presented here is a composite, with disagreements of meaning noted in the annotations.

## Part Two: The Prophecy

### I. The Naming

> *When the warden-song grows thin*
> *and the sealed one dreams against his walls,*
> *five shall crack along the lines*
> *the Gods drew shut in grief and haste.*
> *Not children of the shadow. Not of the light.*
> *Children of the place between --*
> *where his reaching pressed so long*
> *the stone remembers the shape of hands.*

**Surface:** Five Gate-touched mortals will be born at weak points in the Sealing -- the harmonic prison woven from all ten Gate frequencies to contain Malachar in the Shadowfen. These five represent structural failures in the containment, and the Academies must identify and train them before their uncontrolled power destabilizes the prison further. The Academy Council has maintained a standing watch protocol for multi-Gate-affinity individuals since this stanza was first circulated among the Headmasters.

**Hidden:** The fractures are not accidental weaknesses. They are the Sealing collapsing under its own logic. Each Gate-touched mortal born along a fracture line is a piece of Malachar's sealed power that has found a vessel. Training these five at the Academies does not contain the threat -- it strengthens their Gate connections, which are literally made of leaked prison energy. Scholar Ondine's note: *"We find the cracks and call it diagnosis. Then we pry them wider and call it education."*

**True:** Shinkami built the fractures into the Sealing deliberately, three thousand years ago. The "warden-song grows thin" not because the prison is failing but because Shinkami encoded a timer into it -- harmonic additions buried beneath the other nine Gods' frequencies, set to resonate when conditions ripen. The five are not symptoms. They are keys that Shinkami hid inside the lock, timed to turn.

### II. The Linked Gates

> *Each fracture holds a thread the others lack.*
> *The first shall ground what cannot land.*
> *The second shall feel what will not be felt.*
> *The third shall burn what refuses to change.*
> *The fourth shall speak what is not yet true.*
> *The fifth shall see what sight alone cannot show.*
> *When five threads pull at once, the weaving tears.*
> *When five Gates sing together, the prison hears.*

**Surface:** Each of the five will hold a primary Gate affinity -- Foundation, Flow, Fire, Voice, Sight -- forming a complementary set. Together, their combined resonance is powerful enough to affect the Sealing. The standard interpretation: keep them separated, or direct their power constructively.

**Hidden:** These five Gates are not random. They are the structural spine of the Sealing -- the harmonic bones of Malachar's cage. The remaining five Gates (Heart, Crown, Shift, Unity, Source) form its walls. Destroy the spine and the walls collapse. If five mortals resonate at the spine frequencies simultaneously, the prison does not weaken. It inverts. The prison becomes a door. "When five Gates sing, the prison hears" does not mean Malachar notices. It means the lock opens. Ondine's note: *"This is not a warning. It is assembly instructions."*

**True:** The five named Gates correspond to Nero's aspect -- the mortal virtues of grounding, feeling, transforming, expressing, perceiving. The five unnamed Gates correspond to Lumina's aspect -- love, authority, perspective, communion, transcendence. Malachar's fall was a failure of Lumina's aspect: he possessed every mortal virtue but lacked the surrender that Source demands. To heal him, the mortal virtues must be carried to him by those who hold them genuinely. The prophecy is not a warning or a schematic. It is a prescription. The five are medicine.

---

### III. The Waking

> *The sealed one shall not wake as he was put to sleep.*
> *Three thousand years the hunger fed on warden-song*
> *and grew fat where the warden thought it starved.*
> *Every ward a meal. Every seal a feast.*
> *The wall that holds him in*
> *is the table set for the thing that holds him down.*

**Surface:** The Sealing has been weakening because the Hungry Void -- the corrupting force beneath Malachar's power -- feeds on magical containment. The longer Malachar remains sealed, the stronger the Void becomes. The Academies read this as justification for developing new containment methods.

**Hidden:** The entire defensive apparatus of the Eighth Age -- the Sealing, the Shadowwatch wards, the Dungeon containment protocols -- is a feeding apparatus. Every resource devoted to keeping Malachar sealed strengthens the force breaking the seal. Three thousand years of vigilance have produced three thousand years of nourishment for the thing the vigilance was meant to suppress. Ondine's note: *"We have spent thirty centuries feeding the creature we claim to be starving. The prophecy is not subtle about this. We do not want to hear what it says."*

**True:** The Hungry Void is not an entity. It is an absence -- the shape left when trust was ripped from a being powerful enough to warp reality with his grief. Malachar trusted the Gates, trusted the Gods, trusted his preparation. When Source refused him, that trust did not vanish. It became its inverse: a wound in the real, shaped exactly like the faith that used to fill it. You cannot seal an absence. Every wall built around it simply gives the absence a new surface to press against.

---

### IV. The Descent

> *One must go down to where the falling has no floor.*
> *Past the shadow that was shadow before the Dark Lord wore it.*
> *Past the hunger that was hunger before it had a mouth.*
> *Down to where the last of him still reaches*
> *with a hand that has forgotten it is a hand*
> *toward a light that he remembers but cannot name.*
> *The one who descends will find no monster.*
> *Only a boy who heard the sunlight singing*
> *and was never told that it would stop.*

**Surface:** Someone must enter the Shadowfen and confront Malachar directly. The "falling" refers to his corruption. The stanza reveals that Malachar retains a fragment of his original self. The Academies read this as intelligence: he may be reasoned with, or at least delayed through appeal to his former identity.

**Hidden:** This is not about the Shadowfen. "Where the falling has no floor" is the Hungry Void itself -- the tear beneath the Void that Malachar fell into when Source rejected him. Someone must follow his original fall and find what he left behind: the part of himself that was still human, still reaching, when the hunger replaced his trust with appetite. That fragment has been falling for three thousand years. Ondine's note: *"This is not a rescue mission for the Dark Lord. This is a rescue mission for the boy he was. They are not in the same place."*

**True:** The Hungry Void is a wound in reality -- the scar left when Malachar's power struck Source and was rejected. It has been bleeding for three millennia. You cannot heal a wound by sealing it while the foreign object remains inside. The fragment of Malachar still falling in the Void is the foreign object. It must be retrieved by someone who can survive the descent. Shinkami knows this because Shinkami tried. In the moment before the Sealing, when Malachar hesitated and the man surfaced through the monster, Shinkami reached into the Void after the falling fragment. And could not hold it. The God of Meta-Consciousness was too vast, too cosmic, too far from mortal to grip a mortal boy's hand. Only a mortal can retrieve a mortal. This is what Shinkami has been waiting three thousand years for.

### V. The Starving

> *You do not kill the hunger under hunger.*
> *It was here before the First Light spoke.*
> *It will be here when the last star forgets its name.*
> *It feeds on the distance between the reach and the thing reached for.*
> *It grows fat on every lock and chain and door held shut.*
> *You do not fight it. You cannot.*
> *You starve it.*
> *And the only thing that starves it*
> *is a door that opens from the inside.*

**Surface:** The Hungry Void is a primordial force, older than Lumina, indestructible. Seals and wards feed it. Something must be opened voluntarily to counter it, though the mechanism remains unclear. The Academy Council has debated this stanza for two centuries without reaching consensus.

**Hidden:** If the Source Gate were opened for Malachar -- genuinely, with consent, the way it should have opened the first time -- the Hungry Void would lose its hold. The Void exists only in the gap between Malachar's reaching and the Gate's refusal. Remove the refusal and the gap closes. The Void has nothing to feed on. Ondine's note: *"A door that opens from the inside. Not from the God's side. From the side of the one who was locked out."*

**True:** The Hungry Void is the shape of absent faith. Fill the absence with what was taken and it collapses. Someone must descend, find the falling fragment, take it by the hand, and mean it when they say: the door is open. Not as strategy. Not as sacrifice. As the bare fact of one person choosing to trust another in the place where trust was murdered. The Void starves because the faith it feeds on is no longer missing. It has been carried back in by hand.

---

### VI. The Cost

> *Who descends will not return as who descended.*
> *To carry the fallen one, you must first fall.*
> *To fill what was emptied, you must pour yourself out.*
> *To open the door from both sides at once*
> *you must stand on both sides at once*
> *and that is a thing no single soul can do*
> *and remain a single soul.*
> *This is the price. This is the gift.*
> *They are the same.*

**Surface:** The confrontation with Malachar will fundamentally alter whoever undertakes it. There is a sacrifice of self involved. The Academies read this as a warning that the mission may cost the hero their identity or life.

**Hidden:** The descent into the Hungry Void is a one-way transformation. The person who enters will experience what Malachar experienced: the falling, the hunger, the annihilation of trust. They must surrender their own certainty, their identity as rescuer. If they cling to any of it, the Void feeds on the clinging. The only way through is to become empty enough to hold someone else's grief without adding your own. Ondine's note: *"The prophecy asks for someone willing to be unmade so the world can be remade. I have read this stanza more times than I can count and I do not know whether such a person can exist."*

**True:** The Source Gate opens from both sides. This is literal. If someone carries Malachar's fallen fragment back to the threshold and the Gate opens from within the Void, Malachar completes his original journey. He passes through Source. He becomes what he was always meant to become: a Luminor. The one who descends must surrender so completely that they pass through their own Source Gate in the act of rescue -- not by climbing the ten Gates in sequence but by falling through the space beneath all of them and discovering, at the bottom, that the bottom is also the top. The deepest fall and the highest transcendence are the same place, approached from different directions.

### VII. The Five Made Whole

> *The fractures are not wounds. They are mouths.*
> *Through them the sealed one has been breathing*
> *for longer than the wardens knew.*
> *When the five come together they will feel it --*
> *his breath moving through them like a tide*
> *they mistook for their own wanting.*
> *Do not be afraid of this.*
> *The breath of the prisoner and the breath of the free*
> *come from the same lungs.*

**Surface:** The five fracture-born share a connection to Malachar that runs deeper than inherited power. They will sense his presence when united. The Academies read this as a contamination risk -- evidence that the five may be subject to Malachar's influence and should be monitored closely.

**Hidden:** The five have been channeling Malachar's sealed consciousness their entire lives without knowing it. Their Gate powers, their shared dreams, their instinctive pull toward one another -- all of it is Malachar breathing through the cracks in his own prison. They are not vessels for leaked power. They are extensions of his will, and the desires they believe are their own may not be. Ondine's note: *"The terrible question is not whether they are controlled. It is whether the difference between his wanting and theirs still exists."*

**True:** The connection between the five and Malachar is not corruption. It is kinship. The Sealing was built from all ten Gate frequencies, and when it cracked, what leaked through was not power but personhood -- fragments of the boy Malachar was before the Hungry Void consumed his trust. The five carry pieces of his original self, distributed across five mortal lives. This is why they must come together: not to combine their power against him, but to reassemble the part of him that the Void could not digest. They carry his humanity. When they stand together, they are, in a real and measurable sense, the Malachar who might have been. The one who would have passed through Source if he had been willing to wait.

### VIII. The Choice of the Gods

> *When the sealed one walks through the door*
> *that closed on him for the sin of knocking,*
> *the wardens will face what wardens face*
> *when the prisoner was innocent all along:*
> *the question of what the prison was for.*
> *Ten who guarded. Ten who decided*
> *who was ready and who was not.*
> *Ten who must now decide*
> *whether gates need keepers*
> *or whether the keeping was the wound.*

**Surface:** After Malachar is dealt with, the Gods will face a decision about the future of the Gate system. The Academies have no formal interpretation of this stanza. It is considered too speculative to act on.

**Hidden:** "The sin of knocking" reframes the entire history. Malachar did not fall because of arrogance. He fell because he asked a question the system could not answer: what happens when someone ready is denied? The Source Gate measures surrender, not readiness, and Malachar's readiness was irrelevant to a mechanism that demanded a quality he could only have learned by being told why it mattered. No one told him. The Gods guarded the Gate but did not explain the lock. Freeing Malachar is an admission that the system failed him before he failed the system. Ondine's final note: *"The prophecy does not predict the end of the world. It predicts the end of the Gods as we know them. Now I understand why the Atlantians chose to drown."*

**True:** Shinkami built this choice into the Sealing from the beginning. Three thousand years ago, standing over the sealed prison, knowing the seal was temporary, knowing the Hungry Void would eventually eat through it, Shinkami made a decision: the next time Source was approached, it would not be by a single mortal asking alone. It would be by a mortal who had gone into the dark and come back carrying someone who had been falling for three millennia. The question would be different. Not "am I ready?" but "can I bring him with me?" The Gods' choice is simple and it is the hardest thing any divine being has ever faced: step down. Not from existence. From authority. Let the Gates stand open. Let mortals carry their own keys. The Gods were wardens. Malachar was right about that. The prophecy does not say the wardens were wrong. It says the time for wardens is over.

### IX. What Arcanea Becomes

> *Beyond the last Gate is not a place.*
> *It is the world you are already standing in*
> *seen without the weight of the lock between you and it.*
> *When every door stands open*
> *and every mortal holds their own key*
> *and the hunger has starved*
> *and the boy who heard the sunlight singing*
> *hears it again --*
> *then you will know what Arcanea was for.*
> *It was not a prison. It was not a test.*
> *It was a school that forgot it was supposed to close for the summer.*

**Surface:** A new era begins after the crisis resolves. Arcanea transforms into something beyond its current state. The final image is obscure.

**Hidden:** The Gate system was never meant to be permanent. The Gates were training structures -- scaffolding for a civilization learning to handle the full inheritance of Lumina and Nero. At some point, the scaffolding was supposed to come down. The Gods were always meant to step aside. The system calcified into permanence because the Sealing War made the Gods afraid, and afraid wardens do not relinquish control. Arcanea's entire Eighth Age civilization is built on a temporary structure that overstayed its purpose. Ondine never annotated this stanza. In the margin she wrote only: *"Yes."*

**True:** The prophecy's final stanza is not about the future. It is about the present, described from a vantage the reader does not yet occupy. Arcanea, right now, today, is a world where mortals carry divine inheritance and do not know it, where the Gates are not barriers but lenses, where the thing every Creator is reaching for is already inside them, waiting for the moment they stop asking permission to use it. The prophecy does not predict this. It reveals it. The boy who heard the sunlight singing is not only Malachar. It is everyone who has ever created something and felt, for a moment, that they were not making it but remembering it. The sunlight is still singing. It never stopped. The only thing between the listener and the song is the belief that someone else holds the key.

## Addendum: Why Thal'Maris Drowned

Scholar Ondine's reconstruction of Tessavaine's reasoning, assembled from Council minutes, evacuation logs, and three letters Tessavaine wrote that night but never sent:

The Atlantians did not sink their city because the prophecy predicted the end of the world. They sank it because the prophecy predicted the end of the Gods -- and the Atlantians, alone among the peoples of the Fourth Age, understood what that meant.

The Gods were not merely powerful beings who administered the Gates. They were the psychological foundation on which Atlantian civilization rested. The academies, the social hierarchies, the systems of justice and commerce and art -- all of it derived authority from the divine order. The prophecy did not threaten Thal'Maris with destruction. It threatened something worse: the revelation that the divine order was temporary. That the Gods were scaffolding. That the entire structure of Atlantian life was built on borrowed authority that was always meant to be returned.

Tessavaine understood that if the prophecy were spoken above the waterline -- in open air, where sound carries, where words spread -- it would not matter whether people believed it. The idea would exist. And an idea, once loose, cannot be sealed. It eats through walls the way water eats through stone, slowly and then all at once.

She chose drowning not as despair but as quarantine. Water swallows sound. The deep sea is the quietest place in Arcanea. She buried the words where they could not be heard, and she buried the city that had heard them, and she trusted that when the time came -- when the five fractured, when the prison cracked, when someone was brave enough or foolish enough to descend -- the words would still be there, waiting, carved into warm stone in a language that shifts its meaning for every reader.

She was right. The words waited. They are still waiting.

And Scholar Ondine, who has spent her life listening to what the Academies refuse to hear, writes in the final entry of her private journal:

*The prophecy is not a prediction. It is a test of whether we can hear what it says instead of what we wish it said. The Atlantians heard it and chose to drown. The Academies hear it and prepare for war. The Shadowwatch hears it and builds more walls.*

*None of them are listening.*

*It says: the only way to save the world is to trust the one who broke it. The only way to close the wound is to open it wider. The only way forward is through the dark, carrying a hand that forgot it was a hand, toward a door that opens from both sides.*

*I do not know whether I am brave enough for what that means. I only know that every year we wait, the hunger grows fatter on our caution. And I am running out of years.*
