# Chapter Three: Crystalpeak

He woke to aurora light on the ceiling and forgot where he was.

The room they'd given him was small and bare and the walls were crystal and the first light of morning came through them already changed — not the grey-white of mountain dawn but something tinted, layered, a slow progression of colors that moved as the sun moved. He lay on his back and watched it and tried to hold onto the feeling from the previous night, the first moment he'd walked through the main gates and the full scale of the Crystalpeak had resolved into reality around him.

He'd lost the thread somewhere around the third orientation lecture. Long night.

The Crystalpeak was not floating, exactly — he'd been wrong about that. It sat atop an actual mountain, but the crystal spires and structures extended so far above the rock base that from below they appeared to float. The internal architecture was bewildering: corridors that opened into spaces too large for their exteriors, pocket libraries that were smaller on the inside than the outside but somehow held more books, staircases that went up and arrived below. He'd been given a room on the residential tier and told the dining hall was on the eastern spiral and handed a map that he suspected was only loosely correlated with actual geography.

He got dressed. He looked at the map. He found the dining hall on the third try.

---

Sixty new students ate breakfast in a room lit by refracted sunlight that fell in columns through crystal overhead. The columns moved as the sun moved, slow and deliberate, so that by the end of a meal you were sitting in different light than when you'd started. Kael found a seat at the end of a long table, got food that was better than anything he'd expected, and watched the other students work out their initial arrangements.

There were patterns, visible quickly. Students from Academy families had found each other within the first hour and formed their gravitational clusters. Students from cities moved differently than students from villages. Everyone was watching everyone else's hands, which was where the affinity usually showed — small involuntary gestures, light gathering in palms, sparks in fingers — the half-controlled tells of people who'd been managing their Gifts outside of proper training.

A girl sat down across from him without asking. About his age, brown-skinned, dark-haired, with a face that had already made up its mind about most things. Her academic robe was standard issue but she'd removed the House insignia patch — he could see the thread marks where it had been — and sewn nothing in its place.

"You climbed up," she said. It wasn't a question.

"How do you know?"

"Your boots are wrong. Transit students come in clean. You've got granite dust in the seams."

He looked at his boots. She was right.

"What's your Gate?" she asked.

"Foundation. You?"

She was quiet for a moment. "Void."

He didn't know what to do with that. He'd been given one crash course in Gate theory during orientation and had retained approximately forty percent of it. He knew Void was rare. He knew people's reactions to it were not always straightforward.

"I'm Kael," he said.

"Sable."

She picked up her fork and ate with the focused efficiency of someone who had decided that conversation was over for now. Kael ate too. The aurora light shifted, a column of gold moving across the table between them.

"They separated out the Void students for orientation last night," she said, after a while. "Different room. Different schedule." She said it flat, not asking for a response.

"Was it different content or just different room?"

She looked at him. Something shifted in her expression that wasn't quite surprise. "Different content," she said. "Emphasis on contamination risk. Shadow differentiation. How to identify if your Void is trending toward Shadow corruption." A pause. "Which, to be clear, is about as likely as spontaneous combustion from Foundation magic. But they like to make sure we know the difference."

"That sounds like a long way of saying they don't trust you yet."

"Welcome to existing with Void affinity." She returned to her food.

He thought about his father's face the night the walls cracked. The looks from the village. Go inside.

"My village was afraid of me for three days," he said. "I broke six walls by accident. I can't imagine what it's like if the fear doesn't have a specific incident to attach to."

Sable was still looking at her plate. "It attaches to the category," she said. "The incident is you existing."

He had nothing useful to say to that, so he said nothing, which turned out to be the right call. She ate the rest of her meal. He ate the rest of his. The aurora light made its slow rotation across the room.

When she left, she said: "Foundation Gate is in the east wing. First class is Light-weaving." A pause. "Good luck."

He didn't think luck was quite what he'd need but he thanked her anyway.

---

The east wing held six teaching rooms and something labeled the Inkwell Gardens, which he made a note to investigate, and a larger space at the end of a crystal corridor called the Weaving Hall. He arrived four minutes early and found it half-full already.

The Weaving Hall was a tall room, circular, with no apparent light source. Light simply existed in it, ambient and diffuse, sourceless. The walls curved upward to a crystal apex fifty feet above, and the air smelled of something he couldn't name — clean and slightly metallic, like air after lightning, but warmer.

The instructor arrived precisely at the hour.

Master Toren was compact, grey-haired, and still the way old stone was still — not inert but settled, decades of movement compressed into a man who'd decided exactly what to do with his hands. He wore no House insignia. Just a plain robe, the grey of old slate. He walked to the center of the room and looked at the assembled students with a gaze that moved quickly but landed precisely.

"Light-weaving," he said. "That's what this course is called. Before we start, I want everyone to attempt something for me." He held up his right hand, palm up. "Focus on producing light. Any amount. Don't strain. Just reach toward it."

Around the room, hands came up. Light happened. Small blooms of it, varying colors — some warm gold, some white, some with the blue tinge that Kael associated from his reading with Wind-adjacent affinities. Some students produced barely a flicker. A boy near the front made something the size of a lantern and visibly struggled to hold it. The girl beside Kael produced a steady sphere the size of an apple and held it without apparent effort, looking bored.

Kael held up his hand and reached.

Nothing happened. He was clear on the reaching — he could feel the attempt, the outward gesture of attention, the same direction you aimed when you wanted something external to respond. He'd done it with stone. Stone had answered immediately. This — nothing. Pushing against air.

He lowered his hand.

Empty palm. He looked around the room. Every other hand held light.

Not embarrassment, exactly. Something more fundamental. The precise feeling of being wrong about where you fit.

Toren had watched all of it without comment. Now he moved through the room, pausing at each student. Adjusting an elbow here, redirecting an intention there. When he reached Kael he looked at the empty palm, then at Kael's face.

"What's your Gate?" he asked.

"Foundation."

Toren said nothing for a moment. Then: "Come with me."

He led Kael to the side of the room while the other students continued. Up close, Toren's face had the texture of someone who had lived interestingly — not just aged, but *accumulated*, each line placed deliberately by whatever had put it there.

"You can't do this," Toren said. It was not unkind.

"I noticed."

"Do you know why?"

Kael thought about how to answer that. "Foundation Gate is Earth," he said. "This is a Light class."

"That's the surface answer." Toren looked at the other students. "A Luminary student with Wind affinity can learn Light-weaving because Wind and Light share a frequency domain — the upper registers. A student with Water affinity can learn it because Water and Light both work through transmission." He paused. "Foundation magic operates entirely in the lower registers. Pressure, density, weight, structure. Light-weaving isn't just a different skill. It's a different conversation. You don't speak that language."

Kael looked at the other students, their small lights blooming in various states of control, and thought about Sera's words: *safe to be near yourself.* He'd thought that was a beginning. He hadn't thought about what came after.

"Did someone make a mistake?" he asked. "With my placement."

"That depends on what you mean by mistake." Toren's voice was careful. "The Luminary sorted you here. That sorting isn't arbitrary."

"But I can't do what everyone else in this room can do."

"No." Toren walked back toward the center of the room, then stopped and turned. "The Luminary doesn't sort by element. Every Academy recruiter will tell you something different about our admission criteria. They're all partially right and mostly wrong." He looked at Kael with the expression of someone making a decision. "The Luminary sorts by what you're meant to create. Not what you already are. What you're meant to *build.*"

The word landed differently than Kael expected. Not create — build. The distinction mattered to him and he didn't immediately know why.

"Foundation magic is the oldest Gate," Toren continued. "Before Light-weaving, before Mend-song, before any of the creation arts that this Academy is known for, there was the first question: what do you build it on?" He walked back toward Kael. "Every structure requires a foundation. Every new form requires the principles of stability, of weight-bearing, of what lasts. The Luminary has trained dozens of Light-weavers in the past century. We have trained exactly two Foundation mages." A pause. "Both of them changed this place fundamentally. Both of them are the reason certain things here are still standing."

Kael looked around the Weaving Hall. The crystal walls. The impossible height of the apex. The way the light moved without a source.

"This Hall," he said.

Toren said nothing, which was confirmation.

"Someone with Foundation magic built this."

"Someone with Foundation magic built the permanent light matrix into the crystal lattice of the walls." Toren pressed his palm flat against the nearest wall. The light beneath his hand shifted, brightened, as if responding to the contact. "That's why the light here has no source. She understood stone and crystal as living systems, not static materials. She could speak to what the stone wanted to be and guide it there."

Kael looked at the wall. Looked at Toren's hand against it, and the light pooling around his fingers like water finding a low point. He could feel something — faint, just at the edge of perception — the crystal humming the way the quarry granite had hummed. The same language. He pressed his own hand against the wall.

The hum sharpened. Not louder. Clearer. Like a voice coming into focus across a crowded room.

Toren watched him. Said nothing for three full breaths.

"You feel that," Toren said. Not a question.

Kael nodded. His palm was warm where it touched the crystal. The warmth went deep, past skin, past bone, into whatever part of him had cracked six walls in Stonethatch.

"You won't learn Light-weaving this semester, or possibly ever," Toren said. "You will learn something very few people in this Academy know how to teach, because very few people here have ever needed to learn it." He paused. "That's either a problem or an opportunity. Which one it is depends on you."

Kael pulled his hand away. The hum faded. He thought about the granite block at the quarry, split along a dozen natural fault lines, smooth as glass. *The stone had wanted to come apart cleanly.*

He thought about what Toren had said: *guide it there.*

"I'll stay in this class," he said.

Toren raised an eyebrow.

"Even if I can't do Light-weaving." He gestured at the walls. "I want to understand what's in those walls. The light matrix. How it was built." He paused. "I don't know if that's the right instinct, but it's the one I have."

Toren looked at him for a long moment. The expression was opaque. Kael had no read on whether this was welcome or problematic or some combination of both.

"Come back at seventh hour," Toren said finally. "After the main session. Bring nothing — no notes, no expectations. We'll start from the beginning." He turned back to the main class. "For today, sit and observe. You won't be able to produce what they produce, but you can watch how they reach for it. The gesture is the same even when the element is different."

Kael found a place along the wall and sat and watched.

Sixty minutes of Light-weaving from the perspective of someone who couldn't do it was not the lesson he'd expected, but it turned out to be a different kind of lesson. He watched how students reached and reached and reached and occasionally got it wrong and started over, and he saw the quality of the attention required — focused but not forced, present but not controlling — and he recognized it. He'd felt it in the quarry, in the moment before the stone responded. The reaching wasn't a command. It was an offer.

*This is what I would like. If you'd like it too.*

He'd thought that was a weakness, that he'd been doing it wrong. The stone moving not because he'd struck it right but because he'd asked instead of demanded.

Maybe not.

---

He found Sable in the Inkwell Gardens after the main session ended. She was sitting on a bench near a planting bed where the flowers glowed with interior light, reading something with the focus of someone solving a problem. She looked up when he sat down uninvited, assessed him, returned to her reading.

"Toren wants to see me privately at seventh hour," he said.

"I know."

He waited.

"He does that with Foundation students," she said. "And Void students. The ones who can't do the standard work." She turned a page. "I have my own separate session tomorrow morning."

"Is it useful?"

"I don't know yet. I've been here twenty-four hours."

He looked at the glowing flowers. Someone had grown light into them — actually integrated luminescence into living plants. He touched a petal and felt nothing unusual. Just a petal. The light came from somewhere deeper in the structure, some integration he couldn't read from the surface.

He wanted to understand how that worked. The wanting was clear and immediate and uncomplicated by anything else.

"Toren said the Luminary sorts by what you're meant to create," he said. "Not what you are."

Sable lowered her book slightly. "That's idealistic."

"Maybe. But the alternative is that someone made a real mistake with both of us, and I'm less interested in that version."

She looked at him. He got the sense she was deciding something. Outside, through the crystal walls, the Veil Mountains stretched below in late afternoon light, the valley floor invisible under the cloud layer, his whole life prior to three days ago folded somewhere down in that cloud and no longer immediately relevant.

"There's one student in the Void track who's been here three years," she said. "I've been trying to find her."

"The one who stayed." He didn't know how he knew she was referring to someone specific.

Sable's expression shifted fractionally. "You know about her?"

"I don't. You mentioned yesterday — the ones who transferred or withdrew." He paused. "The one who stayed."

She watched him — recalibrating. He got the feeling she didn't recalibrate often.

"Fen," she said. "Her name is Fen. She's a third-year." Sable looked back at her book. "She told me not to expect her to hold my hand. I respect that. I'm going to find her anyway."

"Good strategy."

"Reasonably logical, yes."

He almost smiled. "I meant I agree with it. Knowing the person who survived the same thing seems like relevant intelligence."

Sable turned another page, which might have been dismissal, and then said without looking up: "Toren's seventh-hour session. Tell me how it goes."

Not a question. Not a request. But not nothing, either.

"I will," he said.

He left her reading and walked back through the crystal corridors, the aurora light tracking his progress through the walls. Seventh hour was still three hours away. He had time to find the east wing library, which was supposed to hold technical histories of the Crystalpeak's construction. He had questions that needed answering before his session with Toren, not because it would help but because he needed to arrive knowing what he didn't know rather than not knowing what he didn't know, which was different.

The library turned out to be through the door he'd been avoiding because the dimensions were wrong — smaller outside, much larger inside — and he stepped through it into a space that extended in multiple directions simultaneously and spent thirty seconds very consciously not thinking about the geometry.

Then he found the architecture shelf. He pulled three volumes.

He sat down under a light that came from everywhere and nowhere and read about foundations.

---

Seventh hour came. He walked to the Weaving Hall.

Toren was already there, standing at the center of the room in the sourceless ambient light, waiting with the patience of someone who had been waiting for a long time and had made peace with it.

Behind him, the crystal walls held their permanent light matrix — thousands of years of careful work, a Foundation mage's understanding of what stone could become encoded into the structure of the place itself.

Kael walked to the center of the room and stood across from his instructor and looked at the walls.

"Before we start," he said, "I have a question."

"You read the architectural histories."

"Two of them."

"And?"

"The Foundation mage who built the light matrix. The histories give a name — Avin Ser — but not much else. No biography. No other works. Just this building." He paused. "The histories say she finished the Crystalpeak and then left the Luminary. Left the Academies entirely. And no one knows where she went."

Toren's expression was very still.

"I'm not asking you to explain her choices," Kael said. "I'm asking — did the Luminary know what they had, with Foundation magic? When they had it? Or did they only understand it after she left?"

Toren looked at him for a long time.

"That," he said quietly, "is exactly the right question."

He clasped his hands behind his back and turned toward the walls, toward the light that had been living in crystal for centuries, and spoke.

Two floors below, in the Inkwell Gardens, Sable Luminaire turned the last page of her reading and sat with the silence of the Void — not absence but potential, the space where something had not yet been decided.

She thought about the third-year student she needed to find. She thought about what might be locked beneath the Crystalpeak that no one talked about. She thought about her parents' paper.

She intended to prove it wrong.

She just needed to find someone who'd show her how.
