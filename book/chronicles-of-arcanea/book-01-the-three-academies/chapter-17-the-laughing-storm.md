# Chapter Seventeen: The Laughing Storm

The free evening was Celestine's idea. Ash knew this because Voss told the Forge delegation with the resigned expression of someone who had been overruled and was passing the information along without endorsing it.

"Three hours," Voss said. "The Great Hall is open. Luminary kitchens have prepared food. The bar is running Crystalpeak cider, which I'm told is weak enough not to cause incidents. You are representatives of the Draconis Forge and I expect you to behave accordingly."

She looked at Ash when she said this. Not because he was the most likely to misbehave — Cassin held that distinction and everyone knew it — but because Ash was the one Voss had opinions about, and her opinions were always delivered through eye contact.

"Commander," Ash said. "Understood."

"Good." She paused. "Enjoy yourself. That's an order."

---

The Great Hall had been cleared of its trial configuration and reset for gathering. Long tables with food. The bar Voss had mentioned. Crystal lanterns hanging from the vaulted ceiling, their light softer than the aurora — warm gold instead of shifting spectrum, somebody's attempt to make the space feel less like a cathedral and more like a place where three hundred young people could exist without formal assessment watching their every move.

It almost worked.

Ash got a cup of the cider, which was indeed weak, and found a spot along the eastern wall where the crystal had a natural shelf at sitting height. He settled there and watched the room.

Three hundred students mixing. The patterns were immediate and legible. Forge students clustered in the warmer sections — the Great Hall's thermal map was uneven, and fire-trained students found the warm pockets the way cats found sunlight. Luminary students occupied the center, moving with the fluid social confidence of people who were on home ground. Athenaeum students formed smaller groups near the exits, positioned for observation rather than immersion.

At the boundaries: conversations. Tentative, careful, the language of people trained in fundamentally different disciplines trying to find common vocabulary. A Forge student showing a Luminary student something with her hands — a small flame, cupped, controlled. The Luminary student reaching for it and then pulling back. Laughter from both. Not mockery. Discovery.

Ash watched this and felt something he didn't have a clean name for. Not jealousy — he'd stopped being jealous of easy social contact around the time he stopped sleeping in alleys. Something adjacent. The particular feeling of watching people do something natural and realizing you'd never learned the steps.

He drank the cider. It was, as advertised, too weak to do anything useful.

---

The idea arrived fully formed, which was how his worst and best ideas always arrived. No preamble. No planning. Just the complete shape of a thing he needed to do, sitting in his mind like a flame that had lit itself.

He stood up from the crystal shelf and walked to the center of the Great Hall.

Not to a stage — there was no stage. Not to a designated performance area — there was none. He walked to the center of the room, which was not occupied because center-of-the-room was a location that implied you were asking for attention, and he stood there and waited for the room to notice.

It took eight seconds. Conversations thinned. Faces turned. Three hundred students looking at the Forge student standing alone in the middle of the Great Hall with his hands loose at his sides and no visible reason for being there.

He raised his right hand.

The fire came up clean. Not the marble-sized sphere of his first-week exercises. Not the precise point Voss had drilled into him. Something else — something that had been growing in his hands since the Dungeon, since the still core that Embera had noticed, since the moment on the fifth floor when a sealed door had recognized him.

The fire rose from his palm in a shape that was not a weapon and not a tool. A column, maybe three feet tall, amber at the base darkening to a blue-white at the tip that he'd never achieved in training. The heat of it was contained — anyone within five feet would feel warmth but not burn. The light of it was extraordinary, throwing shifting shadows across the crystal ceiling, fracturing into the spire-light above.

He held it for three breaths. Let the room look.

Then he began.

---

The Ashwalker remembrance was not performed. It had not been performed in public in Ash's lifetime, or his mother's, or her mother's before that. The Ashwalkers — the traveling fire-keepers of the eastern steppes, the people who had carried fire from settlement to settlement during the two centuries of cold that followed the Sealing War — had been suppressed after the Ember Purges. Their traditions had been declared unsafe. Their fire-keeping techniques — which worked differently from the Forge's disciplined approach, which used rhythm and movement and the body's own heat rather than channeled Gate energy — had been classified as uncontrolled manifestation and banned.

Ash's mother had taught him three things before she left: how to start a fire in rain, how to keep a fire alive in snow, and the remembrance. She'd taught him the remembrance the way you taught someone a language you were afraid would die — quickly, urgently, with the specificity of someone who knew she was the last speaker.

He hadn't performed it for anyone. Not the Ember Quarter. Not the Forge. Not Voss, who would have recognized it and would have had to decide what to do about recognizing it, a decision he hadn't wanted to force.

He performed it now.

The fire moved with his body. Not because he was directing it — because he was dancing, and the fire followed the movement the way fire always followed movement when the body was warm enough and the intention was precise enough and the performer was an Ashwalker doing the only thing Ashwalkers had ever been meant to do.

The remembrance was not pretty. It was not the Forge's controlled pyrotechnics, the precise sheets of fire between trained hands. It was ragged and rhythmic and it moved the way grief moved — in surges, with pauses, the fire growing bright when the body moved fast and dimming to embers when the body went still. The pattern was a walk. That was the form: a walk across an imagined landscape, carrying a fire that could not be allowed to go out, the body moving through cold and wind and exhaustion and the fire persisting because the walker persisted.

The fire threw light across the crystal ceiling. Not aurora light. Not the Crystalpeak's cold spectrum. Ashwalker light — warm, amber-brown, the color of a hearth fire in a house on a steppe when the wind was too strong and the fire was the only alive thing in the room. The crystal walls caught it and held it without transforming it, the way they held everything, and for a moment the Great Hall was not a crystal cathedral but a steppe shelter, warm and close and lit by a single fire carried across impossible distance by a single pair of hands.

The room was silent.

He danced the walk. Twenty steps. Each step a generation — his mother had told him this, told him the remembrance covered the full history of the Ashwalkers in twenty steps, each step the distance between one fire-keeper and the next, and the fire passing between them in the way that fire passed between things that were willing to carry it.

On the fifteenth step, something else came in.

Wind.

He hadn't called it. The crystalpeak's interior circulation was sealed — no natural wind in the Great Hall, no drafts, no cross-ventilation. But wind came in anyway, a gust that caught his fire and bent it, and the fire leaned into the wind the way fire leaned when it found air it wanted, and the two elements spiraled together into something that was neither fire nor wind but the conversation between them.

He looked up. In the gallery seating above the main floor, a Luminary student was standing with her hand extended. Wind-affinity. He didn't know her name. She was feeding the display without invitation, her wind catching his fire and carrying it higher, the spiral climbing toward the crystal ceiling.

Another gust. Different direction. A Forge student in the northern section — someone with a secondary wind affinity, the rare dual-Gate configuration that Oryn had — adding to the current. The fire responded. Not one spiral now. Three, braiding together, amber and blue-white and the warm gold of wind that had passed through crystal.

The music was the last thing.

He hadn't heard Elio enter the room. But Elio was there — sitting on the eastern shelf where Ash had been sitting two minutes ago, his instrument in his hands, playing something that was not accompaniment. It was structural. The melody held the fire and the wind in a framework, gave them a rhythm to move against, the way a hearth gave fire a shape without constraining it.

The five-line melody. The one with no name.

Ash danced the last five steps of the remembrance with fire braiding through wind above him and music beneath him and three hundred students watching something they had never seen and would argue about afterward in corridors and dormitories for years. The Ashwalker walk. The suppressed tradition. The thing that had been banned because it was dangerous, because it was uncontrolled, because the people who practiced it had been too close to fire in a way the Academies couldn't manage.

The thing that was, he understood now, not dangerous at all. Just beautiful. And the beauty was what made it unmanageable — you couldn't institutionalize beauty. You could only carry it.

He stopped on the twentieth step. The fire settled. The wind died.

The Great Hall was very quiet.

---

The silence lasted four seconds. Then the applause started, and it was loud, and it came from all three sections simultaneously, which had not happened at any point in the Triennium thus far.

He stood in the center of the Great Hall and breathed.

A hand on his arm.

Not Elio — he'd felt Elio's approach through the residual emotional field, the Heart Gate's particular quality of attention. This was someone else. He turned.

An Athenaeum judge. Senior faculty, by the robe. Male, middle-aged, with the pale eyes and sea-weathered skin of someone who had spent decades at the Athenaeum's depth. His hand was on Ash's arm and his expression was not appreciation.

"That display," the judge said, "utilized techniques classified as prohibited under the Ember Accord of —"

"The Ember Accord was an administrative document," Ash said. "Not a law."

"The Forge's regulations regarding unsanctioned fire-display are —"

"I'm not on Forge grounds." Ash kept his voice level. Voss had taught him this: the power of a controlled voice in a room that expected heat. "The Triennium is held under joint jurisdiction. The free evening has no formal assessment. No regulation of either the Forge or the Athenaeum applies to a social gathering on Luminary territory."

The judge's hand tightened on his arm. Not painfully. With the specific pressure of someone who was used to being obeyed and was discovering, in real time, that the mechanism of obedience was not engaging.

"Young man," the judge said. "The Ashwalker traditions were suppressed for —"

He stopped.

The hand on Ash's arm loosened. The judge's face changed — not slowly, not dramatically. A smoothing. The specific quality of an expression resetting, like water finding a new level. His hand dropped. His mouth opened, then closed. He looked at Ash with the vague puzzlement of someone who had walked into a room and forgotten why.

"I beg your pardon," the judge said. He turned and walked away.

Ash stood very still.

He looked to his left. Sable was standing four feet away, her right hand lowered to her side, her face composed. The Void point was invisible — already dispersed, whatever she'd done already finished.

"What did you do?" Ash asked.

"Nothing permanent." Her voice was calm, and underneath the calm something was running hot — the rare thing in Sable, the quality of genuine anger that she controlled the way other people controlled fire: completely, with effort. "He'll remember the conversation but not why he stopped pursuing it. The interruption will feel like his own decision."

"You Void-touched a judge."

"I redirected a judge's attention. The technique is documented in third-year Void studies. It's not prohibited."

"It's —" He stopped. The right word was not available. The feeling was specific: the experience of someone doing something reckless and transgressive on your behalf and being unable to decide whether to be grateful or alarmed.

"It was beautiful," Sable said. She was not looking at him. She was looking at the crystal ceiling, where the last traces of his fire-light were fading in the spires, amber going to gold going to the Crystalpeak's default aurora. "The remembrance. I've read about the Ashwalker traditions. I didn't know what they looked like."

"Nobody does. That was the point of suppressing them."

"Yes." She looked at him then. The composed face. The blade-precise attention. Something else underneath both, harder to name, that he'd seen once before — in the roots of the Ironwood, in the dark after the Dungeon, when she'd come up from the sealed door with tears going cold on her face and told them about a boy who had heard light sing.

"Thank you," he said.

"Don't thank me. I'm going to have to justify my technique if the judge reports the incident."

"He won't remember to report it. You just said —"

"He won't remember the impulse. He'll remember the conversation and construct a narrative in which he chose to walk away." A pause. The closest thing to a smile he'd ever seen on her face, which was not a smile but a specific relaxation of the muscles around her eyes. "Void manipulation of short-term directional memory. I tested it three times on Forge students who were being rude to Elio. It works."

He looked at her. She looked at him.

In the Great Hall, three hundred students were returning to conversation, the display already being absorbed into the evening's general atmosphere. Elio was still on the eastern shelf, his instrument across his knees, watching the two of them with the particular attention that meant his Heart Gate was picking up something he found significant and was choosing not to name it.

"The Ashwalkers carried fire for two hundred years," Ash said. "Across the steppes, in the cold centuries after the Sealing War. They weren't channelers. Most of them didn't have Gate affinity. They just had fire, and they walked, and they kept it alive because the settlements needed it to survive."

"I know the history."

"The history is wrong. The history says they were suppressed because their techniques were dangerous. They weren't dangerous. They were free. The Forge couldn't control something that didn't need a Gate to work." He paused. "My mother was the last one. She taught me the walk before she left. I was four."

Sable's expression didn't change. But the quality of her stillness did — the difference between performed composure and genuine attention, which was a difference he'd learned to read in her the way she read archival footnotes: by the space between the words.

"Your mother was an Ashwalker," she said.

"She was the last Ashwalker. She left me the walk and a note that said *I'm sorry* and I haven't seen her since." He said it flat. Not because it was flat inside — because the alternative was letting the fire come up, and the fire in the Great Hall had already been sufficient for one evening.

"The note in your pocket," Sable said.

He looked at her sharply. He had never told anyone about the note.

"You touch it," she said. "When you're thinking about something that matters. I've been watching you do it since the Ironwood."

The Great Hall was warm and bright and full of three hundred students who had just watched an Ashwalker remembrance for the first time in a century and were trying to decide what it meant. Ash stood in the middle of it with Sable beside him and felt, for the second time since the sealed door, the particular vertigo of being known.

Not assessed. Not categorized. Known — the way the door had known him, the way Embera knew him, the way the fire in his chest had stopped being a weapon and started being a hearth.

"Don't tell the others," he said.

"About the note?"

"About any of it."

She looked at him. The blade-attention, the precise focus of someone who was deciding, in real time, how much honesty the moment could hold.

"I won't volunteer it," she said. "But Elio already knows. He felt the remembrance. He felt what it cost you." A pause. "He played the melody because he could tell you needed the framework."

Ash looked at Elio, still sitting on the eastern shelf, instrument across his knees. Elio met his gaze. Nodded — brief, the same quality as Kael's nod in the outer court. Sufficient. Not insufficient.

Ash reached into his pocket and touched the note. Old paper, worn to fabric softness by years of handling, his mother's handwriting preserved in the creases.

*I'm sorry.*

He wasn't, anymore. Not about the fire. Not about the walk. Not about standing in the center of a crystal hall and doing the thing his mother had given him the only way it could be done: freely, without permission, without apology.

He drank the rest of the weak cider and went to find Kael, because Kael would not ask about what had just happened, and sometimes the best thing a person could do was be the one who didn't ask.
