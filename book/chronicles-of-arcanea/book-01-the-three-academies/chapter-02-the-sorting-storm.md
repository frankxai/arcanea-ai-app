# Chapter Two: Three Roads

## i. Mira — The Abyssal Athenaeum

Every raindrop had a voice, and every voice was someone else's memory, and Mira was drowning in all of them.

Not in water. The water was fine. She'd grown up on water — salt smell, bilge stink, hull timber creaking under her bare feet — and her body had no fear of waves. What drowned her was the rain hammering the submersible's outer hull three hours out from the coast, each drop carrying something that didn't belong to her.

*—the net coming up empty four mornings straight—*
*—her face when she said it, the way she said it, seven years ago and still—*
*—something about to happen, I can always feel when something's about to happen—*

The pilot — a man named Torreval who had ferried recruits to the Athenaeum for eleven years — handed her a folded cloth without looking up from the controls. "Put this over your ears," he said. "Doesn't stop it but it muffles."

She pressed the cloth over both ears with both hands and breathed through her mouth and stared at the water rolling past the porthole and waited for the submersible to descend below the rain layer into the deep quiet where other people's memories stopped falling on her head.

The quiet arrived like something physical — a pressure change, then absence. She exhaled and let her hands fall.

She was seventeen years old and she was so tired of other people's feelings that she was willing to sink to the ocean floor to get away from them.

The Athenaeum appeared in the porthole like a dream she'd had before but never finished. Bioluminescent coral cathedrals, domed structures larger than anything she'd seen on land, lit from within by something alive and patient. The colors ran deep blue to green to a kind of silver that wasn't quite light, wasn't quite water, but lived in the gap between. Fish moved through the spaces between buildings in schools so dense they rippled like cloth.

Beautiful. Undeniably.

She hated that. She'd been planning to hate everything about this.

The submersible docked. A young woman in a blue academic robe extended a hand to help her up the steps, and Mira took it before she thought, and —

A wash. Not a memory. Just emotion, bright and sincere: *this one's going to be interesting.*

The woman smiled as if she knew exactly what had just happened.

"Welcome to the Athenaeum," she said. "I'm Wren. I process new arrivals." A pause. "You'll want to know — the buildings are stone, not water. You won't feel anything from the walls."

"How did you know I was—"

"You grabbed my hand like it was a test you didn't mean to take. Flow-touched do that." She said it without judgment. "Come on. Orientation doesn't wait."

Mira followed her into the coral cathedral and let the deep-water silence settle around her like something earned.

She wasn't here to learn to use this thing she'd been given.

She was here to make it stop.

She hadn't told anyone. She suspected, looking at the calm faces of the students they passed in the corridor, that she was the only one who felt that way.

She'd deal with that later.

## ii. Ash — The Draconis Forge

He'd told himself he wasn't afraid of fire.

He'd lived in the Ember Quarter for six years. Slept in alleys where trash fires were the only heat you got. Lit them himself — dozens of times — scraps of wood and old paper and once a captain of the city watch's patrol schedule, which had been very satisfying to burn.

None of that prepared him for Mount Pyralis.

The mountain was *alive* in a way geology didn't usually mean. Heat waves rose from fissures in the black rock path. The air smelled of sulfur and something else beneath it — something almost sweet, like charcoal burning slow — and the mountain itself made sounds. Deep sounds below hearing, more feeling than noise, rhythmic and patient as breathing.

Ash climbed the path with seventeen other new students and tried to look like none of it was affecting him.

He was sixteen. He'd been arrested twice, never successfully, on charges that had ranged from theft to arson to "incitement of civil disorder," which was the official term for what happened when you set the garrison on fire during your own attempted arrest. He'd been offered a choice between execution and the Draconis Forge by a magistrate who had clearly found the entire situation embarrassing and wanted it resolved before the city watch filed any more contradictory reports about what exactly had come out of his hands.

"Not a charitable gift," the magistrate had said carefully. "Consider it a conscription."

He'd considered it survival and taken the papers before she changed her mind.

Now he watched the other students — clean clothes, straight backs, the easy confidence of people who had grown up knowing they were going somewhere — and understood that he was the only one who hadn't chosen this freely.

The gate to the Forge was set directly into the volcano face. Obsidian, rough-cut, enormous — not decorative. Functional. Built to say exactly one thing about what waited inside. An instructor stood in the opening. Tall woman, close-cropped hair, burns across the left side of her jaw that she hadn't covered or corrected, which itself told him something.

"Welcome to the Draconis Forge," she said. Her voice carried without effort. "My name is Commander Voss. You will not like me for the first three months. By the sixth month, most of you will have decided I saved your life. The ones who still don't like me by the end of year one are usually my favorite students."

She looked at the group the way a smith looked at raw stock.

When her eyes reached Ash her gaze stopped for one second before moving on. Catalogued. Filed. He kept his face flat and waited.

"You've been brought here for one reason," Voss continued. "Control. Not power — power you already have. Control. Uncontrolled fire burns everything, including the person it came from. You are here to learn to be the one who decides what burns."

She turned and walked into the mountain and everyone followed.

The obsidian corridors were lit by rivers of magma running in channels cut into the walls, and the heat was extraordinary but tolerable, and Ash walked through it with his hands loose at his sides and thought: *I have been cold for six years. I can do hot.*

The corridor opened into a hall that made him stop walking.

A training arena suspended over a lava lake. Not metaphorically. The floor was a lattice of black iron and stone, and through the gaps you could see forty feet down to the slow crawl of magma below — orange-white, the heat rising in visible waves that warped the air above the lattice and made the far walls shimmer. The smell hit him next: sulfur and hot iron and something sweeter underneath, like burned sugar, like the inside of a forge when the metal finally takes the shape you asked for. Other students moved on bridges connecting the outer wall to training platforms. Two instructors at the far end shaped sheets of fire between their hands — controlled, precise, held in form like someone sculpting with heat.

He'd never seen anything like it.

He'd done fire. He'd felt it pour out of his hands in the garrison, wild and terrified and furious, and he'd run before he could see what it had done. The city watch reports had been detailed enough that he'd learned anyway. He'd lived with that knowledge coiled in his chest for three years like something swallowed wrong.

This was the opposite of that.

This was fire being *answered.*

He didn't know he'd been afraid to see that until the fear left him.

"Brenn?" The instructor from the gate — Voss — had doubled back to where he stood. She didn't ask if he was all right. "First platforms are to the right. Fall in."

"Yes sir," Ash said, which he'd never said to anyone in his life and surprised himself with now.

Voss looked at him with an expression he couldn't categorize. "Where are you from originally?"

"Ember Quarter. Capital."

"The garrison incident."

"I don't know what you're referring to," he said automatically.

"Right." She looked back at the arena. "That fire in the report — completely uncontrolled, manifested under threat. But the garrison wall fell inward. Not outward. Whatever you did, it was precise enough not to kill the officers standing on the outside." She glanced at him. "Most uncontrolled manifestations don't discriminate."

He said nothing. He'd never told anyone that — that he'd tried, in the middle of the panic, to push it *away* from the people on the outside of the wall. He hadn't known if it had worked. He'd run before he could see.

"Fall in," Voss said again, and walked away.

He walked to the right-hand platform.

He had not chosen to be here. But he was starting to think there was a difference between a choice you made freely and a choice you made correctly, and that sometimes a thing pushed you in the right direction before you were ready to go.

## iii. Sable — The Luminary

She'd done her research.

The Luminary's intake records, where they were publicly available, showed forty-two students admitted in the past five years with Void affinity. Of those, thirty-eight had transferred to the Draconis Forge within their first year. Three had withdrawn from all Academy training entirely. One had remained.

She'd found the one who remained and written her a letter — a careful, formal inquiry. She'd gotten back a single sentence: *Come if you want. Don't expect me to hold your hand.*

She held that letter now, in the submersible that ferried Luminary students up from the valley floor to the Crystalpeak platform, and studied the handwriting. Precise. A little aggressive in the ascenders. Someone who had learned that people made up their minds about her early and had stopped trying to change them.

Sable understood this.

She was fifteen — young for Academy admission by four years. The daughter of Archmages Corvin and Dara Naethe, who had opened fourteen Gates between them, published two hundred and seven papers on magical theory, and who had, upon discovering their daughter's Void affinity, become very quiet and very careful in a way that was worse than shouting.

She'd read their published work on Void magic while they were deciding how to feel about her. The paper was titled "Shadow Contamination in Void-Adjacent Affinities: Risk Assessment." They'd published it before she was born. She'd read it three times, looking for something reassuring, and found instead a very thorough academic argument for why people like her were dangerous.

She'd underlined three passages and left the paper on the dining table and never mentioned it. Neither had they.

The Crystalpeak rose through the cloud layer ahead. Sable looked at it with the same flat attention she brought to most things. No awe. Something more like recognition — she'd read about this place so thoroughly that arriving felt like finishing a sentence she'd started months ago.

Light refracted through the crystal spires in colors that shifted like living things. The architecture was extraordinary. Her parents would have loved it.

She was not here for that. She was here to prove their paper wrong.

The intake hall was all crystal and natural light and the organized chaos of sixty students arriving simultaneously. She stood in the entry with her bag and waited for someone to direct her, and that was when she noticed the boy who was looking at the architecture.

He was looking at it the way people looked at things they'd been afraid they wouldn't get to see. His face was open in a way that reminded her, briefly and uncomfortably, of what it felt like before she'd learned to keep things inside. Dust on his boots. Altitude-redness in his cheeks. Working clothes, not travel clothes. He'd climbed the mountain on foot, and he was clearly not the sort of person who usually came to the Luminary.

He was also standing directly in the path of a luggage cart that was moving toward him from behind.

She stepped forward. "Move left."

He stepped left. The cart rolled through the space he'd been standing in. He looked at the cart, then at her.

"Thanks," he said.

She nodded and looked away. Found the intake desk. Gave her name. Was handed a schedule and a room assignment and a note that House Nero orientation was scheduled separately from the main Houses.

House Nero. Void students, separated out.

She'd expected it. She filed the irritation into the correct internal drawer and followed the directions to her room, and thought of the letter from the one student who'd stayed, and its single sentence, and decided that tomorrow she would find her.

She needed someone who understood the arithmetic of being feared for what you were born with.

## iv. Elio — Somewhere Between

No one had come for him.

He wanted to be clear about that, in his own mind, because clarity was important when everything else was uncertain. The other Gate-touched had recruiters. He'd heard about them from the three Academy assessors who'd come to his house in the coastal town of Merefall over the past two months and left without making him an offer. He'd overheard one of them speaking to his mother outside the door — *Heart Gate, unusually strong, but the stabilization requirements* — and then her voice dropping, and his mother's voice rising the way it did when someone was telling her no with a smile on.

He was eighteen. His gift had come in at fourteen, slow and flooding. Nothing dramatic. Just a morning when he woke up and every person in the house was inside him — his mother's grief like a stone in his chest, his brother's excitement like sparks in his fingers, the neighbor two doors down having a very bad week about money, the schoolteacher down the lane who was in love with someone who'd left, the whole layered human weight of a town of four hundred people pressing on him from all sides. No seam between where he ended and they began.

He'd learned to manage it. Mostly. Crowds were harder. Rain was easier — the sound broke whatever frequency he received on. He played music when it got bad; the instrument gave his hands something to do and the music gave his attention somewhere to rest.

He was a good musician. He'd been told he was extraordinary. He'd also been told, by three Academy assessors, that his affinity was too strong for the standard track and too unstable for the advanced track and that they were very sorry.

So he'd packed his own bag. Written to the Luminary directly — a careful letter explaining what he could and couldn't do — and received back a provisional invitation to present himself for a second assessment. Not admission. Assessment.

He'd come anyway because sitting at home was not going to resolve anything, and because the alternative was spending the rest of his life in Merefall slowly absorbing every feeling the town had ever had until there was no room left in him for his own.

The Crystalpeak intake hall was full of people feeling things. He stood in the entry and breathed carefully and reminded himself that their feelings were not his emergency.

The assessor who met him was a small elderly man whose energy was the tidiest Elio had ever encountered — everything filed, nothing loose. He introduced himself as Senior Assessor Polm, shook Elio's hand with a firm grip, and made a note on his clipboard.

"You wrote that you can't complete a standard intake," Polm said.

"The medical form asks for basic empathic control demonstrations," Elio said. "In a room with sixty other students, I won't be able to control what I'm receiving long enough to demonstrate what I'm sending."

Polm noted this. "The Draconian Academy's assessment concluded the Heart Gate presents an instability risk."

"Yes."

"The Athenaeum's assessment concluded you'd need to be housed separately from all other students due to contamination risk."

"Also yes."

"And the Luminary's preliminary assessment—"

"Said they'd look again," Elio said. "So I'm here for them to look again."

Polm studied him for a long moment. The assessor's feelings were very quiet — practiced stillness, decades of it, like a room where someone had spent years closing all the windows. Elio found it restful.

"You're not frightened," Polm said.

"I'm terrified," Elio said. "I'm just not going to let that be the deciding factor."

Polm made another note. "Come with me. We'll put you in the quieter intake room." He paused. "Master Toren will want to see you specifically. His schedule is..." A glance at the clipboard. "He has a gap tomorrow morning."

The name landed flat; Elio didn't know who Master Toren was. He followed the assessor through the crystal corridors, the feelings of sixty other new students pressing at him from all sides. He held himself separate from each one. Exhausting work, the kind that left no marks.

He found the quiet room. He sat down. The door closed.

In the silence — relative silence, the deep low hum of people's feelings still there but muffled — he opened the case he'd carried up the mountain and touched the strings of his instrument. Not playing. Just contact.

He'd played music since he was six years old. His mother had taught him the first chords. He'd kept playing because it was the one thing that was unambiguously his — not other people's feelings bleeding through him, not someone else's grief wearing his face, but his own hands making his own sound.

He plucked one string. The note moved through the crystal room and came back transformed, richer, harmonics added by the architecture.

He didn't know if there was a place for him here. He didn't know if any Academy wanted what he was. But he was nineteen years old and very good at sitting with uncertainty, because his whole life had been uncertainty, and you either learned to live there or you spent all your energy trying to leave and never managed.

He plucked another string.

The room listened.

Tomorrow he would find out if one instructor's opinion could change what three Academies had already decided.

He had come too far to be afraid of one more no.

But somewhere beneath the practiced steadiness, beneath the careful management, something sat that was entirely his own. Uncontaminated. Original. The stubborn, unreasonable conviction that this time would be different.

He didn't look at it directly. Hope worked better as fuel than as a subject of study.

He played until the room went dark.
