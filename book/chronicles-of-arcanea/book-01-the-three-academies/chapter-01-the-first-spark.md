# Chapter One: Ironhold

The stone moved before he touched it.

Kael had his hammer raised, ready to drive the wedge into the fault line he'd found along the granite face, when the entire block shuddered — a deep interior trembling, as if the mountain had dreamed something terrible and woken mid-fall. He stepped back. The wedge dropped from his other hand and clattered down the slope.

The stone moved again.

Not the mountain. Not a rockslide. This block — the one he'd been hired to split for Master Aldric's hearth surround — vibrating at a frequency he felt in his molars and behind his eyes. He pressed his palm against it and snatched the hand back. Hot. Not fire-hot. Something older, deeper, like the heat at the center of things.

"Kael." His father's voice from the workshop below. "I need you down here before dark."

He stared at the stone. It had gone still. He told himself the trembling had been something settling, some underground water moving, the ordinary business of mountains. He told himself this because the alternative — that something had come from *him*, from the rage he'd been swallowing all day since Master Aldric called his work rough and graceless and good enough only for field walls — was a thought he didn't know what to do with.

He picked up his hammer and went down.

---

The argument with his father lasted through dinner and into the cool dark of the evening. He couldn't remember afterward how it had started — something about the Aldric commission, about Kael wasting another morning on the upper quarry face when there was real work to be done in the forge — but he remembered how it had felt. Like wearing a shirt two sizes too small. Like holding his breath in a room where all the air had already been used.

He went out to the wall behind the workshop to breathe.

The village of Stonethatch sat in a fold of the Veil Mountains, high enough that the clouds sometimes came in below the treeline, low enough that they still got real winters. Forty-three people. Three families that had been here for generations: the Var family, the Aldrics, and the Brennas — Kael's family. Everyone knew everyone's business. Everyone had a clear idea of what you were supposed to become.

Kael sat on the low stone wall that divided the workshop yard from the goat pen and looked up at the mountain. The Crystalpeak was up there somewhere, lost in weather or too high to see from here — he'd heard about it his whole life the way you hear about things that exist in the same category as dragons and sea serpents. Real, technically. Far away. Belonging to a different order of existence than goats and hammer handles and ungracious granite blocks.

He wasn't angry anymore. The anger had burned through during the walk and left something rawer behind it.

He was nineteen years old and he was very tired of already knowing what he was.

The wall began to hum.

Not the vibration from before — that had been subtle, possible to explain away. This was audible. A resonance moving through the wall under his hands, through the ground under his boots, up through his spine. Every stone in the wall. Every stone in the workshop foundation. He stood up and it followed him, the hum intensifying, and he could feel it now in his chest like a second heartbeat that didn't know its own rhythm yet.

"Stop," he said, which was a stupid thing to say to a feeling.

The wall cracked.

Not a hairline fracture. Not the kind of crack that happens naturally over winter and spring. A split that ran the full length of the wall in both directions from where he'd been sitting, clean and fast as a knife drawn across cloth, and then two of the foundation stones on the workshop's eastern side popped free of the mortar like teeth knocked loose, and the eastern wall began to lean.

Kael ran toward it — some instinct to brace it, to hold it, to stop what was happening — and as his hands hit the stone he felt it clearly for the first time. The energy moving through the rock. Not heat anymore. Pressure. Like every stone wanted to find its original form, its uncut shape, and the mortar and the careful work of his father's hands and his grandfather's hands was a temporary arrangement that the stone had only agreed to provisionally.

He pushed back against it. The feeling surged. He shoved it down somewhere deep and the wall shuddered and groaned and came to rest, leaning eight inches off true but no longer falling.

His hands were shaking.

Down the lane, a light appeared in the Var family's window. Then a door opened. Then shouting.

Because the workshop wall was only the beginning.

---

Six walls cracked that night. The old mill foundation developed three new fissures. The stone path running through the center of Stonethatch heaved up in a ragged line as if something were pushing from below. Kael stood in the middle of it, in the center of the village, and watched forty-three people come out of their homes with lanterns and looks on their faces that he would remember for the rest of his life.

Not anger. Not yet. Something worse.

*Recognition.*

His father came last. Stood in the doorway of their home looking at the cracked lane and the tilting walls and then at his son, and Kael waited for him to say something that made sense of it. To name it. To tell him what he was supposed to do.

His father said: "Go inside."

He meant it as protection. Kael understood that, later. At the time it felt like the door closing on a room he'd never been allowed to leave.

He sat on the cracked lane until well past midnight, when the woman arrived.

---

She came down the mountain path on a horse that didn't breathe hard, which was the first strange thing about her. The second strange thing was that she was wearing summer clothes in the cold, a loose shirt and riding leathers, as if the mountain air had no opinion about her. The third strange thing was that she'd clearly known exactly where to come.

She dismounted in the center of the village, looked at the cracked lane and the tilting wall and Kael sitting in the middle of it all, and said: "Foundation. Thought so. You weren't even trying, were you?"

"I was trying to stop it," Kael said.

"Right, but before that. Something made you angry."

He didn't answer. She crouched down beside the crack in the lane and pressed her fingers into it with the matter-of-fact air of someone reading a letter they'd been expecting.

"My name is Sera," she said. "I'm a Luminary recruiter. I've been monitoring the ley lines in this valley for the past three weeks because they've been doing something unusual and ley lines don't do unusual things without a reason." She looked up at him. "You're the reason."

"I broke six walls."

"You didn't break them. You asked them to become something else and they didn't know how to refuse." She straightened. "The Foundation Gate doesn't give you power over stone. It doesn't give you anything. It opens you to what was already there — the deep language the earth speaks to itself. The problem is that language is very old and very loud and you don't have any training in how to listen to it without letting it come through you at full volume."

She said all of this the way she might explain a weather pattern. No alarm in her voice. No pity either, which Kael appreciated more than he could have explained.

"What happens if I don't get training?" he asked.

She paused. Just long enough.

"The Gate-touched who don't learn to manage their affinity become Unraveled," she said. "It's a slow process, usually. The energy keeps pushing through you, looking for release. Your dreams start affecting the physical world. Then your waking moments. The stone around you fractures. Walls fall. Eventually—" She stopped. "Eventually you become a catastrophe that moves through the world, and then you stop."

Kael looked at the cracked lane under his hands. Cold stone. His knees ached where they pressed against it, and the ache felt honest — the only honest thing left in the night.

"The Luminary can teach you to hear that voice without letting it come through unchecked," Sera said. "That's what the Academies do. Not make you powerful. Make you safe to be near yourself."

He laughed. It came out wrong — not funny, just pressure releasing.

"Safe to be near myself," he repeated.

"It's a reasonable first goal."

Behind him, he heard his father step out of the house. He heard Sera turn and say, quietly, that she needed to speak with his parents, that there were arrangements to be made, papers to review, that the Luminary had provisions for families in Kael's situation. He didn't listen. He looked up at the mountain.

The Crystalpeak. He'd heard that on clear days you could see it from the high passes — a crystalline structure catching light above the cloud layer, throwing aurora colors across the sky like a permanent dawn. He'd never been high enough to see it. He'd never had a reason.

---

They gave him three days to prepare.

He used one of them to go back to the quarry and look at the granite block he'd been working when it started. It was split now, not by his wedge but along a dozen natural fault lines, as if it had shattered from inside. He ran his hand along one of the fractures. Smooth as glass, which granite never was after a split. Like the stone had wanted to come apart cleanly.

He used another day to help his father patch the eastern wall. They worked without speaking, which was not unusual, but there was a different quality to the silence. His father passed him stones. He fitted them back into the mortar. The wall came back to plumb. At some point his father's hand rested briefly on his shoulder and then lifted, and that was all there was.

He used the third day walking the pasture behind the house, the low meadow where he used to go when he was small and everything was too loud. He sat in the grass and tried to feel what Sera had described — the deep language, the constant conversation between stones. He couldn't hear it clearly. It was more like pressure, a low undercurrent, something that had always been present and he'd mistaken for the ordinary feeling of ground under his feet.

He thought: *I have been standing on top of this my whole life and never known.*

He packed light. Tools, mostly — force of habit. Three changes of clothes. A small drawing his mother had made of the family when he was six, before she died, that he'd kept folded in the same pocket for thirteen years. His father stood in the door of the house while he tied the pack.

"Write," his father said.

"I will."

"When you've learned what you need to learn, come back."

This was the most his father had said about it. Kael turned and looked at him — the same angular face he saw in his own reflection, the same shoulders built for carrying stone, the eyes that never quite landed anywhere. He felt the whole complicated mass of being this man's son: everything given, nothing easy, love expressed through kept roofs and steady work and absolutely no acknowledgment that any of it was love.

"I'll come back," Kael said.

He walked north up the mountain path as the sun broke through the cloud layer, and around the second turn he stopped and looked back. The village was small below him. His father had gone inside already. The eastern wall stood straight. The lane was still cracked but someone had swept the loose gravel to the sides, which was very much like the people of Stonethatch — you clean around the break and you keep walking.

He kept walking.

---

The Crystalpeak appeared above the clouds at midafternoon.

He'd expected to be prepared for it. He'd heard descriptions his whole life, and he thought he'd built an adequate mental image: a building made of crystal, large, catching the light. Academic. Distant.

He was not prepared.

It rose from the cloud layer like something that had always been there and the clouds were the recent addition. Crystal spires — not one but dozens, different heights and angles, catching the late sun and splitting it into aurora colors that moved and shifted across the snow and the cliff faces below. The light was not reflected but *transformed*. What entered as ordinary afternoon sun left as something older and stranger, colors he didn't have names for, colors that pressed against his skin like warm air from an open door.

He stopped walking. His pack straps cut his shoulders. Wind moved across the cliff face and tasted of cold metal and altitude and something faintly sweet he couldn't place — crystal dust, maybe, or the particular chemistry of air that had passed through whatever those spires were made of. Below, the clouds moved in slow rotation around the mountain's base, and the aurora light played across them in bands of violet and green and a gold so deep it was nearly red.

He stood there a long time. The cold didn't touch him. He forgot to breathe, remembered, breathed.

He'd thought, somewhere in the back of his mind, that he was going to a school. Teachers and schedules and the daily small indignities of learning things you hadn't chosen to learn.

The Crystalpeak was not a school.

Whatever was up there, it had been built by people who believed completely in what they were doing. People who looked at a mountain and thought: not a barrier. A foundation. And who put something extraordinary on top of it.

Kael shifted his pack. The straps bit his shoulders and the cold pressed against his face and neither thing mattered because up there, above the clouds, someone had built light into stone and it was still burning.

He started up the last stretch of path. Behind him, Stonethatch disappeared into the valley shadow, smaller than he'd ever seen it, his whole life rearranged into the category of *before*.

He didn't know what came next.

The not-knowing felt clean.
