# Chapter Eleven: The Registry's Price

## KAEL

They arrived on a Tuesday, which felt wrong in a way he could not immediately articulate. A Tuesday was a day for lectures and practicum and the particular rhythm of the Luminary's weekly schedule — the rhythm he had been settling into for seven months, the rhythm that had become his architecture the way the stonework of Ironhold had been his architecture before this. Tuesdays were ordered. They were structural.

The Registry team came through the Luminary's main gate at the ninth bell, and the order of the day broke.

There were four of them. Three wore the blue coat with the silver sigil. The fourth wore civilian clothes and carried a leather case that held, Kael would learn later, the forms. The forms were the point. The coats were the frame. The civilian was the one who mattered because the civilian was the one who sat across from you and asked the questions and wrote down your answers and made you into a line on a list.

He watched from the refectory window. He was eating breakfast — or rather, he was sitting in front of breakfast, which was porridge, which was good, which he had not touched because Sable's most recent letter was in his pocket and the contents of it had made food feel irrelevant.

*The prophecy is not a prophecy. It is a score. A set of instructions for a harmonic that requires fragments of crystallized Eldrian sound — the Vael Rain — to remember their original frequency. The inscription says "the door that was never locked will open." I believe the door is the threshold Malachar tried to force. I believe the Eleventh Frequency is not something to be reached but something to be remembered. I believe the Gate system is not the system. It is the approach.*

He had read this four times. He understood it in the way he understood load-bearing calculations: the structure was visible, the forces were traceable, the conclusion followed from the evidence. What he did not understand was what to do with it. What action followed from knowledge this large. He had been trained — by his father, by Ironhold, by the Luminary — to understand structural problems as things to be solved. You identified the load. You identified the weakness. You built the support.

This was not a structural problem. This was a structural revelation. The building was not failing. The building was not what anyone had thought it was.

He watched the Registry team cross the courtyard. They moved with the efficient purposefulness of people who had done this before, at other Academies, and had a procedure. The lead Enforcer — a woman, mid-thirties, with the posture of someone who had been military before she was institutional — spoke briefly to the Luminary's gate-warden and was admitted without delay.

Headmaster Vael had approved the visit. That was the word on the corridor — *approved,* as if the Headmaster had been given a meaningful choice, as if there existed a version of events in which the Luminary's Headmaster said *no* to a Joint Council mandate and the mandate went away.

He left his porridge untouched and went to find the interviews.

---

They were in the East Hall. The civilian had set up at a long table with the leather case open and the forms arranged in a stack and a crystal-tipped stylus that recorded the harmonic signature of the person sitting across from it. The forms were printed. The checkboxes were printed. The only handwritten parts were the answers, which the civilian transcribed in a small precise script that Kael, from the doorway, could not read.

The first student was a girl named Lenna. She was seventeen. Her Foundation Gate had manifested eight months ago during an earthquake that hit her village — not caused by the earthquake, triggered by it. The earth had moved and something in Lenna had moved with it and she had been at the Luminary since, learning to distinguish between her own stability and the earth's, which was a subtler problem than it sounded.

Lenna sat across from the civilian and the civilian said: "Full name."

"Lenna Graystone."

The stylus recorded.

"Place of origin."

"Thornberry. The village."

"Gate affinity or affinities."

"Foundation. Just Foundation."

The civilian wrote. The stylus recorded. "Academy enrollment status."

"First-year. Luminary."

"Current place of residence."

"The Luminary. Room thirty-seven, East Wing."

The civilian looked up. "Any incidents of uncontrolled manifestation since enrollment?"

Lenna's hands were on the table. Kael watched them — the particular tension, the fingers not quite making fists. "One. In the first week. During orientation, there was a — the building shifted. Just a small amount. I didn't — "

"Was it reported?"

"I reported it to Instructor Penris. He said it was within parameters for a new student."

The civilian wrote something. The checkbox he marked was not the one Lenna would have chosen. Kael could see this from the doorway — the civilian's pen moving to a box near the bottom of the form, in a section labeled with text too small to read, and Lenna watching the pen move with the expression of someone watching a decision being made about them that they had no ability to influence.

"Any family members with Gate affinity?"

"My mother. Unregistered. She — she doesn't use it. She grew up before the — she didn't have training."

The civilian's pen paused. "Your mother is Gate-touched and unregistered?"

"She's — it's very minor. She can feel when the ground is going to shift. It's not — people in the village call it intuition."

"I'll need her name for the family registry supplement."

Kael watched Lenna's hands. The tension had gone past holding. She was gripping the table's edge now, not visibly but with the internal force of someone who was trying very hard not to let the ground move.

"She's sixty-three," Lenna said.

"The registration requirement applies to all Gate-touched individuals regardless of age." The civilian's voice was patient. Not unkind. The patience of someone who had said this sentence many times and would say it many more times today. "Her name."

Lenna gave her mother's name. The civilian wrote it. The stylus recorded.

Kael stood in the doorway and watched three more interviews. He watched a boy with a Voice Gate — fifteen, painfully shy, his truth-speaking so new that he could not yet distinguish between what needed to be said and what the Gate simply expelled — sit across from the civilian and answer every question with an honesty so raw it felt like watching someone undress. He watched a dual-affinity student — Earth and Sight, one of the rarest combinations — have her Gate frequencies measured by the stylus and see the civilian write a number on the form that classified her power level on a scale she had not been told existed.

The power scale had six levels. The civilian did not explain the levels. The student did not ask. The number went on the form and the form went in the stack and the student went back to her corridor and the civilian took the next name on the list.

He watched this for an hour.

He was not learning anything he did not already know. He had read the Registry Act. He had read Sable's analysis of its methodology. He had read Mira's formal inquiry to the Council liaison, which had been answered with a response so carefully non-specific that it constituted its own kind of answer. He knew the forms existed and the checkboxes existed and the power scale existed.

What he had not known was the sound.

The sound of a seventeen-year-old giving her mother's name to a stranger with a stylus. The sound of a fifteen-year-old's voice breaking on a truth he had not chosen to speak. The sound of a checkbox being marked in a section of a form that the person the form was about could not read.

The sound of a system turning people into data with the quiet efficiency of something that had been designed by reasonable people to solve a real problem and had, in the solving, become the problem it was supposed to prevent.

---

He found the lead Enforcer during the midday break.

She was in the refectory, eating the same porridge he had not eaten that morning. She ate the way institutional people ate — efficiently, without attention, the meal as fuel rather than experience. She looked up when he sat across from her.

"Thornfield," she said. She knew his name. He was on a list somewhere already — Vael's report, or the Shadowwatch alert, or simply the Academy's student rolls, which the Registry had access to.

"I have a question," he said.

"Go ahead."

"The power scale. On the forms. What are the levels?"

She looked at him. Not hostile. Evaluating. "The classification system is internal to the Registry's assessment methodology."

"The students whose power you're classifying don't know the scale. They don't know what the numbers mean. They don't know what follows from a high number versus a low number." He kept his voice level. This was the voice he used for structural analysis: precise, load-bearing, each word placed where it could hold the most. "I'm asking you to tell me what the numbers mean."

She set down her spoon. "Level one through three are standard Gate manifestation. No enhanced monitoring required. Level four is elevated — above-average power, faster-than-typical progression. Enhanced monitoring means quarterly check-ins with the assigned liaison."

"And five and six?"

She was quiet for a moment. "Level five triggers active monitoring. Regular assessment intervals. Restrictions on unsupervised Gate use outside Academy grounds."

"Restrictions."

"Safety measures."

"What's six?"

Another pause. Longer. She picked up her spoon and put it down again, a gesture that had nothing to do with the porridge. "Level six is a referral to the Oversight Committee for individualized assessment. The Committee determines appropriate measures."

"Appropriate measures." He heard the words and felt them land in his body the way a stone landed in a foundation — settling, becoming part of the structure, impossible to remove without removing the structure around it. "That's the language from the Act. *Other measures as determined by the Council.* The undefined measures."

"I don't determine the measures, Thornfield. I administer the assessments."

"I know." He looked at her. He did not look away. Foundation was not about building alone — it was about seeing load, about understanding where weight was distributed and where it was concentrated and where the structure would give if you pressed. "You're not the enemy. The Unraveled are. If I have a better system, I should build it."

She looked at him with a sharpness that had nothing to do with hostility and everything to do with recognition. "You're quoting me."

"You said that. To someone. I heard it in the corridor."

"I said it because it's true." She leaned forward slightly. "I've seen three Unraveling incidents this year. I was in the radius for two of them. One of them was a child — a nine-year-old boy whose Fire Gate opened in a schoolroom and burned six desks and a teacher's hand before anyone could contain it. The teacher lost the use of three fingers." She looked at him steadily. "I don't enjoy the forms. I don't enjoy the power scale. I don't enjoy telling a girl to give me her mother's name so a sixty-three-year-old woman in a village can be put on a list. But the alternative is more schoolrooms and more burned hands and more children who didn't ask for any of this and don't have anyone who can help them."

He sat with it.

She was not wrong. That was the thing that made the forms and the checkboxes and the power scale so difficult to argue against — the reasoning was sound. The problem was real. The Gate-Touched were manifesting at a rate that exceeded any Academy's capacity to respond. The Unraveling incidents were increasing. People were being hurt. Children were being hurt. The Registry was a system designed to find the people who needed help before the help became too late.

And the system turned people into data. And the data could be used for things that the people who became it had no ability to foresee or prevent. And the checkboxes had six levels and the sixth level meant a referral to a Committee whose measures were undefined.

He did not have a better system. She was right about that.

"Thank you," he said. He stood. He walked out of the refectory.

He did not have a better system. He was twenty years old and he carried Ironhold in his bones and the Foundation Gate in his hands and he did not have a better system and the absence of one felt, for the first time in his life, like a structural failure that was his.

---

That night he sat in his alcove and he did not write letters.

He sat with the quiet. The Luminary at night was a different building — the aurora-light dimmed, the crystal corridors settling into the softer frequencies of the evening cycle, the whole structure breathing in the way that buildings breathed when the people inside them were still. He could feel the building. His Foundation Gate was open and it felt the Luminary the way it always felt structures: reading the load, the distribution, the places where weight was carried and the places where it was not.

The building was sound. It would stand.

He was not thinking about the building.

He was thinking about Lenna's hands on the table. About the fifteen-year-old's voice breaking. About a civilian with a stylus transcribing trauma into checkboxes. About a Enforcer who had watched a child burn a teacher's hand and had decided that forms and power scales were the answer because there was no better answer available.

He was thinking about Sable's letter. *The door that was never locked will open.*

He was thinking about the difference between a system that contained and a system that connected. The Registry was containment: find them, classify them, monitor them, restrict the ones who might be dangerous. The Eldrian Assemblies had been connection: gather them, attune them, let them hold the harmony together, trust the collective to maintain what no individual could maintain alone.

One system had lasted three thousand years. The other was twenty-seven days old and already had six levels and undefined measures.

He put his hands on the stone sill of his alcove window. The stone was real beneath his palms — Luminary granite, quarried from the Crystal Spires, carrying the resonance of centuries of students' Foundation Gates pressing against it. He felt the stone and the stone felt him and they were the same material, ultimately. Carbon and mineral and the patient compression of time into structure.

His eyes filled.

He did not understand why at first. Then he did: the Flow Gate. The second Gate, the one that had been flickering at the edges of his awareness for weeks — not open, not manifesting, just present, like a door with light under it. The Flow Gate was Leyla's domain. Water and emotion. Not separate domains that shared a resonance. The same sense.

He was feeling the building's weight and the students inside it and the fear they were carrying and the forms with their names on them and the power scale with its six levels and the sixty-three-year-old woman in a village whose name was now on a list because her daughter had tried to get help.

He was feeling all of it.

Not with the Foundation Gate's structural awareness. With something else — something liquid, something that ran between the stones rather than through them, that found the cracks and the gaps and the places where the mortar was thin and that filled them. Not fixing. Flowing. Being present in the building's empty spaces the way water was present in old basalt.

He wept.

Not from sadness — or not only from sadness. From the specific overwhelm of a Gate-opening that was not crisis and not power but recognition. The quiet recognition that he could feel what other people were carrying. That the distance between his body and their bodies was less than he had believed. That stone was not only stone. That the building's weight was not only stone and mortar and engineering but also the weight of the people it held, their fear and their hope and their exhaustion and their still, stubborn willingness to be here despite all of it.

His tears hit the stone sill. The stone received them. The Foundation Gate felt the moisture enter the granite's surface and the Flow Gate felt the emotion in the moisture and both sensations were simultaneous and they did not conflict and he sat in the dark with his hands on the sill and he let himself be both things at once.

Foundation and Flow. Earth and water. Structure and feeling.

He did not know how long he sat there. The aurora-light moved through its evening cycle. The building breathed. The students slept, or tried to. Somewhere in the East Hall, the forms were stacked in the leather case, the checkboxes filled, the power scale applied, the list growing.

He could feel all of it.

He wiped his face. He took his hands off the sill. He sat back in his chair and he looked at the ceiling and he breathed.

The Flow Gate had not opened. Not fully. The light under the door was brighter, but the door was still closed. What he had felt was the pressure of it — the leading edge of an opening that would come, when it came, on its own terms.

He was, he realized, not afraid of it.

He had been afraid of everything else — of the Registry, of the forms, of the undefined measures, of the prophecy in the deep chamber, of the accelerating rate of Gate-Touch, of the thirty-day window closing. He had been afraid of all of it because he was a person who carried weight and weight was frightening when you understood what it would do if you dropped it.

But he was not afraid of the second Gate.

The second Gate was the thing that let him feel the weight that other people carried. The thing that connected structure to the people who lived inside it. The thing that would make his Foundation Gate — the gate of stone and endurance and stubborn refusal to fall — into something that was also compassion.

He could work with that.

He picked up his pen. He began writing — not letters this time. Plans. The beginning of a system that was not containment and not forms and not power scales but something else: a structure built around connection. A way of finding Gate-Touched not to classify them but to reach them. A network that did what the Assemblies had done — gathered people together, attuned them to each other, trusted the collective to hold what no individual could hold alone.

He did not know if it would work. He was twenty years old and the thing he was designing was the beginning of an answer to a question he had been asked that afternoon by an Enforcer who had watched a child burn a teacher's hand: *if you have a better system, build it.*

He was building.

The stone beneath his feet held him up. The water at the edges of his awareness held everything else.

He wrote until the morning bell.
