# Chapter One: The Registry

## MIRA

The notice arrived on a morning when the Sapphire Sea lay dead flat — no wind, no breath, the surface so still that the bioluminescent corals beneath it lit the undersides of clouds three hundred feet above, and the whole world became a question about which direction was up.

Mira was in the reading alcove off the third archive level, forty feet below the sea's surface, surrounded by glass and dark water and fish that pressed their noses against the panes with the stubborn curiosity of creatures who had been watching scholars for centuries and were still interested. She had been awake since the third bell. She had three primary source texts open, one cross-reference log, and a cup of something warm that had stopped being warm an hour ago.

The six months since the Hollow Root had been the most productive of her academic life and she was aware this was not a compliment she was paying herself, not entirely. Productivity was what she did when she was afraid. She read. She cross-referenced. She arrived in the archives before they opened and she stayed until the archive duty-scholar turned the lamps down and gave her the look that meant *the building is not technically closed but I need you to know I have noticed you are still here.* She thanked the duty-scholar and left and walked the corridor to her room, and sometimes she walked past her room and continued along the lower observation deck where the bioluminescent jellyfish drifted past the glass in their slow, indifferent constellations, and she stood there and felt the ocean's weight above her and below her and she thought.

She had been trying, for six months, to think correctly about what the Memory Pool had shown her.

Not to think about it — she had no choice about that, the Pool didn't show you something that heavy and then let you file it away. But to think about it correctly: to hold the full shape of what she'd seen without collapsing into certainty, which would be false, or into dismissal, which would be cowardice. The Pool had shown her a possibility. A strong one. She had looked at it honestly and she had said to herself, standing in the water with the future arriving like a tide: *not yet but not never.* That was honest. That was as much as she could honestly commit to.

The problem was that six months of careful honest thinking had brought her, incrementally and against her preferences, to a position that was difficult to argue with. The evidence was accumulating. She was a scholar. Evidence was supposed to accumulate and she was supposed to look at it.

She was looking at it.

The three primary source texts she had open this morning were Leyla's early accounts of Flow-Gate manifestation in unregistered practitioners, cross-referenced against the Athenaeum's Third Age incident records. The pattern she had been tracing for two weeks was not subtle. Unregistered practitioners — people who had never received Academy training — appeared in the historical record with increasing frequency in the decades preceding major disruptions. Not causing the disruptions. Being affected by them. Their power manifesting erratically as if responding to something in the larger field. She had a working theory about the Sealing and ley line saturation that she had not yet put in writing because putting it in writing meant committing to it, and she was not ready to commit to something that alarming without better evidence.

The delivery came by current-tube — the Athenaeum's postal system, a network of pressurized channels that ran through the building's architecture like capillaries, carrying sealed cylinder cases from the main post-office to individual alcoves. The tube made its soft pneumatic sound and deposited a case in the tray beside her.

She did not open it immediately. She had learned, in the past six months, to finish a thought before attending to new information. The thought she was finishing was about Leyla's early accounts and the word *preceding* and what it implied about the current rate of unregistered manifestation, and it was a thought with sharp edges that she wanted to hold correctly before she set it down.

She set it down.

She opened the case.

It was printed on the good parchment — thick, cream-colored, the kind with the Academy watermark pressed into the surface. The letterhead read *The Office of the Academy Council, Joint Session, Third Convocation of the Eighth Age.* She had not seen that letterhead before. She did not know there was a joint session.

She read it twice. The second time slowly.

*The Gate-Touched Registry Act, passed by joint resolution of the Three Academy Councils and ratified by the Shadowwatch Oversight Committee, requires all known Gate-touched individuals to submit to official registration within sixty days of this notice. Registration shall include: full name, place of origin, Gate affinity or affinities, Academy enrollment status, and current place of residence. Registration centers will be established at each Academy and at designated locations in all major population centers. Registered Gate-touched will receive an assigned designation code and an Academy liaison. Failure to register within the sixty-day period will be treated as a violation of the Joint Safety Accord and may result in mandatory Academy placement or other measures as determined by the Council.*

She sat with it.

The fish pressed against the glass outside her alcove. One of them — a long silver thing with trailing fins that she had named, privately, Ondine's Second Cousin — bumped the pane with its mouth and drifted back and bumped it again.

*Mandatory Academy placement or other measures.*

She turned the notice face-down on her desk and walked to the window and looked at the fish, and at the coral beneath them, and at the light coming down through the water in long bent columns from the sea's flat surface above. She put her hands behind her back so they wouldn't touch anything.

The ocean was very quiet at this depth. The Athenaeum was always quiet. The residents moved through it with the held-breath care of people who understood that memory was everywhere in the water and that noise disturbed it.

She was thinking clearly. She was also afraid. Both things were true simultaneously and neither one was canceling the other out.

Progress.

Six months ago, the fear would have collapsed the clarity. Six months ago she would have reached for a cross-reference text and started reading faster.

She was not reaching for a cross-reference text.

She was looking at the notice and thinking: *this is a census of weapons.*

Not the intent. She could read the intent in the phrasing — the Office of the Council believed they were writing a safety measure. The words *liaison* and *designated locations* were chosen by someone who wanted to be reassuring. She believed that person's intentions were exactly what they presented.

She also believed that the document they had produced would function, in practice, as a registry of dangerous individuals — which was to say, a list of people who could be located, monitored, and managed by anyone with access to the list. And that the words *other measures* at the end of a safety document were not an oversight. They were a door left open.

She thought about what was behind a door left open.

She turned back to her desk and picked up a piece of writing paper and a pen. She wrote:

*Elio — have you received the Registry notice? I assume yes. I assume you're already thinking about it. I'm thinking about it. When you're back from Harmonyvale I'd like to talk.*

She folded it and sealed it and wrote his name on the front and put it in the tube for the morning post. Then she sat back down and looked at her three primary source texts and the cross-reference log and the notice face-down beside them.

She turned the notice face-up again.

---

Scholar Ondine came to find her at midmorning, which meant someone had told Ondine that Mira had received a Registry notice and was sitting in an archive alcove alone instead of attending her morning practicum.

She heard the distinctive rhythm of Ondine's footsteps on the stone walkway before she saw her — three quick, one pause, a pattern from an old hip injury that Ondine never acknowledged. She arranged herself at her desk. She did not look as if she had been sitting motionless for two hours.

Ondine came through the alcove arch and looked at her for a moment. Ondine was fifty-three, with the translucent stillness of someone who had spent decades in the Memory Pool — a lightness, a depth within depth, as if you could look at her eyes and find more looking back than you'd expected. Her hair had been fully silver since before Mira arrived at the Athenaeum.

She had been Mira's primary tutor since the second month. After the Hollow Root — after Mira had come back with the others and had stood in the Memory Pool and received the full weight of what it had been trying to show her and had surfaced and said, with deliberate precision, exactly what she'd seen — Ondine had been different.

Not cold. Ondine was incapable of cold the way some people were incapable of carrying a tune. But she had grown watchful. Measured. As if waiting for Mira to become something she was not yet.

"You've read it," Ondine said.

"Yes."

"And."

Mira looked at her. "And I have questions about the methodology."

Ondine sat down in the chair across from her — the reading chair, low and soft, the chair for long sessions — and folded her hands in her lap. "The methodology."

"The registration process requires disclosure of Gate affinities and Academy assignment. It doesn't specify who has access to the completed registry. It doesn't specify retention periods. It doesn't specify the conditions under which those *other measures* mentioned in paragraph four would be triggered, or by whom." She kept her voice neutral. "These are significant omissions for a safety document."

Ondine looked at her for a moment that was exactly one breath too long. "The Councils believe — "

"I know what the Councils believe. I'd like to know what Scholar Ondine believes."

Another pause. Different from the last six months of watchfulness. This one had something reluctant in it — a door being opened from inside.

"I believe," Ondine said slowly, "that the Councils are responding to something real. The number of Gate-touched manifestations in the past year is higher than anything in recorded history. Three in Cinderwall last month alone. The Unraveling incidents — "

"How many Unraveling incidents?"

"Seven confirmed. Possibly eleven."

The fish pressed against the glass. Mira looked at the notice.

"Seven," she said.

"In six months. The last recorded Unraveling incident before this year was forty years ago. The Councils are — they're frightened, Mira. Whatever assessment you're making about this document, I'd ask you to factor in that it was written by frightened people."

"Frightened people write the most dangerous documents."

Ondine said nothing. Her hands stayed folded. Her thumbs pressed together once — hard — and released.

"I know they're frightened. I'm frightened." She put her palm flat on the desk. "A document that can be used to locate, track, and apply undefined measures to a specific class of people doesn't become less dangerous because frightened people wrote it with good intentions."

Ondine looked at her with that watchfulness. "What are you going to do?"

Mira looked at the notice. Outside the glass, the sea held its flat autumn calm.

"Register," she said. "Everyone should register. If you don't register and they find you, those undefined measures apply immediately and without arbitration. Registering at least creates a record." She picked up her pen. "I'm also going to write to the Council liaison for the Athenaeum and request the access provisions in writing, with specific questions about the conditions governing *other measures.* In formal language, with citations, through official channels, so there's a record of the request."

Ondine's expression shifted. The watchfulness eased, just at the edges — the way ice thinned before it gave.

"That's — that's not what I expected you to say."

"What did you expect?"

"You've been different since the Hollow Root." It came out like something she'd been holding for six months, finally too heavy to carry silently. "More — I don't know. Direct. You used to ask permission before you had an opinion."

Mira considered this. "The Hollow Root showed me things I'd been deliberately not looking at. It turns out that when you stop not-looking, you start seeing things that were always there. The situation with the Registry is one of them." She paused. "You've been different since I told you what I saw in the Pool. You've been careful with me."

Ondine was quiet for a moment. "You saw five figures. A convergence."

"Yes."

"The Pool doesn't show arbitrary futures." She said it the way you set down something fragile. "When it shows something to one person, deliberately — "

"I know." Mira touched the fishing cord at her wrist. "I know what it means. I'm not looking away from it anymore." She looked at Ondine directly. "What do you know about the Unraveling? Not the incident reports. What do you actually know."

The pause this time was the longest yet. Ondine looked at her hands. Then she looked up.

"Come to my office," she said. "Not now. Tomorrow morning, early, before the archive opens. There are things I should have told you two months ago. I was — " She stopped. Started again. "I was waiting to see if you'd ask."

Mira looked at her for a long moment.

"I'm asking," she said.

---

She went back to the primary source texts after Ondine left. She did not know what else to do with the hour between the conversation and whatever came next.

Leyla's accounts. The Third Age incident records. The pattern that she had been tracing with the careful incremental attention of someone who was not quite ready to see what she was tracing.

She looked at it directly, now. The way she'd learned, in the Pool, to look at things directly.

The unregistered practitioners in the historical record — the ones whose manifestations accelerated in the decades before major disruptions — shared a characteristic she had been cataloguing for two weeks without naming. Their power did not plateau the way trained Gate-touched power did. Academy training was, among other things, a form of regulation: you learned to work within your capacity, to pace your Gate opening, to develop the internal discipline that kept power from running faster than you could contain it. The unregistered practitioners in the records hadn't had that. Their manifestations showed a consistent profile in the decade before they disappeared from the record entirely: rapidly escalating, accelerating past any previously recorded ceiling, and then gone. No death notices. No Academy enrollment. Simply absent from any subsequent documentation.

She had been telling herself, for two weeks, that the absences were archival gaps. Records from the Third Age were incomplete. The Athenaeum's collection from that period had survived the Sealing War in fragmentary form, water-damaged, partially reconstructed. It was entirely plausible that the apparent absences were simply records that hadn't been preserved.

She had been telling herself this and she had known — the way you knew water was cold before your hand broke the surface — that it was not the complete explanation.

The pattern was too clean. Archival gaps were random — noise. This was signal. A consistent feature across dozens of individuals across multiple centuries. A precise point in each record where the trajectory of accelerating power simply ended.

And nothing came after.

She picked up her pen. She wrote, in the margin of her cross-reference log, in the small cramped script she used for things she was not ready to put in formal notes yet:

*Not archival gaps. Something else. What is the mechanism.*

She looked at the words. She did not write anything below them. The question was enough for today.

Outside the glass, Ondine's Second Cousin bumped its nose against the pane and drifted back and bumped it again, with the patient determined repetition of something that had been doing this exact action for its entire life and had not yet received a satisfying response and was prepared to continue indefinitely.

Mira watched it for a moment.

"I know," she said to it — more a statement about the general condition of asking questions that received no satisfying answers than an acknowledgment of the fish.

The fish bumped the glass.

---

The message from Elio arrived in the afternoon tube, while she was writing her formal inquiry to the Council liaison. It said:

*Mira — yes. Call it what it is: a list of everyone they're afraid of. I've spoken to Kael. He has something. Ask Ash when you can get him to respond to letters (working on it — Embera is apparently faster than the post). Come to Cinderwall. There's a situation here that's easier to understand in person. Ash is already there. Three new Gate-touched. One of them is Unraveling.*

She read it three times.

She sat for a moment with the cold tea and the formal inquiry half-written and the Registry notice beside it and the memory of Ondine saying: *seven confirmed. Possibly eleven.*

She looked at the window. The flat autumn sea. The bioluminescent coral, patient and bright, three hundred feet below the surface.

*Not yet but not never.*

She had said that to herself in the Pool, six months ago. Standing in the still water with the ocean's full weight around her and her hands trailing the surface and the future arriving whether she wanted it or not. She had said it to herself and she had meant it.

She finished the formal inquiry to the Council liaison. She sealed it with the official Athenaeum stamp and sent it through the tube for the afternoon post.

Then she pulled a new sheet of paper and began packing what she needed, which was not much. She had always traveled light. The Athenaeum's archives would be here when she returned, and what she needed right now was somewhere the archives couldn't help with.

She took her notes. She took the Registry notice — the one they'd sent her, specifically, with her name at the top and her Gate affinity printed below it and her Academy assignment and her current place of residence, all of it already known, already on file, already belonging to whoever had access to the file.

She folded it and put it in her pocket and she thought about what Elio had written: *call it what it is.*

One Gate-touched, Unraveling. In Cinderwall. Not forty years ago. Now.

She knew what she'd been told about Unraveling — the stages, the symptoms, the clinical progression. She had read every incident report in the Athenaeum's records, building armor out of information the way she always did. Stage One: power surges without intent, mistaken for emotional instability or physical illness. Stage Two: accelerating loss of containment, physical manifestation, emotional amplification radiating into everyone nearby. Stage Three: full dissolution — the Gate consuming the individual from inside, what the incident reports called, in their coldest language, *catastrophic cascade.*

She had read the reports. She had never seen it.

She had not, until she read the words *one of them is Unraveling,* understood how wide that gap was.

Stage Two: *emotional amplification affecting surrounding individuals.* Clinical language. Clean. What it meant was this — a Flow Gate in the same room as a Stage Two Unraveling would catch every wave of uncontrolled power the way a channel caught a current. Someone else's terror, someone else's pain, flooding through her open frequency like salt water through a crack in the hull.

And the girl. Sixteen. No training. No support. A Gate opening faster than any human nervous system could process.

Mira thought about herself at fifteen. Standing on a shore. Receiving information she didn't understand until it was too late to matter.

She put her pen down.

Scholar Ondine could wait one day. The secrets the Athenaeum had been keeping for eighty years, locked in whatever conversation Ondine was steeling herself to have, would still be there when she returned. The question she had written in her margin — *what is the mechanism* — would still be there. The cross-reference logs and the Third Age incident records and the pattern she was not quite ready to name would all be precisely where she left them, patient as deep water, available when she came back.

She was going to come back with more information than she'd left with. That was the other thing she had learned, slowly, in the past six months: that some questions were only answerable in person. That the most important information she had gathered since the Hollow Root had not come from a text.

She packed her notes. She packed the Registry notice.

She thought about leaving Ondine a message, and then she thought about the careful quality of Ondine's watchfulness for six months and she decided: no. She would be back in a few days. Ondine would figure out what had happened. And there was something she wanted to see when she returned — whether the *there are things I should have told you* held at the same temperature or shifted when Mira had demonstrated that she would leave without asking permission and come back with information gathered by her own means.

Testing a teacher's trust by disappearing was not a behavior the Mira of six months ago would have recognized. That Mira had asked permission before having opinions. She had been trying, in this past year at the Athenaeum, to become someone who needed less permission.

She was making progress.

She left the Athenaeum without formal authorization for the first time in her six months here. The door let her through on the basis of her student seal, which did not know she was leaving without permission and simply registered her departure as a departure, which it was. The door closed behind her with the soft hydraulic exhale of an institution that considered itself complete whether or not she was inside it.

The ocean was above her as she rose through the Athenaeum's exit corridor and surfaced at the shore.

Autumn had changed the light here since she arrived in spring. The sky was a different gray — denser, bruised, the kind of clouds that had crossed open ocean and arrived heavy with it. The sea was still flat. The first winter current moved against her ankles where the shore met the water.

She stood on the shore and felt Cinderwall in the north as if the distance had a texture. She felt what the water was carrying — not memory, not the Pool's specific deliberate communications, just the ambient information that had always been in any water for her, the general weather of what was happening in the world's wet places. There was something in the northern current. Something agitated, a heat-signature in the water temperature that didn't belong to the season.

Fire, she thought. Stage Two, probably. Someone's Gate burning through the banks.

She walked north along the shore until the Athenaeum's coral towers were behind her, and then she turned inland toward the road.

The fishing cord on her wrist. Her mother's voice: *the sea doesn't ask permission, girl. You learn to swim.*

She had been learning, this past six months, to stop asking permission for things that didn't require it. There were things that required it — careful things, institutional things, the kind that needed formal language and citation trails and official channels so that there was a record when the record became important. She had learned to use those channels deliberately rather than out of habit.

And there were things that simply required you to go.

Ahead: Cinderwall. Elio's handwriting. Ash, who had gone back to the Quarter that raised him and found something that was easier to understand in person. A girl whose power was eating her alive — sixteen, unregistered, alone in the Embers Quarter, with no one to tell her what was happening or why or what could stop it.

She walked north. The winter current ran with her for a while, moving the same direction, before the shore bent away and the sea fell behind her.

She thought about the Registry notice in her pocket with her name at the top, and she thought about what it meant that she was already in someone's file — already known, already located, already a designation code waiting to be assigned.

She thought about the pattern in the Third Age records. The accelerating trajectories. The clean absences where lives should have been.

*What is the mechanism.*

She walked faster.
