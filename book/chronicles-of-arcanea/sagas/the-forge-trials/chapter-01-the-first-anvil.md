# Chapter One: The First Anvil

Fire doesn't choose its fuel. It takes what's there.

Ash had figured this out at thirteen, standing in the ash-grey shell of her family's shop, watching the magistrate record the damage with the careful disinterest of someone filling out paperwork rather than documenting the end of something. She'd been standing too close — everyone said that, the magistrate and the neighbors and eventually the judge. Standing too close when it started. But they'd missed the more important detail.

She'd been standing there for an hour before it started.

Not watching the fire. Watching the walls.

The shop had been burning for years. Not with flame — with the slow dry rot of debt and silence and her father's hands shaking more every season. The fire just finished a sentence the walls had already started. She'd been able to feel that, in the hour before the first spark caught. She'd been able to feel the building wanting it. The old wood so tired of holding itself upright. The whole structure ready for the relief of falling.

She hadn't told anyone this. It didn't seem like the kind of thing courts responded well to.

She had told it to the walls of her cell, every night for four years, pressing her palm flat against the stone and feeling the cold and the dampness and the long imprisonment of the rock, the way it wanted to be other things — soil, sand, the open face of a cliff with sky above it. Stone remembered being mobile once, at a temperature that no human hand had ever touched. She'd spent four years in a cell that understood her better than any person in the facility did.

She wasn't sure what that said about her.

---

The warden's transport let her off at the base of Mount Pyralis in the early morning, with her pack, a letter of conditional release, and instructions to present herself to the Forge's intake office before midday.

He handed her the documents through the transport window. He didn't get out.

"Rehabilitation program," he said, with the intonation of someone who had learned that word and remained skeptical of it. "You complete the term, the arson conviction comes off your record. You don't complete it—"

"It goes back on," Ash said. "I understand the arrangement."

He looked at her for a moment. Seventeen, slight, with the kind of stillness that made some adults uncomfortable because it didn't read as deference. She'd been told this, in the facility, more than once, as feedback. *Your stillness reads as hostility.* She'd thought: I'll work on it. She hadn't worked on it.

"The Forge is not—" He paused, looking for a word. "It's not a comfortable place."

"I don't need comfortable."

"What I mean is it's dangerous. The program places convicted Gate-touched in intensive Academy training as an alternative to incarceration, but the Forge runs real trials. Some students don't—" Another pause. "Some students don't complete the program for reasons unrelated to choice."

She looked at the mountain.

It was still partly dark at this hour, the caldera rim catching the first light while the slopes below stayed in shadow. She'd seen Mount Pyralis in illustrations — everyone had, the famous profile, the distinctive red glow that lit the cloud base at night — but illustrations were flat and clean and the mountain was neither. It was vast and ungainly and scarred by its own history, the flanks slashed by old lava flows that had gone dark and grey like healed burns. The air smelled of sulfur and hot stone and something sharper underneath, something she didn't have a name for, a mineral clarity like the air had been through fire and come out changed.

"I know," she said.

The transport left.

She started up the path.

The path was old. Not paved — cleared, which was different, the kind of path that had been used so consistently for so long that the ground itself had given up growing things in the way. She passed scorch marks on the rock faces to her left that had no natural explanation: too high, too clean, the pattern of an exit rather than an origin, as if something had come out of the mountain here rather than something burning inward. She pressed her fingers against one of the marks in passing. Old. Years old. But the stone under the char still held the heat impression: a significant discharge, controlled, someone whose fire had that particular quality — directed rather than spread.

She took her hand back and kept walking.

Above her, the caldera rim was clearing the last of the shadow. The red glow she'd seen in every illustration resolved into something more complex at close range: not a uniform haze but layers of light, the magma below driving up a warmth that the morning air moved through and altered, so the glow wasn't static but living, responsive, breathing in the slow rhythm of something vast and deeply occupied with its own processes.

She'd never been this close to a volcano. She hadn't known you could feel them.

---

The gate to the Forge complex was forged iron — obviously — worked into patterns of flame and ascending spiral that might have been decorative but were also, she noticed, deeply structural. The flame motifs were load-bearing. The art and the function were the same thing. Whoever had designed this gate had believed, on a fundamental level, that beauty and strength were not separate projects.

She stood in front of it for a moment.

It was already open.

The intake courtyard was cut from the volcano's own stone, obsidian and basalt, dark and hot even in the early morning. Heat came up through the soles of her boots. Around the perimeter, first-year students stood in small clusters — maybe thirty of them, various ages and origins, wearing the uniform of conditional admission: grey before you earned your colors. Some of them were talking. Several were looking at the mountain with the particular expression of second thoughts.

A few were looking at her.

She found a space near the courtyard wall and put her back against it. Not defensive — she'd learned not to read her own instincts as defensiveness — just practical. She preferred not to be approached from behind. And she wanted to look at the courtyard without the courtyard looking back at her, which wasn't possible, but the attempt had value.

The obsidian walls held heat differently than any stone she'd encountered. Not storing it — conducting it. Every surface was in conversation with the volcano below, drawing and releasing warmth in cycles she could feel in her teeth. She pressed one shoulder blade against the wall and felt the whole mountain through it.

She'd expected the Forge to feel dangerous. It felt honest, which was harder to prepare for.

"Arson," said a voice to her left.

She turned. A boy, maybe her age, leaning against the same wall with his arms crossed and the studied ease of someone who'd decided to look like he owned wherever he was standing. He was handsome in an aggravating way, the kind of handsome that knew it was handsome and had incorporated this into its personality. His uniform was grey but worn with the carelessness of someone who expected it to change soon.

"Convicted arsonist in the rehabilitation track," he continued, looking at her with something not quite respect but adjacent to it. "That's how you got here. I recognize the grey with the red binding on the pack." He nodded at the documents still in her hand. "Conditional."

"And you?"

"Explosive manifestation at my family's estate." He shrugged. "Unsupervised Fire-touch. There were structural damages. My father chose to describe it as an Academy candidacy rather than an incident. It helped that we had money." He extended a hand. "Cavan. House Pyros."

She didn't take the hand. Not because of him specifically, but because she was watching the gate in her peripheral vision. Someone had come through — she'd felt the air change, the way air always changed when someone who carried serious fire came into a room. The heat signature was different. Deliberate.

"Ash," she said.

"Is that a name or a description?"

She looked at him directly for the first time. He had Fire-touch in his eyes — the faint amber quality that came with a strong gate affinity, like looking at someone with a light source behind them that didn't exist. He was strong, probably stronger than her, probably had been training in private instruction since his gate first opened. He was also, she decided, not cruel. Just accustomed to being the best thing in any given room.

He'd be recalibrating that over the next weeks.

"Both," she said.

---

The instructor who came through the gate was not what she'd expected.

She'd expected: large, loud, fire-elemental presence, the kind of teacher who made you feel the heat before they spoke. The Forge had a reputation.

What came through was a woman perhaps fifty, compact and quiet, with the kind of body that spoke of lifelong physical work — not built for display, built for the work itself. She wore her dark hair close to her skull. Her hands were scarred. She moved through the courtyard without looking at the students, which was itself a kind of looking — she was cataloguing them with peripheral attention, and Ash could feel it.

The woman stopped in the center of the courtyard. The students gathered, not because she called them, but because the gathering was correct and they knew it.

"I'm Forge-Master Bron," she said, and her voice was what her presence had promised: controlled, not loud, carrying. "First-year orientation lasts one morning. Then you're students, and students work." She looked around the courtyard with the slow thoroughness of a craftsperson checking a surface. "You came from different places with different histories. What you had before is not relevant here. What you can hold — literally hold — is what matters."

She reached into the forge bag at her hip and set an object on the floor of the courtyard. It rang against the obsidian like a bell.

An ingot. Hand-length, thumb-thick, iron alloy — Ash's best guess. Forge-worked, still carrying heat visible as a shimmer in the cool morning air.

Not orange. Not red.

White.

"The Forge's first trial," Bron said, "is not a test of power. It's a test of relationship. Specifically: your relationship with your element, as it's expressed through heat." She stepped back from the ingot. "Each of you will hold this ingot for as long as you can manage. When your element manifests — visibly, intentionally — you're done. If you can't manifest in three minutes, you're done. The ingot will be kept at this temperature throughout the day." She looked around again. "There's no passing or failing this trial. There's only what happens and what you learn from what happens."

Silence.

Then Cavan stepped forward.

Of course. First, because he was the kind of person who was always first. He picked up the ingot with both hands, and Ash watched — everyone watched — as the white-hot metal did nothing to him. Not even a flinch. His Fire affinity absorbed the heat so completely that she could see it in the air around his hands, the shimmer redirected, the energy going somewhere other than his skin.

The amber in his eyes went full gold.

Three seconds. Then flame — controlled, deliberate, a column of fire rising from his hands to waist height, orange cored with white, the ingot at its center.

He set it down.

The courtyard was quiet for a moment. The way spaces go quiet after something has been established.

He came back to stand near her. "Your turn will come," he said, not unkindly. He meant it as encouragement, she thought. He also meant: you're not going to be able to do that.

She watched six more students attempt it before her name was called. Most managed manifestation — fire, heat shimmer, one boy who produced no visible flame but left handprints burned into the ingot two centimeters deep. One girl couldn't hold it for fifteen seconds and pressed it back into the instructor's hands shaking, her palms blistered.

"Ash."

She went forward and picked up the ingot.

---

The heat was real. She hadn't expected that.

Not that she'd expected to be immune — she wasn't like Cavan, Fire-touched from birth and trained since, her Gate a wide smooth highway. Hers was a narrow cracked road she'd been walking without knowing it was a road. She'd expected to feel the heat. She hadn't expected to feel the ingot.

Not the heat of it. The history.

She stood in the courtyard with the white-hot iron in both hands and felt the ingot's life: the ore, the smelting, the hammer work, the shaping. Someone had made this. The making was still in it. Every transformation the metal had gone through, from rock to ore to iron to shaped metal, left an impression — a structural memory, embedded the way wood grain embeds the story of a tree's years.

Fire remembers, she thought, not for the first time. Everything fire touches carries the mark.

Including her.

The burns were real, starting now. She felt them on her palms — not agonizing, but honest. The kind of pain that is paying close attention.

She paid attention back.

*You've been shaped by fire,* she thought at the ingot. *So have I. Show me.*

The ingot showed her.

Not flame — not yet. Heat, moving. She could feel the thermal energy in the metal, the way it was trying to dissipate, reaching outward through her hands. Under normal circumstances, dissipation was pain. But she could feel the directionality of it — the heat wasn't random, it was searching, the same way she'd felt the walls of her family's shop searching, reaching toward some destination only fire understood.

She'd felt this her whole life. In the wood of the shop. In the walls. In the ash afterward. The fire hadn't been random destruction. It had been completion. It had been the thing the building had been trying to become, finally becoming.

She'd never found language for this either.

But she felt it now in both hands, white-hot and honest, and instead of resisting or absorbing or redirecting, she did the only thing that felt true.

She listened.

The ingot went quiet.

Not cool — it was still white-hot in her palms. But the thermal energy stopped reaching, stopped pushing. It settled. Like a held note finding its home pitch at last.

Bron was watching her. Thirty students were watching her. She didn't notice any of them because she was three minutes in and still holding and feeling the heat move through her in the way that was not pain but was something pain had been mistaken for, all these years.

Then she felt the boundary.

Not the ingot's heat. Something below it. Her own — whatever she was, whatever lived in her that she'd thought was a curse or an affliction or a trait she'd need to suppress to stay out of further legal trouble. It rose the way tide rises: not dramatically, not explosively, just inevitably.

The courtyard felt it before she saw it.

The temperature dropped.

Not cool — the air was still furnace-hot, but something had claimed the heat. The shimmer that had been radiating off the cobblestones and the obsidian walls and the thirty-one bodies in the courtyard pulled inward, toward her, the way light bends toward a lens. Students stepped back. Cavan put up a hand against the sudden suction of warmth.

She stood at the center of it with white-hot iron in her burned palms and felt her element answer for the first time with intention rather than accident.

No flame. Not yet.

But everything was listening.

Bron said, quietly: "That's enough."

Ash set the ingot down.

Her palms were blistered, the skin tight and hot. She curled her fingers. The pain was large and specific.

*Good,* she thought, which surprised her. She'd expected to feel afraid of it. She'd spent four years being afraid of fire. But she'd spent thirteen years before that loving it without knowing what the love was for, running her hands along old wood to feel the heat in the grain, sitting too close to fires that weren't hers, feeling the buildings want to become other things and wishing she could give them permission.

She was afraid. She was also, underneath the fear, something else.

Satisfied.

---

Cavan found her later, in the first-year common area, while she was wrapping her palms with the burn dressings the infirmary had given her.

"That was interesting," he said.

She didn't respond.

"I've seen strong manifestations before. I've seen fire and force and some pretty impressive destruction. I've seen one person pull all the heat out of a room." He sat down across from her. "I've never seen someone make fire wait."

She tied off the last dressing. Her hands felt distant, which the healer had said would happen. "I didn't make it wait."

"Then what did you do?"

She considered how to answer. The courtyard was mostly empty — other students were in orientation lectures, eating, processing their first trial in various ways. The light here came from channels in the wall, magma-lit, orange and steady, and it felt, as everything in this building felt, like the volcano was a living thing and they were inside it.

"I asked it what it wanted," she said.

He was quiet for a moment.

"And?"

"It wanted to be understood." She looked at her wrapped hands. "So did I."

He watched her for a beat too long, with the expression of someone revising something. "You're here on a conditional," he said. It wasn't an accusation this time. Just confirmation.

"Yes."

"The shop fire."

She looked at him directly. He didn't look away. He had the particular quality of someone who had grown up around fire — not afraid of it, genuinely not afraid, which was different from the performance of not being afraid.

"The shop was already burning," she said. "I just let it finish."

"You could have told them that."

"I did."

"They didn't believe you."

"No."

He nodded, as if this tracked with his understanding of how the world treated people who said true things that sounded impossible.

"Well," he said, standing, "you'll need to pass the next three trials before I take you seriously as competition." He said it with exactly the right amount of lightness — enough to land as humor, thin enough that she could see what was underneath it. He wanted her to be real competition. He was bored of winning easily.

She didn't tell him he'd already lost the thing he thought he'd been winning.

She also didn't tell him she could still feel the exact temperature of the ingot in both palms, through the dressings, through the numbing salve — could feel it the way you feel a song you've heard once and now can't stop hearing. The ingot had been made three weeks ago, she guessed, from the age of the impressions in it. Forged here, in the Forge's own foundry. Hammered on the anvil she could feel somewhere below her and to the left, close to the volcano's primary heat channel. She could have described the forging to someone in detail: the temperature at which the alloy had been worked, the hands that had held the hammer, the three times the iron had been returned to heat because it wasn't quite ready.

Fire-speaker, Bron had called it, to someone else, in a conversation Ash wasn't supposed to have heard.

She filed the word away.

---

That night, in her bunk in the first-year dormitory, she lay with her hands on her chest and felt the heat of the mountain through the stone below her.

The Draconis Forge hummed. Not like the Academy's usual hum — not mechanical, not ventilation. The volcano itself, somewhere in the deep rock, conducting its own vast slow business of pressure and transformation. She could feel it in her spine and her heels and the back of her skull.

She'd felt it the moment she set foot on the mountain. She'd thought it was fear.

It wasn't fear.

It was recognition.

The mountain was doing what everything made of fire did: consuming, transforming, building the world by unmaking it, creating new stone from the destruction of old stone in a cycle so enormous and so ancient it was invisible to anyone standing inside it. No beginning. No ending. Just fire doing what fire does, which was not destruction.

Completion.

She'd been convicted of arson. She was seventeen years old and she had a criminal record and burned palms and she was sleeping inside an active volcano as an alternative to a cell, and she still couldn't find it in herself to regret the shop.

She'd been standing there for an hour before the fire started. She'd felt the walls wanting it. She'd felt her father's failed life in the grain of the wood, and her mother's long-ago departure encoded in the worn threshold, and the quiet desperation of a business that had been collapsing for a decade finally, mercifully, letting itself go.

She hadn't started it. Not with any action she could name.

But she had not stopped it.

And in the moment after it started — in the twenty seconds before anyone else noticed and began shouting — she had stood in the heat and felt the shop release, felt it become something other than itself, and she had felt:

*Yes. That's right. That's what you were always meant to be.*

She'd never told anyone that part.

She told it to the mountain now, silently, in the way you tell true things to things that already know.

The mountain hummed back. Old and certain and patient in the way that only things which have been burning for millions of years can be patient.

*I know,* it said, in the language of heat and pressure and the slow turn of deep stone.

*Tell me why it burns.*

She closed her eyes.

She was going to find out.
