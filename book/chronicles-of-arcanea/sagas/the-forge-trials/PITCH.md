# The Forge Trials

## Concept

The Draconis Forge accepts everyone. Criminals, noble scions, outer-reaches Fire-touched who burned down their family businesses. It doesn't care about your history. It cares about one question:

*What are you willing to hold?*

Seventeen-year-old Ash is here on a conditional release from a juvenile facility where she spent the last four years for arson. The arson charge is technically accurate. The story behind it — the building that was already burning, the wood that wanted to be ash, her ability to hear the structural memory of fire-damaged things — is not the kind of story courts understand. She doesn't want redemption. She wants comprehension. Why does fire talk to her? What has it been saying?

The Forge will answer that question. But the answer requires surviving the Trials.

**The Forge Trials** is a three-book series structured around the Draconian Academy's trial system: a progressive tournament of Gate challenges that strips away pretension and tests what you are when everything comfortable is removed. It is a story about the difference between destruction and transformation, about a girl who has been convicted of something she didn't entirely not do, and about what it means to love a thing that others only fear.

---

## Primary Themes

- Fire as transformation, not destruction: completion vs. ending
- Criminal record as misread gift: what institutions call danger
- Physical intensity as a path to truth: the body knows before the mind does
- Competition as self-revelation: you learn who you are by being tested against who others are
- Earth/Void resonance as hidden depth: the darkness beneath fire that makes fire possible

---

## POV Characters

**Ash** (primary) — Convicted arsonist, Fire/Void-touched, age 17. Her relationship with fire is not destructive by nature but has been interpreted as destructive by everyone who observed it without her perception. The Forge is the first place she's encountered that treats fire as something to understand rather than suppress. Her arc is not redemption from the arson conviction — it's the harder work of understanding what she actually is.

**Cavan** (secondary) — Noble-born, Fire-touched from birth, trained and gifted and accustomed to winning. Doesn't start as an antagonist. Becomes Ash's genuine rival by the second book because genuine rivalry requires genuine respect, and Cavan reaches that respect faster than he expected. His arc is learning what fire tests when you've always had fire on your side.

**Forge-Master Bron** (tertiary/mentor) — Not a warm mentor. A fair one, which is rarer and more useful. She has her own history with the Draconis Forge — she went through the Crucible and survived something specific in it — but this is backstory, not plot. Her function is to name what Ash is becoming before Ash can see it.

**The Forge Itself** — The third primary POV object. The volcano has presence. The deep research chambers beneath it (Malachar's original fortress ruins) are plot, not atmosphere.

## Three-Book Arc

### Book 1: The First Anvil
*The Trials begin.* Ash arrives at the Forge, completes the First Anvil trial with an anomalous result: rather than manifesting visible fire, she pulls all thermal energy inward, making fire wait. This marks her as a type of Fire-touched the Forge hasn't seen in a long time — a Forge-Speaker, someone who can hear the structural memory encoded in fire-transformed materials. Not just heat control: an ability to read what fire has previously changed and what it could still change. This is treated as a significant gift. It is also treated as a risk factor. The first book covers the first four Trials, Ash's developing understanding of her element, her conflict with Cavan (which evolves from condescension to rivalry to the beginning of something more complex), and the discovery that the sealed chambers beneath the Forge have begun, very slightly, to unseal.

### Book 2: The Crucible
*The deeper tests.* The Crucible trial — the Forge's signature test, a chamber where students face their worst fear made manifest — is Book 2's structural center. Ash's worst fear is neither fire nor prison. It's the moment of the shop fire, replayed in full, where she stands in the heat again and feels the building release, and has to answer: was this joy? Was she glad? The answer she finds in the Crucible is not simple. The research chambers beneath the Forge are actively opening. Something in them responds to Ash's Forge-Speaker ability — specifically, it responds to her Void resonance, the Earth/Void combination that allows her to read structural memory in burned things. Malachar's research is in those chambers. She begins, accidentally, to read it. Cavan, who has different access to different risks, makes a choice that complicates everything. Book 2 ends with Ash and Cavan in the sealed chambers, and the thing that was in them already knowing who they are.

### Book 3: What Fire Becomes
*The transformation.* Malachar's research was not weapons research. It was transformation research — specifically, research into whether the Gate system itself could be restructured, whether Gates could be opened by design rather than touch. This research is dangerous because it worked, partially, in ways that produced the first Unraveled. The corruption in the chambers isn't Malachar's evil: it's his unfinished experiment, still running, still trying to complete itself. To stop it, Ash needs to do what Malachar couldn't: read the structural memory of the experiment itself — what it was, what it intended, what it needs to complete safely versus destructively. Her Forge-Speaker ability is exactly this, scaled to an enormous and dangerous task. The final Trial is completing the interrupted transformation: giving Malachar's experiment the ending it was reaching toward, safely, with full comprehension of what it wanted rather than blind confrontation. Fire doesn't destroy. It completes. Ash understands this at last, and the understanding is the only thing that works.

## Target Audience

- Primary: 16-28, readers who love physical/competitive YA and adult crossover: Leigh Bardugo, Pierce Brown, Marissa Meyer
- Secondary: Fans of the Chronicles series who want a more physical, competitive, emotionally intense corner of the Arcanea world
- Tone comparables: *Six of Crows* intensity, Hunter x Hunter structural creativity, *Avatar: The Last Airbender* (complicated relationship with a dangerous element), *The Hunger Games* (tournament structure without the gladiatorial grimness)

## Comp Titles

- *Six of Crows* (Leigh Bardugo) — Criminal protagonist, earned moral complexity, competitive ensemble
- *Red Rising* (Pierce Brown) — Tournament/trial structure, physical intensity, class conflict embedded in the competition
- *Hunter x Hunter* — Exam structure as character revelation, rule systems that reward genuine creativity over brute force
- *The Cruel Prince* (Holly Black) — Protagonist who doesn't want redemption, wants comprehension; antagonist who becomes complicated

---

## Differentiation from Main Chronicles Series

- Tighter scope: three books, one Academy, one specific trial system
- More physical and competitive than the main series's broader ensemble scope
- Explicitly addresses the Forge's dark secret (Malachar's research chambers) in a way the main series references but doesn't explore
- Ash and Cavan could appear as minor characters in the main series timeline as adults — the trilogy works as prequel material or standalone
- Tournament structure provides clear pacing: each trial is a unit, builds momentum without requiring prior Chronicles knowledge
