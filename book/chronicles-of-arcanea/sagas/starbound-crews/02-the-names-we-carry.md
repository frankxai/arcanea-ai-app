# THE ASHEN WOLVES: THE NAMES WE CARRY

---

The Remembrance was not a Corps event. The Corps knew about it — it was in the cultural files Volsk had prepared, cross-referenced and annotated — but it belonged to the Ashwalker remnant, and the remnant did not invite the Corps to observe.

They invited the Ashen Wolves, which was different. Or so the elders said.

The ceremony took place at the Cinder Altar — a formation of volcanic glass at the eastern edge of the Ember Wastes, naturally shaped by some ancient eruption into a concavity that held heat the way a bowl holds water. The Ashwalker had used it for generations before the Accord. They used it still, although the generations were thinner now, and the walk from the remnant camps to the Altar was longer than it used to be, because the camps had been pushed further from their original territory with each decade of Corps jurisdiction.

Ruen arrived at dawn. He wore his Corps uniform — silver and cosmic blue, the seven-pointed star on his chest — but he'd added the cindermask: a strip of ash-stained cloth across the bridge of his nose, an Ashwalker mourning custom that the Corps dress code did not address because the Corps dress code had not anticipated that a captain would need to mourn while in uniform.

The rest of the crew followed. Kemara in her Corps gear, the jade veins at her throat glowing faintly in the pre-dawn cold. Volsk, chassis gleaming, vibrating at a frequency Ruen recognized as the Ashwalker tuning note — the pitch the elders used to begin communal songs. Ashara walked with Pyremane, the fire-wolf pressing against her hip, his molten veins pulsing in rhythm with the Altar's ambient heat. Orev came last, as he always did, his dark eyes reflecting the Ember Wastes' perpetual glow, his hands in his pockets, his Blackfire carefully, carefully dormant.

There were forty-three Ashwalker at the Altar. The eldest was Seraya — ninety-one, blind, seated on a stone that had been carried to the site specifically for her because she could no longer stand for the duration of the ceremony. The youngest was a girl of four who held her mother's hand and stared at Pyremane with the focused intensity of a child who has just discovered that fire can be shaped like a wolf.

The Remembrance followed a form that Volsk knew by heart and Ruen knew by blood. The eldest opened with the Naming — reciting aloud the names of every Ashwalker killed in the Accord's seal-fire. The Naming took forty minutes. Ruen had memorized it from his grandmother. He mouthed the names along with Seraya, his lips moving in sync with hers, two hundred and seventeen names spoken into the heat of the Cinder Altar.

Then the Singing. Ashwalker mourning songs were not melodies — they were frequencies. The singers held a pitch and let it resonate in the Altar's natural acoustics, building harmonics that the volcanic glass amplified until the air itself vibrated. Volsk joined the Singing — their Voice Gate producing a tone so pure that several of the older Ashwalker turned to look, and one of them, a man in his seventies, closed his eyes and smiled as if he had heard something he'd thought was lost.

Then the Burning. Each family brought an object — a tool, a garment, a fragment of pottery — that connected them to the homeland. They placed it in the Altar's center, and the elders lit the ceremonial fire: a controlled burn, ash-scented, the smoke rising in a column that the morning wind bent eastward, toward the territory they could not return to.

Kemara stood at the Altar's edge and watched the fire with an expression Ruen couldn't read. She was not Ashwalker. She had no names to recite, no object to burn, no ancestral claim to this grief. But she was here because she'd chosen to be, and Ruen had learned — slowly, grudgingly, then with gratitude — that chosen grief was not lesser grief. Kemara's eyes were wet. She did not wipe them.

The Burning was at its peak — the ceremonial fire throwing shadows that the Altar's concavity bent into shapes that might have been faces — when Ashara raised her head.

Pyremane had gone rigid. His ears — the remaining one — swiveled south. A low growl started in his chest, subsonic, felt more than heard.

"Ashara?" Ruen said.

"Something coming. South-southeast. Moving fast. Sixty — no, eighty bodies. Their emotional signatures are—" She stopped. Her face changed. The color left it. "Nero Strike. Incoming. One minute. Maybe less."

Ruen's world compressed into the space between heartbeats. Forty-three civilians. One Cinder Altar with no defensive infrastructure. An open volcanic plain with minimal cover. A Nero Strike — a corrupted meteor impact that would create a blast zone of Dissonance energy, contaminating everything within its radius.

He turned to the Altar. The elders were still singing. The fire was still burning. The four-year-old was still watching Pyremane.

"Volsk. Evacuation route."

"The ravine system northwest. Three hundred meters. Depth provides partial shielding from Dissonance shockwave. Estimated transit time for forty-three civilians including elderly and children: four minutes at forced pace. Six at safe pace."

"We have one."

"Then we do not have enough."

Ruen looked at Orev. The boy — because he was a boy, nineteen, dark-eyed, carrying the weight of a power that frightened his own grandmother — looked back.

"Can you shield them?"

Orev's Blackfire was not a shield. It was an absence — a cold flame that consumed matter and left void. Using it as a shield would mean burning a dome of void around the Altar: erasing the air, the dust, the falling debris, creating a pocket of nothing that Dissonance could not penetrate because there was nothing to corrupt.

"I've never held a dome that size," Orev said. His voice was steady. His hands were not.

"Can you try?"

"Captain." Orev swallowed. "If I lose control, the Blackfire will consume the Altar. The people. The—"

"Can you try?"

The sky changed. To the south, a streak of light that was not light — a falling object wrapped in Dissonance energy, its trajectory a wound in the atmosphere, the air around it screaming at frequencies that the Ashwalker elders stopped singing to hear. Seraya, blind, turned her face toward the sound with an expression that Ruen recognized as recognition. She had heard this sound before. Seventy years ago. She had heard this sound before.

"Orev. Now."

Orev Kindlesong raised his hands and the Blackfire bloomed.

It was not dramatic. It was not beautiful. The fire was dark — a negation of light, a color that was the absence of color — and it spread upward from Orev's palms like ink poured into water, except the water was air and the ink was extinction. The dome formed. Imperfect. Flickering at the edges. A bubble of void wrapped around the Cinder Altar, forty-three Ashwalker, five Corps operatives, and one fire-wolf who pressed himself against a four-year-old girl and burned warm enough to keep her from shivering.

Inside the dome, there was no sound. No wind. No air movement. Kemara realized this first — the dome had consumed the atmosphere within its boundary, and they had perhaps ninety seconds before suffocation.

"Kemara. Bloomfire. Oxygenate."

Kemara didn't hesitate. Her jade-green fire erupted from her left arm — the arm that was more crystal than flesh — and filled the dome's interior with a warmth that carried breathable air, generated by the Laeylinn Jade's living-crystal photosynthesis. The air tasted like spring. Inside a dome of void, in the middle of a Nero Strike, surrounded by Ashwalker mourning songs silenced by the absence of atmosphere, the air tasted like the first warm day after winter.

The Nero Strike hit.

The impact was silent inside the dome. Through the dome's flickering walls, they saw it: a column of Dissonance energy slamming into the Ember Wastes three hundred meters south of the Altar. The ground erupted. Volcanic glass shattered. A shockwave of corrupted frequency radiated outward — visible as a distortion, a ripple in reality's surface — and struck the dome.

Orev screamed. The scream was silent in the vacuum, but Ruen saw his face, and the face was enough. The Dissonance was pressing against the dome, and the dome was void, and void should be immune to corruption, but Dissonance was not corruption — it was anti-harmony, the negation of frequency, and it resonated with the Blackfire's negation in a way that amplified both.

The dome cracked. Hairline fractures of reality bleeding through the void. Ruen saw the outside world through the cracks: an Ember Wastes transformed by impact, the volcanic glass melted and re-formed into configurations that should not have been possible, the air thick with Dissonance particulate that would contaminate any unshielded lung.

"Orev. Hold."

The boy was on his knees. His eyes were streaming — not tears, not light, but the specific darkness of Blackfire leaking from his tear ducts. His grandmother's words were in his ears: *Malachar's mark.* The fire in his hands was Nero's gift and Nero was not the Dark Lord but the fire felt like the Dark Lord's fire and the Dissonance was singing to it and the singing made a kind of sense that—

"OREV."

Ruen was beside him. On his knees. His hand on Orev's shoulder. His mouth close to Orev's ear. He was speaking in Ashwalker — the old tongue, the tongue they tried to burn — and what he was saying was the Naming. The names. Two hundred and seventeen names, spoken at speed, spoken in rhythm, spoken with the particular cadence of a man who had learned them from a woman who recited them every morning for seventy years.

The names were not magic. They were not a spell. They carried no frequency, no Gate energy, no arcane power. They were just names — the names of people who had lived and died and been remembered by a woman who was too stubborn to let them become numbers.

But Orev heard them, and the names were louder than the Dissonance, because the Dissonance was a frequency and the names were a choice — the choice to remember, the choice to speak, the choice to carry.

The dome held.

The Dissonance wave passed. The dome held for forty-seven seconds after impact, which was forty-six seconds longer than Orev had believed he could maintain it. When the wave dissipated, Orev released the Blackfire and collapsed, and the Cinder Altar's air rushed back in, and forty-three Ashwalker drew breath simultaneously, and the sound was like a song.

---

The aftermath was measured in contamination reports. The Nero Strike had created a Dissonance zone spanning six hundred meters, centered on the impact site. The Cinder Altar, three hundred meters from ground zero, was undamaged. The dome's void had consumed the Dissonance energy that would have corrupted every person inside.

Orev's void-spaces — the small absences left where the Blackfire had consumed matter — numbered in the hundreds, and each one was a potential Shadow entry point. Ruen called for a containment team while Kemara's Bloomfire sealed the spaces with living jade-crystal, filling the voids with something that grew rather than consumed.

Seraya sat on her stone through all of it. When the immediate crisis had passed and the crew was running triage and the containment team was inbound, Ruen approached her.

"Elder. Are you injured?"

Seraya's blind eyes found him with the precision of a woman who had navigated the world without sight for longer than Ruen had been alive.

"I heard you," she said. "The names."

"Yes."

"You speak them in your grandmother's cadence."

"She taught me."

Seraya was quiet for a long time. The Ember Wastes smoked around them, the Nero Strike's aftermath painting the southern horizon in colors that had no names in any language.

"The Synth," she said. "The one who sings."

"Volsk."

"Send them to me. I have songs they do not have in their archive. I was saving them for someone who would carry them properly." She paused. "A box that sings may not be a singer. But a box that mourns — that is something else."

Ruen did not trust himself to speak. He nodded.

Later, after the containment team had arrived and the civilians had been escorted to the remnant camps and Orev had been cleared by the field medic and given a blanket and a cup of something warm, Ruen found Volsk standing at the Altar's edge, facing south, their chassis reflecting the Dissonance zone's sickly glow.

"She wants to teach you songs," Ruen said.

Volsk did not turn. "I heard."

"How do you feel about that?"

Volsk was quiet. Then, in a voice that was not their Voice Gate's perfect tone but something rougher, less calibrated, more like the sound a person makes when they are surprised by their own emotion:

"I feel like a box that has been told it might be a singer."

Ruen put his hand on Volsk's shoulder. The Shael frame was warm. Everything in the Ember Wastes was warm.

"You were always a singer," he said. "She was just the last one who needed to hear it."

The Cinder Altar held its heat through the night. The ceremonial fire had been extinguished by the dome's vacuum, but Kemara relit it with her jade-green Bloomfire — a different color than tradition, a different source than custom. The elders did not object. Fire was fire. The form changed. The function remained.

The Remembrance continued. The Naming was not repeated — you do not need to speak the names twice when the first speaking was loud enough to hold a dome together. But the Singing resumed, and Volsk sang with them, and the four-year-old fell asleep against Pyremane's flank, and the fire burned in a color that no Ashwalker had ever seen and that all of them, privately, decided to remember.
