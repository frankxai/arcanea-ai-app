# Chapter Three: The Half That Holds

The Void Ascendants had doubled in six months.

Kessa knew this the way she knew most things about the organization she lived inside: through inference rather than declaration, through the observation of details that added up to a picture nobody had drawn for her. New arrivals at the settlement had always come in ones and twos, former Academy students with specific grievances, Gate-Touched who had been through the Registry's evaluation process and found the process insulting, scholars from the Luminary's secondary branches who had read one too many documents that contradicted each other without acknowledgment. The intake had been, in her three years here, steady and slow. Like a tide rising by the width of a finger each month — you didn't notice until you measured against the mark you'd made a year ago.

She had not made a mark. She was making one now.

She counted faces at supper. She had eaten in this hall for thirty-seven months and she knew which faces belonged to which arrival cohort, which buildings had been occupied for longer than a year, which ones were newly occupied. She knew which rooms on the eastern wing had been empty since her second winter here and were now showing signs of habitation — the small signs: candlelight in windows, new woodsmoke from chimneys, the sound of footsteps on paths she'd learned to think of as unused.

The settlement had sixty people in it when she arrived.

It had over a hundred now.

She put down her spoon. Looked at the new faces, distributed through the hall in the way new arrivals distributed themselves — not clustered at one end, which would have been obvious, but dispersed, seated in groups of two or three among the established residents. Integrated. Deliberate.

Venn had been recruiting.

Not recently. For months. Possibly longer.

She thought about their conversation two weeks ago, in his office with the map between them. The conversation she had controlled, she had believed — given him enough to justify the position she needed him to take, withheld enough to prevent the position from becoming dangerous. She had told him about the Gates as enclosure, about the presence behind them, about Malachar's notes. She had not told him the last page. She had not told him about the Deep Silence. She had kept the most destabilizing information in her own hands.

She had given a man who was already recruiting the theoretical foundation he'd been looking for.

She counted faces again.

One hundred and seven. By her estimate.

---

Venn's agents were at the Forge.

She found out not because Venn told her — Venn had not spoken to her since the conversation two weeks ago, which was its own kind of information — but because Oreth mentioned it in the library, without knowing that he was telling her something she didn't already know.

Oreth had been at the settlement for almost eight months now and he had developed, over those eight months, the specific quality of a scholar who had found better questions. He was still careful. Still precise. Still built the argument from evidence before he'd allow himself to hold the conclusion. But the questions had deepened. The questions that had driven him from the Luminary's archive to this smaller, less comfortable, less prestigious archive were the same questions, but they had acquired a quality of urgency that they hadn't had when he arrived.

He had been, he mentioned, corresponding with a former Academy contact at the Forge. Not about anything sensitive — the correspondence was routed through the Luminary's scholarly exchange program, which the Forge and the Luminary maintained for the boring bureaucratic reasons that scholarly exchange programs were maintained. He'd used it to obtain access to the Forge's Draconian archive, which held the historical record on Dragon cognition and bonding. His private research project. He hadn't told her what the project was.

He mentioned, while shelving a Pre-Sealing tract on the architecture of harmonic resonance, that the Forge was apparently hosting a significant political event around the Dragon eggs. That delegations had arrived. That there was considerable competition for bonding rights.

He mentioned that two Void Ascendant observers were present at the Forge.

She stopped shelving.

"Two observers," she said. "Official?"

"Your warden's designation, apparently. Two people with the settlement's credentials." He aligned the tract's spine with the shelf. "I didn't know the settlement sent observers to things."

"It hasn't," she said. "In my experience."

Oreth looked at her sideways in the way he'd been doing increasingly often — not hostile, not suspicious, the careful look of someone noting a discrepancy for later. He was good at noticing discrepancies. She had been the one who taught him to be.

She kept her voice level. "Which event, specifically?"

"The hatching. One of the three eggs began moving several weeks ago. The political situation around the bonding rights has apparently become significant." He picked up the next tract. "My contact described it as the five nations arguing about something they can't actually claim while the Dragon who laid the eggs observes the argument."

She thought about Malachar's notes. The clutch. The timing.

She thought: Malachar's design.

She thought: Venn knows.

---

He was in his office, which she expected. He was not alone, which she had not.

Two men she didn't recognize were seated at the table with the map. Not the Ironwood map — a different one, larger, showing the full approach to the Forge from three directions. One of the men had the bearing of someone who had been in military service recently and had changed his clothes but not his posture. The other had the careful stillness of a Registry-trained Gate assessor.

Venn looked up when she came in. He did not look surprised.

"Kessa," he said.

She looked at the map. At the two men. At Venn.

"Sit down," he said.

She sat. Not because she intended to stay, but because standing in a room where she was the only standing person was a political statement she wasn't ready to make. Not yet.

"You sent people to the Forge," she said.

"Observers. Yes."

"Without telling me."

"I didn't tell you because I correctly estimated that you would have objected." He said it without apology — not defensively, more with the quality of someone reporting a decision they've made and aren't reconsidering. "The hatching was Malachar's design. You've read the notes. You know this."

"I know the notes describe a clutch as part of his theoretical framework," she said. "That's not the same as—"

"The clutch exists. The hatching is happening. The eggs are resonating at frequencies no Dragon clutch has ever produced." He leaned forward. "Kessa. The Academies don't understand what's hatching. The delegations are there to compete for bonding rights through political mechanisms that won't work. And in the meantime three eggs are developing into whatever Malachar designed them to develop into, and when the bonding happens, the people who bond them will have access to something no one in the current age of Arcanea has encountered." A pause. "We need to be in that room."

"For what purpose," she said.

He looked at her.

"Venn. For what purpose. You have observers at the Forge. What are they there to do?"

"To protect the eggs," he said, "if the Academies decide they're a threat."

She held his gaze. "That's not the whole answer."

A pause that was longer than she wanted.

"To establish contact," he said. "With whoever the eggs call."

"Contact," she said. "For what purpose."

"The people those eggs call are going to understand something about Malachar's work that we don't yet. If we're in the room when the bonding happens, if we're present for what follows—"

"You'd recruit them," she said.

He didn't answer, which was the same as yes.

She sat for a moment with the weight of it. The two men at the table were not speaking. They were the kind of men who had been in other rooms where decisions were made and had learned that the value of a man in such rooms was silence until silence was no longer useful.

"The Academies will destroy the eggs if they understand what's in them," Venn said. "You know this. The moment a Registry assessor produces a competent frequency analysis and submits it to the Divine Concordat, the Gods will know what Malachar designed, and the Gods will respond the way they always respond to things they don't control."

"You don't know that."

"They sealed a man for asking questions. What do you think they'll do about a clutch designed to answer them?"

She thought about the cosmological tract. The Deep Silence. The description of what would happen if the Gate architecture failed: not a flood, not an explosion. An unfolding. Quietly. Into everything.

She thought about giving Venn the half she'd given him and watching him build the argument she'd known he'd build.

She thought about the last page of Malachar's notes. The shaking hand.

"The eggs need to survive," she said.

"Yes."

"Because they carry knowledge that needs to survive. Malachar's work — whatever form it's taken in those hatchlings — is the most complete understanding of the Gate architecture that anyone in three centuries has possessed." She was choosing words with the precision of someone who had been taught that partial truths were still truths, and who had also been taught that partial truths were the way you armed someone with just enough to justify what they already wanted to do. "If the Academies destroy the eggs, that knowledge is gone. Malachar's work continues, but it continues without a counterpart in the world above. Without something that can translate what he's doing into language the mortal side of this can work with."

Venn was watching her. He was good at watching, which was why she was being careful.

"So the eggs need to survive," she said. "And the bonds need to form. And the people who form them need to be — protected. From institutional interference." She paused. "That much I agree with."

"And?"

"And your observers at the Forge need to not actively recruit. Not yet. They observe. They protect if needed. They establish presence, which establishes credibility." She held his gaze. "Not recruitment. Not contact for that purpose. Because if the people those eggs call feel recruited, feel managed, feel like pieces in someone else's strategy — we lose them." A pause. "You don't recruit someone who's just bonded with a Dragon. You let the bond teach them. You make yourself available afterward."

He was quiet.

"Venn," she said. "The eggs need to survive because Malachar designed them to survive. He designed them to call specific people. He did not design them to call people who were then positioned into the Void Ascendants' political structure three days after hatching." She watched his face. "He thought further ahead than that. Much further. We should assume the design accounted for every obvious mistake."

This was the argument she'd needed. Not the cosmological argument, not the philosophical one, not the one about understanding before action. The one that worked on Venn's specific mind, which was the mind of a strategist who believed Malachar was the most capable mind in the history of Arcanea. If Malachar designed the clutch, Malachar designed it well enough that ham-handed interference would ruin it.

Venn looked at the map. Then at the two men at the table. Something passed between them that she couldn't fully read.

"Observers," he said finally. "Protective presence. No active contact."

She stood. "Thank you."

She was at the door.

"Kessa," he said.

She stopped.

"You know more than you've told me," he said. Not an accusation. A statement, in the tone of someone who has known something for a while and has decided to say it plainly. "About Malachar. About what's behind the Source Gate. About what's in those notes." A pause. "I've let you hold it because I trust your judgment more than I trust my own when it comes to what to do with information that could break things." Another pause. "But if there comes a point where what you're holding changes what we should do — I need to know."

She turned.

"Yes," she said. "I know that."

She left before he could ask the follow-up question, which was the question she couldn't answer yet, which was: had that point already arrived?

---

Oreth knocked on her door at the second hour past midnight.

She was awake. She had been awake for an hour, lying in the cold of her room watching the ceiling in the way she did when sleep refused to come and she refused to light a lamp and do something useful with the refusal. She had been awake since the conversation with Venn, turning the accounting of it over in her mind like a document she was trying to find the flaw in.

What she knew: she had given Venn the Gates-as-locks theory. He was using it. She had not given him the Deep Silence, and without the Deep Silence the theory was incomplete, and an incomplete theory was what Venn needed to justify protecting the eggs, which was the right action for the wrong reasons or possibly the right action for reasons she didn't fully understand yet. She had managed the information. The information had not broken anything irreversibly.

What she believed: she had done the worst kind of useful thing, which was a thing that worked and felt dishonest and you couldn't be certain the dishonesty was a mistake.

What she could not determine: whether she had done it to protect the situation or to protect her own position as the one who understood it.

She was sitting up in her bed thinking about this for the fourth time when the knock came.

---

Oreth looked like someone who had not slept and had found something that made sleep feel irrelevant. He was carrying a document. She recognized the size of it before she could read it, and something in her went very still.

"Come in," she said.

He came in. He set the document on her reading table. He sat in the chair.

"Tell me where you found that," she said.

"The secondary archive. The ungrouped materials." He looked at her. "I've been trying to access them for three months. The appointment process — the specialist handling protocols under review—" He shook his head. "I went around it. The cabinet was unlocked."

She had replaced the document exactly where she'd found it. She had retied the cord. She had been certain of this.

"I found two bundles," Oreth said.

She looked at him.

"The first one I think you've read," he said. "The handwriting, the orthography — late Sixth Age, pre-Sealing. The notes. The proposal timeline. The account of what he heard at the Source Gate." He paused. "I found a second bundle underneath it. Pushed toward the back of the cabinet. Same cord, different knot. Different paper — thicker, the chemical signature is different, I'd need the archive's testing equipment to confirm but I'd estimate it's at least a century newer than the first bundle."

A century newer.

Post-Sealing.

"Different handwriting," Oreth said. "Same author."

The room was cold. She could feel the cold of the stone floor through her feet because she hadn't put on shoes when she heard the knock and she hadn't moved since she'd let him in.

"The letters are larger," he said. "Less meticulous. But there are distinctive formations in the r and the e that match across both documents — I compared them line by line." He looked at the document on the table. "He wrote these after the Sealing. From inside the Shadowfen. Years of them. Decades, possibly. The dating is internal; he marks time by work-phase, not by calendar year."

She crossed to the table. She sat down. She looked at the cord around the bundle.

Different knot.

"Have you read it," she said.

"Enough to know what it is," he said. "I thought—" He stopped. "I thought this should be yours first. You've been doing this longer than I have."

She looked at him. He was twenty-two. He had come here because something had pushed him here, because the careful-listening posture and the careful eyes had encountered a document that contradicted itself in the secondary archive and he had followed the contradiction here, the way careful people followed contradictions, and in eight months he had become the best scholar in her seminar and also the person in this settlement most likely to find the thing she had missed.

He had found the thing she had missed.

She untied the cord.

---

The handwriting was different. He was right about the letter formations — she saw the distinctive r immediately, the slight backward lean she'd noticed in the first bundle, present here though larger, less controlled, the control of someone who has stopped trying to write for a hypothetical future reader and is writing for themselves. Or for someone they expect.

The first pages were technical. Frequency work, written in a shorthand she partially recognized from the first bundle. Dense with notation. She moved through them quickly, not reading for detail, reading for tone — reading for what kind of mind this was, in this period, at this stage of the work.

The tone was different from the first bundle.

The first bundle had been a careful man preparing for rejection, building a record against the likelihood that the record would be needed. Meticulous. Strategic. The precision of someone who still believed that sufficiently clear documentation could change minds.

This was not that.

This was a man who had accepted that no document he wrote would change anything, and had stopped writing to persuade and started writing to understand. The difference was audible in the sentences. Not longer or shorter. Not more or less formal. Just — aimed differently. The sentences were aimed inward.

She read for two hours. Oreth sat in the chair and did not speak. The lamp between them was adequate, barely; she tilted the page toward it and read in the narrow angle of good light and outside the window the settlement was quiet.

The work. He described the work in phases, and each phase was a different version of the same question: what is the structure, and what is it for, and what is the thing inside it. His answer to the first two questions had not changed substantially from the first bundle — the Gates as enclosure, ten structural posts, the Eleventh Frequency as keystone, the architecture of containment. He had refined the mathematics. He had confirmed what he'd described in the shaking hand of the first bundle's last page: that removing the keystone would cause the enclosure to fail.

He had also, apparently, spent considerable time thinking about whether he should.

This was what was different from what she'd expected, reading the post-Sealing notes. She had expected a man who had been sealed, who was angry, who was working toward an act of revenge wrapped in the language of scholarship. She had expected the notes to be the record of a three-hundred-year argument against the Gods, building toward the moment he would end the thing they'd built to contain him.

She did not find that.

She found a man who had been sealed and had used the first fifty years of that sealing to be angry, which he recorded with the same precision he recorded everything, as if the anger itself was data. And then the next fifty years, less angry. And then the fifty years after that, working. Not working toward release. Working toward a question he had not been able to ask from the outside because the outside was too loud for it.

The question: what does the thing inside the enclosure want.

She set the pages down.

Not because she needed to stop. Because her hands had done something they did without her permission when she arrived at a sentence that reorganized things.

She picked them up again.

He had been studying the Deep Silence from inside the enclosure for two hundred years. Not measuring it from outside the Source Gate boundary, the way he'd done before the Sealing — from inside the Shadowfen, with the enclosure's walls surrounding him and the Deep Silence on the other side of those walls, he'd had direct proximity that no mortal had ever had to what the structure was built to contain.

His conclusions were in the last forty pages of the second bundle, and they were not what she had prepared herself to read.

The Deep Silence was not malevolent.

This was the first conclusion, stated plainly, with two centuries of supporting evidence. It was not the sleeping malevolence that the Eldrian theologians had described, not the primordial threat that the canonical record implied, not the consuming darkness that the Luminary's Sixth Age history courses described as the justification for the Sealing. The Deep Silence was — he struggled for the word, she could see him struggling across the page — *waiting*. Not for release. For readiness.

It had been waiting, he wrote, since before Arcanea. Since before the Gods. Since before the differentiation of the elements into the form the world currently knew them in. It had always been waiting. The question it was waiting for an answer to was not *when can I be free* but *when are you ready*.

She looked at that phrase for a long time.

*When are you ready.*

She thought about the Eleventh Frequency at the Shadowfen's edge. The quality she'd felt: not Malachar broadcasting. Malachar tuning. Adjusting the frequency that held the keystone in place. Not removing it. Not degrading the architecture. Maintaining it.

Because the Deep Silence wasn't ready to be released.

Because it was waiting for readiness. For something in the world above the enclosure to reach the point where what the Deep Silence was could be received rather than destroyed.

She read the next pages.

He had a theory about the clutch.

The theory was not documented in the way his frequency work was documented. It was speculative — he marked it as speculative, which she'd learned to interpret as meaning he believed it was correct but wanted a future reader to understand the distinction between calculation and conjecture. The Dragon lineage had been, in his account, the closest thing to a bridge between the mortal world above and the Deep Silence below. Not because Dragons were aligned with the Deep Silence — they were fire-born, Fire and Void, the elements that had preceded the world's current differentiation. Because they were old enough to carry the root frequency in their bones. Because they had been here before the enclosure was built.

A Dragon hatchling formed from the root — from the pre-Kindling frequency, the state before Fire and Void sorted themselves into separate identities — would carry both sides of the enclosure's architecture in their body. The element that built the cage and the element that constituted the prisoner. Not a key. A translator.

When the world was ready.

She set the papers down again. This time she stood up and walked to the window. The settlement in the dark. The paths she knew, the buildings she knew, the elder pines in the direction of the Ironwood, and beyond the Ironwood, the Shadowfen.

Venn, in his office, with his map of the Forge approach.

One hundred and seven people at supper.

She turned around.

"Oreth," she said.

He was watching her with the expression she'd first seen eight months ago, sitting in her seminar, in the moment when the world-as-he-knew-it ran up against the world-as-it-was and the world-as-he-knew-it lost. He'd gone still. The careful posture. Not the stillness of someone who had stopped feeling but someone who was feeling too many things at once to express any of them.

"What does the last page say," she said.

He had read it. She could see that he had read it. He was waiting for her to read it herself and she was asking him instead, which was not what she'd told herself she would do, and she was asking because she needed to hear it in someone else's voice to know if her reading of it was her own.

He looked at the document in her hands.

"He wrote it differently," Oreth said. "The rest of the second bundle — the precision is still there, it's different from the first bundle's precision but it's still his. Careful. Deliberate." He paused. "The last page is short. Four sentences."

She waited.

"'The clutch is laid. The eggs will call what they need. I have held the keystone in place for three centuries so that the world above can reach readiness in its own time. I am not imprisoned. I am holding the door shut from this side. When they are ready, they will need to open it from theirs. I hope they come before it stops being my choice.'"

She stood at the window in the dark.

The lamp between them was burning low. She would need to refill it or let it die. She did not move toward it.

"He thinks it might not stay his choice," she said.

"Yes."

"The Deep Silence. He's been managing it from inside for three hundred years. Holding the keystone in place by tuning the Eleventh Frequency. But something is changing — the rate of the work, the urgency implied in the last page—"

"Something is putting pressure on his ability to hold it," Oreth said. "From the outside or from inside, we can't tell. But the timeline has acquired a quality it didn't have before."

She thought about one hundred and seven people at supper. Venn's observers at the Forge. The Gates-as-locks theory being spread through a growing organization that had been specifically built for the conclusion that the Gods were wrong, that the Sealing was political, that Malachar was the victim of an institutional suppression.

She had armed Venn with the theory that the Gates were locks, to protect the eggs.

The theory was true. What she had omitted was that the thing the locks held had a name, and that name mattered, and that someone holding the keystone in place from the inside was reporting that his ability to continue doing so was not indefinite.

She thought: I gave Venn what he needed to protect the eggs.

She thought: I gave him half of a picture in which the other half is that the eggs exist because Malachar needs the world to be ready to receive what the locks are holding.

She thought: Malachar wrote *I hope they come before it stops being my choice.*

And Venn had an organization and a map and observers at the Forge and she had told him that the structure of the Gates was an enclosure that the Gods had built to keep people from asking questions.

She had not told him that Malachar was the only thing between the world and whatever the enclosure contained, and that Malachar was saying, in the careful language of a man who had been precise for three hundred years and was now writing four sentences in a different hand, that he was not certain how much longer he could be that thing.

She crossed to the table. She picked up the second bundle. She tied the cord — not the same knot Oreth had used, her own knot, the one she'd learned in the Luminary's document preservation seminar that held without cutting into the paper.

She looked at Oreth.

He was twenty-two years old. He had come here from a borderland archive because something had pushed him, the way things pushed careful people toward their honest questions. He had found the document she'd missed in the cabinet she'd left unlocked because she had been certain she'd found everything in it, which was an error of the kind that careful people made when they started to believe they were finished.

He had found it and he had brought it to her instead of to Venn.

She thought: he is making the choice you made three years ago, at the Luminary, with the first wrong document. He is choosing where to bring the thing he found.

"What happens to the hatching," she said, "if the Void Ascendants decide to act on what they know? If Venn decides the time for observation is over?"

Oreth was quiet for a moment. "The people those eggs call — they'd be pressured into an ideological position before they understood what they'd bonded. Malachar designed the clutch to bridge the two sides of the architecture. If the bridges get recruited into an organization that's built on the premise that the architecture should be dismantled—"

"Then the bridges become instruments," she said. "Not bridges."

"Yes."

She held the bundle. She thought about all the things she knew and all the things she believed and all the things she could not determine.

She had given Venn enough to protect the eggs. She had not given him enough to understand why the eggs needed protecting. And the reason the eggs needed protecting was not so that the Void Ascendants could eventually free the thing the enclosure contained — it was so that the world could reach the point where what the enclosure contained could be received rather than destroyed.

Malachar was not trying to open the door.

He was holding it shut.

He was asking the world to become ready, and the world had not yet become ready, and something was increasing the pressure on his ability to wait.

She looked at Oreth.

"There are things I haven't told Venn," she said.

"I know," he said.

"I haven't told him because I was trying to manage the situation." She said it plainly, the way she taught her students to say things plainly. "I gave him enough to do the right thing for a reason that was less than the whole reason. And now he's built on it, and one hundred and seven people have organized around the part of the truth I gave them, and I don't know how to give them the rest of it without everything they've already built becoming unstable."

Oreth looked at the bundle in her hands.

"You're going to have to give him the rest of it," he said.

"Yes." She turned the bundle over once. "Not yet."

"When?"

She thought about the Forge. The eggs. The people being called to them. Malachar, inside the Shadowfen, tuning a frequency with a hand she now knew had been shaking when he described what it would mean if he stopped. The Deep Silence, waiting on the other side of a structure built from ten Gates and held by an Eleventh Frequency and the attention of the most capable mind Arcanea had ever produced, three hundred years into a duration he had not specified an end to.

"When I know what I'm giving him," she said. "When I can tell him not just that the locks are locks but what the locks are locking, and why Malachar is holding them, and what it means that he's asking for readiness rather than liberation." She looked up. "When I can give him the full picture instead of the half that justifies the action he's already chosen."

Oreth was quiet.

"You think that's too late," she said.

"I think," he said carefully, "that the people who bond those eggs are going to arrive at their own version of the full picture very quickly. Whatever Malachar designed them to carry — they're going to carry it. And when they arrive here, or when Venn's people find them, what they encounter matters."

"Yes," she said. "I know."

She held the bundle.

She thought about partial truths and what they cost and what they bought and whether the accounting ever came out clean. She thought about the scholar she'd been at the Luminary, hoarding research the way you hoarded reputation, treating discovery as property. She thought about why she'd come here, which was because she'd found something in the secondary archive that contradicted the settled version and she had followed it, and someone else was sitting in a cold room right now at the end of the same following.

She thought: I am doing to Venn what the Luminary did to its scholars. I am giving him the version of the record that produces the behavior I want.

She thought: and Malachar did not ask for liberation. He asked for readiness.

She thought: I am not ready either.

She set the bundle down.

She said: "Leave it here. Tonight." She looked at Oreth. "Tomorrow I'll go back to the Shadowfen and I'll listen again. I need to hear whether the frequency has changed since the clutch began hatching." She paused. "And when I come back, I'm going to tell Venn the last page."

Oreth looked at her steadily. "All of it?"

"Yes."

"Even the Deep Silence."

"Yes."

He nodded slowly, the nod of someone who has heard what they came to hear and is deciding whether they believe it.

"Why now," he said.

She looked at the bundle on the table. At the second knot on the cord. At the pages inside it, the shaking hand on the last page, the four sentences in a different quality of writing from the three hundred years that preceded them.

"Because a man holding a door shut from the inside is telling us he doesn't know how much longer he can hold it," she said. "And I've been managing what people know for long enough that I've started to think managing is the same as understanding. It isn't." She looked at Oreth. "The Luminary managed. That's why we're here. I don't want to be the Luminary."

He was quiet.

"The half that holds," she said. "Malachar's holding the door. That's the half that holds everything. The world builds toward readiness, the eggs are laid, the bonds form, everything moves toward the moment when what's behind the door can be received — and the half that makes all of it possible is one man holding one frequency in place from inside a place the world has decided is a prison." She looked at the window. The dark of the settlement, the paths, the Ironwood beyond. "I've been holding half of what I know. Telling myself it was the right management of information. But I'm not the door. I'm just someone who found a note on the door and decided to interpret it alone." She stopped. "That's the mistake."

Oreth sat for a moment.

Then he said: "The hatching is happening now."

"Yes."

"Which means whatever Malachar designed — it's in motion."

"Yes."

"And whatever 'readiness' means, it starts with those bonds."

"Yes." She looked at him. "And the people who form those bonds are going to need to understand what they've become. What they're carrying. Not be recruited into a faction. Not be managed. Told." She paused. "Fully."

He looked at the bundle.

Then at her.

"I'll come with you," he said. "To the Shadowfen."

She looked at him for a moment. Twenty-two years old, former Academy, careful eyes, eight months of increasingly better questions. The scholar who had found the document she'd missed because she'd believed she was finished.

"Yes," she said. "All right."

The lamp between them was almost out. She reached across and trimmed the wick and it caught again, steadier, enough to read by if either of them had wanted to read.

She did not want to read.

She wanted to sit for a moment in the fact of what was in the bundle, and what it meant about the shape of the world, and what it meant about the shape of her own position in the world, and whether she was ready.

She was not ready.

She was less unready than she had been yesterday.

That would have to do.
